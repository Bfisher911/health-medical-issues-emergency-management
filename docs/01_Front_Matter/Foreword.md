# Foreword

Emergency management and public health have long operated in parallel lanes. They share urgency, they share a commitment to saving lives, and they share the conviction that preparation matters more than reaction. Yet for much of their institutional histories, they have spoken different languages, organized along different chains of command, and answered to different authorities. The result has been a persistent gap — not in the dedication of individual professionals, but in the systems designed to coordinate them.

That gap has narrowed considerably over the past two decades. The attacks of September 11, 2001, and the anthrax letter events that followed redirected federal investment toward bioterrorism preparedness and forged new partnerships between emergency management and public health agencies. Hurricane Katrina in 2005 exposed, with brutal clarity, what happens when those partnerships fail to function under operational pressure. The H1N1 influenza pandemic of 2009 tested medical countermeasure distribution at scale. The COVID-19 pandemic of 2020 and beyond did something no previous event had done: it placed the entirety of the health system — hospitals, public health departments, long-term care facilities, outpatient clinics, behavioral health providers, and emergency medical services — into simultaneous surge for an extended and indefinite period.

Each of these events produced after-action reports, commissions, legislative amendments, and revised doctrine. Each also produced a new generation of professionals who lived through them and carry the operational memory of what went wrong, what worked, and what was discovered too late to matter.

This textbook is written for that next generation. It is also written for their instructors, their colleagues in adjacent fields, and the practitioners already working in the field who find themselves at the intersection of health and emergency management without a map.

The authors have organized this volume around a core conviction: that health is not a specialty function within emergency management. It is a fundamental concern that shapes every phase of the disaster cycle, touches every population that planners serve, and involves every agency that responds. A chapter on mass casualty triage is not separate from a chapter on health equity — it is the same subject viewed from different angles. A discussion of the legal authorities governing quarantine is not separable from a discussion of community trust in public health institutions. The medical surge capacity of a hospital system is not an abstract capacity figure; it is the outcome of years of policy choices about workforce, reimbursement, supply chains, and capital investment.

Readers will find that the authors hold two things in productive tension throughout this book. The first is operational specificity: what professionals actually do when a disaster happens, what systems they activate, what decisions they face, and what constraints shape those decisions. The second is structural honesty: an acknowledgment that disasters do not affect all communities equally, that the health system being mobilized in an emergency carries the inequities of the system that existed before the emergency, and that genuine preparedness requires grappling with those inequities rather than working around them.

This is not a simple book, because the subject is not simple. It is, however, an honest one.
