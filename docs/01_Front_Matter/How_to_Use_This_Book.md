# How to Use This Book

## For Instructors

This textbook is designed to support a full semester course in health and medical emergency management at the graduate level, or an upper-division undergraduate course with appropriate prerequisites. The six parts of the book do not all need to be covered sequentially, but Part I (Foundations) provides conceptual grounding that is assumed throughout the remaining chapters, and instructors are strongly encouraged to assign it early in the term.

A sample course structure for a 15-week graduate semester might organize the material as follows: Parts I and II in the first half of the term, establishing the legal and institutional context and the preparedness and planning frameworks; Parts III and IV in the mid-portion, building operational knowledge of specific response functions and hazard types; and Part V and VI in the final weeks, addressing the human and structural dimensions of response and applying all prior learning through case analysis and scenario exercise.

For shorter courses or modules within broader programs, instructors may select individual chapters or subsections without requiring the full arc. Each chapter is substantially self-contained, with cross-references to related material noted within the text.

The scenario exercises at the end of each chapter can be assigned as individual written exercises, small group activities, or structured tabletop discussions depending on class format. Chapter 30 is designed specifically as a multi-phase functional exercise and includes facilitator guidance.

Discussion questions range from recall and comprehension to analysis and synthesis. Instructors should select and adapt questions to match course learning objectives and student preparation levels.

## For Students

Each chapter follows a consistent structure. Reading the **Chapter Overview** and **Learning Objectives** before engaging with the main text will help orient your reading and identify the core competencies each chapter is building. After working through the body of the chapter, the **Applied Vignette** illustrates how abstract concepts play out in realistic situations — pay attention to the decisions characters in the vignette face and why they face them, not just what happens.

**Key Terms** are bolded at first use in the text and collected at the end of each chapter with concise definitions. Building fluency with this vocabulary is essential: emergency management and public health operate through shared language, and miscommunication around terminology has real operational consequences.

The **Discussion Questions** and **Scenario Exercise** at the end of each chapter are not optional enrichment — they are where the real learning happens. The scenario exercises in particular are designed to put you in the position of a practitioner who has to make decisions under uncertainty, with incomplete information, and with competing priorities. Working through them honestly, including acknowledging what you do not know, is more valuable than arriving at a confident answer.

The **Practitioner Takeaways** at the end of each chapter translate the chapter's content into operational insights. These are written for working professionals, and for students who will become working professionals, they provide a practical bridge between academic content and field application.

**A note on citation placeholders:** Throughout the text, you will see bracketed placeholders such as `[CITATION]`, `[DATA]`, and `[STATUTE]`. These indicate locations where specific data, legal citations, or research findings should be consulted in primary sources. Rather than reproducing figures that may change, the authors have flagged these locations so readers can access the most current information from authoritative sources. Instructors will direct you to relevant primary sources as appropriate.

## For Practitioners

Practitioners using this book as a reference will find the operational sections of Parts III and IV most immediately useful, along with the appendices, which compile key federal frameworks, triage systems, and crisis standards of care indicators in accessible formats. The glossary in Appendix C covers terminology that varies across disciplines and regions. The case analyses in Chapter 29 offer structured post-incident analysis that practitioners may find useful as models for after-action work.

Practitioners who are returning to formal education will find that their field experience enriches engagement with the material significantly. The scenario exercises are designed to reward operational experience rather than simply academic preparation.
