# Preface

## About This Book

This textbook emerged from a practical problem that instructors in emergency management and public health programs encounter regularly: there is no single volume that brings together the full scope of health and medical concerns across the disaster cycle at a depth appropriate for advanced undergraduate and graduate students. Existing resources tend to fall into one of two categories. Some are operational field guides, useful in practice but designed for reference rather than pedagogy. Others are academic edited volumes, rigorous in their individual chapters but variable in depth and difficult to sequence into a coherent course. A handful of emergency management textbooks include chapters on public health and medical response, but these chapters typically serve as overviews rather than substantive explorations.

This book attempts to fill that gap. It covers the breadth of health and medical concerns in emergency management — from the legal framework governing emergency declarations to the operational details of running a mass fatality incident, from the epidemiology of disaster health impacts to the behavioral health needs of displaced populations, from the basics of the Incident Command System to the complexity of crisis standards of care. It does so with enough depth to support graduate-level instruction while remaining accessible to advanced undergraduates who bring some background in either public health or emergency management.

## Intended Audience

The primary audience for this book is students in graduate programs in emergency management, public health, healthcare administration, homeland security, and related fields. It is also suitable for upper-level undergraduate courses in these disciplines, particularly where students have completed at least one introductory course. Practitioners seeking a comprehensive reference will find the operational sections directly applicable to their work, and the scenario exercises throughout the book are designed to support agency-level training and tabletop exercises as well as classroom use.

## How the Book Is Organized

The book is divided into six parts.

**Part I: Foundations** establishes the historical, institutional, legal, ethical, and epidemiological context that every subsequent chapter assumes. Readers are encouraged to work through these chapters before moving to later parts, as the conceptual framework built here — including the centrality of equity, the structure of the public health and medical system, and the legal authorities that govern emergency response — recurs throughout the book.

**Part II: Preparedness, Planning, and Incident Management** addresses how health and medical systems prepare before an event. It covers all-hazards health planning, the application of the Incident Command System to health and medical settings, and the framework of crisis standards of care. These chapters bridge the conceptual foundations of Part I with the operational content of Parts III and IV.

**Part III: Medical Response Operations** moves into the operational detail of disaster medicine and public health response. It covers mass casualty incidents, emergency medical services, hospital surge, pharmaceutical logistics, fatality management, environmental health, and shelter medicine. These are the functions that activate when an event occurs.

**Part IV: Major Health and Medical Threats** examines specific hazard categories in depth: infectious disease and pandemics, biological threats, chemical hazards, radiological and nuclear emergencies, extreme weather events, floods and hurricanes, and wildfire smoke. Each chapter applies the operational frameworks from Part III to a specific threat type.

**Part V: Human Factors, Equity, Communication, and Recovery** addresses the dimensions of disaster response that cut across all hazard types and all operational functions: risk communication, responder health, health equity and disparate impacts, long-term health recovery, and the One Health framework that connects human, animal, and environmental health.

**Part VI: Applied Learning** offers two integrative chapters. Chapter 29 provides in-depth case analyses of five major health emergency events, applying concepts from across the textbook to real-world history. Chapter 30 presents a multi-phase capstone scenario that requires students to integrate planning, operations, equity, communication, and legal reasoning simultaneously.

## Pedagogical Features

Each chapter includes a chapter overview, explicit learning objectives, substantive body sections, an applied vignette drawn from real or realistic composite scenarios, a glossary of key terms, discussion questions, a scenario exercise, and practitioner takeaways. These features are designed to support active learning in seminar formats as well as more traditional lecture-based courses.

## A Note on Citations and Data

Emergency management and public health research is a living field. The foundational frameworks described in this book — including ICS, ESF-8, the National Response Framework, crisis standards of care, and the Strategic National Stockpile — are subject to policy revision. Epidemiological estimates and capacity figures change with new data. The authors have used bracketed citation placeholders throughout the text to indicate where instructors and students should consult current primary sources rather than relying on figures that may have changed since publication. Instructors are encouraged to use these markers as teaching opportunities, directing students to relevant government databases, peer-reviewed journals, and agency publications.

## A Note on Language

The authors have made deliberate choices about terminology throughout this book, and those choices are explained in the Style Guide included in the project materials. Of particular note: the text uses "access and functional needs" rather than "special needs" to describe populations requiring specific planning accommodations, consistent with current federal guidance. It uses "behavioral health" as an inclusive term that encompasses mental health, substance use, and stress-related responses, rather than "mental health" alone. It uses "mass fatality incident" specifically for events involving large numbers of deaths, distinct from "mass casualty incident," which encompasses injured survivors as well. These and other terminology choices reflect current professional practice.
