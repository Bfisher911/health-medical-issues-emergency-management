# Chapter 1: The Scope and History of Emergency Management and Public Health

**Part I: Foundations**

---

## Chapter Overview

Emergency management and public health have traveled parallel tracks for most of their institutional histories, occasionally converging in crisis and then drifting apart once the immediate threat passed. This chapter traces both fields from their earliest formal expressions through the twentieth century and into the complex, multi-hazard landscape of the twenty-first. The goal is not simply to tell a history but to explain why that history matters for practice today. How a field defines its mission, builds its institutions, and distributes its resources shapes what it can and cannot do under pressure. Students who understand where these two systems came from will be better positioned to recognize the institutional fault lines that still produce failures and to design the bridges that can cross them.

The chapter begins with the origins of organized disaster response, moves through the founding of federal emergency management and modern public health infrastructure, examines the landmark events that forced closer integration, and concludes with a working definition of the field this textbook addresses: the health and medical dimensions of emergency management.

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, students will be able to:

5. Articulate the primary tension between the all-hazards and threat-specific approaches to emergency preparedness.

## 1.1 Before There Were Agencies: Early Disaster Response in American History

The desire to organize a collective response to catastrophe is as old as organized society itself. What has changed over time is which institution bears the primary obligation to respond, how that obligation is funded, and how systematically the lessons of one disaster are carried into preparation for the next.

In the colonial and early national periods of American history, disaster response was a local and deeply informal enterprise. When fire destroyed much of Boston in 1760, relief efforts were organized by churches, mutual aid societies, and wealthy merchants acting through voluntary association. When yellow fever swept through Philadelphia in 1793, killing an estimated [DATA: percentage of population] in a matter of months, the responses mixed heroic voluntary nursing by the Free African Society with the flight of wealthy residents and the near-collapse of city government. There was no federal public health apparatus to call on. The argument over whether yellow fever was contagious or whether it arose from miasmas in the local environment was not merely academic; it determined whether the city would quarantine incoming ships or instead focus on cleaning the streets, and the answer varied by who was doing the deciding.

Congress passed the first federal disaster relief legislation in 1803, authorizing the suspension of customs duties for a town in New Hampshire destroyed by fire [CITATION: Congressional Globe or National Archives reference]. This act is often cited as the earliest expression of federal disaster engagement, but it was purely a financial accommodation, not the establishment of any permanent capacity. Through much of the nineteenth century, federal disaster response remained episodic, reactive, and bound to the political calculations of the moment. The Army Corps of Engineers, authorized under various rivers and harbors acts, became an early federal presence in flood control and disaster-related construction, but its mandate was infrastructure, not human welfare.

The late nineteenth century saw two developments that would eventually converge. In emergency response, the American National Red Cross was chartered by Congress in 1881, providing the first durable national voluntary organization oriented toward disaster relief. Clara Barton's model emphasized rapid deployment of resources and trained volunteers—a logistical innovation at the time [CITATION: scholarly source on Red Cross history]. In public health, the period from 1880 through 1910 saw the establishment of state and local health boards empowered to conduct quarantine, investigate disease outbreaks, and enforce sanitation codes. The germ theory of disease, increasingly validated through the 1880s, gave public health a clearer scientific foundation and a stronger argument for government action.

The 1906 San Francisco earthquake and fire provided an early illustration of what integrated response could look like under extreme pressure—and of its limits. Military personnel from the Presidio responded rapidly and in force. Local government effectively ceased to function for days. The Red Cross coordinated relief supplies. But public health was an afterthought; the makeshift refugee camps that formed across Golden Gate Park became vectors for typhoid and other communicable diseases before adequate sanitation could be established [CITATION: historical account of 1906 earthquake public health response]. The lesson—that the immediate physical disaster and the subsequent public health emergency require coordinated attention—would be relearned many times over the following century.

## 1.2 The Twentieth Century: Institutionalization and Divergence

The first half of the twentieth century accelerated the institutionalization of both fields without necessarily bringing them closer together. Emergency management grew through the lens of war, civil defense, and flood control. Public health grew through the lens of infectious disease control, maternal and child health, and eventually chronic disease prevention.

### 1.2.1 Civil Defense and the Cold War Framework

The experience of World War II shaped American emergency management profoundly. The Federal Civil Defense Administration, established in 1950, oriented preparedness almost entirely toward the threat of nuclear attack. Bomb shelters, evacuation planning, and the famous "duck and cover" school drills were products of this era [CITATION: civil defense historical source]. This framework had lasting consequences. It embedded a federal-to-local command structure in preparedness thinking. It also embedded an assumption that the primary emergency was a large-scale, sudden-onset event with a clear external enemy—an assumption that fit poorly with floods, hurricanes, industrial accidents, and certainly with pandemic disease.

The Federal Disaster Relief Act of 1950 established the first standing framework for federal disaster declarations and assistance, but it was modest in scope and execution [CITATION: federal disaster relief legislation source]. Over the next three decades, disaster assistance was scattered across more than 100 federal agencies, with no coordinating mechanism and no consistent approach to mitigation or preparedness. A presidential commission report in the late 1970s described the situation as "a patchwork quilt" of programs that served neither efficiency nor equity [CITATION: pre-FEMA review source].

FEMA—the Federal Emergency Management Agency—was established by executive order in 1979, consolidating functions from five different agencies under a single director [CITATION: Executive Order 12127]. The consolidation was a genuine administrative achievement, but FEMA's early years were troubled by resource shortfalls, political appointee leadership with limited emergency management expertise, and the lingering pull of the civil defense mission. The agency's slow and widely criticized response to Hurricane Hugo in 1989 and Hurricane Andrew in 1992 exposed structural weaknesses that a simple reorganization had not resolved. The post-Andrew analysis led to reforms that eventually shaped the National Response Plan and its successors.

### 1.2.2 Public Health Infrastructure in the Twentieth Century

Public health followed its own trajectory. The U.S. Public Health Service, with roots in the Marine Hospital Service established in 1798 for the care of merchant seamen, grew steadily in authority and scope [CITATION: PHS historical source]. The Public Health Service Act of 1944 reorganized and consolidated federal public health functions and remains the foundational statutory authority for much of what HHS does today, including emergency authorities invoked during public health crises.

The Centers for Disease Control, established in 1946 in Atlanta as the Communicable Disease Center, grew from a malaria-control program and gradually expanded its scope to include virtually all infectious diseases and eventually chronic disease, injury, and environmental health [CITATION: CDC founding history]. The Epidemic Intelligence Service, founded in 1951, created a cadre of disease detectives whose methods became the template for outbreak investigation worldwide [CITATION: EIS history].

The twentieth century brought remarkable successes: smallpox eradication was declared in 1980, polio was eliminated from the Western Hemisphere, and vaccines for a series of childhood diseases dramatically reduced morbidity and mortality. But public health infrastructure at the state and local level remained chronically underfunded, with wide variation in capacity across jurisdictions. When the next major challenge came—as it inevitably would—this variation would matter enormously.

## 1.3 Convergence Through Crisis: Landmark Events That Reshaped the Relationship

The relationship between emergency management and public health was not gradually built through policy design. It was largely forged through shared experience of catastrophic events that exposed the cost of separation and demanded improvised integration.

### 1.3.1 The HIV/AIDS Epidemic and Public Health Emergency Frameworks

The HIV/AIDS epidemic that emerged in the early 1980s did not fit the emergency management framework at all. It was not a sudden event. It had no geographic boundary. It could not be addressed by evacuating a population or establishing a perimeter. What it required was sustained surveillance, rapid scientific investigation, resource mobilization at a scale government had not previously contemplated for a disease, and communication with communities whose members faced stigma that compounded physical vulnerability.

The public health response to HIV/AIDS produced lasting institutional innovations: expanded authorities for public health emergencies, greater investment in laboratory capacity, and new models for community-based outreach and service delivery [CITATION: public health emergency law review]. It also revealed, with painful clarity, how social inequities determined who bore the burden of a health emergency—a lesson that would apply across every subsequent disaster.

### 1.3.2 September 11, 2001, and the Bioterrorism Inflection

The attacks of September 11, 2001, and especially the anthrax letter attacks that followed weeks later, forced a rapid and dramatic expansion of the emergency management-public health interface. The anthrax letters killed five people and sickened seventeen, but they triggered a national alarm about bioterrorism preparedness that was far disproportionate to the immediate casualty count [CITATION: GAO or public health preparedness report on anthrax]. Congress passed the Public Health Security and Bioterrorism Preparedness and Response Act of 2002, investing billions of dollars in state and local public health preparedness, the Strategic National Stockpile, and hospital preparedness through what became the Hospital Preparedness Program [CITATION: Bioterrorism Act 2002].

The Department of Homeland Security was established in 2003, consolidating 22 agencies—including FEMA—under a single cabinet department organized around counterterrorism. This reorganization created immediate friction with the existing federal public health infrastructure. HHS retained authority over public health emergencies and maintained the Office of the Assistant Secretary for Preparedness and Response (ASPR), but coordination with DHS was imperfect from the start. The question of who was in charge of what during a biological emergency—a question that remains contested—had its roots in these post-9/11 organizational decisions.

### 1.3.3 Hurricane Katrina and the Equity Reckoning

Hurricane Katrina made landfall on August 29, 2005, and the failures that followed reshaped emergency management more profoundly than any event since the creation of FEMA. The death toll exceeded 1,800 people. The failures were failures of coordination, resource deployment, communication, and leadership, but they were also—unmistakably—failures of equity [CITATION: congressional report on Katrina response].

The people who died in the Superdome, who were stranded on rooftops for days, who lacked transportation to evacuate, who had no one to call—were disproportionately Black, elderly, disabled, and poor. The disparate impact was not a coincidence or merely a product of geography. It reflected decades of decisions about where to build flood control infrastructure, which communities to invest in, and whose needs to prioritize in evacuation planning. Katrina forced emergency management to confront the relationship between its practices and the social structures that determine who is most vulnerable.

The Post-Katrina Emergency Management Reform Act of 2006 restored FEMA's cabinet-level access and strengthened its authorities, but the more durable legacy of Katrina may be the incorporation of equity into federal emergency management doctrine [CITATION: PKEMRA citation]. Subsequent iterations of the National Response Framework, FEMA's strategic plans, and preparedness guidance have progressively embedded language about access and functional needs, whole community planning, and reducing disparate impact.

### 1.3.4 The H1N1 Influenza Pandemic of 2009

The 2009 H1N1 influenza pandemic was the first true test of the bioterrorism-era public health preparedness infrastructure. The response demonstrated measurable progress: surveillance systems identified the novel strain rapidly, the Strategic National Stockpile was activated, and a vaccine was developed and distributed within months—faster than any prior influenza vaccine development [CITATION: CDC after-action report on H1N1].

But the response also revealed gaps. Vaccine distribution was uneven. Communication about the pandemic's severity was inconsistent and at times counterproductive. The healthcare system was strained in ways that exposed thin surge capacity, particularly in outpatient and primary care settings. And the populations that bore the heaviest burden—pregnant women, people with underlying health conditions, and some racial and ethnic minority groups—were not always the first to receive resources [CITATION: equity analysis of H1N1 response].

The H1N1 experience produced improvements in surveillance, logistics, and inter-governmental coordination that would be drawn on in subsequent responses. It also reinforced the value of prior investment in preparedness infrastructure, demonstrating that capacity built in relative quiet has real operational value when crisis arrives.

### 1.3.5 COVID-19: A System-Level Test

The COVID-19 pandemic beginning in 2020 subjected every element of the public health-emergency management interface to stress at a scale not seen in a century. The pandemic is addressed in detail in Part IV of this textbook. For the purposes of this historical chapter, it is enough to note that COVID-19 confirmed with brutal clarity several lessons that prior disasters had only partially taught: that chronic underinvestment in public health infrastructure has a measurable cost; that trust, communication, and community engagement are as important as medical countermeasures; that inequities in the baseline health of populations translate directly into inequities in disaster outcomes; and that no jurisdiction can fully protect its population without functional coordination across levels of government, across sectors, and across national boundaries.

## 1.4 Defining the Field: What "Health and Medical Issues of Emergency Management" Means

The phrase "health and medical issues of emergency management" requires careful definition because it is genuinely broad, and that breadth is intentional.

Medical issues in emergency management are most intuitive: they include the clinical care provided to disaster victims, the mass casualty management protocols that govern triage and treatment when demand exceeds capacity, the evacuation and transport of patients with pre-existing medical needs, and the logistical management of medicines, equipment, and personnel across a disaster response.

But health issues extend further. They include the surveillance functions that detect emerging threats early enough to act. They include the social determinants that make some populations more vulnerable to disaster impacts in the first place. They include the mental health sequelae that persist for years after a community has physically rebuilt. They include environmental contamination, food and water safety, vector-borne disease risk that spikes after flooding, and the population-level effects of displacement on chronic disease management.

This textbook adopts a working definition grounded in three commitments. First, health and medical considerations are relevant across all phases of the emergency management cycle—not only in response but in mitigation, preparedness, and long-term recovery. A community that has reduced its baseline health disparities will recover from a disaster faster than one that has not. A hospital that has pre-positioned its surge capacity plan will perform better when activated than one that has not. Second, the field demands both clinical and population-level thinking. Individual patient care and community health are complementary, not competing, frameworks. Third, equity is not an add-on. The distribution of disaster risk and disaster burden is not random; it reflects prior decisions about investment, land use, social policy, and political representation, and effective practice requires engaging those determinants directly.

## 1.5 All-Hazards Versus Threat-Specific Approaches

A practical tension runs through the entire history of the field. Should preparedness be organized around specific threats—bioterrorism, hurricanes, pandemic influenza—or around the generic capacities that are useful across any threat type?

The all-hazards approach, which has been the organizing principle of federal emergency preparedness doctrine since at least the early 2000s, argues that core capabilities—command and coordination, communications, logistics, public information, medical surge—are relevant regardless of the specific hazard. This approach produces more efficient investment and avoids the problem of preparing only for the last disaster. It also makes preparedness more sustainable politically, because it is harder to justify sustained investment in, say, bioterrorism preparedness during years when no bioterrorism events occur.

The threat-specific critique holds that all-hazards frameworks can obscure the ways in which different hazards require genuinely different responses. A biological event managed primarily through non-pharmaceutical interventions—school closures, quarantine, masking—looks nothing like a hurricane response. Radiation exposure requires specialized knowledge and equipment that is not "generic." Chemical mass casualty events demand specific antidotes and decontamination protocols that cannot be improvised from general emergency management principles.

The practical resolution is a hybrid: an all-hazards platform of core capabilities, overlaid with threat-specific annexes, specialized training, and targeted stockpiles for high-consequence hazards. This textbook reflects that hybrid approach. Core concepts of emergency management structure, healthcare system preparedness, and population health protection appear throughout; specific chapters address the particular challenges of pandemic disease, chemical and radiological events, extreme weather, and mass violence.

## Applied Vignette: A County Health Department in 1953 and in 2005

Imagine a county health department in a mid-sized southern county. In 1953, the department's primary responsibilities are tuberculosis control, maternal and infant health clinics, restaurant inspections, and mosquito abatement. The director has a standing relationship with the county civil defense coordinator, but they rarely meet; civil defense is oriented toward nuclear attack and the health department's role in that scenario is limited to planning for mass casualty triage in the event of a blast. The health department has no plan for responding to a flood that displaces thousands of residents, because floods in this county are not frequent, and no one has asked the health department to develop one.

By 2005, the same county health department has an emergency preparedness coordinator funded by a CDC bioterrorism grant. It has a memorandum of understanding with the regional hospital for medical surge. It participates in quarterly tabletop exercises coordinated by the state emergency management agency. Its health officer has a seat in the county Emergency Operations Center.

When a major hurricane strikes in 2005 and the county takes a direct hit, the health department activates its Emergency Operations Plan for the first time in its history. The preparedness infrastructure—imperfect as it is—functions better than it would have in 1953. The medical needs shelter opens within 12 hours. The coordination with the regional hospital on patient tracking begins on day two rather than day five. The water quality monitoring protocols are activated immediately.

But gaps appear quickly. The plans did not adequately account for residents who lacked transportation or did not speak English. The mental health support resources identified in the plan turn out to be mostly unavailable because the mental health system, too, has been disrupted by the storm. The coordinator is exhausted by day three because she is the only person who knows the whole plan.

The vignette illustrates a theme that recurs across this textbook: preparedness investment is real and its value can be observed in disasters. But plans are tested by the populations and conditions they did not fully anticipate, and the gap between the plan and the reality often falls hardest on the people who were already most vulnerable.

## Key Terms

**All-hazards approach:** A preparedness philosophy that builds core capabilities applicable across multiple threat types rather than planning exclusively for specific hazards.

**Civil defense:** Government programs, particularly during the Cold War era, oriented toward protecting civilian populations from the effects of military attack, including nuclear weapons.

**Emergency Support Function (ESF):** A framework within the National Response Framework that groups federal capabilities and resources by function; ESF-8 covers public health and medical services.

**Epidemic Intelligence Service (EIS):** A CDC training program established in 1951 that trains disease investigators and has become a global model for applied epidemiology.

**Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA):** The primary federal agency responsible for coordinating disaster preparedness, response, recovery, and mitigation in the United States, established in 1979.

**National Response Framework (NRF):** The federal doctrine that guides how the nation responds to all types of disasters and emergencies; the current version emphasizes whole-community engagement and scalable response.

**Post-Katrina Emergency Management Reform Act (PKEMRA):** 2006 legislation that strengthened FEMA's authorities and independence following the widely criticized federal response to Hurricane Katrina.

**Public health emergency:** A formal declaration, issued under statutory authority, that unlocks specific federal or state powers and resources in response to a significant threat to population health.

**Social determinants of health:** The conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age, which shape health outcomes more powerfully than clinical care alone.

**Strategic National Stockpile (SNS):** A federal repository of medicines, vaccines, and medical supplies maintained for deployment during public health emergencies.

## Practitioner Takeaways

- The institutional separation between emergency management and public health is structural, not accidental. Effective practitioners understand the history behind each system's design and use that understanding to build bridges where the systems are weakest.

- The all-hazards framework provides a useful organizational platform, but practitioners should resist the temptation to treat it as a complete answer. Threat-specific annexes, specialized training, and targeted resource stockpiles are necessary complements.

- Equity is not a secondary concern. The populations that bear disproportionate disaster risk almost always experience that risk because of pre-existing social conditions. Preparedness plans that do not explicitly account for those populations will underperform when tested.

- History is not merely interesting; it is practically useful. The after-action reports, academic analyses, and case studies that document past disaster responses are among the most valuable resources available to a preparedness practitioner. Reading them critically—not to celebrate what went right, but to understand what failed and why—is a core professional skill.

- Relationships built before disasters are more durable than relationships improvised during them. The county health officer who knows the hospital's emergency preparedness coordinator by name, and who has sat through exercises together, will coordinate more effectively under pressure than one who is exchanging introductory emails on the night a storm makes landfall.
