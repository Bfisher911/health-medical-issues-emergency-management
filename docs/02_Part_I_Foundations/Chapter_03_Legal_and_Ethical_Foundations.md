# Chapter 3: Legal and Ethical Foundations

---

## Chapter Overview

Every decision made during a health emergency sits inside a legal and ethical frame, whether the person making it recognizes that or not. The physician choosing which patient receives the last ventilator, the public health officer issuing a mandatory quarantine order, the emergency manager activating a point of dispensing — each act carries legal authority, legal risk, and an ethical dimension that cannot be separated from the operational one.

This chapter examines the legal architecture that governs health emergencies in the United States, with particular attention to federal statutes beyond those covered in Chapter 2: the Pandemic and All-Hazards Preparedness Act (PAHPA), the Public Readiness and Emergency Preparedness (PREP) Act, and the Emergency Use Authorization (EUA) process. It then turns to the constitutional limits of emergency authority and the tensions between public health power and individual rights. The second half of the chapter addresses ethical frameworks — not as abstract philosophy, but as practical tools for structuring the reasoning behind difficult decisions. The chapter concludes with a treatment of professional obligations during disasters, including the duty to treat, abandonment, and the legal protections that exist for responders acting in good faith under emergency conditions.

Understanding the legal and ethical foundations of health emergency management is not optional for practitioners. It is the difference between confident, defensible decision-making and improvised action that exposes individuals and agencies to liability, erodes public trust, and violates the rights of the people being served.

## 3.1 The Pandemic and All-Hazards Preparedness Act

The Pandemic and All-Hazards Preparedness Act (PAHPA), enacted in 2006, was the federal government's most significant structural response to the failures exposed by Hurricane Katrina and the ongoing concerns about pandemic influenza and bioterrorism. It created the Office of the Assistant Secretary for Preparedness and Response (ASPR) within the Department of Health and Human Services, established the Biomedical Advanced Research and Development Authority (BARDA), and reorganized the federal medical countermeasure enterprise [CITATION: PAHPA, P.L. 109-417, 2006].

ASPR was designed to fill a gap that Katrina made undeniable: HHS lacked a single senior official with operational authority, clear accountability, and the ability to coordinate across the department's many components during a disaster. Prior to PAHPA, emergency functions were distributed across CDC, HRSA, SAMHSA, and other components without a unified operational command. ASPR brought that function under one roof, though the coordination challenges have never been fully resolved [CITATION: GAO review of ASPR coordination].

BARDA addressed a different gap: the market failure in medical countermeasure development. Private pharmaceutical companies have limited commercial incentive to develop vaccines and therapeutics for rare or sporadic threats like anthrax, smallpox, or pandemic influenza. BARDA was given authority and funding to enter long-term contracts with manufacturers, invest in advanced development, and sustain the production infrastructure needed to respond to threats that the market alone would not support [CITATION: BARDA mission description, HHS website]. This was the architecture that enabled the extraordinary speed of COVID-19 vaccine development — the underlying platform investments and manufacturing relationships had been built over years before the pandemic arrived.

PAHPA has been reauthorized and amended multiple times, including through the Pandemic and All-Hazards Preparedness Reauthorization Act (PAHPRA) in 2013 and subsequent reauthorizations. Each reauthorization has adjusted authorities, funding levels, and organizational responsibilities in response to lessons from the H1N1 response (2009), MERS, Ebola (2014-2016), and Zika [CITATION: PAHPRA 2013, P.L. 113-5; subsequent reauthorization history]. Emergency managers and public health practitioners need to understand the current version of this statutory framework, not just its original form.

## 3.2 The PREP Act and Liability Protection

The Public Readiness and Emergency Preparedness Act (PREP Act) of 2005 created a liability protection system for the administration of medical countermeasures during declared public health emergencies [CITATION: PREP Act, 42 U.S.C. § 247d-6d]. Its practical purpose is to remove a barrier to large-scale emergency response: if administering a vaccine or antidote during a mass casualty event carries the same tort liability as routine clinical care, healthcare providers and pharmacists will hesitate. The PREP Act addresses that hesitation by providing broad immunity from federal and state liability for "covered persons" administering "covered countermeasures" under an active PREP Act declaration issued by the HHS Secretary.

The immunity is not absolute. The PREP Act creates a narrow exception for willful misconduct — intentional, deliberate harm is not protected — and it establishes a Countermeasures Injury Compensation Program (CICP) to provide no-fault compensation to individuals who suffer serious injuries from covered countermeasures [CITATION: CICP program description, HRSA]. The CICP has been criticized as inadequate in practice: its benefit levels are lower than traditional tort remedies, the burden of proof falls on the claimant, and the administrative process is slow [CITATION: CICP assessment literature].

PREP Act declarations were issued with significant breadth during the COVID-19 response, extending coverage to pharmacists, pharmacy technicians, and others administering vaccines in settings not traditionally associated with immunization practice [CITATION: HHS PREP Act declarations, 2020-2021]. This raised important questions about the scope of professional practice law, since pharmacy practice acts vary by state, and a federal PREP Act declaration does not automatically resolve scope-of-practice conflicts under state law. The interplay between federal immunity and state professional regulation is an area of ongoing legal uncertainty that practitioners administering countermeasures need to understand before events occur, not during them.

## 3.3 Emergency Use Authorizations

The Emergency Use Authorization (EUA) is a regulatory mechanism administered by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) that allows unapproved medical products — or approved products for unapproved uses — to be used during a declared emergency when there is no adequate, approved alternative [CITATION: EUA authority, 21 U.S.C. § 360bbb-3]. The Secretary of HHS must first declare that circumstances exist justifying an EUA; FDA then evaluates whether the available evidence suggests the product may be effective and that the known and potential benefits outweigh the known and potential risks.

EUAs are not the same as full FDA approval. Products authorized under an EUA have not completed the full clinical trial process and long-term safety follow-up that approval requires. This creates an ethical tension that is especially acute for vaccine products: the public is being asked to accept a product with a shorter evidence record than they might expect, during a period of fear and information chaos, with pressure from employers, institutions, and governments to comply. Informed consent under EUA conditions is legally required — the statute specifies that individuals must be informed of the option to accept or refuse — but informed consent in a pandemic is not the same as informed consent in a clinic with a familiar provider, private consultation, and time for questions [CITATION: EUA informed consent requirements].

The COVID-19 response produced an unprecedented volume of EUAs: diagnostics, therapeutics, vaccines, and protective equipment were all authorized under this framework. The rapid evolution of the evidence base — and the revocation of some EUAs when products proved ineffective (hydroxychloroquine) — created public communication challenges and contributed to vaccine hesitancy in some communities [CITATION: HCQ EUA revocation, FDA]. This is an object lesson in how regulatory decisions interact with public trust: the speed that is a virtue in emergency response can become a liability when the public perceives it as corner-cutting.

## 3.4 Constitutional Constraints on Emergency Health Authority

Emergency health authority is real and broad, but it is not unlimited. The federal Constitution and the constitutions of all fifty states impose constraints that apply even during declared emergencies.

**Police Power and the Tenth Amendment.** Health and safety regulation is a traditional function of state government. The Tenth Amendment reserves to states powers not delegated to the federal government, and the federal government has no direct police power to quarantine individuals within states. Federal authority in this domain operates through spending power (attaching conditions to federal funds), the commerce power (regulating movement across state lines), and treaty power (international health regulations). When the federal government wishes to impose public health measures on state residents, it generally must act through or with the states.

**Substantive Due Process.** Deprivations of liberty — including mandatory quarantine and isolation — require procedural protections under the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments. The minimum requirements include notice, an opportunity to be heard, and a hearing before a neutral decision-maker within a reasonable time. The quarantine orders issued during COVID-19 varied enormously in their procedural rigor, and some were challenged in court successfully [CITATION: COVID quarantine litigation review]. The 1905 Supreme Court decision in Jacobson v. Massachusetts, which upheld a mandatory smallpox vaccination requirement, is frequently cited as establishing broad state police power in public health, but it included important limiting language: the police power cannot be exercised in an "arbitrary, oppressive or unreasonable" manner [CITATION: Jacobson v. Massachusetts, 197 U.S. 11 (1905)].

**First Amendment Implications.** Emergency orders that restrict gatherings implicate First Amendment assembly rights. Courts have generally applied intermediate scrutiny to such restrictions when they are content-neutral and narrowly tailored, but pandemic-era restrictions on religious gatherings generated significant litigation and ultimately Supreme Court intervention [CITATION: Tandon v. Newsom; Roman Catholic Diocese of Brooklyn v. Cuomo, 2020]. Emergency managers and public health officials need to be aware that restrictions on expressive activity, religious practice, and assembly will be scrutinized more carefully than restrictions on commercial activity.

**Equal Protection.** Emergency measures cannot be applied in discriminatory ways. Race, national origin, religion, and other protected characteristics cannot be the basis for differential application of health emergency authority. Historical examples — including the quarantine of Chinese immigrants in San Francisco during the 1900 bubonic plague outbreak — stand as warnings about how emergency authority can be used to target vulnerable communities [CITATION: historical quarantine discrimination literature]. During COVID-19, enforcement patterns for masking and social distancing orders drew scrutiny in some jurisdictions for disparate impact on communities of color [CITATION: disparate enforcement documentation].

**ADA and Section 504.** As discussed briefly in Chapter 2, disability rights law applies in emergencies. Crisis Standards of Care protocols that categorically exclude individuals based on disability status or long-term prognosis have been challenged under the ADA and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act. The HHS Office for Civil Rights issued guidance during COVID-19 that CSC protocols must not use disability, including categorical exclusions based on underlying conditions, as a standalone basis for allocation decisions [CITATION: HHS OCR bulletin, 2020]. This constraint is not merely aspirational — it is enforceable.

## 3.5 Ethical Frameworks for Impossible Decisions

Legal authority determines what a public health official or healthcare provider may do. Ethics determines what they should do. These are not the same question, and the gap between them is where the hardest decisions in health emergency management live.

There is no single ethical framework that all practitioners accept, and no framework that produces clean answers to genuinely tragic choices. What ethical frameworks provide is a structured way to reason through decisions, to identify what values are being prioritized and what values are being sacrificed, and to document that reasoning in a way that can be reviewed, challenged, and improved.

**Utilitarianism** is the most intuitive framework in mass casualty contexts. It directs decision-makers to take actions that produce the greatest good for the greatest number. In triage, this often translates to prioritizing patients who have the greatest probability of survival and the greatest benefit from immediate intervention — precisely the logic underlying the START and SALT triage systems. A utilitarian calculus supports ventilator allocation to patients most likely to survive to discharge, and vaccine prioritization for healthcare workers whose continued function benefits many others.

The problems with utilitarian reasoning in disasters are well documented. It can produce outcomes that feel deeply wrong even when the math seems right: it may direct resources away from the elderly or disabled as a categorical matter, without individual assessment. It can be biased by who defines "benefit" and who does the counting. And it cannot account for the value of fairness itself — for the fact that people care not just about outcomes, but about whether they were treated with equal dignity and given a fair chance [CITATION: utilitarian critique in disaster bioethics].

**Egalitarian frameworks** attempt to correct for this by insisting on equal treatment. In its most radical form, egalitarianism would require randomization when resources cannot meet need — a lottery for ventilators or organ transplants. This preserves equal dignity and procedural fairness, but it is generally unacceptable in medical contexts because it treats a patient with a 90% chance of survival the same as one with a 10% chance. Modified egalitarianism retains the commitment to equal moral worth while allowing clinical differences to be considered once baseline dignity protections are in place.

**Procedural justice** frameworks shift the focus from outcomes to process. They ask not "did we make the right decision?" but "did we make the decision in the right way?" A procedurally just allocation process is transparent, uses criteria that are clearly stated in advance, applies those criteria consistently, provides mechanisms for appeal, and is developed with meaningful input from the communities affected. The appeal of procedural justice in disasters is that it provides legitimacy even when outcomes are painful — people are more likely to accept decisions they disagree with if they trust the process that produced them [CITATION: procedural justice in public health literature].

**Social role and instrumental value frameworks** argue that certain individuals warrant priority because of their function in the response system. Healthcare workers, public health officials, and essential responders are protected not because they are more valuable as human beings, but because their continued function enables the system to help everyone. This reasoning is widely used for vaccine prioritization and has strong intuitive support, but it requires careful limiting principles: it can become a rationalization for protecting the powerful under cover of system efficiency.

**Community-centered approaches** draw on values that are not fully captured by any Western bioethical tradition: the importance of community cohesion, cultural obligations to elders, collective decision-making, and spiritual dimensions of life, death, and resource sharing [CITATION: community ethics in disaster literature]. These frameworks have gained significant attention in Indigenous and other traditionally marginalized communities that have historical reasons to distrust utilitarian and procedural systems administered by external authorities.

The practical takeaway is not that practitioners must choose one framework and apply it mechanically. It is that ethical reasoning in a disaster must be explicit, documented, and defensible. When a crisis standards of care committee makes a ventilator allocation decision, they should be able to say: "We used these criteria, for these reasons, derived from these values, and here is how this decision was reviewed." That documentation is both ethically required and legally protective.

## 3.6 Triage Ethics and the Limits of Sorting

Triage is the most visible ethical act in mass casualty medicine. It is also, paradoxically, the act practitioners are most likely to have rehearsed without having thought carefully about its ethical dimensions.

The word "triage" comes from the French word for sorting, and that is exactly what it is: a systematic process for categorizing patients by urgency and treatability when resources cannot meet demand. START (Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment) and SALT (Sort, Assess, Lifesaving Interventions, Treatment/Transport) are the dominant systems in the United States. Both direct immediate resources toward patients who will survive without treatment for a short period, prioritize immediate treatment for those with survivable injuries requiring urgent intervention, and — in their most ethically difficult provision — designate patients who are unlikely to survive even with treatment as "expectant," meaning they will not receive immediate attention even if they are conscious and in pain [CITATION: START triage system documentation; SALT triage development].

The "expectant" category is where triage ethics becomes most urgent. A patient designated expectant is not being abandoned — they should receive comfort care, and their category should be reassessed as conditions change. But in practice, reassessment in a mass casualty event may never come. The person lying in a parking lot with a devastating traumatic brain injury may die in pain while responders who have been trained to use START move past them. This is not a failure of character or compassion. It is the logic of triage operating as designed, in a context of genuine scarcity. But it requires practitioners to have processed the ethical weight of that logic before the incident — not in the moment.

Pediatric triage adds an additional layer of complexity. JumpSTART, the pediatric adaptation of START, uses different physiologic criteria because children's vital signs and respiratory patterns differ from adults. But there is also a deeper ethical question: should children receive priority in triage because of their life-years remaining? Or does that logic unfairly deprioritize elderly patients whose life experience and contributions are no less valuable? The disaster bioethics literature does not provide a consensus answer [CITATION: pediatric triage ethics literature].

## 3.7 Professional Obligations During Disasters

### Duty to Treat and Personal Risk

Healthcare professionals have professional and, in some jurisdictions, legal obligations to provide care. But those obligations have limits, particularly when providing care places the provider at significant personal risk. Medical and nursing codes of ethics generally hold that professionals have a duty to provide care during epidemics and disasters, but that this duty has limits — that practitioners are not obligated to act in ways that pose extraordinary risk to themselves or their families without adequate protection [CITATION: AMA Code of Ethics disaster obligations; ICN nursing code of ethics].

The COVID-19 pandemic forced this question into practical reality. Healthcare workers were asked to provide care without adequate personal protective equipment in the early months of the pandemic. The ethical weight of this situation was profound: duty to patient, duty to family, duty to profession, and the legitimate expectation that employers and governments would provide the tools necessary for safe practice. The obligation to treat does not transfer full risk from the system to the individual practitioner [CITATION: professional obligation literature, COVID-19 context].

### Professional Abandonment

Professional abandonment occurs when a provider terminates the physician-patient, nurse-patient, or other professional-patient relationship without adequate notice, without the patient's consent, and before the patient can secure alternative care. Abandonment in a disaster context is a complex question because the normal rules of practice may be suspended by emergency declarations, patients may be unable to secure alternative providers, and the geographic disruption of the event may make continuity of care impossible.

Emergency declarations in most states include Good Samaritan-type protections and scope of practice expansions that modify the normal professional relationship framework. But these protections are not automatic and are not uniform. Practitioners who evacuate without transferring care, who refuse to accept patients without clinical justification, or who close facilities without coordination with emergency management structures may face professional licensing consequences even when they believe they had no choice [CITATION: professional abandonment in disaster law review].

### Legal Protections for Responders

Most states have enacted Emergency Management Assistance Compact (EMAC) enabling legislation and volunteer protection acts that provide immunity for healthcare providers acting in good faith under emergency conditions. The extent of this immunity varies significantly by state. Some states provide broad immunity for all actions taken under an emergency declaration; others limit immunity to specific types of volunteers or specific types of declarations.

Emergency System for Advance Registration of Volunteer Health Professionals (ESAR-VHP) programs, maintained by states with federal support, provide pre-credentialed rosters of licensed healthcare volunteers whose credentials have been verified in advance [CITATION: ESAR-VHP program description, HRSA]. Practitioners who register in advance and are activated through official channels generally receive stronger legal protection than those who self-deploy.

The practical advice for practitioners is straightforward: understand the liability framework in your state before an event, register with available advance registration systems, operate within official incident command structures when deployed, document all clinical decisions and the conditions under which they were made, and do not assume that good intentions substitute for legal protection.

## 3.8 Ethics in Practice: Documentation, Accountability, and Humility

Ethical decision-making in disasters is not just a matter of choosing the right framework and applying it. It requires institutional structures that make ethical reasoning possible under pressure: pre-established criteria published in advance, multidisciplinary ethics committees or panels that can be activated quickly, clear documentation requirements, appeals processes, and after-action review that examines ethical decisions with the same rigor applied to operational ones.

It also requires humility about the limits of any framework. The decision-maker who is absolutely certain they made the right call in a mass casualty triage scenario has probably not grappled fully with what they did. Ethical action in a disaster involves loss — there is no choice that saves everyone. Acknowledging that loss, honoring the people who were placed in the expectant category or denied a ventilator, and using those experiences to improve future planning is itself an ethical obligation. Systems that rush past their own failures in the name of moving forward carry those failures into the next event.

The National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine report on Crisis Standards of Care is among the most useful practical guides to institutionalized ethical decision-making in health emergencies [CITATION: National Academies CSC reports, 2009 and 2012 and subsequent updates]. It is not a philosophy text. It is a workbook for building the structures that make ethical reasoning possible when time is short and the stakes are irreversible.

## Applied Vignette: The Ventilator Protocol

*Scenario: March 2020. A mid-sized urban medical center has 22 ventilators, all in use. The state has activated Crisis Standards of Care. The ethics committee has been asked to apply the hospital's ventilator allocation protocol to determine whether any patients should be transitioned off ventilator support to make a ventilator available for a new patient who is expected to survive with treatment.*

*The protocol uses a modified Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score to estimate mortality probability. A patient with a SOFA score above a threshold is designated for re-evaluation. The committee identifies two patients above threshold. One is a 71-year-old woman with advanced COPD who contracted COVID-19. The other is a 34-year-old man with a traumatic brain injury from a motor vehicle crash who was on the ventilator before the pandemic surge began.*

*The committee must decide: Should either patient be transitioned to palliative-only care? What process is required? Who notifies the family? What documentation is needed? What happens if a family refuses?*

This vignette does not have a single correct answer. It is designed to surface the tensions between utilitarian allocation logic (SOFA score), equal moral worth of patients regardless of diagnosis, family autonomy, legal requirements for informed consent and surrogate decision-making, and the institutional obligations of the hospital. A full response to this scenario requires applying the CSC framework, the hospital's legal obligations under state emergency law, and the ethical commitments of the clinical and administrative teams.

What the vignette also reveals is that this decision cannot be made well in real time without advance work: a protocol that was developed transparently, approved by the governing board, published publicly before the crisis, and integrated into staff training. Ethical systems that exist only on paper — that were never operationalized, never tested in tabletop exercises, never explained to the clinical staff who will apply them — will fail under the pressure of a real event.

## Key Terms

- **Pandemic and All-Hazards Preparedness Act (PAHPA):** Federal statute enacted in 2006 creating ASPR, BARDA, and the current federal medical countermeasure enterprise.
- **PREP Act:** Public Readiness and Emergency Preparedness Act; provides liability immunity for covered persons administering covered countermeasures under an active HHS declaration.
- **Emergency Use Authorization (EUA):** FDA mechanism allowing use of unapproved products or unapproved uses of approved products during a declared emergency.
- **Utilitarian ethics:** Ethical framework directing decision-makers to maximize aggregate benefit; often used in triage and allocation systems.
- **Egalitarian ethics:** Framework insisting on equal treatment and equal moral worth; may favor randomization in resource allocation.
- **Procedural justice:** Framework focusing on fairness of the decision-making process rather than outcomes.
- **Expectant category:** Triage designation for patients unlikely to survive even with treatment; they receive comfort care but not immediate life-saving intervention during resource-limited conditions.
- **START triage:** Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment; the dominant mass casualty triage system in the United States.
- **SALT triage:** Sort, Assess, Lifesaving Interventions, Treatment/Transport; a more recent mass casualty triage system with some advantages in pediatric application.
- **Duty to treat:** Professional obligation of healthcare providers to care for patients, subject to limits related to personal safety and available resources.
- **Professional abandonment:** Unilateral termination of a professional-patient relationship without adequate notice or transfer of care.
- **ESAR-VHP:** Emergency System for Advance Registration of Volunteer Health Professionals; state-level pre-credentialing systems for disaster volunteers.
- **Crisis Standards of Care (CSC):** Framework for adjusting the standard of care during a disaster when demand exceeds supply; see Chapter 9 for full treatment.
- **BARDA:** Biomedical Advanced Research and Development Authority; HHS component responsible for advanced development and acquisition of medical countermeasures.
- **CICP:** Countermeasures Injury Compensation Program; no-fault compensation program for individuals harmed by covered medical countermeasures.
- **Jacobson v. Massachusetts:** 1905 Supreme Court case upholding mandatory vaccination authority while establishing limits on arbitrary exercise of police power.

## Discussion Questions

1. The PREP Act grants liability immunity to persons administering covered countermeasures. How should this immunity be balanced against the interests of individuals who experience serious adverse effects from those countermeasures? Is the Countermeasures Injury Compensation Program an adequate substitute for traditional tort recovery?

2. The COVID-19 pandemic prompted Emergency Use Authorizations for vaccines that were then subject to mandates by employers and governments. How do you evaluate the ethics of mandating an EUA product? Does your answer change if the alternative to vaccination is being excluded from a workplace or school?

3. A utilitarian allocation system directs resources toward patients most likely to survive. Critics argue this disadvantages elderly and disabled patients in ways that violate equal dignity. How would you design a ventilator allocation protocol that takes clinical prognosis into account without categorically excluding protected groups?

4. Healthcare workers were asked to care for COVID-19 patients without adequate PPE. What ethical and legal obligations did hospitals and governments have in that situation? At what point, if any, does a healthcare worker's professional duty to treat give way to a legitimate refusal to work in unsafe conditions?

5. The ventilator allocation vignette involves a patient who was on the ventilator before the pandemic surge — a traumatic brain injury patient whose condition is not related to the mass casualty event. Should pre-crisis patients be subject to the same crisis allocation protocol as new admissions? What ethical and legal considerations apply?

## Scenario Exercise

**Setting:** You are the medical director of a state health department. The governor has declared a public health emergency following an anthrax attack that has exposed an estimated 12,000 people in a major metropolitan area. The Strategic National Stockpile has been activated and doxycycline is en route for post-exposure prophylaxis. State law authorizes you to issue a public health order requiring exposed persons to appear for prophylaxis.

**Complication 1:** A religious community in the exposure zone objects to the antibiotic on religious grounds. Their leader has informed media that community members will not comply. You estimate the community has approximately 400 members.

**Complication 2:** Legal counsel advises you that a mandatory order may be challenged in court, but that a voluntary program will likely result in lower uptake among populations who distrust government. She estimates that a mandatory order, if upheld, would achieve 95% coverage; a voluntary program would achieve 70%.

**Complication 3:** The anthrax strain has not yet been confirmed to be drug-resistant, but laboratory results are pending. If it is resistant to doxycycline, the entire stockpile deployment will be ineffective.

**Tasks:**
1. Draft the legal authority analysis: Under what statutory and constitutional authority can you issue a mandatory prophylaxis order? What procedural protections must accompany it?
2. Apply at least two ethical frameworks to the question of whether to make prophylaxis mandatory or voluntary.
3. Identify what you would communicate to the public about the situation, including what you do not yet know (pending lab results).
4. Describe how you would approach the religious community objection — legally and operationally.

## Practitioner Takeaways

- **Know your legal authority before the event.** Understand what statutes authorize your agency's emergency health actions, what procedural requirements accompany those authorities, and what limits courts have placed on their use. Do not assume authority you have not verified.

- **The PREP Act is not a blank check.** Coverage depends on an active declaration, covered persons, and covered countermeasures. Verify that a PREP Act declaration is in effect and that your specific role and product are covered before assuming immunity applies.

- **EUA products require real informed consent.** The compressed evidence base for EUA products does not reduce the ethical obligation to explain what is known, what is not known, and what the alternative is. Document that conversation.

- **Choose your ethical framework explicitly.** In any resource allocation decision, name the framework you are using, explain why, and document the reasoning. Implicit ethics are vulnerable ethics — they cannot be reviewed, challenged, or improved.

- **Build the ethics infrastructure before the event.** CSC protocols, ethics committees, allocation criteria, documentation systems, and appeals processes need to exist in policy before they are needed in practice. Ethical improvisation in a disaster is not ethical.

- **Protect yourself legally through proper channels.** Register with your state's ESAR-VHP system if you may deploy as a volunteer. Operate within ICS structures during deployments. Document clinical decisions and the conditions under which they were made.

- **Equity is a legal constraint, not just a value.** HHS OCR guidance, the ADA, and Section 504 impose enforceable obligations on crisis standards of care protocols. Disability status, race, and national origin cannot be the basis for differential allocation decisions.
