# Chapter 6: Behavioral Health in Disasters

---

## Chapter Overview

Disasters do not end when floodwaters recede or when the last structural fire is extinguished. For many survivors, the most debilitating effects begin precisely at that moment — when the immediate threat is over and the human nervous system, having held itself together under pressure, begins to register what happened. Behavioral health consequences of disasters are among the most prevalent, most enduring, and most underprepared aspects of emergency management. They affect survivors, responders, community members who watched from a distance, and the practitioners charged with leading the response.

This chapter provides a foundation for understanding behavioral health in the disaster context. It begins with the theoretical and empirical landscape — what the research tells us about psychological responses to mass casualty and mass displacement events, what distinguishes normal adaptive distress from clinical disorder, and how community-level factors shape collective recovery. It then moves to the operational side: how behavioral health is organized within incident command structures, how Crisis Counseling Programs function, what Psychological First Aid is and how it differs from clinical intervention, and what the equity dimensions of disaster behavioral health look like in practice.

Behavioral health is not a specialty appendage to emergency management. It is a core public health function. Understanding it — even at the level of a non-clinician practitioner — is essential for anyone designing, operating, or evaluating a disaster response.

---

## Learning Objectives

Upon completing this chapter, students will be able to:

1. Describe the spectrum of normal and pathological psychological responses to disaster, including Acute Stress Disorder, Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder, Major Depressive Disorder, complicated grief, and substance use escalation.
2. Explain the concept of community resilience and the factors that predict differential psychological outcomes across populations.
3. Define Psychological First Aid and describe its core principles and evidence base as a field-deployable intervention.
4. Distinguish between Crisis Counseling Programs (CCP) and clinical mental health treatment in terms of purpose, staffing, and population reach.
5. Identify the behavioral health functions within the Incident Command System and the roles of the Medical Branch and ESF-8 in coordinating behavioral health response.
6. Describe the behavioral health needs of disaster responders and the organizational obligations associated with responder mental health.
7. Articulate the equity dimensions of disaster behavioral health, including differential exposure, differential access to services, and structural barriers to care.
8. Apply key behavioral health principles to an operational planning or response scenario.

## 6.1 The Scope of Disaster Behavioral Health

The behavioral health consequences of disasters are substantial, widespread, and frequently underestimated by emergency planners who focus primarily on physical injury counts and mortality. Research consistently demonstrates that the psychological burden of major disasters far exceeds the burden of physical injury, often by an order of magnitude. For every person who sustains a physical wound requiring clinical care, many more will experience significant psychological distress; and for a meaningful proportion of survivors, that distress will evolve into a diagnosable mental health condition without access to appropriate support.

The range of psychological responses is broad. At one end sits normal, adaptive distress — the expected emotional reactions of grief, fear, shock, anger, and disorientation that virtually all survivors experience and that typically resolve without clinical intervention given adequate social support and stability of basic needs. At the other end sit diagnosable conditions that cause lasting functional impairment and require professional treatment. Between these poles lies a large middle range of subthreshold symptoms that may not meet diagnostic criteria but that substantially affect quality of life, occupational function, parenting capacity, and health behavior.

The conditions most commonly associated with disaster exposure include:

**Acute Stress Disorder (ASD)** emerges in the days to weeks immediately following a traumatic event and is characterized by intrusive memories, avoidance of reminders, negative alterations in mood, hyperarousal, and dissociative symptoms. By definition, ASD resolves within one month; when symptoms persist, the diagnosis typically transitions to Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder.

**Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)** involves the same core symptom clusters as ASD but persists beyond one month and often beyond years. PTSD is the most studied disaster-related mental health condition. Prevalence estimates in disaster-affected populations vary substantially depending on the nature and severity of the disaster, the baseline vulnerability of the population, and the access to social support and services. [CITATION: Meta-analytic review of PTSD prevalence in disaster-affected populations]. Key risk factors include direct life threat, injury to self or witnessed injury to others, loss of family members, displacement, and prior trauma history.

**Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)** is highly prevalent in disaster contexts and frequently comorbid with PTSD. Depression in disaster survivors is often driven by grief, loss of role function and social networks, economic devastation, and the chronic stress of displacement and recovery uncertainty. Untreated depression significantly impairs the recovery trajectory — affecting the capacity to navigate FEMA processes, maintain housing, sustain employment, and engage in parenting.

**Complicated Grief** (also termed Prolonged Grief Disorder in current diagnostic frameworks) occurs when the normal process of grieving becomes protracted and disabling. It is particularly common when deaths are sudden, traumatic, multiple, or involve children, and when circumstances prevent survivors from completing culturally meaningful mourning rituals — a frequent consequence of mass casualty events and prolonged displacement.

**Substance Use Escalation** frequently follows disaster. Both new-onset problem use and exacerbation of existing substance use disorders are documented following major events. [CITATION: Epidemiological studies on substance use escalation following hurricanes and floods]. Alcohol is the most commonly implicated substance, though stimulant and opioid use also increase in certain post-disaster contexts. This represents a significant behavioral health and public health concern that often goes unaddressed in disaster response frameworks focused primarily on acute trauma symptoms.

**Functional Impairment Without Diagnosable Disorder** deserves specific attention. A large portion of the disaster-affected population experiences sleep disruption, irritability, difficulty concentrating, social withdrawal, and somatic complaints that do not meet diagnostic thresholds but that meaningfully impair function. These individuals often do not seek or receive any form of support because they do not identify as "mentally ill" and the available services do not reach them where they are.

## 6.2 Community-Level Psychological Response

Disasters affect communities, not just individuals. Understanding behavioral health requires a community-level lens alongside the individual clinical lens. Communities undergo their own psychological processes following major events, and those processes are shaped by factors that emergency managers can, to varying degrees, influence.

**Community Resilience** is the collective capacity of a community to anticipate, prepare for, adapt to, and recover from stressors, including disasters. Resilience is not the absence of suffering; communities with high resilience still experience distress, loss, and disruption. What distinguishes resilient communities is the speed and completeness of recovery and the capacity to maintain essential social functions during disruption. Key determinants of community resilience include:

Social cohesion and trust — communities with dense social networks and high baseline trust in institutions and neighbors tend to mobilize mutual aid more effectively and recover more quickly. Social capital functions as a psychological buffer.

Economic capacity — communities with greater economic resources have more ability to repair physical damage, relocate when necessary, and access mental health services, all of which support psychological recovery.

Cultural and spiritual resources — religious institutions, cultural organizations, and community gathering spaces serve as informal behavioral health infrastructure. Their loss or disruption in a disaster represents a double harm: physical and psychological.

Prior disaster experience — communities with prior experience may be better prepared but may also carry cumulative trauma from repeated events, particularly in communities repeatedly struck by major hurricanes, floods, or wildfires. [CITATION: Research on cumulative trauma in repeatedly disaster-affected communities, including Gulf Coast and California wildfire-affected areas].

Institutional trust and perceived fairness of the response — communities that perceive the disaster response as inequitable, slow, or dismissive of their needs tend to exhibit higher rates of psychological distress and lower rates of engagement with recovery services. This is one of the most important and underappreciated intersections between equity practice (Chapter 4) and behavioral health outcomes.

**The Convergence of Grief** at the community level can produce collective mourning processes that, when disrupted, impair community recovery. Memorial rituals, public acknowledgment of loss, and opportunities for communal expression are not soft extras in a disaster response; they are functional behavioral health infrastructure. Emergency managers and public health practitioners who fail to plan for these needs or who inadvertently obstruct them — for example, by closing public spaces during recovery operations without providing alternative gathering sites — can inadvertently extend community psychological suffering.

**Secondary Stress and Vicarious Trauma** affect community members who were not directly exposed to the event but who absorb the distress of those who were, or who are repeatedly exposed to traumatic imagery through media and community networks. Parents of young children, teachers, community health workers, and faith leaders are particularly susceptible because their roles require sustained engagement with the affected population.

## 6.3 Psychological First Aid

Psychological First Aid (PFA) is the field-level approach to supporting survivors in the acute post-disaster period. It is endorsed by SAMHSA, NCPTSD, WHO, and numerous national and international emergency management bodies as the recommended approach for non-clinical responders working with disaster survivors. Understanding PFA is essential for emergency management practitioners even if they will never personally deliver it, because they will be responsible for deploying personnel who do.

PFA is explicitly not therapy. It does not require a clinical license, does not involve processing traumatic memories, and does not attempt to produce clinical recovery. Its goals are more basic and more immediately achievable: helping survivors establish safety, connect with support, and access practical assistance.

**The Eight Core Actions of PFA** provide the operational framework:

*Contact and Engagement* involves approaching survivors in a non-intrusive, compassionate manner that respects their current state. Many survivors in the acute phase are disoriented, emotionally dysregulated, or hypervigilant. Effective initial contact acknowledges their distress without pathologizing it.

*Safety and Comfort* focuses on establishing or reinforcing the perception and reality of physical safety. Survivors who remain in or near a danger zone, or who believe they may still be in danger, cannot engage in the cognitive and emotional processing that supports recovery. Physical comfort — water, warmth, a dry place to sit — is inseparable from psychological first aid.

*Stabilization* addresses survivors who are overwhelmed or in emotional crisis. Simple grounding techniques — focused breathing, attention to immediate sensory experience, calm presence — can reduce acute dysregulation. This is a skill that non-clinical responders can learn and provide.

*Information Gathering* involves briefly and sensitively assessing the survivor's immediate needs: Are they looking for family members? Do they need medical attention? Are they concerned about children left elsewhere? PFA is needs-driven rather than protocol-driven; the sequence of subsequent actions depends on what the survivor actually needs.

*Practical Assistance* addresses those immediate concrete needs — helping someone contact a family member, connecting them with registration services, providing transportation information. Practical assistance is behavioral health intervention. Meeting the concrete needs of displaced and disoriented survivors directly reduces psychological distress.

*Connection with Social Supports* mobilizes the single most powerful predictor of psychological recovery: human connection. This means facilitating contact with family, friends, neighbors, faith community, or peer support. PFA providers who facilitate family reunification are doing as much for behavioral health as any clinical intervention.

*Information on Coping* provides psychoeducation about normal reactions to abnormal events. One of the most powerful interventions available in the acute phase is simply telling survivors that their reactions — the intrusive memories, the sleep disruption, the irritability, the physical symptoms — are normal human responses to overwhelming events and are not signs of weakness, mental illness, or permanent damage. This psychoeducation reduces stigma and normalizes help-seeking.

*Linkage with Collaborative Services* ensures that survivors who do need ongoing clinical support are connected to it. PFA providers identify survivors at higher risk — those with prior mental health history, those who have lost family members, those with severe acute symptoms — and link them to clinical resources.

**The Evidence Base for PFA** is important to understand clearly. PFA is based on principles derived from research on psychological recovery — social support, safety, stabilization, practical assistance — rather than on randomized controlled trials directly evaluating PFA protocols. No such trials exist, and designing one raises significant ethical challenges. [CITATION: NCPTSD review of PFA evidence base]. This is not a weakness unique to PFA; the acute disaster context makes controlled research extremely difficult. The PFA framework represents the best available synthesis of what is known about what helps people recover from traumatic events.

## 6.4 Crisis Counseling Programs

When a presidentially declared major disaster occurs, FEMA's Crisis Counseling Assistance and Training Program (CCP) provides grant funding to state mental health authorities to deliver community-based behavioral health services to disaster survivors. Understanding the CCP architecture is essential for emergency managers and public health practitioners who will interact with it during major disaster responses.

**The Two Components of CCP** reflect the different timeframes of disaster behavioral health need:

The *Immediate Services Program (ISP)* covers the first 60 days following a disaster declaration. ISP funding supports rapid deployment of crisis counselors into affected communities to provide outreach, screening, psychoeducation, and referral. The emphasis is on reaching survivors where they are — in shelters, at distribution points, in neighborhoods — rather than requiring survivors to seek services.

The *Regular Services Program (RSP)* extends for up to nine months beyond the ISP period (up to about 11 months total). RSP supports a more sustained community-based program including individual and group crisis counseling, community outreach, public education, training of community gatekeepers, and referral to clinical services for those who need more intensive treatment.

**Crisis Counselors vs. Clinicians** is a distinction that matters operationally. CCP crisis counselors are typically paraprofessionals or professionals with community mental health experience, trained in disaster crisis counseling. They are not providing clinical psychotherapy; they are providing supportive counseling, psychoeducation, practical problem-solving assistance, and linkage. The CCP model explicitly avoids pathologizing normal distress and emphasizes reaching survivors who would not voluntarily seek clinical mental health services. This community-outreach model is specifically designed to reach the large middle range of survivors experiencing significant distress who fall below clinical treatment thresholds or who would not engage with traditional mental health services.

**Limitations of the CCP Model** are important to acknowledge. The nine-to-eleven-month timeframe is insufficient for the full arc of post-disaster behavioral health need; research consistently documents elevated rates of PTSD, depression, and complicated grief extending years beyond major disasters. [CITATION: Longitudinal behavioral health studies following major hurricanes, earthquakes, and wildfires]. CCP programs are also dependent on state mental health authority administrative capacity, which varies considerably. Application, administration, and documentation requirements can create significant implementation burdens for state agencies already managing response and recovery operations. Finally, CCP services reach only FEMA-declared disaster areas and only in the immediate geographic scope of the declaration, which may not capture all affected communities.

**Referral Pathways to Clinical Treatment** are a critical and frequently weak link in the disaster behavioral health system. Crisis counselors can identify survivors who need clinical care — medication evaluation, trauma-focused psychotherapy, substance use treatment — but the availability of that clinical care is often grossly inadequate, particularly in rural and underserved areas that lack sufficient mental health workforce capacity even before a disaster. Disaster planning must include explicit assessment of clinical treatment capacity and identification of strategies for expanding it, including telehealth platforms, mobile crisis units, and partnerships with Federally Qualified Health Centers.

## 6.5 Behavioral Health in the Incident Command Structure

Behavioral health is a functional component of the disaster response structure, not an external specialty that operates independently of it. Understanding how behavioral health fits within Incident Command System (ICS) and National Incident Management System (NIMS) frameworks is essential for coordinating services effectively.

**The Medical and Public Health Branch** within the Operations Section of an activated Emergency Operations Center typically carries primary responsibility for behavioral health coordination at the EOC level. The specific organizational structure varies by jurisdiction, but behavioral health should be explicitly named as a functional area rather than left implied under a general health designation. Jurisdictions that do not explicitly assign behavioral health responsibility within their ICS structure tend to experience coordination failures during major events.

**ESF-8 (Public Health and Medical Services)** at the federal level includes behavioral health as a component function. SAMHSA and its Disaster Technical Assistance Center (DTAC) serve as key federal behavioral health resources under ESF-8, providing technical assistance, training, and coordination support to states during major disaster declarations. State emergency management plans should specifically identify the state mental health authority as the primary ESF-8 behavioral health lead and establish pre-event relationships with SAMHSA DTAC.

**Behavioral Health in Shelter Operations** is frequently one of the most acute operational challenges. Mass care shelters concentrate populations with high pre-existing behavioral health vulnerability — individuals experiencing homelessness, those with serious mental illness, people with substance use disorders, and others who lack the resources to shelter-in-place or evacuate to private accommodations. Shelter staff and volunteers — who are typically not behavioral health professionals — are often the primary human contact for these individuals. Training shelter staff in PFA, de-escalation, and crisis recognition is a standard component of shelter planning that is nonetheless frequently neglected.

**Crisis Intervention in the Field** becomes necessary when survivors or shelter residents experience acute psychiatric crises that exceed the capacity of PFA-trained staff. Every jurisdiction needs clear protocols for crisis intervention in disaster settings, including mobile crisis team deployment, psychiatric evaluation resources, and emergency hospitalization pathways that remain functional even when the general healthcare system is stressed. These protocols should be established before an event occurs and exercised in preparedness activities.

**The Operational Stress Continuum** is a framework developed in military contexts that has application in disaster behavioral health as well. It describes a spectrum from green (ready and functional) through yellow (reacting — mild and reversible distress), orange (injured — more severe distress requiring support), to red (ill — clinical disorder requiring treatment). This framework is useful both for assessing the status of survivor populations and for monitoring the behavioral health of responder teams.

## 6.6 Responder Behavioral Health

The behavioral health needs of disaster responders — emergency managers, first responders, medical personnel, public works crews, volunteers, and community health workers — are a major operational concern that receives substantially less attention than survivor behavioral health. Responders are occupationally exposed to repeated traumatic stimuli, operational stress, moral injury, and the cumulative burden of witnessing mass suffering. They frequently work extended shifts under significant physical and cognitive load. And they often operate in organizational cultures that stigmatize help-seeking and define strength as the absence of psychological need.

**Occupational Stress and Cumulative Exposure** distinguish responder behavioral health from survivor behavioral health. Responders may not experience a single acute traumatic event but rather accumulate exposure across multiple incidents and extended operations. Body recovery operations, mass casualty medical response, prolonged family separation, and the administrative stress of operating within broken systems all contribute to cumulative burden. Research on firefighters, emergency medical personnel, and emergency managers documents elevated rates of PTSD, depression, burnout, and substance use compared to general population norms. [CITATION: Occupational health research on behavioral health outcomes in emergency response professions].

**Moral Injury** is a concept originating in military psychology that has increasing application to disaster response contexts. Moral injury occurs when a person participates in, witnesses, or fails to prevent an event that violates their moral code — or when they feel betrayed by leaders or institutions in ways that violate their sense of justice. Crisis Standards of Care activation, triage decisions under mass casualty conditions, resource allocation choices that leave some communities underserved, and instructions to continue operations despite evidence of preventable harm are all potential sources of moral injury for emergency management and healthcare personnel. Moral injury is distinct from PTSD and responds to different forms of support; clinical protocols developed for PTSD are not sufficient to address it.

**Organizational Obligations** toward responder behavioral health are both ethical and operational. High rates of responder burnout, PTSD, and substance use directly affect organizational capacity, workforce retention, and public safety. Jurisdictions and organizations have affirmative responsibilities to:

Provide pre-deployment behavioral health preparation that includes realistic expectations, coping strategies, and clear information about available support resources.

Monitor behavioral health status during extended operations through structured check-ins and crew welfare assessments rather than relying on voluntary self-reporting by individuals embedded in cultures that discourage disclosure.

Establish peer support programs — responder-to-responder support delivered by trained peers who have credibility within the organizational culture. Peer support does not require clinical licensure and often reaches personnel who would not engage with formal mental health services.

Provide access to critical incident stress management (CISM) services including defusing (brief group support within hours of a critical incident) and debriefing processes. The evidence base for specific CISM protocols, particularly single-session Critical Incident Stress Debriefing (CISD), has been contested in the literature; practitioners should be aware of this controversy and prioritize evidence-informed approaches. [CITATION: Evidence review of critical incident stress debriefing effectiveness].

Ensure access to confidential clinical mental health services, including employee assistance programs, that responders can use without employer knowledge.

Provide post-deployment support including gradual demobilization processes, time away from operational exposure, and active follow-up contact with personnel who exhibited signs of distress during operations.

**Secondary Traumatization of Behavioral Health Workers** themselves is a specific concern. Crisis counselors, therapists, and community health workers who are embedded in post-disaster communities and providing sustained support to traumatized survivors are at risk for compassion fatigue and secondary traumatic stress. Supervision, peer support, and workload limits are essential components of behavioral health workforce management in extended disaster recovery operations.

## 6.7 Equity in Disaster Behavioral Health

The behavioral health consequences of disaster are not equitably distributed, and the services designed to address those consequences do not equitably reach the populations most affected. Both of these facts — the differential distribution of harm and the differential distribution of response — require explicit attention in planning and operations.

**Differential Exposure** begins before the disaster. As established in Chapter 4, communities with lower social vulnerability have greater capacity to evacuate, shelter safely, and avoid direct traumatic exposure. Communities with high social vulnerability — lower incomes, racial and ethnic minority status, language isolation, unstable housing — experience greater direct exposure to disaster events and greater subsequent loss of material resources, social networks, and housing stability. Each of these loss pathways is an independent risk factor for post-disaster behavioral health disorder.

**Differential Access to Behavioral Health Services** reflects structural inequities that predate the disaster. Communities of color, low-income communities, and rural communities have chronically inadequate access to mental health services even in non-disaster conditions — fewer mental health providers, fewer providers who accept Medicaid, greater geographic barriers to care, fewer providers who share the language or culture of the people they serve. Disasters do not create these gaps; they expose and amplify them.

**Cultural Factors in Psychological Response and Help-Seeking** are substantial and frequently misunderstood. Many communities have cultural frameworks for understanding suffering, loss, and recovery that differ from the Western clinical frameworks embedded in standard disaster behavioral health programs. Help-seeking for mental health concerns carries different levels and types of stigma across cultural communities, and the meaning attributed to disaster events — theological, relational, ancestral — shapes both the experience of suffering and the kinds of support that feel meaningful. Crisis counseling programs that operate exclusively within Western clinical frameworks will consistently fail to reach and support many of the most affected community members.

Effective culturally responsive behavioral health practice requires:

Deployment of crisis counselors who share the language, culture, and community identity of the populations they serve, or who have deep training in the specific cultural context.

Partnership with community-based organizations, faith institutions, and community leaders who already have trust relationships with affected populations, rather than deploying outside professionals as the primary service delivery mechanism.

Flexibility in service modalities — group support, family-based support, community ritual and ceremony — that align with cultural frameworks for collective healing rather than assuming that one-on-one individual counseling is the universal appropriate format.

Explicit outreach to communities whose distrust of government and healthcare institutions leads them to avoid standard service delivery channels.

**Documentation Status and Behavioral Health Service Access** is a critical intersection point. Undocumented immigrants who are affected by a disaster may avoid CCP services, shelter services, and other support programs out of fear of exposure to immigration enforcement. This fear is not irrational — it reflects real risk in the current policy environment. [CITATION: Research on behavioral health service avoidance among undocumented immigrant populations following disasters]. Emergency management agencies that seek to reach undocumented disaster survivors must explicitly address this barrier, including by establishing data-sharing limitations with immigration enforcement, partnering with trusted community organizations, and communicating clearly about data practices.

**Children and Youth** experience behavioral health consequences of disaster in developmentally specific ways and are particularly dependent on the stability of their caregiving adults and school environments for psychological recovery. Young children may regress to earlier developmental behaviors, exhibit sleep disturbances, or become highly clingy with caregivers. School-age children may show academic regression, attention difficulties, and behavioral changes. Adolescents may exhibit risk-taking behavior, social withdrawal, or take on adult roles in family recovery that place excessive burden on them. Disruption of school — one of the most important stabilizing structures in children's lives — significantly compounds behavioral health risk. Planning for child-specific behavioral health services, including school-based crisis counseling, is a component of comprehensive disaster behavioral health planning. [CITATION: Research on child behavioral health outcomes following major disasters].

**Older Adults** face specific behavioral health risks in disaster settings, including grief from loss of community and neighbors who have died, anxiety associated with loss of the familiar environment and routine, cognitive impacts of extreme stress that may be misattributed to dementia, and the compounding effect of prior loss from previous disasters or life events. Older adults who are cognitively impaired are at particular risk for severe disorientation in shelter settings and may exhibit significant behavioral disturbance that shelter staff are not equipped to manage without specific training.

## 6.8 Long-Term Recovery and the Behavioral Health Timeline

One of the most important and consistently underestimated facts about disaster behavioral health is the timeline. The psychological consequences of major disasters extend far beyond the acute phase — months, years, and in some cases decades for the most severely affected individuals and communities. The emotional arc of disaster response does not parallel the operational arc of emergency management. By the time the Emergency Operations Center has closed and FEMA's regular programs have ended, the behavioral health consequences of the event are often at or approaching their worst.

**The Honeymoon Phase and Its End** is a well-documented phenomenon in disaster behavioral health. In the immediate aftermath of a major disaster, many communities experience an elevation of social solidarity, mutual aid, and positive affect — a sense of shared purpose that temporarily suppresses some of the negative psychological effects. This phase, sometimes called the heroic phase or honeymoon phase, is real and functional. But it ends. As recovery resources run out, bureaucratic obstacles compound, social networks disperse, and the cumulative fatigue of displacement and loss accumulates, psychological distress typically escalates. Many disaster behavioral health programs are designed for the acute phase and are already ramping down precisely when need is escalating.

**Anniversary Reactions** are predictable and should be anticipated in planning. Survivors frequently experience intensified grief, anxiety, and trauma symptoms around the anniversary of a major disaster event, as well as during subsequent storm seasons, flood events, or wildfire seasons that trigger traumatic memory. Mental health authorities in disaster-affected regions should anticipate anniversary-period surges in need and resource accordingly.

**Long-Term Surveillance and Population Health Monitoring** for behavioral health outcomes is an underdeveloped component of disaster recovery infrastructure. Longitudinal population health monitoring following major disasters — tracking rates of depression, PTSD, substance use, domestic violence, and suicide — would allow early identification of escalating need and more targeted resource deployment. As discussed in Chapter 5, this kind of surveillance requires deliberate planning, stable funding, and inter-agency coordination that are rarely in place.

**Retraumatization Through Recovery System Failures** is a documented phenomenon. When survivors navigate bureaucratic recovery systems that deny claims without explanation, require repeated documentation of loss, or move slowly while survivors remain in inadequate temporary housing, the recovery system itself becomes a source of ongoing trauma. Emergency managers and public health practitioners have a responsibility not only to connect survivors with behavioral health services but to advocate for recovery system design that does not compound the harm survivors have already experienced.

## Applied Vignette: The Counselor in the Cafeteria Line

The Millhaven flood displaced approximately 8,400 people in four days. By the sixth day, the regional evacuation shelter — a converted high school gymnasium — held 940 residents, most of whom had lost homes to water that rose faster than the forecast had suggested. The Red Cross chapter had deployed a trained disaster mental health team of eight workers. The shelter population included a substantial proportion of older adults, many of whom had previously experienced a major flood in the same area approximately twelve years earlier.

Maria Delgado, a licensed clinical social worker serving as the behavioral health team lead, understood quickly that her eight-person team could not function as a clinical outreach unit for 940 people. Nor was that the right model. Most of the 940 residents did not need clinical intervention. They needed what any human being needs after losing their home and most of their possessions in forty-eight hours: safety, information, connection, and to feel that someone recognized what had happened to them.

She reorganized her team's daily schedule around the shelter's natural gathering points. Two counselors stationed themselves in the cafeteria line each morning, not behind a table with a sign, but in the flow of the line, talking with people about what they were worried about and what they needed. Two more moved through the family areas in the afternoon. The remaining four focused on high-need individuals flagged by shelter staff — the older adult who had stopped eating and refused to leave her cot, the man who had been awake for three nights and was becoming increasingly irritable with everyone around him, the young mother who had dissociated during an intake conversation.

Maria briefed shelter management each morning. She requested a private space for conversations that needed more than a passing exchange — a room off the main gymnasium, a corner with chairs. She identified two bilingual community members among the shelter residents who, with their agreement, helped her team communicate with the Spanish-speaking families who were not engaging with any services.

On Day Eight, she convened a community meeting in the gymnasium. She provided a brief, plain-language explanation of normal responses to abnormal events. She told the 940 people in that room that sleeping badly, feeling angry, having trouble thinking straight, missing things that might seem small — these were not signs that something was wrong with them. These were signs that something overwhelming had happened. People wept. Some nodded. A few stayed afterward to talk.

On Day Twelve, Maria submitted her preliminary incident report to the state mental health authority. She noted that the shelter population included a substantial number of older adults with documented prior flood exposure and recommended that the CCP application include explicit provisions for anniversary-period behavioral health outreach twelve months hence, anticipating elevated psychological burden when the rainy season returned.

She had delivered no formal therapy. She had conducted no clinical assessments beyond the informal needs-based triage that shaped her team's priorities. What she had done was behavioral health at scale: practical, relational, norm-correcting, equity-attentive, and organized around what survivors needed rather than what service categories were easiest to deliver.

## Key Terms

**Acute Stress Disorder (ASD):** A diagnosable anxiety disorder occurring in the immediate aftermath of a traumatic event (within three days to one month), characterized by intrusion, avoidance, negative mood, dissociation, and hyperarousal.

**Behavioral Health:** An umbrella term encompassing mental health and substance use health, including both clinical disorders and subclinical distress affecting psychological well-being and function.

**Community Resilience:** The collective capacity of a community to anticipate, prepare for, adapt to, and recover from adversity, including disaster events.

**Compassion Fatigue:** Emotional and physical exhaustion resulting from the prolonged provision of care and support to individuals experiencing trauma or suffering; a risk for disaster behavioral health workers and other caregivers.

**Crisis Counseling Assistance and Training Program (CCP):** A FEMA-funded program providing grant support to state mental health authorities to deliver community-based behavioral health services following presidentially declared major disasters.

**Critical Incident Stress Management (CISM):** A comprehensive, multi-component approach to supporting responders following exposure to critical incidents, including defusing, debriefing, peer support, and follow-up services.

**Disaster Technical Assistance Center (DTAC):** SAMHSA's primary technical assistance resource for disaster behavioral health, providing training, consultation, and support to states and territories during disaster response and recovery.

**Major Depressive Disorder (MDD):** A clinical mood disorder characterized by persistent depressed mood, loss of interest or pleasure, and associated neurovegetative and cognitive symptoms; frequently comorbid with PTSD in disaster-affected populations.

**Moral Injury:** Psychological harm resulting from participation in, witnessing of, or failure to prevent events that violate a person's moral code, or from experiencing institutional betrayal; distinct from PTSD and relevant in disaster response contexts.

**Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD):** A diagnosable anxiety disorder that may develop following exposure to actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence; characterized by intrusion, avoidance, negative alterations in cognition and mood, and alterations in arousal and reactivity.

**Prolonged Grief Disorder:** A recently formalized diagnostic category describing persistent, functionally impairing grief extending beyond expected timeframes; particularly relevant following disaster-related mass loss.

**Psychological First Aid (PFA):** A field-deployable, non-clinical approach to supporting disaster survivors in the acute period following a traumatic event, organized around eight core actions: contact and engagement, safety and comfort, stabilization, information gathering, practical assistance, connection with social supports, information on coping, and linkage with services.

**Secondary Traumatic Stress:** The emotional and psychological distress experienced by individuals who are repeatedly exposed to the traumatic experiences of others; a significant occupational risk for behavioral health workers, community health workers, and other caregivers in disaster settings.

**Social Capital:** The networks of relationships and norms of reciprocity and trust that facilitate collective action; a key predictor of community resilience and individual psychological recovery following disaster.

**Substance Use Escalation:** The documented pattern of increased alcohol and drug use in disaster-affected populations, including new-onset problem use and exacerbation of existing substance use disorders.

## Discussion Questions

1. PFA is explicitly not therapy, and crisis counselors in the CCP model are not providing clinical treatment. What are the ethical implications of this distinction, particularly for survivors whose need may exceed what non-clinical services can address? How should the emergency management system respond when clinical treatment capacity is inadequate to meet post-disaster demand?

2. The honeymoon phase following major disasters is real and temporary. How should behavioral health planning account for the typical arc of post-disaster psychological response, including the eventual escalation of distress as the honeymoon phase ends? What planning provisions would extend behavioral health services to match this arc?

3. Responder behavioral health in operational cultures that stigmatize help-seeking presents a genuine tension between individual psychological need and organizational culture. What strategies have evidence of effectiveness in reducing stigma and increasing utilization of behavioral health support among first responders and emergency managers?

4. CCP programs are available only in presidentially declared disaster areas and only for certain timeframes. What are the implications of this eligibility structure for communities affected by smaller, locally declared disasters that do not receive federal declarations? ## Practitioner Takeaways

Behavioral health consequences of disaster are among the most prevalent outcomes and the most persistently underresourced responses. Effective emergency management requires explicit planning for behavioral health services, not just awareness that they exist.

Psychological First Aid is the appropriate field-level tool for non-clinical responders engaging with survivors in the acute phase. It is not therapy and does not require clinical licensure. PFA training for a broad range of response personnel — shelter staff, community health workers, volunteer coordinators — meaningfully extends behavioral health reach.

The Crisis Counseling Program provides an important federal funding mechanism for post-disaster behavioral health services, but its timeframe is shorter than the actual arc of behavioral health need. Practitioners should plan for this gap and advocate for extended monitoring and service availability.

Behavioral health must be explicitly assigned within the ICS/EOC structure. Jurisdictions that treat behavioral health as an implied function under general health will experience coordination failures. Name it, assign it, resource it.

Responder behavioral health is an organizational responsibility, not an individual choice. Command staff at all levels must take active steps to monitor responder status, normalize help-seeking, and ensure access to confidential support resources.

The equity dimensions of disaster behavioral health — differential exposure, differential access, cultural responsiveness, documentation status — must be explicitly addressed in behavioral health planning. Programs designed without attention to these dimensions will systematically fail the most affected populations.

Community behavioral health assets — faith institutions, cultural organizations, community health workers — are frequently more trusted and more accessible to affected populations than formal service delivery channels. Behavioral health response planning should be built around these community assets rather than treating them as supplementary to a professionally led primary system.

The psychological effects of disaster extend far longer than emergency management operations. Advocacy for survivor-centered recovery systems, long-term surveillance, and anniversary-period service availability is a legitimate and necessary component of the emergency manager's role.

## Chapter Notes

**On the Term "Behavioral Health":** The field uses "behavioral health" as an umbrella term covering both mental health and substance use health. Some practitioners and scholars prefer "mental health" for its greater specificity; others argue that "behavioral health" inappropriately implies that mental illness is a product of behavioral choices. This text uses "behavioral health" because it is the dominant term in emergency management and public health system contexts and because it explicitly incorporates substance use, which is a critical but often overlooked dimension of disaster psychological response.

**On PFA Evidence:** The debate about PFA's evidence base deserves honest acknowledgment. PFA has not been evaluated in randomized controlled trials. Researchers and practitioners who are candid about evidence quality note this limitation while also noting that the alternative — ad hoc responder contact with no guiding framework — is likely worse. The practical recommendation remains: train personnel in PFA, deploy it consistently, and continue building the evidence base.

**On CISD Controversy:** The evidence base for single-session Critical Incident Stress Debriefing (CISD) has been significantly challenged. Several systematic reviews have questioned its efficacy and raised concerns about potential harm in some populations. Practitioners should be aware of this controversy. More comprehensive, multi-component CISM approaches have a more favorable evidence profile than single-session debriefing alone.

**On Moral Injury Research:** The construct of moral injury emerged primarily from research with military veterans and has been increasingly applied to healthcare and emergency response contexts, particularly following COVID-19. The evidence base specific to emergency management populations is still developing; practitioners should engage with this literature as it matures.

**Cross-Chapter Connections:** This chapter connects directly to Chapter 4 (equity and vulnerable populations), Chapter 5 (behavioral health surveillance), Chapter 8 (behavioral health within ICS), Chapter 14 (shelter operations and behavioral health), Chapter 22 (mass violence events and responder behavioral health), Chapter 24 (leadership and organizational culture), and Chapter 26 (equity strategy in recovery). Behavioral health threads through virtually every chapter in this text because psychological consequences are universal features of disaster, not specialty topics.
