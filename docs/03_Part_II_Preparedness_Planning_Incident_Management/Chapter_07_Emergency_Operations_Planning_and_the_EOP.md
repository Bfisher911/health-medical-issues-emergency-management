# Chapter 7: Emergency Operations Planning and the EOP

---

## Chapter Overview

Emergency operations planning is the structured process through which jurisdictions, organizations, and agencies think through in advance what they will do when a disaster strikes. The Emergency Operations Plan (EOP) is the primary document that results from that process — a living framework that translates hazard analysis, resource inventories, and stakeholder relationships into operational guidance. For health and medical emergency managers, the EOP is not a bureaucratic artifact stored in a binder; it is the foundational agreement among partners about who will do what, with what resources, under whose authority, and in coordination with whom.

This chapter examines the anatomy of a well-constructed EOP, the planning process that produces one, the federal framework that shapes local and state planning efforts, the relationship between EOPs and other planning instruments, and the specific health and medical components that emergency managers must ensure are present and functional. Throughout, equity integration is treated not as an add-on section but as a quality standard that applies to every component of the plan.

---

## Learning Objectives

Upon completing this chapter, students will be able to:

1. Describe the three-component structure of a comprehensive EOP (basic plan, functional annexes, hazard-specific appendices) and explain the purpose of each.
2. Summarize the Comprehensive Preparedness Guide 101 (CPG 101) planning process and its major phases.
3. Explain the Emergency Support Function (ESF) structure and identify the responsibilities assigned under ESF-8 (Public Health and Medical Services).
4. Articulate the relationship between the EOP and NIMS/ICS doctrine, and explain why alignment matters during response.
5. Identify the key components of a public health annex and describe how it integrates with the broader EOP.
6. Describe how equity considerations should be embedded throughout EOP development, not limited to a special populations section.
7. Explain the EOP testing and maintenance cycle, including the types of exercises used and the review triggers that prompt plan updates.
8. Identify the legal authorities and statutory frameworks that underpin emergency operations planning at the federal, state, and local levels.

## Main Body

### 7.1 What an EOP Is — and What It Is Not

The Emergency Operations Plan is a pre-event document that describes how a jurisdiction will mobilize and coordinate its capabilities to respond to and recover from emergencies and disasters. A well-constructed EOP provides enough structure to guide action under conditions of stress and uncertainty while remaining flexible enough to accommodate the unexpected. It is not a script; it is a framework.

A common misconception among new emergency managers is that the EOP is primarily a reference document — something consulted during response. In practice, the value of an EOP accrues primarily during the planning process itself. The conversations, analyses, and agreements that produce the plan build the relationships, shared mental models, and organizational readiness that make effective response possible. The plan is the artifact; the planning is the process that matters most.

The Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) provides the primary federal guidance for EOP development through Comprehensive Preparedness Guide 101 (CPG 101), first issued in [CITATION: CPG 101 original publication year] and revised most recently in [CITATION: CPG 101 revision year]. CPG 101 establishes a six-step planning process and describes the expected structure of a complete EOP. While CPG 101 is not a legal mandate for most jurisdictions, FEMA ties preparedness grant eligibility and program compliance reviews to CPG 101 standards, which makes alignment functionally necessary for most local and state programs.

The EOP exists within a broader planning architecture. At the federal level, the National Response Framework (NRF) and the National Disaster Recovery Framework (NDRF) establish doctrine and overarching organizational structures. State EOPs align with the NRF and provide state-level frameworks within which local plans are nested. Mutual aid agreements — including the Emergency Management Assistance Compact (EMAC) at the interstate level — are incorporated by reference into EOPs and activated through mechanisms the EOP describes. The EOP does not stand alone; it is one node in an interlocking system of plans, agreements, and authorities.

### 7.2 The Three-Component Structure of the EOP

CPG 101 describes the EOP as consisting of three major components: the basic plan, functional annexes, and hazard-specific or incident annexes. Each component serves a distinct purpose and addresses a different audience.

**The Basic Plan** is the overarching document that applies to all hazards and all activations. It describes the jurisdiction's approach to emergency management, the legal and policy basis for emergency operations, the organization of the Emergency Operations Center (EOC), the activation and demobilization processes, and the general roles and responsibilities of primary agencies. The basic plan is typically where the jurisdiction's mission, vision, and values for emergency management are articulated — and it is where equity commitments, if they exist, should be stated at the level of organizational principle. The basic plan also contains the concept of operations (CONOPS), which explains the general sequence of actions from pre-event through response and recovery. For health and medical emergency managers, the basic plan is where the relationship between the local health authority and the EOC is first defined.

**Functional Annexes** address specific functions that cross hazard types — functions like mass care, communications, logistics and resource management, volunteer and donations management, and public health and medical services. Each annex describes the functional area's purpose, the lead agency and supporting agencies, the specific tasks and responsibilities of each, the resources and authorities required, and the coordination mechanisms that will be used. The public health and medical annex (often aligned with ESF-8) is where most of the health sector's planning guidance lives, and Chapter 9 explores the content of that annex in depth. Other annexes with significant health implications include mass care (sheltering), public information and warning (risk communication), and logistics (supply chain, pharmaceuticals, equipment).

**Hazard-Specific or Incident Annexes** (sometimes called support annexes or appendices depending on the jurisdiction) provide detailed guidance for specific hazard types or incident scenarios. A pandemic influenza annex, a radiological incident annex, a mass fatality annex, and a public health emergency annex are all examples. These documents supplement the basic plan and relevant functional annexes with hazard-specific considerations — unique legal authorities, distinct resource requirements, specialized coordination structures, or specific protocols that do not apply across all hazards. The value of hazard-specific annexes lies in their specificity; the risk is that they become siloed from the main EOP and fail to be updated in parallel.

CPG 101 also describes a fourth type of document — the standard operating procedure (SOP) or standard operating guideline (SOG) — which provides step-by-step instructions for specific tasks within a functional area. SOPs and SOGs are not part of the EOP itself, but they are the bridge between EOP-level guidance and field-level action. Emergency managers should ensure that health and medical SOPs exist for each major EOP function and that they are current, tested, and accessible to the personnel who will use them.

### 7.3 The CPG 101 Planning Process

CPG 101 describes emergency operations planning as a six-step process that is iterative rather than linear. In practice, planning cycles are often compressed, resource-constrained, and politically shaped, but understanding the intended process helps emergency managers identify where their local planning is strong and where it has gaps.

**Step 1: Form a Collaborative Planning Team.** Effective EOPs require input from across the jurisdiction's agencies and sectors. For health and medical emergency managers, this means ensuring that the local health department, hospital systems, EMS agencies, behavioral health providers, and community health organizations are represented in the planning process — not just consulted after a draft is written. It also means including community members, particularly those from historically underserved populations, whose perspective on what will and will not work is essential. Planning teams that lack diverse representation tend to produce plans with blind spots that become visible only during disaster response, at exactly the moment when blind spots are most costly.

**Step 2: Understand the Situation.** This step involves reviewing hazard vulnerability analyses (HVA), threat and hazard identification and risk assessments (THIRA), and community capability assessments to understand what hazards the jurisdiction faces, what capabilities it has, and what gaps exist. For health and medical planners, this step includes assessing the health-specific vulnerability of the community — baseline burden of chronic disease, hospital capacity, mental health service infrastructure, pharmaceutical supply chains, and population demographics including AFN populations (see Chapter 4). The THIRA and Stakeholder Preparedness Review (SPR) process [CITATION: FEMA THIRA/SPR guidance] is the primary federal mechanism for this assessment, and its outputs should directly shape EOP content.

**Step 3: Determine Goals and Objectives.** Using the situational understanding developed in Step 2, the planning team identifies what the plan needs to accomplish. Goals are broad statements of intent; objectives are specific, measurable outcomes. For health and medical emergency management, goals might include ensuring continuity of emergency medical services, maintaining pharmaceutical access for dependent populations during extended emergencies, or ensuring that all shelter sites are accessible to people with disabilities. Objectives might specify target timelines, coverage percentages, or resource thresholds.

**Step 4: Plan Development.** This is where the actual EOP content is drafted. Plan development involves translating goals and objectives into the specific tasks, assignments, resources, and procedures that will guide operations. It requires resolving questions about authority, coordination, and resource allocation that may be politically sensitive. For health and medical planners, this step typically involves negotiating with hospital systems about their obligations under the plan, aligning with state health department protocols, integrating federal guidance from HHS/ASPR, and ensuring that community health workers and behavioral health providers are included in the response structure.

**Step 5: Plan Preparation, Review, and Approval.** Drafted plans require review by stakeholders, legal counsel, and senior leadership before approval. This step also involves ensuring that the plan is internally consistent — that the basic plan, functional annexes, and hazard-specific annexes do not contradict each other, and that resource commitments in one annex are consistent with availability described elsewhere. Equity review at this stage should specifically ask: does this plan, as written, produce equitable outcomes across population groups? Are there provisions that could inadvertently disadvantage certain groups (e.g., evacuation routes that do not serve transit-dependent populations, shelter intake procedures that create barriers for people without documentation)?

**Step 6: Plan Implementation and Maintenance.** The plan must be trained to, exercised, and maintained to remain useful. This step is covered in detail in Section 7.6.

### 7.4 The Emergency Support Function Structure

The National Response Framework organizes the federal government's response capabilities into fifteen Emergency Support Functions (ESFs). Each ESF has a designated coordinating agency, primary agencies, and support agencies, and each addresses a specific domain of response activity. While the ESF structure is federal, most states and many local jurisdictions have adopted analogous structures in their own EOPs, adapting ESF assignments to their specific agency landscape.

**ESF-8: Public Health and Medical Services** is the ESF most directly relevant to health and medical emergency managers. At the federal level, the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) serves as the ESF-8 coordinating agency. Within HHS, the Assistant Secretary for Preparedness and Response (ASPR) and the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) are the primary agencies. At the state level, the state health department typically serves as the ESF-8 lead, coordinating with local health departments, hospital systems, EMS agencies, and federal partners. At the local level, ESF-8 responsibilities are assigned to the local health authority, although the specific organizational structure varies significantly by jurisdiction.

ESF-8 encompasses a wide range of functions, organized into fifteen support areas under the NRF:

- Public health surveillance and epidemiological investigation
- Medical surge (mass casualty support, patient tracking, alternate care sites)
- Medical countermeasures (SNS coordination, dispensing, administration)
- Patient movement (coordination with NDMS, state patient movement systems)
- Behavioral health
- Public health laboratory services
- Veterinary medical support
- Worker health and safety
- Mass fatality management (coordination with ESF-13 and medical examiner)
- Food, drug, and potable water safety
- Radiological/nuclear/chemical/biological hazard support

The breadth of ESF-8 reflects the intersection of public health with virtually every aspect of disaster response. Health and medical emergency managers should be familiar with the full scope of ESF-8, even if their agency focuses primarily on one or two of these functions.

Other ESFs have significant health implications that are often underappreciated:

**ESF-6 (Mass Care, Emergency Assistance, Temporary Housing, and Human Services)** covers shelter operations, feeding, emergency first aid, and reunification. The coordination between ESF-6 and ESF-8 — particularly around medical needs shelters and access and functional needs accommodations — is a common point of friction during response and should be explicitly addressed in the EOP.

**ESF-11 (Agriculture and Natural Resources)** covers food and water safety during disasters, which has direct public health implications, particularly during infrastructure failures.

**ESF-13 (Public Safety and Security)** affects health operations in several ways, including security at points of dispensing (PODs) during mass prophylaxis events, security at alternate care sites, and forensic support for mass fatality incidents.

**ESF-15 (External Affairs)** covers public information and risk communication, which is central to public health emergency response. The relationship between the public information officer (PIO), the public health agency, and ESF-15 should be defined in the EOP.

### 7.5 The Public Health and Medical Annex

The public health and medical annex (often labeled as ESF-8 in state and local EOPs) is the core document guiding health sector operations. A well-developed annex addresses several essential content areas.

**Authority and Activation.** The annex should clearly identify the legal authority under which public health emergency powers are exercised, the conditions and process for activation of those powers, and the relationship between public health legal authority and general emergency authority. This includes state public health emergency statutes, gubernatorial emergency declarations, the Public Health Service Act authorities relevant to federal involvement, and local health authority provisions under state law. The annex should also address the interaction between health authority and other authorities — for example, the role of law enforcement in enforcing quarantine or isolation orders. See Chapter 3 for a detailed treatment of public health law and authority.

**Organizational Structure.** The annex should describe how health operations are organized within the EOC and in the field, including the use of ICS (see Chapter 8). This includes identifying the Health and Medical Branch Director position within the EOC Operations Section, the positions that report to that branch, and the liaison relationships with state and federal health partners.

**Resource Inventories.** The annex should document what resources are available — hospital beds, ventilators, medical countermeasures stockpiles, pharmaceutical caches, EMS units, laboratory capacity — and the thresholds at which additional resources would be requested from state or federal sources. Resource inventories date quickly; the annex should specify the process for maintaining current data and the mechanism for requesting mutual aid resources.

**Medical Surge and Alternate Care.** The annex should describe how the jurisdiction will scale up medical care capacity during a mass casualty or patient surge event. This includes hospital coordination mechanisms, protocols for establishing alternate care sites (ACS) or federal Medical Stations, criteria for activating Crisis Standards of Care (see Chapter 9), and the relationship between medical surge planning and mutual aid agreements with neighboring jurisdictions.

**Mass Countermeasures.** For jurisdictions that participate in the SNS system, the annex should describe the process for requesting SNS assets, the receiving, staging, and storing (RSS) operations, and the dispensing strategy including points of dispensing (PODs) and closed POD arrangements with healthcare facilities and employers. Equity considerations around mass countermeasures — physical accessibility, language access, hours of operation, trusted messenger engagement — must be addressed in the annex, not assumed.

**Vulnerable Population Considerations.** The annex should include explicit provisions for populations with access and functional needs, including people with disabilities, older adults, people with limited English proficiency, people experiencing homelessness, and people in institutional settings. These provisions should describe specific operational adaptations, not simply acknowledge that these populations exist. For example: what is the process for identifying and prioritizing home oxygen users during power outages? How will the POD be accessible to individuals who use power wheelchairs? These are operational questions that require operational answers.

**Behavioral Health.** The annex (or a behavioral health sub-annex) should address the CCP structure, the process for requesting SAMHSA DTAC support, integration with ESF-6 shelter operations, and responder behavioral health protocols. See Chapter 6 for the substantive framework.

**Mass Fatality.** The annex should describe coordination with the medical examiner or coroner, protocols for victim identification, family assistance center operations, and the conditions under which temporary interment or alternative mortuary arrangements may be needed. Mass fatality planning often falls between ESF-8 and ESF-13, and the annex should clarify lead responsibilities.

### 7.6 EOP Testing and Maintenance

An EOP that is not exercised is, at best, an untested hypothesis. The Homeland Security Exercise and Evaluation Program (HSEEP) [CITATION: HSEEP guidance] provides the federal framework for exercises and ties directly to EOP maintenance. HSEEP describes a spectrum of exercise types, organized from discussion-based to operations-based:

**Discussion-based exercises** include seminars, workshops, tabletops, and games. They are primarily designed to familiarize participants with plans, policies, and procedures or to explore concepts and issues in a low-stakes environment. Tabletop exercises (TTX) are among the most commonly used preparedness tools for health and medical emergency managers — they bring together key stakeholders to walk through a scenario and identify plan gaps, coordination challenges, and resource shortfalls. TTXs are valuable precisely because they surface assumptions that planners did not know they were making.

**Operations-based exercises** include drills, functional exercises (FE), and full-scale exercises (FSX). Drills test a single, specific function — a medical countermeasures dispensing drill, for example, or a hospital communications drill. Functional exercises test the coordination of multiple functions in a simulated operational environment, typically using an EOC or a simulated command post. Full-scale exercises deploy actual resources and personnel in a real-world environment, simulating an actual response. Full-scale exercises are resource-intensive and should be reserved for testing capabilities that have already been validated at lower exercise levels.

CPG 101 specifies that EOPs should be reviewed and updated following:
- An actual incident or activation (after-action review findings should drive plan revisions)
- A significant change in the jurisdiction's capabilities, resources, or organizational structure
- A change in relevant law, regulation, or policy
- A significant change in the hazard profile (e.g., a new industrial facility, a new disease threat)
- At a minimum, on a scheduled cycle — typically every four years for the full plan, with annual reviews of annexes

For health and medical planners, after-action reviews (AARs) are one of the most valuable — and most underutilized — tools for plan improvement. An honest AAR captures not only what went wrong but why, and its corrective action plan (CAP) should assign specific tasks with specific timelines to specific individuals. AARs that produce generic findings and no accountability tend to produce plans that fail in the same ways next time.

### 7.7 Equity Integration in EOP Development

Federal preparedness guidance has moved progressively toward more explicit equity language, but implementation at the local level remains uneven [CITATION: research on equity gaps in local EOPs]. The shift from "special needs" to "access and functional needs" to more comprehensive equity frameworks reflects growing recognition that planning assumptions built on a default population model systematically underperform for a significant portion of the actual community.

Equity integration in EOP development is not a discrete planning step; it is a quality standard that applies at every step of the CPG 101 process:

**In Step 1 (Planning Team Formation):** Is the planning team demographically representative of the community? Does it include community health workers, disability advocates, representatives of immigrant and refugee communities, tribal liaisons, and organizations that serve older adults? If not, the plan will have blind spots.

**In Step 2 (Situation Understanding):** Does the THIRA/HVA incorporate Social Vulnerability Index data? Are health equity measures — not just averages — included in the baseline assessment? Are AFN populations inventoried with enough detail to support operational planning?

**In Steps 3 and 4 (Goals, Objectives, and Plan Development):** Are there specific objectives related to equitable outcomes, not just coverage of the average population? Does the plan include operational specifics for serving populations with communication barriers, mobility limitations, or economic constraints?

**In Step 5 (Review and Approval):** Has the plan been reviewed by community organizations representing historically underserved populations? Has it been reviewed specifically for provisions that could be inadvertently harmful — for example, shelter policies that effectively exclude people with criminal histories, or evacuation plans that require private vehicle access?

**In Step 6 (Implementation and Maintenance):** Do exercises include participants and scenarios that test equity-relevant capabilities? Does the AAR process capture performance gaps related to equity?

FEMA's Whole Community approach [CITATION: FEMA Whole Community guidance] provides a philosophical foundation for equity integration, but health and medical emergency managers should look beyond FEMA guidance to the more robust equity frameworks developed within public health, disability rights, and community organizing. See Chapter 4 for the foundational equity framework and Chapter 26 for equity strategy in recovery.

### 7.8 Legal Authorities Underlying Emergency Operations Planning

EOPs do not operate in a legal vacuum. They are built on layers of legal authority — federal, state, and local — that define what emergency managers can do, with what resources, under whose oversight, and with what accountability mechanisms. Health and medical emergency managers must be familiar with the primary legal frameworks that shape emergency operations.

**At the federal level**, the Robert T. Stafford Disaster Relief and Emergency Assistance Act (Stafford Act) [CITATION: Stafford Act statutory citation] is the primary legal authority for federal disaster declarations and federal assistance to states and localities. A presidential major disaster declaration triggers federal assistance programs and activates the NRF/ESF structure. A public health emergency declaration by the HHS Secretary under Section 319 of the Public Health Service Act (PHSA) [CITATION: PHSA Section 319 citation] triggers additional authorities specific to public health response, including emergency use authorizations (EUAs) for medical countermeasures. The Public Health Service Act also contains the legal authority for NDMS, quarantine at ports of entry, and other federal public health powers.

The Post-Katrina Emergency Management Reform Act (PKEMRA) [CITATION: PKEMRA citation] significantly restructured FEMA's authorities and accountability mechanisms following the failures of Hurricane Katrina in 2005. Among other provisions, PKEMRA established requirements for disability integration in emergency management and created the Disability Integration Advisory Council.

The Pandemic and All-Hazards Preparedness Act (PAHPA) [CITATION: PAHPA statutory citation] and its reauthorizations established ASPR and BARDA, created the SNS strategic review process, and strengthened the federal medical countermeasures development and deployment infrastructure.

**At the state level**, emergency management authority flows from state statutes — typically a state emergency management act and a state public health emergency authority statute. These statutes define the conditions under which the governor may declare a state emergency, what powers are available under such a declaration (including commandeering of resources, mandatory evacuation orders, and suspension of certain regulations), and the relationship between state authority and local authority. State law also defines the scope of local health authority — whether the local health officer has independent authority to issue isolation and quarantine orders, what powers attach to a local health emergency declaration, and what liability protections apply to actions taken in good faith during an emergency.

**At the local level**, local ordinances, board of health regulations, and inter-agency agreements create the local legal framework within which the EOP operates. Mutual aid agreements — including the state EMAC compact, regional healthcare coalition compacts, and bilateral agreements with neighboring jurisdictions — are legal instruments that must be activated according to their terms and that create obligations for signatory agencies.

The legal authorities relevant to health and medical emergency management are significantly more complex than this summary suggests, and emergency managers should have access to legal counsel familiar with public health law. Chapter 3 provides a more detailed treatment of the legal and regulatory framework.

## Applied Vignette

**The Planning Team Problem in Millbrook County**

Millbrook County has a population of [DATA: population figure] people, with a significant agricultural sector that employs a large undocumented immigrant workforce concentrated in two census tracts. The county's EOP was last formally updated in [DATA: year] and its public health annex has not been substantially revised since [DATA: year]. The county emergency manager, Denise Holloway, has been tasked with leading a full EOP revision over the following eighteen months.

Denise convenes her planning team with representatives from the Sheriff's Office, Public Works, the county hospital, the local health department, and the county school district. She submits the meeting summary to the county administrator, who approves moving forward.

When she meets with Dr. Yolanda Pryce, the county health officer, to discuss the public health annex, Dr. Pryce immediately asks who is on the planning team. Denise lists the agencies represented. Dr. Pryce asks: "Do you have anyone from the farmworker health clinic? The food bank? The disability services coalition? Any of the churches that actually work in those census tracts?"

Denise does not.

Dr. Pryce explains the problem. The prior public health annex assumed that mass countermeasure dispensing would be conducted through the county fairgrounds in a drive-through format. An estimated [DATA: percentage] percent of the households in the two agricultural census tracts do not own a vehicle. The prior annex had no provision for this. During a tabletop exercise eighteen months ago, this gap was noted in the AAR. The corrective action plan said, simply: "explore mobile dispensing options." No one had followed up.

Denise restructures the planning team. She contacts the Farmworker Justice Collaborative, the county disability services director, and the pastor of the largest Spanish-language congregation in the area. She schedules a community input session at the farmworker health clinic in the evening, when workers are available.

The resulting revision of the public health annex includes a mobile dispensing protocol with three satellite POD sites in the agricultural census tracts, operating during hours that accommodate shift workers. It includes a Spanish-language communication template developed with community input — not machine-translated from English. It includes a protocol for coordinating with the farmworker health clinic as a trusted point of contact for SNS asset distribution.

The plan is not perfect. Denise knows that the mobile dispensing protocol has never been exercised and that several resource commitments depend on agreements that are still informal. She schedules a functional exercise in nine months to test the system before it needs to be used for real. The AAR from that exercise, she decides, will be the most important document her office produces that year.

## Key Terms

**Basic Plan:** The overarching component of an EOP that applies to all hazards and describes the jurisdiction's general approach to emergency management, legal authorities, EOC organization, and high-level roles and responsibilities.

**Comprehensive Preparedness Guide 101 (CPG 101):** The primary federal guidance document for emergency operations planning, published by FEMA, describing the planning process and expected structure of a complete EOP.

**Corrective Action Plan (CAP):** A document produced following an exercise or incident that assigns specific improvement tasks to specific personnel with specific timelines.

**Emergency Support Function (ESF):** One of fifteen organizational units through which the federal government (and most state and local governments) organizes emergency response capabilities; ESF-8 covers Public Health and Medical Services.

**Functional Annex:** A component of the EOP that provides detailed guidance for a specific response function that applies across multiple hazard types.

**Hazard-Specific Annex:** A component of the EOP that provides detailed guidance tailored to a specific hazard or incident type, supplementing the basic plan and functional annexes.

**Homeland Security Exercise and Evaluation Program (HSEEP):** The federal framework for planning, conducting, and evaluating exercises; used to test EOPs and identify corrective actions.

**Concept of Operations (CONOPS):** A section of the basic plan that describes the general sequence of operational activities from pre-event through response and recovery.

**Threat and Hazard Identification and Risk Assessment (THIRA):** A federal process used by jurisdictions to identify threats and hazards, assess their potential consequences, and estimate the resources needed to achieve target capabilities.

**Whole Community:** A FEMA planning approach that emphasizes engagement of the full range of community stakeholders — including individuals, families, businesses, nonprofit organizations, faith-based organizations, and all levels of government — in preparedness and planning.

5. What is "good enough" in emergency operations planning, and how would you know when you have reached it?

6. The chapter describes equity integration as a quality standard applied throughout the planning process, rather than a discrete section added to the plan. What does this mean concretely for a health and medical emergency manager conducting a plan review? What questions would they ask, and at what points in the document?

## Practitioner Takeaways

- The value of an EOP accrues primarily during the planning process, not during response. Relationships, shared mental models, and organizational readiness built through planning are the real preparedness product.

- A public health and medical annex that lacks operational specificity — that describes goals rather than tasks, assigns responsibilities to roles rather than agencies, and defers to "protocols to be developed" — is not an operational document.

- CPG 101's planning process is designed to be iterative and continuous. Emergency managers who treat plan development as a discrete project that concludes with approval are missing most of the cycle.

- Equity gaps in EOPs rarely result from deliberate exclusion; they result from planning teams that are not representative of the community, assumptions built on a default population model, and operational details that work for the majority but fail for the most vulnerable.

- After-action reviews are among the most powerful improvement tools available to emergency managers, and among the most underutilized. An AAR that produces vague findings and no accountability is a missed opportunity.

- ESF-8 is broad. Health and medical emergency managers who limit their planning focus to clinical care or mass casualty response, without attending to behavioral health, mass fatality, food and water safety, and the other ESF-8 functions, will produce plans with predictable gaps.

- Exercises that test only what works — designed to succeed rather than to find failure — do not improve operational readiness. The purpose of an exercise is to find the plan's weaknesses before an actual disaster does.

**On the scope of ESF-8:** The fifteen support areas of ESF-8 identified in this chapter reflect the structure of the NRF as of the knowledge cutoff for this text [CITATION: NRF edition/year]. Emergency managers should verify the current ESF-8 structure against the most recent NRF and ASPR guidance, as ESF assignments and support areas have been revised in prior NRF updates.

**On CPG 101 compliance:** CPG 101 is guidance, not regulation.
