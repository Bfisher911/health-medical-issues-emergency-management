# Chapter 8: Incident Management Systems and the Health Branch

---

## Chapter Overview

When a disaster occurs, the problem is never only medical or clinical. It is organizational. Hospitals receive patients faster than they can process them. Supplies arrive without documentation. Volunteer clinicians appear with unknown credentials. Agencies issue contradictory public statements. Personnel receive orders from multiple supervisors. Information flows up to leadership who cannot act on it and fails to reach field personnel who need it. The core challenge of disaster response is not primarily a shortage of good intentions — it is the breakdown of organized action under pressure.

Incident management systems are the operational infrastructure that transforms a collection of well-meaning organizations into a coordinated response. This chapter examines the National Incident Management System (NIMS) and its core component, the Incident Command System (ICS), with particular attention to how health and medical functions are organized within these structures. It describes the Medical and Public Health Branch within the EOC Operations Section, the Health Branch within field-based ICS structures, and the coordination mechanisms that link field operations to the EOC and to state and federal partners. It also addresses the persistent challenges of applying ICS doctrine — developed largely in the context of wildfire suppression — to the complex, multi-agency, multi-jurisdictional environment of public health and medical response.

---

## Learning Objectives

Upon completing this chapter, students will be able to:

2. Explain the fundamental organizational structure of ICS, including the five major sections and their functions.
3. Describe the concepts of span of control, unity of command, and unified command, and explain how they apply in health and medical response.
4. Identify the roles and responsibilities within the Medical and Public Health Branch in an EOC-based ICS structure.

7. Discuss the limitations of ICS as a framework for public health emergency response and identify adaptations that health agencies have developed.
8. Describe the coordination mechanisms between field ICS, the EOC, and state and federal partners.

## Main Body

### 8.1 The Purpose and Principles of NIMS

The National Incident Management System (NIMS), first published by the Department of Homeland Security in [CITATION: NIMS original publication year] and most recently revised in [CITATION: NIMS current edition year], provides a comprehensive, national approach to incident management. NIMS establishes a common doctrine, terminology, and organizational framework that allows agencies from different jurisdictions and sectors to work together effectively, regardless of whether they have worked together before.

The need for NIMS arose from a recurring pattern of coordination failures in large-scale disasters. When agencies use different terminology for the same function, communication breaks down. When personnel receive orders from multiple supervisors, they cannot know which instruction to follow. When agencies do not share a common organizational framework, the connections between them during response must be invented under pressure — exactly when improvisation is most costly. NIMS does not prescribe the specific actions taken during response; it standardizes the management structure within which those actions are organized.

NIMS has three major components: resource management, command and coordination, and communications and information management. For health and medical emergency managers, the command and coordination component — anchored in the Incident Command System — is the framework that most directly shapes day-to-day preparedness and response work.

The core principles of NIMS are:

**Flexibility.** NIMS is designed to be scalable to incidents of any size, type, and complexity. A small EMS response to a multi-vehicle crash and a multi-year pandemic response both use ICS, although at very different scales. The same organizational framework applies whether two agencies are involved or two hundred.

**Standardization.** NIMS establishes standardized terminology, position titles, forms, and procedures. This standardization is what allows a Logistics Section Chief from one jurisdiction to plug into an ICS structure in another jurisdiction and immediately understand their role.

**Unity of effort.** NIMS is designed to achieve coordinated action across organizations that have different chains of command, different authorities, and different missions. This is accomplished through unified command structures and clear liaison mechanisms.

**Accountability.** NIMS establishes clear lines of authority, documented resource tracking, and check-in/check-out procedures. These accountability mechanisms serve both operational and safety functions.

### 8.2 The ICS Structure

The Incident Command System is the organizational backbone of NIMS. It provides a flexible, scalable structure for managing any incident — fire, flood, mass casualty event, pandemic, chemical release, or complex public health emergency. ICS organizes response around five major functional sections, each headed by a Section Chief who reports to the Incident Commander (IC) or Unified Command.

**The Incident Commander (IC)** holds overall responsibility for the incident. The IC establishes incident objectives, approves the Incident Action Plan (IAP), and has authority over all incident activities. The IC is supported by four Command Staff positions: the Public Information Officer (PIO), the Safety Officer (SO), and the Liaison Officer (LNO). A fourth position, the Legal Advisor, is increasingly used in complex public health emergencies. In a Unified Command, multiple ICs from different agencies share command authority and work from a jointly developed IAP. Unified Command is the appropriate structure when an incident crosses jurisdictional boundaries or requires multiple agency leads.

**Operations Section** is responsible for the direct management of tactical field activities. The Operations Section Chief organizes field resources into Branches, Divisions, Groups, and Units according to the scope and complexity of the incident. For health and medical response, the Operations Section contains the structures most directly relevant to field medical care, mass casualty operations, sheltering, and mass countermeasure dispensing.

**Planning Section** is responsible for collecting, evaluating, and disseminating information about the incident. The Planning Section produces the Incident Action Plan (IAP), manages resource tracking, and develops the Incident Status Summary (ICS-209). The Planning Section Chief also leads the operational period briefing and coordinates the development of future operational period plans. Situation awareness — who has what information about what is happening — is one of the most persistent challenges in complex health and medical emergencies, and the Planning Section is the organizational home for managing it.

**Logistics Section** is responsible for providing facilities, services, and materials to support incident operations. In health and medical response, logistics functions include pharmaceutical supply chain management, medical equipment procurement and tracking, feeding and hydration of response personnel, and transportation coordination. The logistics challenges of complex health emergencies — maintaining cold chain for vaccines, tracking controlled substances in the field, managing just-in-time supply for alternate care sites — are among the most technically demanding aspects of the response.

**Finance/Administration Section** is responsible for tracking incident costs, managing contracts and procurement, processing time records, and managing claims. In complex, extended health emergencies, the Finance/Administration Section is critical for documenting expenditures that may be eligible for federal reimbursement under a Stafford Act declaration.

### 8.3 Span of Control, Unity of Command, and Unified Command

Three ICS principles deserve particular emphasis for health and medical emergency managers because they are frequently misunderstood or incorrectly applied.

**Span of control** refers to the number of subordinates that a supervisor can effectively manage. ICS doctrine specifies an optimal span of control of one supervisor for every three to seven subordinates, with a target of one to five [CITATION: ICS span of control guidance]. This principle exists because research on organizational performance under stress consistently shows that performance degrades when supervisors are responsible for too many subordinates. In health and medical response, span of control violations are common — a single ESF-8 coordinator may be expected to track simultaneously the activities of hospital liaisons, public health lab operations, medical countermeasures logistics, and behavioral health coordination. When span of control is violated, things fall through the cracks, and the things that fall through are typically the least immediately visible, not the least important.

**Unity of command** means that each individual reports to a single supervisor. When an emergency medical technician receives orders from the EMS supervisor, the hospital emergency department nurse manager, and the EOC Medical Branch Director simultaneously, unity of command has broken down. In health and medical response, unity of command is frequently challenged because many response personnel have dual roles — a hospital nurse serving as a volunteer at an alternate care site, for example, or a local health department epidemiologist embedded in the state health operations center. Clear pre-event agreements about chains of command during activations prevent the confusion that arises when multiple principals assume that a particular individual is their subordinate.

**Unified command** is the ICS mechanism for managing incidents that involve multiple agencies with different authorities, jurisdictions, or missions. In a unified command, designated officials from each participating agency share command, develop objectives jointly, and approve a single IAP. Unified command does not mean that agency authority is surrendered — each agency retains its legal authority and organizational identity — but it does mean that agencies must develop shared objectives and commit to a joint plan. The challenges of establishing effective unified command in public health emergencies are significant: public health agencies, hospital systems, EMS, emergency management agencies, and law enforcement all have different authorities, cultures, and operational rhythms. Building unified command relationships through pre-event exercises and memoranda of understanding substantially reduces the friction of establishing them under the pressure of an actual incident.

### 8.4 The EOC-Based Health and Medical Function

In most medium to large jurisdictions, the primary coordination point for health and medical response is not a field incident command post but an Emergency Operations Center (EOC). The EOC is the facility from which senior officials coordinate strategic-level response — resource acquisition, policy decisions, multi-agency coordination, and public communication. Within the EOC, health and medical functions are typically organized in the Operations Section under a Medical and Public Health Branch (the specific title varies by jurisdiction).

The Medical and Public Health Branch in a fully activated EOC typically includes the following positions:

**Medical and Public Health Branch Director:** Responsible for coordinating all health and medical operations within the EOC. Reports to the Operations Section Chief. Serves as the senior health authority in the EOC and the primary point of contact for state health department liaisons.

**ESF-8 Coordinator (or Public Health Officer):** Manages the specific functions assigned under ESF-8, including surveillance, laboratory coordination, and public health emergency legal authority. In some jurisdictions, this role is combined with the Branch Director role at lower activation levels.

**Medical Surge Coordinator:** Manages the coordination of hospital capacity, patient tracking, alternate care site operations, and the movement of patients within and beyond the jurisdiction. Maintains situational awareness of hospital diversion status, bed availability, and ventilator capacity.

**EMS Coordinator:** Maintains situational awareness of EMS unit availability and deployment, coordinates EMS mutual aid, and manages mass casualty operations from the EOC level.

**Medical Countermeasures Coordinator:** Manages the SNS request process, coordinates receipt and distribution of medical countermeasures, and oversees POD operations from the EOC level.

**Behavioral Health Coordinator:** Coordinates crisis counseling program deployment, manages behavioral health resources across shelter and field sites, and liaises with state behavioral health authority. See Chapter 6.

**Mass Fatality Coordinator:** Coordinates with the medical examiner or coroner, manages family assistance center operations, and liaises with mortuary services.

**Hospital Liaison Officers (LNOs):** Embedded at major hospital systems, reporting back to the EOC on hospital status. The Hospital Incident Command System (HICS) provides the ICS-compatible structure within which hospital operations are managed during emergencies; hospital LNOs are the bridge between HICS and the EOC.

Not all of these positions will be activated at every incident. ICS doctrine specifies that positions are activated based on the needs of the incident, not filled by default. A small-scale public health response might be managed by a single ESF-8 Coordinator reporting to the Operations Section Chief, with Branch and Unit positions activated only if the incident expands. The Incident Action Plan specifies which positions are active for each operational period.

### 8.5 Field-Based Health and Medical ICS

When health and medical operations are conducted in the field — at a mass casualty incident, an alternate care site, a mass countermeasure dispensing operation, or a community shelter — ICS structures are established at the field level. Field health and medical ICS structures use standard ICS terminology and organization, adapted to the specific operational context.

In field-based ICS for medical response, health functions are typically organized under a **Medical Group** or **Medical Branch** within the Operations Section, depending on the complexity of the incident. Key positions include:

**Medical Group Supervisor (or Medical Branch Director):** Responsible for overall medical operations at the incident site. Reports to the Operations Section Chief.

**Treatment Unit Leader:** Manages the patient treatment area, which in ICS-MCI (mass casualty incident) operations is divided into immediate, delayed, and minor treatment areas based on triage category.

**Triage Unit Leader:** Manages primary and secondary triage of patients, using a standardized triage algorithm (START, JumpSTART, SALT — see Chapter 10).

**Transport Unit Leader:** Coordinates patient transport, tracks patient destination, and manages ambulance staging. Patient tracking from the field to the receiving hospital is a critical function that is frequently poorly executed; loss of patient tracking data has significant implications for family reunification, fatality identification, and after-action epidemiological surveillance.

**Staging Area Manager:** Manages ambulance staging, ensuring that transport vehicles are available when needed without congesting the treatment area.

**Medical Supply Unit Leader:** Manages medical supply inventories at the field site, tracks consumption, and requests resupply through the Logistics Section.

For public health field operations — SNS Points of Dispensing, community vaccination clinics, emergency shelters — ICS structures are adapted accordingly. A mass dispensing operation, for example, typically organizes functional groups around queue management, intake assessment, medication dispensing, adverse event monitoring, and public information. The specific structure depends on the dispensing model (open POD, closed POD, drive-through) and the scale of operation.

### 8.6 Hospital Incident Command System (HICS)

Hospitals are complex organizations with their own operational and governance structures. When a hospital activates its emergency response, it uses the Hospital Incident Command System (HICS), which is an ICS-compatible framework adapted to the hospital environment [CITATION: HICS guidance document, edition/year].

HICS organizes hospital emergency response around the same five-section structure as ICS. The Hospital Incident Commander (typically the hospital administrator on call, or the designated emergency operations officer) leads the response. The Operations Section in HICS is organized around the hospital's core functions: medical staff operations, nursing operations, ancillary services, and — during surges — expanded care operations that may include patient care in non-traditional hospital spaces.

HICS is designed to interface with community-level ICS structures through the hospital LNO assigned to the EOC. This liaison role is critical for maintaining situational awareness about hospital capacity, coordinating patient distribution during mass casualty events, and ensuring that resource requests from the hospital reach the EOC where they can be addressed. The LNO is also the conduit through which EOC decisions about patient routing, resource allocation, and public communication reach hospital leadership.

In jurisdictions with Healthcare Coalitions (HCC) — now widely established through ASPR's HPP grant program [CITATION: ASPR HPP program citation] — the HCC serves as the coordination body for the hospital sector, aggregating situational awareness from member hospitals, coordinating inter-hospital resource sharing, and liaising with the EOC's Medical and Public Health Branch. The HCC does not command hospitals; it coordinates them. This distinction is important because hospital systems retain independent authority over their own operations, and the HCC's coordination role depends on cooperative relationships, not legal authority.

### 8.7 Area Command and Multi-Agency Coordination

When multiple incidents of the same type are occurring simultaneously in a jurisdiction, or when a single large incident requires coordination across multiple incident management organizations, two additional ICS structures become relevant: Area Command and Multi-Agency Coordination Systems (MAC Systems).

**Area Command** is activated to oversee multiple incidents that are each managed by an ICS organization. Area Command does not replace incident command; it provides strategic coordination above it. In health and medical response, Area Command might be used during a widespread disease outbreak affecting multiple counties simultaneously, with each county managing its own incident but requiring coordination of shared resources (personnel, pharmaceutical stockpiles, laboratory capacity) at a regional level.

**Multi-Agency Coordination (MAC) Systems** — which include the EOC as their primary operational component — support coordination across agencies and organizations that retain their own command authority. The MAC System does not have command authority over participating agencies; it facilitates information sharing, resource coordination, and joint decision-making. In practice, the MAC System function is performed by the EOC, and the EOC Policy Group (senior officials from participating agencies) makes the cross-agency decisions that the MAC System is designed to enable.

Understanding the distinction between command authority and coordination authority is essential for health and medical emergency managers. The EOC does not command hospitals, private physicians' offices, or community health clinics. It can coordinate, request, incentivize, and — if the jurisdiction has enacted appropriate public health legal authorities — potentially require participation. But the management of hospital operations remains with hospital leadership, and the relationship between the EOC and hospital sector is fundamentally one of coordination, not command.

### 8.8 The Incident Action Plan and Operational Periods

One of the most operationally important concepts in ICS is the operational period — the specific time interval for which an Incident Action Plan (IAP) is developed and executed. In a fast-moving incident, operational periods may be as short as four to eight hours. In a prolonged public health emergency, operational periods may be twenty-four to seventy-two hours. The operational period provides a regular cadence for assessing progress, updating the situation, revising tactics, and managing personnel fatigue.

The IAP documents the objectives for the operational period, the tactical assignments of each ICS unit, the resource roster, the communications plan, the safety message, and the medical plan (ICS-206, which describes protocols for managing responder injuries and medical emergencies at the incident). The IAP is a management tool for the incident leadership and a reference document for all response personnel; it should be distributed to all active ICS positions at the start of each operational period.

For health and medical emergency managers who are new to ICS, the IAP process can seem bureaucratic relative to the pace of response. The value of the IAP is not the document itself but the planning cycle it enforces. The requirement to articulate specific objectives for the next operational period forces response leadership to assess what has been accomplished, what has not, why, and what will be done differently. This disciplined cycle of objective-setting, execution, and assessment is what distinguishes effective incident management from reactive crisis response.

### 8.9 Limitations of ICS for Public Health Response

ICS was developed in the early 1970s by a consortium of California fire agencies responding to wildfire coordination failures [CITATION: ICS origin history]. It was designed for incidents with a defined geographic boundary, a single incident commander with clear authority, a relatively homogeneous workforce, and a response arc that moves from escalation to stabilization to demobilization over days to weeks. These characteristics describe wildfires reasonably well. They describe pandemic influenza poorly.

Public health emergencies often lack a defined geographic boundary. A disease outbreak crosses county and state lines, requiring coordination across dozens of jurisdictions, each with its own EOP, legal authority, and organizational culture. Public health emergencies may extend for months or years, far beyond the typical ICS operational arc. The "response workforce" includes clinicians, epidemiologists, community health workers, behavioral health professionals, laboratory scientists, and communications specialists — a far more heterogeneous group than a firefighting crew — with different professional cultures and institutional allegiances.

The authority structure is also different. The Incident Commander in a wildfire ICS has clear authority over all personnel on the incident, including those from other agencies who have been dispatched under EMAC or NWCG [CITATION: NWCG citation] protocols. Public health agencies do not have command authority over hospital systems, private practitioners, or community organizations. The "command" in public health incident command is frequently a coordination role wearing command terminology.

These limitations have been recognized by public health practitioners and researchers [CITATION: research on ICS limitations in public health], and adaptations have been developed:

**Public Health Emergency Operations Center (PHEOC)** frameworks, developed by CDC and WHO, provide guidance for organizing public health emergency management functions in ways that are ICS-compatible but adapted to the public health operational environment [CITATION: CDC PHEOC guidance, WHO EPRP citation].

**Incident Management Teams with public health specialization** have been developed at the federal level (HHS Secretary's Operations Center, ASPR's NDMS), at the state level (state public health rapid response teams), and increasingly at the regional level through Healthcare Coalitions. These IMTs bring pre-organized, pre-trained public health ICS capability that can be deployed to support local jurisdictions.

**Extended operations ICS adaptations** acknowledge that maintaining a 24/7 incident command structure over a months-long pandemic response is not sustainable with normal jurisdictional staffing. Succession planning, position rotation schedules, demobilization planning even within ongoing responses, and hybrid activation models (partial ICS for surge periods, routine operations at other times) are all adaptations that experienced public health emergency managers have developed.

The goal is not to apply ICS rigidly to contexts for which it was not designed; it is to apply the underlying principles — clear objectives, defined authority, regular planning cycles, documented resource tracking, systematic information management — to the management of public health emergencies, using structures that are adapted to those emergencies' distinctive characteristics.

## Applied Vignette

**Standing Up the Health Branch: Day One of the Riverside Chemical Release**

At 11:14 AM on a Tuesday, a railcar carrying anhydrous ammonia derails in the industrial district of Riverside City, releasing a toxic cloud that triggers immediate evacuation of approximately [DATA: number] people within a [DATA: radius]-mile radius. Three schools and two nursing homes are in the evacuation zone.

The City EOC activates within twenty minutes. The Emergency Manager, Carlos Reyes, assumes overall incident management and assigns the Operations Section Chief role to his deputy. The Fire Department Branch is activated immediately. Carlos calls Dr. Amara Weston, the City Health Officer, and asks her to activate the Medical and Public Health Branch.

Dr. Weston faces an immediate challenge: the Medical and Public Health Branch positions — Medical Surge Coordinator, EMS Coordinator, Medical Countermeasures Coordinator, Behavioral Health Coordinator — are supposed to be filled by personnel from the health department, hospital system, and EMS agency. It is 11:30 AM on a Tuesday. Most of those personnel are at their normal jobs, some of them already responding to the incident in their field roles rather than their EOC roles.

Dr. Weston starts with what she has. She assigns her deputy director to the Surge Coordinator role and begins tracking hospital ED diversions, which are already increasing as the first wave of symptomatic evacuees arrives. She calls the hospital coalition coordinator, who activates the HCC's situational awareness system and begins aggregating hospital status reports. She calls the EMS Director, who is at the scene but delegates a lieutenant to the EOC EMS Coordinator role.

Forty minutes into the incident, the nursing home evacuation is incomplete. Two nursing homes had a combined [DATA: number] residents, many of whom require specialized transport. The EOC has not yet established a Medical Needs Transportation function — it was assumed that EMS would handle nursing home transport, but EMS units are committed to triage at the primary scene.

Dr. Weston requests a Medical Needs Transportation Unit under the Medical Branch and asks the Operations Section Chief to assign it resources. The Operations Section Chief escalates to the Policy Group to authorize contracting for accessible medical transport from a regional vendor. Thirty minutes later — seventy minutes after the incident began — specialized transport begins moving nursing home residents.

The after-action review notes three things. First, the activation sequence for the Medical and Public Health Branch had too many dependencies on personnel who were simultaneously needed in the field. The corrective action is a staffing plan that identifies primary and alternate personnel for each EOC position. Second, the Medical Needs Transportation function was not pre-organized because the planning team assumed EMS would manage it — an assumption that was never validated. The corrective action is a nursing home and special medical needs transportation annex. Third, Dr. Weston's decision to start with available personnel and build out the Branch during the first operational period — rather than waiting until all positions were filled — was the right call, and the corrective action plan notes it as a best practice for future activations.

## Key Terms

**Area Command:** An ICS structure activated to oversee multiple incidents of the same type occurring simultaneously or a single large incident requiring coordination across multiple incident management organizations.

**Incident Action Plan (IAP):** A plan developed for each operational period that documents response objectives, tactical assignments, resources, communications plan, and safety information.

**Incident Command System (ICS):** The on-scene incident management component of NIMS, providing a standardized organizational structure for directing resources and managing response activities.

**Medical and Public Health Branch:** The ICS organizational unit within the EOC Operations Section responsible for coordinating health and medical response activities.

**Multi-Agency Coordination (MAC) System:** A combination of facilities, equipment, personnel, procedures, and communications that support coordination across agencies that retain their own command authority.

**National Incident Management System (NIMS):** The comprehensive national approach to incident management that provides common doctrine, terminology, and organizational frameworks for multi-agency response.

**Operational Period:** The time interval for which an Incident Action Plan is developed and executed; provides the management cycle for updating objectives, assessing progress, and managing resources.

**Span of Control:** The ICS principle specifying that supervisors should be responsible for three to seven subordinates, with five as the optimal number; designed to maintain effective oversight and prevent coordination failures.

**Unified Command:** An ICS structure for managing incidents that involve multiple agencies with different authorities or jurisdictions; designated officials share command, develop joint objectives, and approve a single IAP.

**Unity of Command:** The ICS principle that each individual reports to a single supervisor; designed to eliminate confusion about authority and instruction during complex, multi-agency response.

**Hospital Incident Command System (HICS):** An ICS-compatible organizational framework adapted for hospital emergency response, providing structured roles and processes for managing hospital operations during emergencies.

## Discussion Questions

1. NIMS requires all organizations receiving federal preparedness funding to be NIMS-compliant. What does NIMS compliance actually require, and how would you assess whether a local health department is genuinely NIMS-compliant versus merely formally compliant?

2. Unified Command requires participating agencies to develop shared objectives and commit to a single IAP. What are the practical barriers to effective unified command in a multi-agency public health response, and how might pre-event relationships and agreements reduce those barriers?

3. The chapter describes the EOC's relationship with hospital systems as coordination rather than command. What legal and practical mechanisms exist to influence hospital behavior during public health emergencies, and what are their limits? How should health and medical emergency managers plan for situations where a hospital system's independent decisions significantly affect community-level response?

4. ICS was designed for incidents with defined boundaries and a command authority that controls all response resources. How does the structure need to be adapted for a pandemic response that crosses jurisdictional boundaries and extends for many months? What ICS principles remain applicable, and which require adaptation?

5. The vignette describes a Health Officer who begins activating the Medical Branch with incomplete staffing rather than waiting for all positions to be filled. What are the tradeoffs in that decision, and under what circumstances might it be better to wait?

6. The Healthcare Coalition does not command hospitals; it coordinates them. What does this distinction mean for how an HCC can respond when a hospital is making decisions that affect community-level operations negatively? What leverage does the HCC actually have?

## Practitioner Takeaways

- ICS is a management tool, not a bureaucratic requirement. The underlying principles — clear objectives, defined authority, regular planning cycles, documented resource tracking — improve operational performance in complex, multi-agency responses regardless of whether every ICS form is completed correctly.

- Span of control violations are among the most common and most consequential ICS failures in health and medical response. When a single coordinator is responsible for tracking too many functions simultaneously, critical information is missed.

- The EOC's relationship with hospitals, clinics, and community health organizations is one of coordination, not command. Health and medical emergency managers should invest in building the cooperative relationships and pre-event agreements that make coordination effective, because they cannot compel cooperation through command authority alone.

- HICS provides a framework for hospital emergency management that is compatible with community-level ICS. Emergency managers should be familiar with HICS and ensure that the hospital LNO role is clearly defined in the EOP.

- The Incident Action Plan is not paperwork; it is the management cycle. The discipline of setting objectives for each operational period, executing against them, and assessing outcomes is the mechanism through which incident management improves over time.

- Public health emergencies have characteristics — geographic diffusion, extended duration, authority complexity — that require adaptations to standard ICS doctrine. The solution is not to abandon ICS principles but to apply them intelligently to a different operational context.

- After-action reviews following every EOC activation — even small-scale activations — build the organizational learning that makes large-scale responses more effective. The best time to identify ICS gaps is not during a major disaster.

## Chapter Notes

**On NIMS compliance:** FEMA's NIMS Implementation Objectives provide detailed guidance on what NIMS compliance requires of different types of organizations. Emergency managers should verify current requirements at fema.gov, as specific compliance metrics have been updated periodically.

**On HICS editions:** The HICS Guidebook has been revised multiple times. Emergency managers should ensure that hospital partners are using a current edition consistent with NIMS compliance requirements. The California Emergency Medical Services Authority (EMSA) has historically maintained the HICS program nationally, but local hospitals may adapt HICS for their specific operational context.

**On Healthcare Coalitions:** HPP-funded Healthcare Coalitions are present in all states, but their organizational capacity, geographic scope, and operational maturity vary considerably. Emergency managers should assess the specific capabilities of their regional HCC and not assume that all HCCs offer equivalent services.

**On ICS training:** FEMA offers ICS training through the Emergency Management Institute (EMI), including IS-100 (Introduction to ICS), IS-200 (ICS for Single Resources and Initial Action Incidents), IS-300 (Intermediate ICS), and IS-400 (Advanced ICS). Public health agencies have developed health-specific ICS training through CDC and TRAIN.
