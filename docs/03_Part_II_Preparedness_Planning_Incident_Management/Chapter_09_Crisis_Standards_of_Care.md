# Chapter 9: Crisis Standards of Care

## Chapter Overview

When a disaster overwhelms healthcare capacity, the system faces a problem with no comfortable solution: more patients need care than resources can support. Crisis Standards of Care (CSC) describes the formal, structured response to that reality. It is a legal and ethical framework that permits clinicians and health systems to shift from individualized, optimal care toward population-based, equitable allocation of scarce resources. CSC is not an improvised triage practice invented under pressure. It is a deliberately constructed policy framework built before disasters strike so that the hardest decisions rest on defensible principles rather than chaos.

This chapter examines every layer of the CSC framework. It begins with definitions and the triggering conditions that differentiate CSC from routine surge management. It moves through the foundational ethical principles that govern all CSC frameworks, the three-tier continuum that structures the transition from conventional to crisis care, and the roles of state health departments, hospital systems, and regional coordination bodies in activating and deactivating CSC. It addresses specific resource allocation challenges including ventilator allocation frameworks and the critical-care scoring instruments used to operationalize them. It confronts the equity failures documented during past CSC activations, particularly racial and disability bias embedded in triage criteria. It explains the legal protections available to clinicians and institutions during CSC and the documentation requirements that support those protections. And it addresses the often-neglected process of deescalation from crisis back to conventional care. Across all sections, this chapter integrates the ethical, operational, and equity dimensions of CSC as inseparable rather than parallel concerns.

## Learning Objectives

Upon completing this chapter, the reader will be able to:

1. Define Crisis Standards of Care and distinguish it from routine surge management and contingency care.
2. Describe the three-tier CSC continuum — conventional, contingency, and crisis — and identify the indicators that signal movement between tiers.
3. Explain the core ethical principles underlying CSC frameworks, including utility, equity, duty to care, and procedural fairness.
4. Identify the triggering conditions and legal authorities that permit formal CSC activation at the state, regional, and institutional levels.
5. Describe the structure and limitations of ventilator allocation frameworks, including the Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score and its documented equity problems.
6. Analyze how racial bias, disability discrimination, and socioeconomic factors have been embedded in prior CSC triage criteria and explain how frameworks should address these concerns.
7. Explain the legal protections available to clinicians and institutions operating under CSC, including liability shields and documentation requirements.
8. Describe the process for deescalating from crisis care back to conventional care and the indicators that guide that transition.
9. Identify the roles of state health departments, healthcare coalitions, and hospital systems in developing, activating, and operationalizing CSC.

## 9.1 Defining Crisis Standards of Care

The phrase "crisis standards of care" became a formal policy term through the 2009 work of the Institute of Medicine (IOM), now the National Academy of Medicine (NAM). The 2009 IOM report *Guidance for Establishing Crisis Standards of Care for Use in Disaster Situations* provided the first comprehensive federal-level framework for this concept. That foundational work, refined through subsequent NAM publications in [CITATION: IOM/NAM CSC series years], defined CSC as a substantial change in usual healthcare operations and the level of care it is possible to deliver, implemented only when shortfalls in resources exist after all surge efforts have been exhausted.

This definition contains three key elements. First, CSC involves a substantial change, meaning it is not an incremental adjustment. Canceling elective procedures, opening overflow units, and calling in off-duty staff are surge responses, not CSC. CSC represents a qualitatively different operating mode, not simply a busier one. Second, CSC is implemented when shortfalls in resources exist, meaning demand for a specific resource definitively exceeds all available supply including stockpiles, mutual aid, and coalition resources. Third, CSC is the response after all surge efforts have been exhausted, meaning it is a last resort, not an early convenience.

The NAM framework also articulated five essential components of a CSC system. These are: (1) a strong ethical grounding that is publicly communicated in advance; (2) an integrated and adaptive community and provider engagement, education, and communication plan; (3) clear indicators and triggers for transitioning between care standards; (4) clinical processes and operations; and (5) legal authority and legal protections for institutions and individuals operating within the system. These five components serve as an organizing scaffold for much of the content in this chapter.

### Distinguishing CSC from Related Concepts

Emergency managers and health professionals frequently confuse CSC with adjacent concepts. Clarity on three distinctions is essential.

**CSC vs. Standard of Care.** In normal circumstances, the legal standard of care is the level of care that a reasonably competent healthcare provider with similar training would provide under similar circumstances. CSC does not eliminate this standard; it modifies what "similar circumstances" means. When a healthcare system formally activates CSC through appropriate legal authority, clinicians are held to the standard of care that a reasonably competent provider would deliver in a declared CSC environment, not in normal circumstances.

**CSC vs. Triage.** Mass casualty triage is one tool used during CSC, but the two are not synonymous. Triage — the rapid sorting of patients to prioritize care — occurs in many contexts that are not CSC. CSC is a system-level policy framework that establishes the authority, criteria, and accountability structures within which resource-allocation tools like triage protocols operate. A mass casualty incident with 30 patients may involve triage; it may not rise to CSC level if resources remain adequate for all patients.

**CSC vs. Rationing.** The term "rationing" carries political and emotional freight that CSC frameworks generally avoid, though the substantive reality is similar. CSC frameworks prefer the language of resource allocation and standardized clinical decision-making precisely because "rationing" implies a bureaucratic imposition on clinical judgment. CSC frameworks try to preserve clinical judgment within a structured and defensible process.

## 9.2 The Three-Tier CSC Continuum

The most operationally useful concept in CSC is the three-tier continuum developed through the NAM framework and subsequently refined by state health departments and healthcare coalitions across the country. The three tiers — conventional, contingency, and crisis — describe a progression of adaptive strategies available to healthcare systems under increasing resource stress.

### Conventional Care

Conventional care is normal operations. Resources are adequate, staffing ratios are standard, all patients receive individualized care optimized for their clinical needs, and facility operations are within expected parameters. Most disaster responses, even large ones, remain within conventional care if healthcare system capacity is adequate. A major trauma center treating surge patients from a nearby mass casualty incident is operating within conventional care if its resource levels remain sufficient to meet all individual patient needs.

### Contingency Care

Contingency care begins when resources are stressed but not exhausted. Healthcare systems implement adaptive measures that may slightly reduce the quality of care or increase risk to individual patients, but those measures remain clinically and ethically defensible and carry the expectation of returning to conventional care when the event resolves. Examples of contingency measures include extending ventilator circuits beyond normal replacement intervals when supply is constrained, using non-FDA-approved equipment analogues under emergency use authorization, quartering patients in hallways or converted spaces, extending nursing staff ratios from 1:4 to 1:6 for stable patients, and implementing crisis formulary substitutions when specific medications are unavailable.

The ethics of contingency care are important to understand. Because contingency measures represent modest deviations from normal practice that are clinically defensible and reversible, they do not require the full formal activation of crisis standards. They do require internal institutional review, documentation, and communication to staff, patients, and families.

### Crisis Care

Crisis care is the tier in which the gap between patient need and available resources is so significant that providing individual optimal care to every patient is not possible. Crisis care requires the explicit implementation of resource-allocation frameworks and clinical decision-making criteria that would be inappropriate under normal or contingency circumstances. Examples include ventilator allocation protocols that withdraw mechanical ventilation from patients who do not meet survival likelihood thresholds, reusing single-use personal protective equipment across multiple patient encounters without decontamination capacity, assigning single nurses to ratios that would normally require staffing interventions, and implementing blood product allocation criteria that restrict transfusions to patients with defined clinical thresholds.

Crisis care must be formally activated through appropriate legal authority. The decision to activate crisis care is not a clinical decision made by a single physician or hospital administrator. It is a system-level decision made through a defined governance process that involves state health authority, healthcare coalition coordination, and in most frameworks, explicit recognition of a significant emergency condition.

### Transitions Between Tiers

One of the most underappreciated aspects of the CSC continuum is that transitions between tiers are not automatic or obvious. They require defined triggers and decision processes. Moving from conventional to contingency care occurs when specific resource thresholds are crossed — such as ICU occupancy reaching [PLACEHOLDER: state-defined percentage threshold], ventilator availability falling below [PLACEHOLDER: percentage of peak-capacity reserve], or emergency department diversion rates exceeding [PLACEHOLDER: regional threshold]. Moving from contingency to crisis care requires a higher bar and usually formal activation. Moving back from crisis to contingency or conventional care — deescalation — requires equally defined criteria and is addressed in Section 9.9.

## 9.3 Ethical Foundations of CSC

CSC frameworks are ethics frameworks before they are clinical protocols. The clinical procedures are the operationalization of underlying ethical principles. Those principles are contested in some respects and well-established in others.

### The Utilitarian Core and Its Limits

Most CSC frameworks are grounded in a utilitarian principle: when resources cannot meet all needs, priority should go to uses that produce the greatest benefit. In the clinical context, this typically means prioritizing patients most likely to survive with treatment, specifically those who will benefit from the scarce resource. The SOFA score and similar severity-of-illness instruments attempt to operationalize this principle by quantifying short-term survival likelihood.

The utilitarian core is defensible but carries limits that CSC frameworks must explicitly acknowledge. Pure utilitarian maximization can generate outcomes that violate other ethical principles, including equity, dignity, and duty of care. Maximizing lives saved, for example, can justify excluding entire categories of patients whose disability or underlying illness reduces their statistical survival probability even when treatment would offer meaningful benefit and quality of life. CSC frameworks must articulate where the utilitarian logic stops.

### Equity as a Coequal Principle

The NAM framework and subsequent state CSC guidance emphasize that equity must be treated as a coequal principle to utility, not a secondary consideration applied as an afterthought. An equitable CSC framework ensures that allocation criteria do not systematically disadvantage groups based on race, disability, socioeconomic status, insurance status, or other social characteristics unrelated to the expected benefit of treatment. This is not a platitude; it is a specific design requirement. Section 9.7 addresses the documented equity failures of prior frameworks in detail.

### Duty to Care

CSC frameworks must also address the tension between duty to care and clinician safety. Clinicians have professional and ethical obligations to treat patients even under difficult circumstances. But duty to care is not absolute, and it does not require clinicians to accept unreasonable personal risk. CSC frameworks should address the conditions under which duty to care may be modified and the institutional supports — including adequate personal protective equipment, psychological support, and moral injury resources — that allow clinicians to fulfill their obligations safely.

### Procedural Fairness

Because CSC involves decisions about who receives scarce resources and who does not, procedural fairness is a critical ethical requirement. Procedurally fair CSC frameworks use criteria developed through transparent, inclusive processes; apply criteria consistently; separate clinical decision-makers from bedside treating clinicians where feasible; provide a mechanism to review or reconsider allocation decisions; and communicate criteria, decisions, and rationale to patients and families. The legitimacy of CSC decisions depends in substantial part on the legitimacy of the process used to make them.

### Reciprocity

CSC frameworks that require sacrifice from specific individuals or populations — patients who forgo treatment, clinicians who assume additional risk, families who accept altered care for loved ones — have an ethical obligation of reciprocity. The health system and the public it represents owe those who bear burdens under CSC meaningful compensation, support, and acknowledgment. This includes financial support for families affected by allocation decisions, psychological and moral injury resources for healthcare workers, and long-term follow-up and accountability.

## 9.4 Triggering Conditions and Legal Authorities

CSC does not self-activate. It requires a set of operational conditions, a defined decision-making process, and appropriate legal authority. The absence of any of these elements leaves healthcare systems and clinicians without the protections CSC is designed to provide.

### Triggering Conditions

Triggering conditions for CSC activation are the observable indicators that the healthcare system's resource position has deteriorated to the point where crisis-level allocation criteria are necessary. Common triggering conditions include:

**Healthcare system capacity indicators.** These include ICU occupancy rates, ventilator availability, bed availability, blood product supply levels, staff availability accounting for illness and injury, and burn bed and trauma capacity. Most state CSC frameworks identify specific quantitative thresholds that trigger formal review for CSC activation.

**Supply chain indicators.** These include critical medication shortages affecting essential treatments, oxygen supply disruption, personal protective equipment depletion below defined reserves, and blood product shortages beyond the capacity of the regional blood banking network to address.

**Demand surge indicators.** These include mass casualty event size relative to regional capacity, rate of new critical illness cases relative to ICU capacity expansion ability, and projected illness curve trajectory in a pandemic or chemical exposure scenario.

**Infrastructure indicators.** These include utility outages affecting hospital operations, road network damage that prevents patient transfer, and communication system failures that prevent regional coordination.

The important principle is that triggering conditions should be defined before events occur. A state CSC plan with pre-defined, quantitative triggers allows healthcare coalitions and health departments to monitor them in real time and initiate the activation process based on objective thresholds rather than subjective impressions.

### Decision-Making Authority

Who decides that CSC has been activated varies by state, but most frameworks vest primary authority in the state health officer, state health department, or state emergency management agency in consultation with the health officer. This is deliberate. System-level decisions about standards of care require system-level authority and accountability. Individual hospital administrators or clinicians should not bear the authority or the burden of making this determination alone.

In practice, the activation process involves coordination between the state health authority, regional healthcare coalitions, and hospital systems. The healthcare coalition plays a critical role in aggregating facility-level status data, facilitating mutual aid and resource sharing to delay or avoid crisis activation, and communicating the regional picture to state health authorities. The state health authority makes the formal activation decision. Hospitals and health systems implement the resulting allocation frameworks within their facilities.

### Federal Authorities

At the federal level, several legal mechanisms support CSC implementation. The Secretary of Health and Human Services has authority under the Public Health Service Act [CITATION: 42 U.S.C. § 247d] to declare a public health emergency, which activates access to emergency funds and authorities under the Strategic National Stockpile. The declaration of a Section 319 public health emergency also activates the authority to issue Emergency Use Authorizations (EUAs) under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act [CITATION: 21 U.S.C. § 360bbb-3], permitting the use of unapproved drugs, devices, and diagnostics — a critical authority for contingency and crisis care.

The PREP Act (Public Readiness and Emergency Preparedness Act) [CITATION: 42 U.S.C. § 247d-6d] provides liability immunity for manufacturers, distributors, and administrators of covered medical countermeasures when the Secretary of HHS issues a PREP Act Declaration. While the PREP Act primarily addresses countermeasure liability, its declaration conditions are relevant to the broader legal environment in which CSC operates.

The Robert T. Stafford Disaster Relief and Emergency Assistance Act [CITATION: 42 U.S.C. §§ 5121-5207] governs federal disaster declarations, which can unlock federal resource support and trigger FEMA's coordination role, including support for medical and public health operations. However, Stafford Act declarations do not themselves alter medical standards of care; that authority remains at the state level.

### State Legal Authorities

The primary legal authorities for CSC activation are at the state level. State emergency management statutes and public health laws vary considerably in how explicitly they address CSC. Some states have enacted specific CSC legislation or adopted formal CSC plans through the state health department. Others address it through the emergency powers provisions of existing public health law, which authorize the governor or state health officer to declare a public health emergency and implement emergency measures including alterations to healthcare operations.

States that have developed explicit CSC frameworks generally include provisions for: authorized deviation from normal staffing ratios, scope of practice, and licensing requirements; liability protections for clinicians and institutions acting in good faith within an activated CSC framework; authority for the state health officer to issue allocation guidance with binding effect on licensed healthcare facilities; and processes for communicating activation status, implementing criteria, and tracking outcomes.

Emergency managers should understand that the absence of a state CSC framework is itself a preparedness gap. Healthcare systems operating without clear legal cover during a crisis-level event face significant liability exposure and may face inconsistent, ad hoc decision-making that the NAM framework was specifically designed to prevent.

## 9.5 Ventilator Allocation Frameworks

Mechanical ventilator allocation received intense public scrutiny during the COVID-19 pandemic, during which ventilator availability was initially projected as a critical constraint in many jurisdictions [CITATION: pandemic ventilator projections date/source]. Ventilator allocation frameworks — formalized protocols for determining who receives available ventilators when demand exceeds supply — are the most visible and controversial component of CSC.

### The SOFA Score and Related Instruments

Most ventilator allocation frameworks developed in the United States and elsewhere use the Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score or a modified version of it as the primary clinical triage instrument. The SOFA score was developed to assess organ failure severity in ICU patients and was not designed for emergency allocation purposes [CITATION: original SOFA publication citation]. It scores dysfunction across six organ systems: respiratory, coagulation, liver, cardiovascular, central nervous system, and renal. Higher scores indicate more severe organ dysfunction and are associated with higher short-term mortality risk.

The rationale for using SOFA in allocation frameworks is that it provides an objective, reproducible, quantitative measure of short-term survival likelihood. A patient with a very high SOFA score is statistically unlikely to survive even with mechanical ventilation, while a patient with a moderate score may have a high likelihood of survival with ventilatory support. Frameworks using SOFA typically establish cut-points that define who is eligible for ventilator allocation, who is already receiving a ventilator and may be considered for reallocation, and who receives palliative care rather than mechanical ventilation.

Some frameworks also incorporate measures of medium-term survival likelihood, such as major comorbidity scores or life-limiting illness criteria, to address situations where short-term survival is possible but life expectancy is very short regardless of ventilator support. This is where equity and disability rights conflicts most sharply arise, as discussed in Section 9.7.

### Triage Teams and Separation of Roles

Most current ventilator allocation frameworks attempt to separate the roles of allocating clinician and treating clinician. The bedside clinician — the intensivist, hospitalist, or respiratory therapist — continues to provide the best possible care for the patient in front of them within whatever resources are available. A separate triage team, composed of clinicians not directly responsible for the patient's care, applies the allocation framework, reviews scoring, and makes allocation decisions.

This separation serves several purposes. It protects bedside clinicians from bearing sole responsibility for allocation decisions that may conflict sharply with their therapeutic obligations. It reduces the likelihood of allocation decisions being influenced by interpersonal relationships, social characteristics of patients, or fatigue and moral injury in individual clinicians. And it creates a defined accountability structure with its own documentation requirements.

### Resource-Limited vs. Absolute Scarcity

An important operational distinction exists between resource-limited CSC and absolute scarcity CSC. Resource-limited conditions occur when a specific resource is constrained but not exhausted — a hospital has ten ventilators available but may need fifteen by tomorrow if disease progression continues at current rates. Absolute scarcity occurs when the resource is genuinely exhausted and no amount of mutual aid, transfer, or emergency procurement can meet all needs. Most allocation frameworks are designed for absolute scarcity, but resource-limited conditions may warrant activating the allocation framework's intake criteria even before exhaustion to prevent a disorderly transition.

### Reallocation and Withdrawal

The most ethically fraught component of ventilator allocation is the reallocation provision — the protocol by which a patient already receiving mechanical ventilation may be considered for withdrawal of that resource to provide it to a patient with higher survival likelihood. Reallocation provisions exist in some state CSC frameworks and are absent or prohibited in others. They are among the most legally and ethically contested elements of CSC.

Emergency managers do not make these clinical decisions, but they must understand that a CSC plan without a reallocation provision may provide inadequate protection in a true absolute scarcity scenario. States and healthcare coalitions must address this element explicitly during planning, including consultation with disability rights advocates, legal counsel, ethics boards, and community stakeholders.

## 9.6 State and Regional CSC Frameworks

The development, maintenance, and activation of CSC frameworks is a state and regional responsibility, not a federal one. The federal government — primarily through HHS, ASPR, and CDC — provides guidance, technical assistance, funding (through the Hospital Preparedness Program and Public Health Emergency Preparedness Cooperative Agreements), and model frameworks. Implementation is a state function exercised in collaboration with regional healthcare coalitions and individual healthcare systems.

### State Health Department Role

State health departments are responsible for developing the legal and policy framework within which CSC operates. This includes drafting the specific allocation criteria or principles that will govern scarce resource allocation, establishing the governance and activation process, providing legal cover for clinicians and institutions, maintaining and updating the framework between events, and exercising the authority to activate and deactivate CSC status.

State health departments typically develop CSC plans in collaboration with the state emergency management agency, the state hospital association, medical boards, nursing boards, state medical societies, disability rights organizations, healthcare coalition leads, bioethics experts, and community representatives including those from historically underserved populations.

### Healthcare Coalition Role

Healthcare coalitions, funded under the ASPR Hospital Preparedness Program, serve as the primary regional coordination infrastructure for CSC implementation. Healthcare coalitions aggregate situational awareness data from member facilities in real time, facilitating the monitoring of the triggering condition thresholds described in Section 9.4. During an event, the coalition coordinates mutual aid and resource sharing to preserve conventional and contingency care for as long as possible. When crisis-level conditions are reached, the coalition communicates the regional picture to state health authorities, supports consistent implementation of the allocation framework across member facilities, and tracks outcomes including allocation decisions and patient outcomes.

The healthcare coalition does not have command authority over individual hospital systems. Its role is coordination, information sharing, and facilitating the activation of state-level authority. The legal authority to alter standards of care lies with the state; the coalition's authority is advisory and facilitative.

### Hospital and Health System Role

Individual hospitals and health systems are responsible for operationalizing CSC frameworks within their facilities. This includes developing internal CSC protocols consistent with the state framework, training staff on allocation criteria and the triage team structure, establishing triage team rosters and activation procedures, ensuring adequate documentation systems are in place, providing palliative care infrastructure for patients who will not receive scarce resources under allocation criteria, and supporting staff psychological and moral well-being throughout a CSC activation.

Hospitals also have an obligation to communicate with patients and families about CSC activation and its implications for care, to ensure that allocation decisions are documented and reviewable, and to cooperate with post-event review and accountability processes.

## 9.7 Equity Failures in CSC Frameworks: Documented Problems and Required Solutions

CSC equity failures are not hypothetical concerns. They are documented problems that have appeared repeatedly in the frameworks developed and debated over the past two decades. Emergency managers and public health professionals who are serious about CSC must confront this record directly.

### The SOFA Score and Racial Disparity

The SOFA score, described in Section 9.5, was not designed as an allocation tool, and its component measures carry documented racial disparities. Creatinine levels, which contribute to the renal component of SOFA, are systematically affected by differences in muscle mass across racial groups. Studies have found that SOFA-based frameworks, applied without correction, would disproportionately disadvantage Black patients because of these physiological differences that do not reflect actual organ function differences [CITATION: relevant study citation]. Several state CSC frameworks developed or revised after 2020 have addressed this by removing the creatinine component from SOFA or replacing SOFA with alternative instruments in specific contexts.

### Disability Discrimination

Multiple disability rights organizations filed federal civil rights complaints against state CSC plans that incorporated quality-of-life judgments or long-term survival estimates into their allocation criteria. These plans, in some cases, explicitly excluded patients with specific diagnoses — including intellectual disabilities, spinal cord injuries, and degenerative neurological conditions — from ventilator allocation regardless of their short-term survival likelihood with treatment. The Department of Health and Human Services Office for Civil Rights issued a bulletin in 2020 [CITATION: OCR bulletin date and citation] clarifying that Sections 504 and 1557 of the Rehabilitation Act and the Affordable Care Act prohibit discrimination against people with disabilities in CSC frameworks, and that pre-existing disability, degree of intellectual disability, and long-term life expectancy based on disability status cannot be used as exclusion criteria.

Several states revised their CSC frameworks after 2020 in response to these complaints and the OCR guidance. Emergency managers should confirm that their state's current CSC framework has been reviewed for ADA and Section 504 compliance.

### Socioeconomic and Insurance Status

In practice, CSC activation decisions and implementation can be influenced by socioeconomic status through mechanisms that are less explicit than the racial and disability disparities described above. Patients who present to under-resourced safety-net hospitals when their state has not activated CSC may receive lower-quality contingency care than patients at well-resourced private hospitals in the same region. The absence of consistent, region-wide CSC activation creates a system in which the burden of resource scarcity falls disproportionately on facilities serving low-income and uninsured populations.

Regional healthcare coalition frameworks that coordinate consistent CSC activation across all facilities regardless of ownership or payer mix are an essential equity protection. A CSC that is activated for some hospitals but not others in the same region is not functioning as a system.

### Language Access and Communication Equity

CSC frameworks include patient and family communication requirements. Communicating allocation decisions — especially withdrawal of treatment decisions — to families who have limited English proficiency requires professional interpretation, not ad hoc use of family members or bilingual staff in non-interpreter roles. The absence of language access infrastructure during CSC is both a civil rights compliance failure and a cause of harm to patients and families. CSC plans should explicitly address language access as an operational requirement, not an afterthought.

### Building Equity Into Framework Design

Contemporary guidance on CSC framework design, including NAM reports and disability rights legal analysis, suggests several design principles for equity-conscious frameworks. These include: using only criteria with demonstrated empirical validity for predicting short-term survival with treatment; removing criteria based on pre-existing disability, quality of life, or long-term prognosis unrelated to the acute illness; building in demographic monitoring to detect disparate impacts during activation; providing a rapid review mechanism to challenge allocation decisions; and requiring post-event demographic analysis of allocation decisions with public reporting. Emergency managers overseeing CSC planning should ensure these design principles are part of their framework's development and review processes.

## 9.8 Legal Protections and Documentation Requirements

The ability to attract and retain healthcare workers in crisis conditions, and the legal legitimacy of CSC decisions, depends in substantial part on the legal protections available to clinicians and institutions operating within an activated CSC framework.

### Liability Shields and Limitations

Most state emergency health powers statutes include provisions for civil immunity for healthcare providers acting in good faith during declared emergencies. The scope of these protections varies considerably by state. Some states provide broad immunity for all acts and omissions during a declared emergency; others limit immunity to specific actions taken pursuant to a formal CSC framework. Some states require that the action be taken under the direction of the state health officer or consistent with official guidance; others provide immunity based on the clinician's good faith judgment within the general emergency context.

The PREP Act, as noted in Section 9.4, provides immunity specifically for covered countermeasure activities, not for general clinical care decisions. Emergency managers should be aware of this distinction and should not describe PREP Act immunity as general CSC liability protection for clinical staff.

Immunity statutes generally do not protect against gross negligence or willful misconduct. Clinicians who deviate from the established CSC allocation framework without justification, who apply criteria inconsistently in ways that suggest bias, or who fail to document their decisions appropriately may lose the protection of liability shields.

### Documentation Requirements

Documentation is the operational foundation of legal protection in CSC. Documentation requirements for CSC events typically include the following elements.

**Triage decisions.** Each allocation decision must be documented including the patient's clinical data at the time of the decision, the allocation criteria applied, the specific scores or assessments used, the triage team members involved, and the outcome of the decision. This documentation serves both accountability and quality improvement purposes.

**Reassessments.** Patients whose clinical status changes require documented reassessment under the allocation framework. A patient initially determined ineligible for a scarce resource may become eligible as their condition improves, or a patient receiving a resource may require reallocation consideration as their condition deteriorates. All reassessments require documentation.

**Palliative care provision.** Patients who do not receive scarce resources under the allocation framework must receive documented palliative and comfort care. The absence of documentation for palliative care provision is both an ethical failure and a legal vulnerability.

**Communication with patients and families.** The communication of allocation decisions to patients and families, including the content of the conversation, must be documented. The identity and role of the person who communicated the decision, the interpreter used if any, and the family's questions and responses should all be documented.

**System-level records.** At the facility and coalition level, documentation of the triggering conditions that prompted CSC activation, the decision-making process for activation, the criteria in use, and aggregate data on allocation decisions and outcomes is required for post-event review, legal accountability, and quality improvement.

Most hospital electronic health record systems are not designed to support CSC documentation needs out of the box. CSC planning should include the development of supplemental documentation tools — paper-based forms, electronic templates, or structured entries — that can be implemented quickly during activation.

## 9.9 Deescalation from Crisis to Conventional Care

Crisis Standards of Care creates operational momentum that is difficult to reverse. Clinical teams adapt to resource constraints, new staffing patterns become normalized, and the sense of urgency that prompted CSC activation may persist even after objective conditions improve. The failure to formally deescalate from crisis care when conditions permit is both an ethical problem — patients receive suboptimal care when better care is available — and a legal problem — clinicians continue operating under altered standards without the authority that justified those alterations.

Deescalation is the formal process of transitioning from crisis or contingency care back toward conventional care as resource constraints are resolved. It is as important a planning topic as initial activation, and it receives less attention in most CSC frameworks.

### Deescalation Triggers

Deescalation triggers are the mirror image of activation triggers. They should be pre-defined, quantitative where possible, and linked to the same operational indicators used to monitor for escalation. When those indicators return to normal ranges — ventilator availability recovers to adequate levels, ICU occupancy drops below threshold, critical medication supplies are restored — the framework should have defined criteria for moving back down the tier continuum.

Deescalation from crisis to contingency care does not mean immediately restoring all normal operations. Contingency measures may remain appropriate for a period while full supply chain recovery occurs and staff return from illness or injury. The goal is systematic, documented movement back toward conventional care as conditions permit, not an abrupt return to normal.

### Operational Considerations for Deescalation

Deescalation operations include: notifying facility staff, patients, and families that CSC status has been reduced; restoring normal staffing ratios as returning staff allow; reinstating suspended services and elective procedures in priority order; resuming normal documentation and consent processes; returning borrowed equipment to regional stockpiles; and issuing a formal notification to the state health authority and healthcare coalition that crisis-level operations have ended.

Deescalation also includes the initiation of the after-action review process. The detailed review of allocation decisions, patient outcomes, communication processes, and equity indicators should begin as early as possible while memories are fresh and documentation is accessible. This review serves quality improvement purposes, informs future framework development, and provides the accountability to patients and communities that the ethical principle of reciprocity requires.

### Psychological Deescalation

The psychological dimensions of CSC deescalation are as important as the operational ones and are routinely underresourced. Healthcare workers who have been operating under crisis conditions — making or implementing allocation decisions, delivering palliative care to patients who might have survived under normal conditions, working in unsafe conditions, and carrying the moral weight of CSC — do not automatically recover when the operational crisis ends. Moral injury, burnout, and post-traumatic stress symptoms are documented sequelae of mass casualty and pandemic healthcare work [CITATION: moral injury healthcare literature citation]. 

CSC plans should include explicit provisions for psychological deescalation including: mandated rest and reduced scheduling after extended crisis operations, access to peer support programs, mental health resources for staff who request them, and recognition of the burden borne by the workforce. Emergency managers who treat CSC deescalation as purely an operational problem will find they have undermined their workforce's capacity for the next event.

## Applied Vignette: The Greenfield Regional CSC Decision

**Setting:** A severe influenza pandemic in [PLACEHOLDER YEAR] is entering its fourth week in the Greenfield Regional Health System service area, a mid-sized metropolitan region of approximately [PLACEHOLDER] residents served by six acute care hospitals, two critical access hospitals, and a regional healthcare coalition.

**The situation:** The Greenfield Regional Healthcare Coalition has been tracking system-level indicators for two weeks. ICU occupancy across all member facilities has exceeded [PLACEHOLDER]% for seven consecutive days. Ventilator availability system-wide is at [PLACEHOLDER]% of pre-pandemic capacity, after [PLACEHOLDER] ventilators were deployed from the Strategic National Stockpile twelve days earlier. Three hospitals have declared internal diversion. Mutual aid transfers have been exhausted — neighboring regions are at comparable or worse capacity. The state health officer has been briefed daily and has been tracking these indicators. This morning, the final threshold was crossed: projected ventilator demand over the next 48 hours exceeds all available regional supply by [PLACEHOLDER].

**The activation decision:** The state health officer, in consultation with the emergency management director and the Greenfield Healthcare Coalition lead, formally activates Crisis Standards of Care for the Greenfield region. The activation applies to all licensed acute care facilities in the region regardless of ownership status. The CSC plan already developed through the coalition provides the allocation framework, a modified SOFA-based protocol reviewed for disability equity compliance following the 2020 OCR guidance, with the creatinine component removed. Triage team rosters at each facility have been pre-identified and trained. The palliative care infrastructure, including a palliative care unit at Greenfield Medical Center and a community-based hospice partnership, has been activated.

**The equity problem:** Within 36 hours of activation, the coalition's demographic monitoring protocol — a requirement built into the regional framework following advocacy by disability rights and racial equity groups during planning — flags a disparity. Black patients are being categorized as ineligible for ventilator allocation at a rate [PLACEHOLDER] percentage points higher than white patients, controlling for SOFA score. The coalition quality improvement lead reviews the case data and identifies that one facility's triage team has been using the full SOFA score including creatinine, contrary to the protocol. This is corrected immediately, the affected cases are reviewed, two allocation decisions are revised, and a rapid communication goes out to all triage teams reinforcing the creatinine exclusion.

**The lesson:** The equity monitoring provision built into the regional framework detected a protocol deviation within 36 hours. Without it, the disparity might not have been identified until post-event review, after multiple patients had received inequitable allocation decisions. This vignette illustrates why equity monitoring is not a supplemental feature of CSC frameworks but an operational accountability requirement.

**The deescalation:** Fourteen days after activation, regional ventilator availability has recovered to [PLACEHOLDER]% through a combination of stockpile resupply and pandemic case decline. ICU occupancy has dropped below the activation threshold. The state health officer, in coordination with the coalition, formally deactivates crisis-level CSC and moves the region to contingency status. The notification goes out to all facilities within the hour. Over the following two weeks, facilities systematically restore normal operations. A formal AAR process is initiated at day 20, with a preliminary report to the state health department within 60 days.

## Key Terms

**Crisis Standards of Care (CSC):** A substantial change in usual healthcare operations implemented when resource shortfalls exist after surge measures have been exhausted, permitting population-based allocation criteria to govern scarce resource distribution.

**Conventional Care:** Normal healthcare operations in which resources are adequate and all patients receive individualized, optimized care.

**Contingency Care:** Adaptive healthcare operations in which resources are stressed but not exhausted; measures that slightly reduce care quality may be implemented with expectation of return to conventional care.

**Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) Score:** A clinical scoring instrument measuring organ dysfunction across six systems, used in many ventilator allocation frameworks as a proxy for short-term survival likelihood; documented racial equity concerns have prompted modifications to its use.

**Triage Team:** In CSC, a defined team of clinicians separate from bedside treating providers who apply allocation criteria and make formal resource allocation decisions.

**Triggering Conditions:** Quantitative, pre-defined indicators that prompt transition between CSC care tiers; should be specified in state CSC plans before events occur.

**Deescalation:** The formal process of transitioning from crisis or contingency care back toward conventional care as resource constraints resolve.

**Healthcare Coalition:** Regional coordination body funded under the ASPR Hospital Preparedness Program that aggregates situational awareness data, facilitates resource sharing, and supports CSC implementation across member facilities.

**PREP Act:** Public Readiness and Emergency Preparedness Act, federal legislation providing liability immunity for covered medical countermeasure activities during declared emergencies.

**Moral Injury:** Psychological harm resulting from participating in or witnessing actions that violate one's moral or ethical beliefs; documented in healthcare workers following extended crisis operations.

## Practitioner Takeaways

Emergency managers and public health professionals should take the following practical lessons from this chapter.

Crisis Standards of Care is a planning framework, not a crisis response invented under pressure. The entire value of CSC depends on the framework being developed, tested, and legally grounded before the event that requires it. Emergency managers who wait until a disaster to begin CSC development have failed their communities.

Equity is an operational requirement, not a secondary concern. Demographic monitoring, disability equity review of allocation criteria, and consistent regional activation across all facilities regardless of payer mix are not optional enhancements. They are the difference between a framework that is defensible and one that will fail legal review, harm communities, and undermine the institutional trust that emergency management depends on.

State and regional consistency matters more than individual facility excellence. A hospital with a gold-standard CSC plan operating in a region without a coordinated regional framework will still face inequitable outcomes because resource availability, mutual aid, and activation decisions are regional, not facility-specific.

Deescalation is not automatic. Build formal deescalation triggers and processes into every CSC framework. Assign accountability for monitoring those triggers and initiating the deescalation process when they are met.

Clinician well-being is a readiness requirement, not a wellness program add-on. Healthcare systems that do not invest in psychological and moral injury support for staff during and after CSC activations will face workforce attrition, burnout, and reduced capacity for future events.

## Chapter Notes

This chapter draws on the NAM/IOM CSC framework series as its primary organizing scaffold. Emergency managers should obtain current state-specific CSC plans from their state health department and healthcare coalition, as state frameworks vary considerably in scope, legal authority, and equity provisions. The NAM reports are free to download from the National Academies Press website and are essential primary reading.

The equity failures documented in Section 9.7 reflect publicly documented events and policy history, including filed federal civil rights complaints and the 2020 OCR bulletin. Emergency managers should approach CSC equity review as a recurring, not one-time, planning obligation; frameworks developed before 2020 are particularly likely to require review.

The moral injury literature cited in Section 9.9 is growing rapidly, and emergency managers developing CSC plans should expect this evidence base to continue expanding. Planning for clinician psychological support is an area where CSC plans have historically lagged; building it in as an explicit requirement during framework development is strongly recommended.
