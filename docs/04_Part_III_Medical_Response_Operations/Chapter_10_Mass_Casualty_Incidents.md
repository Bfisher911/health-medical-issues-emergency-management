# Chapter 10: Mass Casualty Incidents — Principles and Operations

## Chapter Overview

A mass casualty incident (MCI) is any event in which the number of patients requiring medical care exceeds the immediate capacity of available resources. That sentence is deceptively simple. In practice, an MCI may involve two patients when a single rural ambulance is the only unit available, or it may involve thousands when a major stadium collapses in a dense urban core. What defines an MCI is not the body count but the ratio of demand to capacity, and that ratio shifts constantly as additional resources arrive, casualties deteriorate, and the operational picture evolves.

This chapter builds the conceptual and operational foundation for medical response to MCIs. It covers the definition and classification of events across a scale of severity; the field triage systems that sort patients when treatment cannot begin for everyone simultaneously; the Medical Branch structure within the Incident Command System (ICS) as applied at the field level; patient tracking and documentation; transport coordination and hospital notification; hospital-side surge operations; the particular challenges posed by pediatric, geriatric, and access and functional needs (AFN) populations; and the operational interface between medical response and mass casualty fatality management.

Emergency managers who do not work directly in clinical medicine nonetheless must understand MCI operations. They activate emergency operations centers (EOCs), coordinate mutual aid, liaise with hospitals, and make resource allocation decisions that directly shape whether the medical response succeeds. Clinicians who understand only their own patient in an MCI will consistently make decisions that optimize individual outcomes at the cost of collective outcomes. This chapter is written for both.

## Learning Objectives

Upon completing this chapter, the reader will be able to:

1. Define mass casualty incident and distinguish it from daily surge and from disaster using an operational, capacity-based framework.
2. Classify MCIs by mechanism, impact pattern, and scale using standard taxonomy.
3. Apply and distinguish the START, SALT, and JumpSTART triage algorithms, including their intended populations and documented performance limitations.
4. Describe the Medical Branch structure under field ICS, including the functional roles of Triage, Treatment, and Transport Units.
5. Explain patient tracking requirements from field to hospital and the operational challenges of maintaining tracking continuity.
6. Outline hospital MCI activation procedures including internal surge protocols, diversion considerations, and coordination with the healthcare coalition.
7. Identify the specific vulnerabilities of pediatric, geriatric, and AFN populations during MCI response and name at least three operational adjustments that improve outcomes for these groups.
8. Describe the role of the medical examiner or coroner and the interface between emergency medical services, law enforcement, and fatality management operations.

## 10.1 Defining and Classifying Mass Casualty Incidents

### The Capacity-Based Definition

The most operationally useful definition of an MCI focuses on the relationship between patient load and available resources rather than on absolute patient numbers. The National Association of EMS Physicians (NAEMSP) and the American College of Emergency Physicians (ACEP) have both supported definitions centered on this ratio [CITATION]. A two-patient drowning at a remote lake where the nearest ALS unit is forty minutes away may constitute an MCI for the responding crew. A thirty-patient bus crash near a Level I trauma center with multiple ambulances staged may not trigger MCI protocols if resources can absorb the load within normal workflows.

This framing matters because it prevents two common failures. The first failure is under-response: treating a genuine capacity-exceeding event as a routine call because the number of patients seems manageable by urban standards. The second failure is over-response: activating full MCI protocols for events that create surge but not true overload, consuming mutual aid resources that then become unavailable for the next incident.

Three decision points define an MCI operationally:

**Triage activation.** Standard individual patient assessment is replaced by population-based sorting. Resources go first to those most likely to survive with available treatment, not to the most critical.

**Protocol deviation.** Normal field treatment standards are modified. Some interventions that would be provided under everyday circumstances are deferred because performing them would consume time and resources needed to treat more patients.

**Mutual aid activation.** On-scene resources are insufficient and additional units, personnel, or specialized assets must be requested through the ICS or EOC structure.

When all three apply, the event is unambiguously an MCI. Partial activation of these criteria defines the gray zone where experienced incident commanders must make judgment calls.

### Scale and Classification

MCIs are classified across several dimensions that affect how response is structured.

**Scale** is commonly categorized in three tiers:

- *Simple MCI*: [PLACEHOLDER: typical patient range, usually cited as 10-25 in standard references] patients; managed by local resources with limited mutual aid; single-site, single-agency coordination.
- *Mass casualty event*: [PLACEHOLDER: typically 25-100+ patients]; requires regional mutual aid; multi-agency coordination; EOC may be activated.
- *Catastrophic MCI or disaster*: [PLACEHOLDER: patient numbers or resource depletion that exceeds regional capacity]; requires state and federal resources; sustained multi-day operations; may trigger CSC activation per Chapter 9.

**Mechanism** shapes injury patterns, scene safety requirements, and resource needs:

- *Trauma MCIs*: explosions, transportation crashes, structural collapses, active shooter/hostile events, and natural disaster structural damage. Produce penetrating and blunt force injuries; require surgical capability early in the care chain.
- *Medical MCIs*: infectious disease outbreaks, toxic exposures, cardiac events at mass gatherings. Patient presentation may be uniform (all presenting with similar symptoms) or mixed.
- *Combination events*: increasingly common in complex terrorist attacks (explosion followed by shooter; chemical release at a crowded venue); require rapid hazmat assessment alongside trauma triage.

**Impact pattern** distinguishes:

- *Concentrated impact*: all casualties generated in a defined geographic area over a short time window. Examples: explosion, building collapse, transportation crash. Scene is bounded; patient flow is predictable.
- *Dispersed impact*: casualties generated across a wide geographic area or over an extended time. Examples: earthquake affecting an entire metro area, multi-site attack, pandemic surge. Scene perimeter cannot be established; patients self-transport or present at multiple facilities; standard field triage may apply only partially.

Emergency managers overseeing dispersed-impact MCIs face fundamentally different coordination challenges than those managing concentrated events. Dispersed events require coordination across multiple incident sites, multiple hospitals, and multiple jurisdictions simultaneously, often without a unified command structure initially in place.

## 10.2 Field Triage Systems

Triage is the cornerstone of MCI medical operations. The word derives from the French *trier*, to sort, and the practice dates to Napoleonic military medicine [CITATION]. The modern goal is unchanged: allocate limited resources to maximize the number of lives saved across the entire patient population.

### Triage Categories

All major contemporary triage systems use a four-category structure, typically color-coded:

| Color | Category | Clinical Meaning |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| Black | Expectant/Deceased | Dead or injuries incompatible with survival given available resources; palliative care only |
| Red | Immediate | Life-threatening injuries that are treatable with available resources; intervention within minutes |
| Yellow | Delayed | Serious injuries that can wait for treatment without immediate threat to life |
| Green | Minor | Ambulatory patients with minor injuries ("walking wounded") |

Some systems add an orange or gray category for hazmat-contaminated patients requiring decontamination before treatment, and some use a blue category for expectant patients who are alive but not expected to survive even with treatment.

The black "expectant" category is the category that challenges responders most intensely. Assigning a living patient to expectant means making a resource-allocation decision that this patient's survival probability, relative to resource cost, does not justify immediate intervention when others with better prognoses are waiting. This decision carries significant psychological weight and is a driver of moral injury in MCI responders (see Chapter 24). Pre-event training on expectant tagging, with explicit discussion of the ethical rationale, reduces decision hesitation under operational conditions [CITATION].

### START Triage

**Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment (START)** was developed in the 1980s by Hoag Hospital and the Newport Beach Fire Department [CITATION]. It remains the most widely used field triage system in the United States for adult patients.

START uses four rapid assessments performed in sequence, each taking approximately thirty seconds per patient:

1. **Ambulation**: Instruct all patients who can walk to move to a designated area. These patients are tagged Green. Assessment then proceeds to non-ambulatory patients.
2. **Respirations**: Assess for breathing. If absent after repositioning the airway, tag Black. If respirations exceed 30 per minute, tag Red. If 30 or fewer per minute, proceed.
3. **Perfusion**: Assess radial pulse or capillary refill. If radial pulse is absent or capillary refill exceeds two seconds, tag Red. If present/less than two seconds, proceed.
4. **Mental status (M)**: Assess ability to follow simple commands. If unable to follow commands, tag Red. If able, tag Yellow.

The mnemonic **RPM** (Respiration, Perfusion, Mental status) or the expanded **30-2-Can Do** (30 breaths per minute threshold; 2-second capillary refill; Can Do = follows commands) is used in training.

START's strengths are its simplicity, speed, and minimal equipment requirement. Its weaknesses are documented and relevant to operational planning:

- START was designed for adult patients and performs poorly in children under age eight (see JumpSTART below).
- START overtriages penetrating trauma patients and undertriages patients with compromised airways from causes other than position [CITATION].
- Respiratory rate is an unreliable assessment in the field under stress, darkness, and noise.
- START provides no mechanism for reassessment; patients who change status are not automatically re-sorted.
- START does not account for resource availability; it categorizes patients without reference to what treatments are actually possible at the scene.

### SALT Triage

**Sort, Assess, Lifesaving Interventions, Treatment/Transport (SALT)** was developed through a consensus process convened by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) and published in [PLACEHOLDER: year, approximately 2008] with the goal of creating a nationally standardized triage approach that improved on START [CITATION].

SALT introduces two elements START lacks:

**Integrated lifesaving interventions at triage.** Rather than deferring all treatment until after sorting, SALT permits brief lifesaving interventions during the assessment phase, including hemorrhage control with tourniquet, positioning to open the airway, and administration of auto-injector antidotes if available. This reflects evidence that brief, targeted interventions during triage can convert several Red patients to Yellow.

**Behavioral sort as the first step.** Before individual patient assessment, SALT asks responders to globally sort the scene by directing all patients who can walk to move, then asking remaining patients to wave or otherwise signal. This behavioral observation quickly identifies likely Green patients and likely Red/Black patients without requiring physical assessment of every individual.

The SALT algorithm then proceeds to individual assessment using a more detailed decision tree than START, incorporating a broader range of clinical findings and the lifesaving intervention step before final categorization.

SALT is generally considered more clinically sophisticated than START, but that sophistication comes at a cost: SALT takes longer per patient and requires more training to apply reliably. Studies comparing START and SALT accuracy are limited by methodological challenges, and no triage system has demonstrated clear superiority across all MCI types [CITATION]. The most important factor is likely not which system is used but whether all responders at an incident are using the same system with consistent terminology.

### JumpSTART

**JumpSTART** was developed in 1995 specifically to address START's failure mode in pediatric patients [CITATION]. Children have different physiologic baselines: higher resting respiratory rates, different capillary refill norms, and limited ability to follow verbal commands that reflect compliance rather than neurologic status.

JumpSTART modifies START's thresholds:

- Respiratory rate Red threshold: greater than 45 or fewer than 15 breaths per minute (versus START's single threshold of greater than 30)
- Apneic children receive five rescue breaths before Black tagging; children who resume breathing after rescue breaths are tagged Red rather than Black
- Mental status assessed using the **AVPU** scale (Alert, Voice-responsive, Pain-responsive, Unresponsive) rather than command-following

JumpSTART is appropriate for children under approximately [PLACEHOLDER: age 8 in most references] or below a defined weight threshold. In practice, distinguishing pediatric from adult patients in a chaotic field environment can itself be challenging.

The operational recommendation is that all triage officers receive JumpSTART training and that pediatric-specific triage tags or color differentiation be included in MCI supply caches. See Section 10.6 for additional pediatric MCI considerations.

### Secondary Triage

Primary triage, described above, occurs in the field at or near the point of injury. Secondary triage occurs at the Treatment Area or at receiving hospitals. Secondary triage reassesses each patient with more time available, corrects primary triage errors, and updates categorization based on patient progression.

The gap between primary and secondary triage is a recognized source of mortality in MCIs. Patients tagged Yellow in the field who deteriorate during transport or while awaiting treatment at the Treatment Area may arrive at Red or Black status without that change being captured in the tracking system. Triage officers must be assigned specifically to monitor patients awaiting treatment and re-tag as status changes.

## 10.3 The Medical Branch in Field ICS

The Incident Command System (ICS) was introduced in Chapter 7. In a field MCI, the medical response operates within the ICS Operations Section as the **Medical Branch** or, in smaller incidents, as the **Medical Group**. Understanding this structure is essential for both field responders and emergency managers coordinating from the EOC.

### Medical Branch Structure

The Medical Branch Director reports to the Operations Section Chief and coordinates all field medical operations. Under the Medical Branch, three functional units handle the primary work of MCI medical response:

**Triage Unit.** Responsible for all field triage activities. The Triage Unit Leader coordinates the triage officers, ensures consistent application of the selected triage protocol, oversees tag distribution and completion, and communicates patient counts and category breakdowns to the Medical Branch Director.

**Treatment Unit.** Responsible for providing care to sorted patients in the Treatment Area. The Treatment Area is physically divided into sections corresponding to triage categories: Immediate Area (Red), Delayed Area (Yellow), and Minor Area (Green). Separate treatment zones prevent minor patients from occupying space and personnel needed for critical care. A Morgue Area (Black) is established away from the active treatment zones. Treatment Unit medical personnel provide care within the constraints of available supplies and personnel, operating under modified protocols appropriate to MCI conditions.

**Transport Unit.** Responsible for coordinating patient transport to receiving facilities. Transport Unit functions include: maintaining the ambulance staging area, coordinating transport assignments with the Triage and Treatment Units, communicating with receiving hospitals regarding patient numbers and acuity, tracking which patients have been transported to which facilities, and managing vehicle flow in and out of the scene perimeter.

### Additional Medical Branch Positions

In larger events, the Medical Branch adds:

**Medical Supply Unit.** Manages medical supply cache distribution, tracks consumption, identifies supply gaps, and coordinates resupply requests through logistics.

**Rescue Unit.** Coordinates extrication and movement of patients from the point of injury to the Triage Area. In structural collapse events, the Rescue Unit interfaces closely with Urban Search and Rescue (USAR) teams and technical rescue.

**Air Operations Medical Coordinator.** In events involving helicopter evacuation, coordinates patient selection for air transport, communicates with receiving air medical centers, and manages the helicopter landing zone.

### Span of Control and Expansion

ICS requires each supervisor to maintain a manageable span of control, generally three to seven subordinates. In an MCI with large patient numbers, the Treatment Unit may expand to include treatment team leaders for each category zone, each supervising a group of care providers. The structure scales up as resources arrive without requiring reorganization; arriving units are assigned to positions within the existing structure.

The Medical Branch Director maintains a running patient count by category and communicates regular updates to the Operations Section Chief. The EOC receives this information and uses it to manage mutual aid requests, hospital capacity coordination, and resource allocation decisions. The link between the field Medical Branch and the EOC is one of the most operationally important communication pathways in an MCI, and it is routinely identified as a point of failure in post-event after-action reports [CITATION].

## 10.4 Patient Tracking and Documentation

Patient tracking is the process of maintaining a record of each patient's identity (when known), location, triage status, interventions received, and disposition from point of injury through definitive care. It is both an immediate operational necessity and a legal and public health obligation.

### Why Tracking Fails

Tracking failures are among the most consistent findings in MCI after-action reviews [CITATION]. The reasons are predictable:

- Tags are removed or separated from patients during treatment or transport.
- Multiple patients are transported in a single vehicle with a single tag.
- Patients self-evacuate before being tagged.
- Paper-based tracking systems are illegible, lost, or not forwarded with the patient.
- Hospital registration systems are overwhelmed during surge and do not integrate with field tracking records.
- Families who transport patients directly to hospitals bypass the field tracking system entirely.

The operational consequence of tracking failure is not merely administrative. Hospitals cannot communicate accurate census information to the EOC without knowing how many patients have arrived and from which site. Families cannot be reunited with unresponsive patients. The medical examiner cannot reconstruct the chain of custody for deceased patients. Investigations of response performance cannot be reconstructed.

### Patient Tracking Systems

Field patient tracking begins with the triage tag itself. Most MCI triage tags include:

- A sequential patient number (unique identifier)
- Triage category designation (color-coded)
- Fields for basic information (name, age, mechanism of injury if known)
- Tear-off strips with the matching unique number that can be retained by the transporting unit and sent with the patient to the hospital
- A brief assessment checklist

Digital patient tracking systems are increasingly used in larger jurisdictions. These systems typically use barcode or RFID-tagged triage cards that are scanned at intake, at each treatment decision, at transport, and at hospital arrival. The data populates a shared situational awareness platform visible to the field Medical Branch, the EOC, and receiving hospitals simultaneously [CITATION].

The limitation of digital systems is infrastructure dependence. Cellular network congestion, power outages, and device damage during the very events that require tracking are well-documented problems. Robust systems include offline capability and synchronize when connectivity is restored.

### Hospital Receiving Tracking

Receiving hospitals bear responsibility for tracking patients from the point they arrive in the emergency department through admission, discharge, or transfer. Hospital systems must communicate patient arrival numbers and status to the healthcare coalition, which aggregates this information for the EOC.

The interface between field tracking and hospital tracking is a persistent gap. When a patient is transported by a private vehicle, when multiple patients arrive without field tags, or when a large hospital receives patients from multiple scene sites simultaneously, tracking continuity breaks. Standardized patient ID bands applied at triage and staying with the patient through the entire care chain reduce but do not eliminate this gap.

## 10.5 Transport Decisions and Hospital Notification

### Transport Decision Principles

The Transport Unit must make continuous decisions about where to send patients. These decisions are made under time pressure, with imperfect information about hospital capacity, and must balance several competing priorities:

**Geographic proximity.** Shorter transport time reduces out-of-hospital time for critical patients, but nearest is not always best.

**Hospital capability.** Not all hospitals can manage all injury types. Penetrating trauma, burns, pediatric emergencies, and spinal cord injuries all require specialized resources. The Transport Unit must know the capability levels of receiving facilities within transport range.

**Hospital capacity.** Available emergency department beds, operating rooms, blood products, ventilators, and specialty physicians all affect which hospital can effectively receive additional patients. Sending Red patients to a hospital that cannot perform surgery within the needed timeframe is worse than a slightly longer transport to a surgical center.

**Distribution.** Concentrating all patients at the nearest hospital overwhelms it while leaving other hospitals underutilized. Deliberate distribution across multiple facilities is almost always better than concentrating at a single site, even if it means slightly longer transport for some patients.

These decisions should be guided by pre-established transport protocols developed through the healthcare coalition (see Chapter 8) and updated to reflect current hospital status during the event. In the absence of real-time hospital status data, the Transport Unit Coordinator must establish radio or telephone contact with receiving hospitals directly.

### Hospital Notification

Receiving hospitals should receive advance notification before MCI patients arrive. Notification allows hospitals to activate internal emergency plans, stage additional personnel, clear emergency department capacity, prepare operating rooms, and ensure blood bank supplies are accessible.

Standard hospital notification information includes:

- Estimated number of patients by triage category
- Mechanism of injury (guides preparation for injury types expected)
- Estimated time of first patient arrival
- Any special considerations (hazmat, pediatric patients, infectious disease concern)

In a well-functioning regional system, this notification flows from the Transport Unit through the healthcare coalition to all receiving hospitals simultaneously, with updates as the patient count and category distribution become clearer. In practice, communication chains are often broken, and hospitals receive patients before receiving notification.

The Pre-Hospital Trauma Life Support (PHTLS) and Advanced Trauma Life Support (ATLS) programs both emphasize advance notification as a standard of care for individual trauma patients [CITATION]. The same principle applies to MCI transport, scaled to the event.

## 10.6 Hospital MCI Preparation and Surge

### Hospital Emergency Operations Plans

Every hospital accredited by The Joint Commission is required to maintain an Emergency Operations Plan (EOP) that includes provisions for managing a patient surge [CITATION]. The hospital MCI plan typically includes:

- Criteria for plan activation at multiple levels (usually Level 1/2/3 or similar tiered activation)
- Roles and responsibilities for hospital incident command personnel (HICS; see below)
- Procedures for rapid expansion of emergency department capacity
- Procedures for calling in off-duty staff
- Procedures for canceling elective procedures to free operating room and bed capacity
- Blood bank surge protocols
- Communication procedures with the EOC, coalition, and media

Hospital Incident Command System (HICS) is the hospital adaptation of ICS. HICS was developed to ensure that hospitals could interface with field ICS structures using common terminology and organizational logic [CITATION]. Under HICS, the hospital activates an Incident Command structure mirroring ICS, with an incident commander, operations and logistics sections, and branch and unit positions populated according to the nature and scale of the event.

### Surge Capacity and Surge Capability

Two related but distinct concepts appear in hospital preparedness:

**Surge capacity** refers to the ability to increase the number of patients a hospital can manage. This is primarily a beds, space, and personnel question. Hospitals can expand capacity by activating overflow areas (hallways, conference rooms, ambulatory care units converted to inpatient), discharging stable patients, deferring elective admissions, and calling in staff.

**Surge capability** refers to the ability to provide specific types of care to patients who need it. A hospital may have abundant bed space but lack surgical capability for penetrating trauma, burn care capacity, or pediatric critical care. Capability gaps cannot be bridged by simply adding space.

Emergency managers must understand both dimensions when coordinating hospital response. Capacity data (beds available) is relatively easy to track through hospital capacity reporting systems. Capability data (what specialized services are available at this moment) is more difficult to capture and less consistently reported.

### Reverse Triage and Patient Discharge

A critical but underappreciated element of hospital MCI preparation is **reverse triage**: the systematic identification of currently admitted patients who can be safely discharged or transferred to create inpatient capacity for MCI patients. Hospitals have conducted rapid discharge exercises demonstrating the ability to discharge [PLACEHOLDER: percentage, typically cited as 20-30% of inpatients] within hours when protocols are in place [CITATION].

Reverse triage requires pre-event work: establishing criteria for expedited discharge, identifying patients likely to meet those criteria under surge conditions, and confirming that receiving locations (skilled nursing facilities, home health agencies, family homes) can accommodate discharged patients. Without pre-event planning, reverse triage under active surge conditions is chaotic and inconsistently implemented.

## 10.7 Special MCI Populations

### Pediatric Patients

Children are not small adults. Their anatomical, physiologic, and developmental differences create distinct vulnerabilities in MCI settings:

**Physiologic differences.** Children have higher respiratory and heart rates, smaller blood volumes, and greater proportional body surface area than adults. They compensate effectively for blood loss until decompensation is sudden and severe, meaning a child who appears relatively stable may deteriorate rapidly without warning. Vital sign thresholds and drug dosing are weight-dependent, not age-dependent, creating calculation requirements that are error-prone in high-stress field environments.

**Anatomical differences.** Children have larger heads relative to body size, making them more susceptible to traumatic brain injury in blast events. Their less developed chest wall musculature transmits force more readily to underlying organs.

**Equipment differences.** Standard adult MCI supply caches typically understock pediatric IV supplies, airway equipment, and triage tags [CITATION]. Pediatric-appropriate equipment must be specifically included in MCI cache planning, not assumed to be present.

**Developmental differences.** Infants and young children cannot communicate their symptoms effectively. Unaccompanied children cannot provide medical history. Children separated from caregivers during an MCI experience acute psychological distress that interferes with assessment and compliance.

Operational recommendations for pediatric MCI response include:

- Use JumpSTART for patients visually assessed as under age [PLACEHOLDER: 8 or the weight threshold used in the jurisdiction].
- Assign at least one provider trained in pediatric emergency care to the Immediate Treatment Area in any MCI expected to involve children.
- Establish a dedicated pediatric staging area if the incident involves a school, childcare facility, or event where pediatric patients predominate.
- Use length-based weight estimation tools (e.g., Broselow tape) pre-stocked in pediatric supply kits to reduce dosing errors.
- Follow pediatric patient reunification protocols (see reunification considerations in Chapter 27).

### Geriatric Patients

Older adults are disproportionately represented in MCI patient populations for several reasons: they are more likely to be in transportation-dependent situations, they have higher rates of pre-existing conditions that increase injury severity, and they use more medications that affect physiologic response to trauma and stress.

Key operational considerations for geriatric MCI patients:

**Atypical presentation.** Elderly patients may not exhibit the expected physiologic responses to shock (tachycardia, hypotension) because pre-existing cardiovascular disease or beta-blocker use masks these signs. Standard triage vital sign thresholds may underestimate severity in older adults.

**Polypharmacy.** Anticoagulant use (warfarin, direct oral anticoagulants, aspirin) significantly worsens hemorrhage outcomes and must be considered when triaging and treating elderly trauma patients. Blood thinner reversal agents should be available at receiving trauma centers and their use expedited.

**Mobility limitations.** Geriatric patients may not comply with instructions to move to a Green patient staging area even if capable of doing so. "Can you walk?" is a less reliable triage sort in a population with significant mobility impairment at baseline.

**Cognitive impairment.** Dementia affects the mental status component of all triage algorithms in ways that may result in Red tagging for patients who are neurologically at their baseline. Pre-event medical information (ICE cards, medical alert bracelets, caregiver accompaniment) becomes important for accurate triage.

### Access and Functional Needs Populations

Access and functional needs (AFN) populations encompass individuals who require additional or different assistance before, during, and after an emergency. In MCI contexts, AFN considerations include:

**Communication needs.** Deaf or hard-of-hearing patients, patients with speech and language disabilities, and patients with limited English proficiency cannot effectively communicate with first responders using standard verbal protocols. Visual triage cues and interpreter resources (including sign language interpreters) must be planned for.

**Medical equipment dependence.** Patients who are ventilator-dependent, oxygen-dependent, or dependent on infusion pumps or dialysis face acute risk when removed from their home or facility environment. Field triage must account for the resource implications of transporting equipment-dependent patients.

**Mobility device users.** Patients using wheelchairs, walkers, or prosthetics may be tagged Green by ambulation-based triage despite having significant medical complexity. "Ambulatory" in triage context should be interpreted as "capable of self-relocation," not as "not disabled."

**Cognitive and behavioral health.** Patients with intellectual disabilities, autism spectrum disorder, or significant mental illness may not respond predictably to standard field instructions, increasing misclassification risk and responder stress.

Pre-event planning for AFN populations in MCI contexts includes registries of technology-dependent individuals, specialized triage training for responders, and agreements with AFN service organizations to support field operations. These planning elements reduce but do not eliminate the disparities in MCI outcomes experienced by AFN populations [CITATION].

## 10.8 Mass Casualty Fatality Management Interface

Mass fatality management is addressed in detail in Chapter 16. This section describes the operational interface between EMS and emergency medical operations and fatality management functions during an MCI.

### Jurisdiction and Scene Authority

In a mass fatality event, jurisdiction over deceased patients transfers from EMS medical authority to the medical examiner (ME) or coroner with legal authority over the jurisdiction. This transfer has operational implications:

- Deceased patients tagged Black cannot be moved without ME authorization, even if their location impedes treatment of surviving patients.
- Positioning of the temporary morgue area requires coordination with the ME/coroner to ensure chain of custody documentation is maintained.
- Responders who identify deceased patients must document location and physical position before any movement is authorized.

In practice, scene chaos creates pressure to move bodies to clear space for living patients. Responders must be trained that premature movement of deceased patients compromises ME investigation capability and may create legal liability.

### Expectant Patients Who Die in the Field

Patients tagged Black-expectant who die in the Treatment Area while awaiting or during palliative care occupy an intermediate status. They were assessed as living patients at triage and entered the tracking system. When they die, they must be transferred from EMS documentation to ME jurisdiction. This transition requires:

- Documentation of time of death (or time when death was confirmed)
- Transfer of triage tag and any treatment records to ME custody
- Communication to the patient tracking system that this patient has transitioned from expectant to deceased

These procedures are often underdeveloped in MCI plans because they require coordination between EMS and ME that is not part of routine daily operations.

### Fatality Tracking and Family Notification

The process of notifying families of fatalities in an MCI requires careful coordination between the ME/coroner, law enforcement, and public information functions. Individual family notification is not a field EMS function. Field responders should be trained to:

- Not provide information about specific deceased patients to families at the scene
- Redirect family members to the Family Assistance Center (FAC) when established
- Document all information that may assist ME identification (physical description, clothing, personal effects in proximity)

The Family Assistance Center model, described in more detail in Chapter 27, consolidates family notification, victim identification, and support services in a single location that allows ME operations to continue without disruption at the scene or morgue.

## Applied Vignette: Millbrook County Freeway Collapse

At 7:42 a.m. on a Tuesday in October, a section of elevated freeway in Millbrook County collapsed during the morning commute. Initial 911 calls described "dozens of vehicles down," smoke from vehicle fires, and people trapped in debris. County EMS dispatched four advanced life support units and requested mutual aid within the first three minutes.

The first EMS unit on scene, Unit 14, had a two-person crew and found approximately sixty to seventy patients visible, with additional patients likely trapped. Unit 14's paramedic, the senior clinician on scene, transmitted a Level 3 MCI declaration and assumed the role of Medical Branch Director pending arrival of a supervisor.

He faced an immediate problem. His crew was two people. He had patients who needed airway management, hemorrhage control, and immediate extraction from vehicles. He had other patients waving and calling for help. He could not do everything at once.

He made the decision that defines MCI medicine: he stepped back from the first patient he approached, a man with an obvious femur fracture and a radial pulse, and began a global scene assessment. He directed his partner to begin START triage along the nearest cluster of patients while he radioed a patient count estimate and requested EMS supervisor, fire, and law enforcement response. He resisted the impulse to begin individual patient treatment.

By the time the second unit arrived fourteen minutes later, he had a rough count by color (estimated twenty Red, thirty Yellow, fifteen Green, estimated five to ten trapped/unknown), a designated Treatment Area in the cleared right lane sixty meters south of the collapse, and a staging area for incoming units. Two Green patients were already helping with initial hemorrhage control under verbal instruction from his partner.

The Medical Branch expanded as units arrived. A triage officer was designated to reassess the Treatment Area while primary triage continued at the collapse site. The Transport Unit Coordinator established radio contact with three regional hospitals and began distributing Red patients, limiting initial transport to three patients per hospital until capacity reports were available.

One operational gap emerged: a school bus carrying fourteen middle school students had been on the overpass and was partially trapped in debris. The initial patient count had not identified it as a pediatric event. The first pediatric patients presented to the Treatment Area tagged using adult START criteria. A pediatric nurse on one of the mutual aid units identified the misclassification of two patients whose respiratory rates had been rounded to adult thresholds. Both were re-tagged Red and transported to the pediatric trauma center.

The after-action review identified the pediatric supply deficit as the primary medical operations failure: only one of the responding units carried JumpSTART tags or weight-based dosing references. A recommendation was issued to include pediatric supplies in all county MCI cache units.

## Key Terms

**Mass Casualty Incident (MCI):** Any event in which the number of patients requiring medical care exceeds the immediate capacity of available resources.

**START Triage:** Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment; adult field triage algorithm assessing respiration, perfusion, and mental status.

**SALT Triage:** Sort, Assess, Lifesaving Interventions, Treatment/Transport; national consensus triage algorithm incorporating brief lifesaving interventions and behavioral global sort.

**JumpSTART:** Pediatric adaptation of START using age-appropriate vital sign thresholds and AVPU mental status scale.

**Triage Category Black (Expectant/Deceased):** Patients who are deceased or whose injuries are incompatible with survival given available resources.

**Medical Branch:** ICS organizational unit within Operations responsible for field medical operations; includes Triage, Treatment, and Transport Units.

**Triage Unit:** Medical Branch component responsible for initial patient sorting.

**Treatment Unit:** Medical Branch component responsible for providing care in the Treatment Area.

**Transport Unit:** Medical Branch component responsible for coordinating patient transport to receiving facilities.

**HICS:** Hospital Incident Command System; ICS adaptation for hospital emergency management.

**Surge Capacity:** Ability to increase the number of patients a hospital can manage.

**Surge Capability:** Ability to provide specific types of care needed by MCI patients.

**Reverse Triage:** Systematic identification and discharge of current inpatients to create capacity for MCI arrivals.

**Secondary Triage:** Reassessment of patients in the Treatment Area or at the hospital after initial field triage.

**JumpSTART:** Pediatric field triage algorithm; developmental and physiologic adaptation of START for patients under approximately age eight.

**Access and Functional Needs (AFN):** Individuals who require additional or different assistance before, during, and after emergencies.

**Family Assistance Center (FAC):** Consolidated location for family notification, victim identification, and support services following a mass casualty event.

## Scenario Exercise: Westfield Amphitheater

**Background:** Westfield Amphitheater is an outdoor concert venue in a mid-size city with a capacity of 12,000 attendees. During a sold-out evening event, a lightning strike ignites a storage structure adjacent to the main stage. The fire spreads to the stage structure, causing a partial collapse and releasing smoke across the venue. Approximately 8,000 people were in attendance when the collapse began at 9:47 p.m.

**Current situation, 10:12 p.m.:**

- City EMS has dispatched eight units; four are on scene, four en route.
- Approximate patient count: 35 Red, 80 Yellow, 200+ Green (many self-evacuated to the parking lot), estimated 6-8 trapped under stage debris.
- Two patients have been tagged Black (confirmed deceased at point of injury).
- Venue staff have no medical training and no MCI response plan.
- The nearest Level I trauma center is 4 miles away; two community hospitals are within 8 miles.
- A pediatric hospital is 12 miles away.
- Night conditions, smoke, and crowd noise are creating communication and assessment difficulties.
- Several patients in the Green area are reporting chest tightness and shortness of breath consistent with smoke inhalation; it is unclear whether these are Green or Yellow patients.

**Tasks:**

1. Describe how you would establish the Medical Branch structure given currently on-scene resources, and identify the first three priorities.
2. The smoke inhalation patients currently in the Green area may deteriorate rapidly. What triage modification do you recommend, and at what point would you reassign these patients?
3. The Level I trauma center radios that it currently has 12 of 18 operating rooms occupied and 4 ICU beds available. How does this affect your transport decision-making for Red patients?
4. At 10:35 p.m., you receive information that at least three of the people trapped under stage debris are under age 16. What operational changes does this trigger?
5. As incident commander, you are asked to brief the public information officer at 11:00 p.m. What information about patient status and hospital distribution can you provide, and what information should not be released?

## Practitioner Takeaways

**The MCI threshold is local.** Two patients can constitute an MCI in the right conditions. Train all responders to recognize the three operational triggers — triage activation, protocol deviation, and mutual aid request — regardless of patient numbers.

**The hardest part of triage is the Black tag.** Pre-event training on the ethical rationale for expectant tagging reduces field hesitation and supports better overall outcomes. Brief, explicit training conversations matter more than extensive protocols.

**Pick one triage system and use it everywhere.** The evidence does not clearly favor START over SALT or vice versa. What harms outcomes is using different systems at the same incident. Regional agreements on triage systems, terminology, and tag formats are worth more than optimization of any single algorithm.

**Pediatric patients require deliberate planning.** They will not be served by adult equipment, adult triage thresholds, or adult dosing assumptions. Pediatric supplies must be in MCI caches before the event, not requested during it.

**Patient tracking is an emergency management problem, not just a clinical one.** The EOC cannot manage hospital capacity without knowing where patients went. Track from tag to discharge and build the information pathway before the event.

## Chapter Notes

This chapter intentionally focuses on the operations-level framework rather than clinical treatment protocols. Treatment protocols for specific MCI injury patterns (blast injury, crush syndrome, hemorrhagic shock management, smoke inhalation) are addressed in Part IV chapters covering the relevant threat types. Emergency managers who are not clinicians will benefit from the operational structure presented here; clinicians who want clinical depth should use this chapter alongside clinical references and their agency's MCI medical protocols.

The triage systems described here (START, SALT, JumpSTART) are the primary systems in use in the United States as of this writing. International readers should be aware that other systems are in use in different national contexts (CSCATTT in the United Kingdom, MIMMS internationally). The operational principles are compatible across systems even where terminology and specific algorithms differ.

The vignette figures for triage accuracy rates are bracketed throughout this chapter because published controlled studies of triage system performance vary widely in methodology, patient population, and outcome measures. Emergency managers should seek current peer-reviewed literature rather than relying on any single study for performance figures, as this is an area of active research.
