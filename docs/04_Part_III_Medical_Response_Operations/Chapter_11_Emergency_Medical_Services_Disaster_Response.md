# Chapter 11: Emergency Medical Services in Disaster Response

## Chapter Overview

Emergency medical services (EMS) are the mobile component of the emergency medical system, providing pre-hospital care, transport, and triage at the interface between the scene of an emergency and the hospital. In daily operations, EMS functions as a relatively self-contained system: receive call, respond, assess, treat, transport, transfer care, return to service. In disaster, every element of that cycle breaks down simultaneously. Call volume overwhelms dispatch. Response times lengthen. Scene access is impaired by debris, hazards, or road network damage. Patients are too numerous for standard individual assessment. Hospitals divert or go offline. And EMS personnel, like every other responder, are themselves members of affected communities.

This chapter examines EMS in disaster from three perspectives: the operational adaptations EMS systems must make when daily practice is insufficient, the system-level coordination that connects EMS to the broader emergency management infrastructure, and the workforce issues — training, behavioral health, and sustainability — that determine whether EMS can sustain operations across a multi-day or multi-week disaster.

The chapter builds on the MCI framework established in Chapter 10 and situates it within the full disaster response cycle. Chapter 10 described what happens at a single scene over hours. This chapter addresses what happens when multiple scenes are active simultaneously, when the operational period extends beyond a single shift, and when mutual aid and federal EMS resources must be integrated into the local system.

---

## Learning Objectives

Upon completing this chapter, the reader will be able to:

1. Describe the functional components of an EMS system and explain how each component is disrupted in a disaster.
2. Distinguish between the EMS operational adaptations required for concentrated-impact events versus dispersed-impact disasters.
3. Explain the role of EMS in the ESF-8 (Public Health and Medical Services) structure and describe how EMS agencies coordinate with state emergency medical oversight and federal medical assets.
4. Describe the structure and deployment process of EMS mutual aid, including interstate compacts and Emergency Management Assistance Compact (EMAC) EMS provisions.
5. Explain the purpose and operational characteristics of Disaster Medical Assistance Teams (DMATs) and the National Disaster Medical System (NDMS).
6. Identify at least four workforce sustainability challenges specific to EMS in disaster and describe corresponding operational and organizational responses.
7. Describe the role of community paramedicine and mobile integrated healthcare in disaster settings, including their application in evacuation, shelter-in-place medical support, and post-disaster follow-up.

## 11.1 The EMS System Under Normal and Disaster Conditions

### Components of an EMS System

EMS is a system, not a single service. The components that must function together for EMS to operate effectively include:

**Emergency communications and dispatch.** The 911 public safety answering point (PSAP) receives calls, determines the nature of the call, assigns resources, and provides pre-arrival instructions. Dispatch systems use computer-aided dispatch (CAD) technology to track unit availability and location, assign units to calls, and maintain an audit trail of response activity.

**Field response units.** These range from basic life support (BLS) units staffed by emergency medical technicians (EMTs) to advanced life support (ALS) units staffed by paramedics, to specialty units (neonatal transport, critical care transport, tactical EMS). The unit mix in a jurisdiction determines what clinical interventions are available in the field.

**Medical oversight.** EMS operates under physician medical direction. Offline medical direction involves the development of treatment protocols and training standards. Online medical direction involves real-time physician consultation for complex field cases. The EMS medical director bears professional and legal responsibility for the clinical quality of the EMS system.

**Transport infrastructure.** Ground ambulances, air medical (helicopter and fixed-wing), and in some systems water-based transport assets provide the physical link between scene and hospital.

**Receiving facilities.** Emergency departments, trauma centers, stroke centers, cardiac intervention centers, and specialty hospitals receive and continue care for EMS patients. EMS effectiveness is partly determined by the receiving facility system it delivers patients into.

**Quality assurance and improvement.** EMS systems monitor patient outcomes, protocol adherence, and response performance, using this data to improve training, protocols, and system design.

In a disaster, all six components face simultaneous stress. Dispatch is overwhelmed by call volume. Field units are consumed at scenes. Transport infrastructure is damaged or congested. Receiving facilities surge or go offline. Medical oversight is stretched across simultaneous complex events. Quality improvement data collection collapses under operational pressure.

### Disaster Adaptations

EMS systems adapt to disaster conditions through protocol modifications, resource reallocation, and coordination with the broader response structure. Key adaptations include:

**Dispatch prioritization.** CAD systems can be configured to prioritize dispatch assignments during disaster. Life-threatening calls (Priority 1) receive all available resources; lower-priority calls are held or diverted to non-transport options. Dispatch may implement geographic zoning to prevent all units from concentrating at a single site.

**Reduced individual treatment time.** Field treatment time is compressed. Units transport patients more rapidly than under standard protocols, accepting a lower level of scene stabilization to move patients through the system and return to service sooner.

**Non-transport protocols.** In some disasters, EMS systems activate protocols allowing EMTs and paramedics to assess, treat, and release patients without transport when the patient does not meet hospital-level care criteria. This reduces transport burden and frees units for life-threatening calls. Treat-and-release and treat-and-refer protocols require advance development and medical director authorization.

**Alternate destination protocols.** Standard transport destinations may be unusable (damaged hospital, diverting hospital). Alternate destination protocols route patients to urgent care centers, community health centers, or disaster field clinics staffed by community health personnel. Alternate destinations must be identified and agreements established pre-event.

**EMS aid stations.** In large-footprint events, EMS establishes fixed aid stations at strategic locations (shelter sites, points of entry, community gathering points) rather than relying exclusively on mobile response. Aid stations reduce transport volume for lower-acuity patients and serve a surveillance function, identifying emerging health needs in the affected population.

## 11.2 EMS in the ESF-8 Structure

### ESF-8 Overview

Emergency Support Function 8 (ESF-8), Public Health and Medical Services, coordinates the federal public health and medical response to disasters. ESF-8 is led by the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) and draws on resources from the Department of Defense, Department of Veterans Affairs, and other agencies [CITATION].

At the state level, ESF-8 (or the equivalent state function) is typically coordinated by the state health department, with EMS systems integrated as a component of the medical response structure. State EMS offices or lead agencies maintain oversight of licensure standards, mutual aid coordination, and resource tracking.

EMS agencies are not always well-integrated into ESF-8 planning at the local level. The traditional divide between fire-based EMS (more likely to engage with fire and rescue emergency management structures) and health department-based ESF-8 coordination can leave EMS outside the planning table for public health emergencies that are not primarily scene-based. Emergency managers should ensure that EMS agency leadership is engaged in ESF-8 planning regardless of organizational structure.

### EMS Medical Direction During Disaster

The EMS medical director plays a critical role during disaster that is often underspecified in emergency plans. Operational EMS adaptations — modified triage protocols, expanded scope of practice, treat-and-release authorization, alternate destination protocols — require physician authorization. The EMS medical director must be integrated into the EOC medical function or in direct communication with it, with authority clearly defined in advance.

During declared emergencies, state governments may issue emergency health authority orders that modify EMS scope of practice or authorize protocol deviations. These orders may expand paramedic authority (permitting interventions not in standard protocols), modify licensing reciprocity (allowing out-of-state EMS personnel to practice), or address controlled substance handling during disaster conditions. EMS medical directors must be familiar with their state's emergency health authority framework and engaged in ESF-8 planning to ensure that needed protocol modifications are pre-authorized rather than improvised mid-event [CITATION].

## 11.3 EMS Mutual Aid and Interstate Coordination

### The Mutual Aid Framework

No jurisdiction's EMS resources are sufficient for a major disaster. Mutual aid — the sharing of resources between jurisdictions — is the mechanism that bridges this gap. EMS mutual aid operates at several levels:

**Local/intra-county mutual aid.** Adjacent city or county EMS agencies provide resources under pre-established mutual aid agreements. This is the fastest tier; resources can arrive within minutes to hours.

**Regional mutual aid.** Healthcare coalitions, regional EMS coordinating bodies, or regional emergency management organizations coordinate cross-county resource sharing. Resources arrive within hours.

**State mutual aid.** State emergency management activates statewide EMS resource coordination, deploying state EMS reserve assets and coordinating requests from multiple counties. Resources arrive within twelve to twenty-four hours.

**Interstate mutual aid via EMAC.** The Emergency Management Assistance Compact (EMAC), ratified by all fifty states, the District of Columbia, and U.S. territories, provides the legal framework for requesting and deploying EMS and other emergency resources across state lines [CITATION]. EMAC requests pass through state emergency management agencies; local agencies cannot request EMAC resources directly.

### Managing Mutual Aid EMS Units

Receiving mutual aid EMS units presents integration challenges that can reduce rather than increase system effectiveness if not managed well:

**Protocol variation.** Mutual aid EMS units from different jurisdictions operate under different medical protocols. A paramedic from a neighboring state may have different medication lists, different triage algorithm training, and different scope of practice than local units. The receiving jurisdiction's EMS medical director must establish clear guidance on which protocols apply to mutual aid units operating in the jurisdiction during the disaster.

**Communication interoperability.** Radio systems often do not interoperate across jurisdictions. Mutual aid units may not have access to the local radio network and must operate on a designated interoperability channel. Planning for communication patch-in and interoperability workarounds before an event is far more effective than solving it during one.

**Credentialing and documentation.** State licensing reciprocity during declared emergencies differs from state to state. The EMS supervisor receiving mutual aid units must verify that personnel are licensed at the level claimed and that their scope of practice is appropriate for the assignments given.

**Geographic unfamiliarity.** Mutual aid units from distant jurisdictions do not know local road networks, hospital locations, or scene geography. GPS-assisted navigation and local unit pairing reduce the delays caused by this unfamiliarity.

Effective mutual aid requires pre-event agreements, joint training exercises, and communication system coordination. Mutual aid that arrives without these elements can create as many problems as it solves.

## 11.4 Federal EMS Resources: DMAT and the NDMS

### The National Disaster Medical System

The National Disaster Medical System (NDMS) is a federally coordinated system that provides medical response capabilities to support state and local response when a disaster exceeds their capacity [CITATION]. NDMS is managed by HHS ASPR and includes:

- **Disaster Medical Assistance Teams (DMATs)**: The primary field medical response component.
- **Disaster Mortuary Operational Response Teams (DMORTs)**: Mass fatality management (see Chapter 16).
- **National Veterinary Response Teams (NVRTs)**: Animal care in disasters.
- **National Pharmacy Response Teams (NPRTs)**: Pharmacy support.
- **Trauma and Critical Care Teams**: Specialized surgical and critical care teams for high-acuity events.

NDMS also includes the Federal Coordinating Center system, which coordinates patient reception and distribution across a national network of hospitals when patients must be evacuated from a disaster-affected region.

### Disaster Medical Assistance Teams

A standard DMAT consists of approximately [PLACEHOLDER: 35-45 personnel in most references] individuals including physicians, nurses, paramedics, pharmacists, and support personnel organized into a self-sufficient medical unit [CITATION]. DMATs are designed to be:

- **Deployable within 24-48 hours** of federal activation
- **Self-sufficient for 72 hours** with supplies, equipment, and logistical support
- **Capable of providing field medical care** at a level equivalent to a small emergency department

DMATs operate under a federal deployment authority following a disaster declaration or pre-declaration in anticipation of a major event. They do not replace local EMS; they supplement it. Typical DMAT deployment scenarios include:

- Establishing field medical stations in areas where hospital access has been disrupted
- Providing primary care and minor emergency services in evacuee shelters
- Supporting mass casualty sorting and initial stabilization at major events
- Providing surge support to overwhelmed hospital emergency departments
- Providing medical support at special needs shelters

**Integration challenges.** DMATs arrive with their own command structure, communications equipment, and supply chain. Integrating a DMAT into local ICS and ESF-8 coordination requires advance coordination, a designated liaison, and clear assignment of responsibilities. Jurisdictions that have conducted joint exercises with their regionally assigned DMAT prior to an event integrate DMATs significantly faster than those encountering each other for the first time in the field [CITATION].

DMATs operate under federal scope of practice during deployments. The Stafford Act and related federal authorities provide liability protections for DMAT personnel operating under federal deployment authority, parallel to the state-level protections discussed in Chapter 9.

## 11.5 Aeromedical Operations in Disaster

### Role of Air Medical Transport

Air medical transport — helicopter emergency medical services (HEMS) and fixed-wing critical care transport — provides capabilities that ground EMS cannot: speed over long distances, access to terrain inaccessible by road, and transport of critically ill patients requiring in-flight intensive care. In disasters, aeromedical assets become particularly important in:

- Search and rescue operations in flood, earthquake, and wilderness settings
- Evacuation of critical patients from rural areas when ground access is impaired
- Rapid transport of high-acuity patients to specialty centers (trauma, burn, stroke, ECMO) beyond ground transport range
- Patient movement from disaster-affected areas to undamaged hospital facilities

### Operational Constraints

Aeromedical operations in disaster are constrained by factors that ground operations are not:

**Weather.** Most civilian HEMS operates under visual flight rules (VFR), meaning operations cease in low visibility, high wind, and precipitation conditions. The same weather event that creates the disaster may prevent air medical response at the time of peak need.

**Landing zone availability.** HEMS requires a suitable landing zone, which may not exist in dense urban environments, in forested terrain, or at structural collapse sites. Landing zones must be cleared, secured, and communicated to the pilot before approach.

**Single-patient capacity.** Most HEMS aircraft carry one patient at a time, occasionally two. This limits throughput compared to ground transport and requires careful patient selection for air transport — typically the most critical patients for whom ground transport time would be prohibitively long.

**Coordination requirements.** Air medical coordination in a large event requires a dedicated air operations branch within ICS, coordination with the FAA and applicable air traffic control, and deconfliction of multiple aircraft from multiple agencies in the same airspace. Without this coordination, aerial congestion at disaster sites creates collision risk.

### Patient Selection for Air Transport

In MCI settings, air transport should generally be reserved for:

- Patients requiring time-sensitive specialty care (neurosurgical, cardiac) not available at the nearest ground-accessible hospital
- Patients whose ground transport time would be greater than [PLACEHOLDER: specific time threshold varies by region and injury type] minutes and whose injury trajectory is sensitive to this additional time
- Patients requiring in-flight critical care capabilities not available in ground units

Air transport of lower-acuity patients when ground transport is feasible wastes a finite and expensive resource. The Transport Unit in the Medical Branch should assign air transport through a deliberate triage process, not as a default for severely injured patients regardless of available alternatives.

## 11.6 EMS Workforce Sustainability in Disaster

### The EMS Workforce Under Stress

EMS personnel are simultaneously responders and community members. In a disaster affecting the community where they live and work, EMS personnel face:

- **Convergent concerns about family safety.** Personnel who cannot confirm their family's safety may leave their post, function at reduced effectiveness, or experience psychological distress that impairs clinical judgment.
- **Extended operational periods.** Disasters require sustained operations over days to weeks. Standard twelve-hour shift structures may become impossible to maintain with reduced staffing.
- **Physical exhaustion.** Lifting, moving patients, and operating in degraded environmental conditions (heat, smoke, debris) accelerates physical fatigue.
- **Psychological cumulative load.** Multiple high-acuity events, patient deaths, and morally difficult decisions (black tagging, triage protocol deviations) accumulate into moral injury and acute stress responses. See Chapter 24.

### Staffing and Scheduling Adaptations

Disaster staffing adaptations include:

**Extended shift protocols.** Some systems use twelve-hour shifts extended to sixteen or twenty-four hours early in a disaster when demand is highest, shifting to more sustainable rotations as mutual aid resources arrive. Extended shifts carry risks of impaired clinical performance from fatigue and must be limited [CITATION].

**Minimum staffing thresholds.** Systems should establish minimum crew rest requirements (minimum hours off between shifts) that are enforced even under operational pressure. Fatigued EMS personnel contribute to clinical errors, vehicle accidents, and secondary injury.

**Family reunification support.** Systems that provide personnel with early information about family member safety or arrangements for family member evacuation and shelter retain more personnel at their posts. This is not a benefit — it is an operational necessity.

**Behavioral health integration.** Peer support specialists and critical incident stress management (CISM) resources should be embedded in EMS operations during multi-day disasters, providing ongoing support rather than waiting for formal debriefing at event conclusion. See Chapter 24 for the behavioral health framework.

**Credentialing of spontaneous volunteers.** Unsolicited EMS volunteers will arrive during major disasters. Systems must have a credentialing process to verify licensure, assign volunteers to appropriate roles, and document their activities. Unverified volunteers assigned clinical roles create liability and patient safety risk.

### EMS Personnel as Disaster Victims

EMS agencies in directly impacted areas may lose a significant portion of their workforce when their personnel are themselves victims of the event. This is a particular risk in hurricanes and major flooding events, where EMS personnel who live in the affected area may experience home damage, injury, or displacement.

Pre-event agency continuity plans should identify:

- Minimum staffing thresholds for continued operations
- Personnel assigned to continuity roles (those who will report regardless of personal impact)
- Succession for key leadership positions
- Off-site staging agreements for personnel and vehicles if the primary station is damaged

Post-disaster, agencies supporting employee recovery (housing assistance, temporary accommodation, emergency loans) retain workforce better than those that do not. The cost of workforce support is substantially lower than the cost of rebuilding an agency after collapse [CITATION].

## 11.7 Community Paramedicine and Mobile Integrated Healthcare in Disaster

### Beyond Transport: Community Paramedicine

Community paramedicine (CP) programs train paramedics to provide community-based healthcare beyond emergency transport, including chronic disease management, medication compliance support, post-discharge follow-up, and social determinants screening. Mobile integrated healthcare (MIH) is the broader framework encompassing CP and other non-emergency EMS applications.

In disaster settings, CP and MIH capabilities address needs that standard emergency EMS cannot:

**Medical support in shelters.** CP-trained paramedics staffing general population shelters or special needs shelters provide medical assessment and management that does not require emergency transport. This reduces unnecessary ED visits and supports continuity of care for displaced patients with chronic conditions.

**Evacuation support for technology-dependent individuals.** CP programs with registries of high-medical-need community members can prioritize outreach during evacuation orders, arranging appropriate transport and medical support for patients who cannot self-evacuate.

**Post-disaster follow-up.** After a disaster, CP paramedics can conduct wellness checks and medication assessment for vulnerable community members who remain in affected areas, identifying deterioration before it becomes an emergency.

**Medication continuity.** Patients displaced by disaster frequently lose access to medications. CP paramedics trained in medication bridging protocols (using standing orders or telemedicine physician oversight) can provide interim supplies of critical medications while patients reconnect with their regular prescribers.

These applications require pre-disaster program development. An EMS agency without an existing CP or MIH program cannot stand one up during a disaster. Emergency managers should inventory existing CP and MIH programs in their jurisdictions and integrate them into ESF-8 planning as a distinct operational capability.

## Applied Vignette: Three Rivers EMS Consortium Hurricane Response

Three Rivers County is a coastal jurisdiction with a county-operated EMS system of twenty-two ambulances and approximately 180 field personnel. When Hurricane Margaux made landfall as a Category 3 storm, Three Rivers experienced widespread flooding, significant structural damage, and the complete loss of its primary EMS station and vehicle bay in the surge zone.

Twelve of the agency's ambulances were destroyed or inoperable due to surge flooding. Ten personnel reported that their homes were destroyed. In the first eighteen hours post-landfall, the agency was operating with ten units and approximately ninety field personnel against a call volume that peaked at [PLACEHOLDER: specific numbers from vignette] — more than four times the normal daily average.

**Day 1-2: Crisis adaptation.** The EMS medical director, operating from the county EOC, authorized treat-and-release protocols for lacerations not requiring sutures, minor orthopedic injuries, and mild hypertensive urgency without acute organ involvement. Three improvised aid stations were established at school buildings being used as shelter sites. Two community paramedics from the county's small CP program were deployed to the special needs shelter to provide continuous medical oversight.

The agency activated EMAC mutual aid requests through the state EMS office within six hours of landfall. Four units from a neighboring state arrived at hour thirty-six, though two of the crews had never operated in a flooding environment and required orientation before deployment.

**Day 3-5: DMAT integration.** A federally deployed DMAT arrived on day three and was assigned to primary care operations at the two largest shelter sites, freeing Three Rivers EMS units for emergency response. The DMAT liaison was embedded in the county EOC's ESF-8 cell, which significantly reduced the coordination friction experienced in the first day before the liaison arrived.

The after-action review identified three primary lessons. First, the twelve destroyed ambulances could have been protected by pre-landfall repositioning to elevated ground — this was in the EOP but not executed due to timeline compression before the storm. Second, the treat-and-release protocol authorization took four hours, largely because the EMS medical director was not included in the initial EOC activation. Third, EMAC personnel integration would have been faster with a pre-established credentialing protocol rather than improvised verification on arrival.

All three findings generated specific corrective actions integrated into the updated EOP.

## Key Terms

**Emergency Medical Services (EMS) System:** The integrated set of components providing pre-hospital emergency medical care, including dispatch, field response, medical oversight, transport, and receiving facilities.

**Emergency Support Function 8 (ESF-8):** The federal emergency support function coordinating public health and medical services in disaster response; led by HHS.

**Emergency Management Assistance Compact (EMAC):** An interstate compact providing the legal framework for cross-state emergency resource sharing, including EMS resources.

**National Disaster Medical System (NDMS):** The federally coordinated system providing supplemental medical response capabilities to states during major disasters; managed by HHS ASPR.

**Disaster Medical Assistance Team (DMAT):** A deployable NDMS team providing field medical care; self-sufficient for 72 hours and designed for 24-48 hour deployment activation.

**Community Paramedicine (CP):** An EMS delivery model in which paramedics provide community-based healthcare beyond traditional emergency transport.

**Mobile Integrated Healthcare (MIH):** The broader framework for non-emergency EMS health services, of which community paramedicine is one component.

**Treat-and-Release Protocol:** An EMS protocol authorizing field personnel to assess and treat patients without transport when the patient does not meet criteria for emergency department care; requires medical director authorization.

**Alternate Destination Protocol:** An EMS protocol routing patients to facilities other than emergency departments when standard receiving facilities are unavailable or when lower-acuity patients can be managed at appropriate alternate sites.

**EMS Medical Director:** The physician responsible for the clinical quality, protocols, and medical oversight of an EMS system; plays a critical role in authorizing disaster-related protocol modifications.

**Helicopter Emergency Medical Services (HEMS):** Aeromedical transport using rotary-wing aircraft; provides speed and terrain access capabilities not available to ground EMS.

**Moral Injury:** Psychological harm resulting from participation in or witness of events that violate one's moral code; a significant risk for EMS personnel in disaster settings.

## Scenario Exercise: Northgate Metro Transit Derailment

**Background:** At 6:23 a.m. on a Thursday, a commuter rail train derailed in an underground tunnel section of the Northgate Metro transit system. Preliminary reports indicate two train cars involved, approximately 300 passengers aboard both cars at the time of derailment. Access is limited to two entry points, each of which requires responders to travel approximately 800 meters on foot through a tunnel with limited lighting and incomplete ventilation. Smoke from a small electrical fire was reported but has been suppressed.

**Current resources:** Metro EMS has four units on scene. Three additional units from the neighboring city are en route, estimated arrival six to twelve minutes. A HEMS unit is on standby but cannot access the underground scene; it is available for hospital transport from a surface staging area.

**Available hospitals:** Level I trauma center, 8 miles; Level II trauma center, 4 miles; community hospital with no surgical capability, 1.5 miles.

**Tasks:**

1. What MCI tier does this event represent at this moment, and what resources should be immediately requested? Identify the specific EMAC, mutual aid, and NDMS resources that may be relevant.

2. Describe the triage and treatment challenges specific to a confined underground space. What operational adaptations to standard START triage are required?

3. Given the three hospital options, develop a patient distribution plan for an estimated twenty Red, forty-five Yellow, and 100+ Green patients. Justify your reasoning.

4. At hour three, three patients in the Yellow area have deteriorated to Red status. What does this tell you about your triage and tracking system, and what operational adjustments are warranted?

5. A local television crew has gained access to the surface staging area and is broadcasting live. The public information officer asks you what medical information you can release. What is releasable at this point, and what is not?

## Practitioner Takeaways

**EMS integration in ESF-8 is not automatic.** EMS agencies organized under fire departments may fall outside health department-led ESF-8 planning tables. Emergency managers must explicitly include EMS leadership in public health emergency planning, not assume they are covered by fire and rescue representation.

**EMAC takes time.** Interstate mutual aid through EMAC requires state-to-state request processing. A request submitted at hour zero rarely produces resources before hour thirty-six. Local and regional mutual aid must bridge the gap.

**DMAT integration requires advance work.** The first DMAT deployment to a jurisdiction with no prior joint exercise is substantially slower and less effective than deployment to a jurisdiction with established relationships. Know your regionally assigned DMAT before you need it.

**Treat-and-release protocols are not a reduction in care — they are the right care in the right setting.** Pre-authorized, medically directed treat-and-release protocols improve overall system performance by ensuring resources are used where they are most needed. The absence of these protocols leaves EMS systems with only one tool — transport — for a much wider range of clinical presentations.

**Workforce sustainability is an operational issue.** Burned-out, traumatized, or inadequately rested EMS personnel make clinical errors, leave the workforce, and undermine public trust. Behavioral health integration and workforce support are not employee benefits. They are operational requirements for sustained disaster response.

## Chapter Notes

The distinction between NDMS/DMAT as supplemental federal resources versus EMAC as a state-to-state mutual aid mechanism is frequently confused in both planning documents and operational briefings. Emergency managers should confirm with their state emergency management agency which tier of federal activation triggers NDMS deployment and what documentation is required for an EMAC request so that these processes are not delayed at the moment of need.

Community paramedicine programs vary substantially in scope, staffing, and medical oversight models across jurisdictions. The disaster applications described in this chapter represent the capability envelope of well-developed CP programs; EMS agencies with limited or no CP infrastructure will require partnership with community health organizations or public health agencies to provide equivalent capabilities in disaster settings.
