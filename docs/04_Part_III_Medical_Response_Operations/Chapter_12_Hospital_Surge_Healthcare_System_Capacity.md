# Chapter 12: Hospital Surge and Healthcare System Capacity

## Chapter Overview

Hospitals are the endpoint of disaster medical care. When field operations succeed, patients arrive at emergency departments; when they fail, patients also arrive at emergency departments, just in greater numbers and worse condition. Either way, the hospital must absorb what the disaster delivers. This chapter examines how hospitals plan for, activate, and sustain operations under surge conditions, and how those individual hospital responses connect to a regional healthcare system that must function as a coordinated whole.

Hospital surge is one of the most demanding operational challenges in emergency management because it involves simultaneous pressure on every hospital resource category at once. Staff are asked to work longer, in environments that may be structurally compromised, treating patients whose usual records are unavailable, without the equipment and pharmacy supply chains they rely on in normal operations. The systems that make modern hospital care possible — automated medication dispensing, electronic health records, centralized laboratory processing, just-in-time supply delivery — are also among the first systems to strain or fail under disaster conditions.

This chapter addresses surge from a planning and operations perspective. It presents the conceptual frameworks emergency managers and healthcare professionals use to measure and manage surge, the organizational tools hospitals deploy, the mechanisms that connect hospitals to regional healthcare coalitions, and the difficult policy space of altered standards of care. The chapter pays particular attention to equity dimensions of surge — whose needs are most likely to be deprioritized when capacity contracts, and how to design surge plans that mitigate rather than amplify existing health disparities.

## 12.1 Surge Capacity and Surge Capability: A Conceptual Foundation

The terms *surge capacity* and *surge capability* are sometimes used interchangeably in casual usage, but they describe different things, and conflating them leads to planning errors.

**Surge capacity** refers to the ability to expand throughput — to handle more patients than under routine operations. It is primarily a question of volume. A hospital with strong surge capacity can move patients through assessment, treatment, and disposition faster, hold more patients simultaneously, and add physical space (beds, treatment areas, waiting areas) when needed. Surge capacity is measured in patient volume relative to baseline.

**Surge capability** refers to the ability to handle patients whose needs differ from the usual patient mix — to treat conditions for which the facility is not normally staffed, equipped, or organized. A rural critical access hospital normally treating medical and minor trauma patients has limited capability to manage complex burn injuries, pediatric critical care, or decompression illness. Surge capability is measured not by volume but by clinical complexity range.

Both matter simultaneously in most disasters. A structural collapse event produces trauma patients exceeding normal volume (capacity challenge) and may produce patients with crush syndrome, traumatic amputations, and severe rhabdomyolysis in quantities the local system has rarely managed (capability challenge). A mass-exposure chemical event produces patients with unusual toxidromes requiring specific antidotes that many hospitals stock in limited quantities (capability challenge) who also arrive rapidly and simultaneously (capacity challenge).

The distinction drives planning in a specific direction: surge capacity planning focuses on physical space, bed management, and patient throughput systems; surge capability planning focuses on specialized equipment stockpiles, staff competency, and mutual aid agreements for specialty transfers.

### The Four S's Framework

The Four S's framework provides a practical way to map surge limitations. Originally developed in the context of bioterrorism planning [CITATION], the framework has been applied broadly across all-hazards hospital preparedness. The four categories are:

**Staff.** Hospitals require people with specific skills: physicians, nurses, respiratory therapists, surgical technicians, pharmacists, phlebotomists, and dozens of other roles. During a surge event, staff face competing demands. Some will be unavailable due to being personally affected by the disaster. Others will be called to work extra shifts but will fatigue. The staff dimension of surge requires contingency staffing plans, credential expansion protocols, and behavioral health support to sustain workforce function across extended operational periods.

**Stuff.** Equipment and supplies define what a hospital can clinically do. Ventilators, surgical instruments, medication stockpiles, blood products, imaging equipment, decontamination supplies, and personal protective equipment are all "stuff." In normal operations, supply chain systems maintain just-in-time inventory that keeps carrying costs low. In surge, those just-in-time systems fail exactly when needs spike. The stuff dimension requires pre-positioned strategic stockpiles, supplier diversification, and protocols for equipment sharing and reuse under austere conditions.

**Structure.** The physical plant of the hospital imposes hard constraints. A hospital with 200 licensed beds cannot simply declare 400 beds into existence. However, structure is more flexible than it appears: corridors can be staged with treatment areas, waiting rooms can be converted to observation wards, conference rooms can serve as medication distribution stations, and alternate care sites — convention centers, gymnasiums, schools — can extend the structural footprint. The structure dimension requires detailed space conversion protocols, IT and power extension plans, and pre-identified alternate care site locations.

**Systems.** Electronic health records, laboratory information systems, automated medication dispensing, supply chain management software, and communications infrastructure are systems. When systems fail — because power is disrupted, servers are overloaded, or network connectivity is lost — hospitals revert to paper-based backup operations that are slower, more error-prone, and harder to coordinate. The systems dimension requires downtime procedures for every clinical system, paper backup forms, and tested communication redundancy.

A hospital that has addressed all four S's has not eliminated surge vulnerability; it has mapped its limiting factors clearly enough to make resource requests, mutual aid activations, and operational adjustments with accurate situational awareness rather than guesswork.

## 12.2 Hospital Incident Command System (HICS)

The Hospital Incident Command System is the standard organizational framework for hospital emergency management. HICS adapts the Incident Command System principles — unity of command, span of control, modular expansion, common terminology, integrated communications — to the hospital environment.

### HICS Structure

HICS is organized around a Hospital Incident Commander (HIC) — typically the senior administrator or designee on duty — and four functional sections:

**Operations Section** manages the direct provision of patient care and clinical services. This section includes the Medical Staff Branch (patient care, clinical services, infrastructure), Infrastructure Branch (utilities, safety, communications), Security Branch, and Business Continuity Branch. The Operations Section Chief is typically the Chief Nursing Officer or designee in most hospital HICS configurations.

**Planning Section** collects, evaluates, and disseminates information about the incident and current hospital status. The Planning Section Chief tracks resource utilization, maintains situation status, produces Incident Action Plans (IAPs), and manages documentation. This section includes Unit Leaders for Situation, Resources, Documentation, and Demobilization.

**Logistics Section** acquires and maintains the personnel, materials, and services needed to support operations. Logistics includes Service Branch (communications, food/nutrition, IT) and Support Branch (supply, staffing/credentialing, transportation, employee health/safety).

**Finance/Administration Section** tracks costs, manages contracts and purchases, maintains time records, and handles compensation and claims. This section is frequently understaffed or entirely bypassed in short-duration events, creating significant post-event recovery complications [CITATION].

### The HICS Incident Commander Role

The Hospital Incident Commander holds command and control authority over all hospital incident operations. The HIC is supported by a Command Staff that mirrors ICS Command Staff: Public Information Officer (PIO), Safety Officer, and Liaison Officer.

The PIO manages internal and external communications, including media inquiries and patient information releases. In a surge event, the PIO works closely with the hospital's communications department, the county public information function, and ESF-8 public affairs staff.

The Safety Officer monitors conditions for risks to patients and personnel, recommends protective measures, and has authority to stop unsafe operations. During surge, safety monitoring must extend to areas being converted for clinical use, temporary medication storage areas, and corridors used for patient movement.

The Liaison Officer serves as the primary contact for cooperating agencies — including county emergency management, healthcare coalition representatives, public health, and mutual aid partners.

### HICS Activation and Scalability

A core strength of HICS is scalability. Not every position needs to be filled in every activation. For a brief, localized power outage, the Hospital Incident Commander may manage the incident with a small command team and minimal section activation. For a multi-day surge event, all four sections may be fully staffed across multiple operational periods.

HICS activation is typically tiered:

**Level 4 (Monitoring):** Routine monitoring of an emerging situation. No formal incident management structure activated. Duty administrators notified.

**Level 3 (Limited Activation):** A defined event with limited impact on hospital operations. Key personnel notified and available. Partial HICS structure activated. Hospital EOC opened.

**Level 2 (Partial Activation):** Significant event affecting multiple hospital departments or services. HICS structure substantially activated. All section chiefs on site or available. Hospital EOC fully operational.

**Level 1 (Full Activation):** Major event with significant or potentially catastrophic impact on hospital operations. All HICS positions staffed. Hospital EOC operational continuously. ESF-8 and healthcare coalition notification initiated.

The activation level should track the incident, not exceed it prematurely (which wastes resources) and not lag behind it (which delays coordinated response). Real-world after-action reviews consistently identify delayed activation as a significant contributor to response inefficiency [CITATION].

## 12.3 Hospital EOC Activation and Escalation

The Hospital Emergency Operations Center (Hospital EOC) is the physical location from which HICS command, planning, logistics, and finance functions coordinate. It is the hospital's operational nerve center during an incident.

### Hospital EOC Essentials

A fully functional Hospital EOC requires:

**Communications.** Primary telephone and radio systems, backup communications (satellite phone, amateur radio, runner protocols), connectivity to county/regional EOC, ESF-8 reporting channels, and internal PA/overhead systems.

**Information Management.** Whiteboards, status boards, or electronic dashboards displaying bed availability by unit, diversion status, patient volume by acuity, staff availability, and supply levels. Information management in the Hospital EOC should be visible to all personnel in the room — this is the shared operating picture that enables coordinated decision-making.

**Documentation.** All incident actions, resource requests, communications with external agencies, and operational decisions must be documented in real time. Failure to document during the incident creates significant post-event recovery complications, impairs after-action review, and can expose the hospital to liability.

**Decision Support.** Key policies, protocols, and plans must be immediately accessible in the Hospital EOC — Hospital Emergency Operations Plan, HICS Job Action Sheets, contact lists, alternate care site activation protocols, and memoranda of understanding with mutual aid partners.

### Escalation Triggers

Escalation from a lower to a higher HICS activation level should be triggered by specific operational thresholds, not by general "feeling" that things are worsening. Defined escalation triggers might include: emergency department diversion declared; ICU beds at [X]% capacity for [Y] hours; surgical schedule canceled beyond routine; [Z] or more disaster-related patients registered; security incident in progress; critical supply category below [X]-day reserve; or mass fatality notification received.

Pre-defining escalation triggers reduces decision latency under stress, distributes authority to activate higher levels to duty administrators (not only executives), and ensures that activation decisions are made at the right time rather than too early (organizational fatigue) or too late (uncoordinated response during the critical early phase).

### Operational Periods and IAP Development

HICS uses the Incident Action Plan as its operational management tool. In a surge event, the IAP establishes objectives for a defined operational period (typically 12 or 24 hours), specifies resources assigned to each objective, and identifies safety precautions and communication protocols. Each subsequent operational period begins with a briefing, reviews progress against the prior IAP, and generates a new IAP with updated objectives.

For multi-day surge events — a prolonged infectious disease surge, a post-earthquake response — the operational period structure becomes the rhythm of the hospital's incident management. Transitions between operational periods require explicit handoffs, including status briefings for incoming section chiefs and clear documentation of outstanding resource requests and incomplete actions.

## 12.4 Healthcare Coalition Surge Coordination

Individual hospital response, even when well organized, has hard limits. A single hospital cannot redistribute its own patients, secure mutual aid from other hospitals, or coordinate with public health and emergency management without external relationships. The healthcare coalition is the regional structure that makes those connections.

### Healthcare Coalition Structure

Healthcare coalitions are regional organizations — typically organized at a county, multi-county, or regional level — that bring together hospitals, emergency medical services, public health agencies, emergency management, and other healthcare entities to plan, train, exercise, and coordinate across an entire healthcare system.

The Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) now require hospitals participating in Medicare and Medicaid to participate in healthcare coalitions as part of their emergency preparedness Conditions of Participation [CITATION]. This requirement has significantly increased coalition formalization and membership breadth.

Coalition functions during a surge event typically include:

**Real-Time Situational Awareness.** Coalitions operate hospital availability tracking systems (such as EMSystem, ReddiNet, or state-specific platforms) that allow the coalition, hospitals, and EMS to see bed availability, diversion status, and specialty capability in near real time across the region. This shared situational awareness enables informed patient distribution decisions.

**Resource Sharing.** Pre-established mutual aid agreements within the coalition allow hospitals to request and receive equipment, supplies, pharmaceuticals, or personnel from partner facilities. Coalition-level mutual aid is distinct from facility-to-facility informal arrangements — it operates under legal frameworks that address liability, workers' compensation, credentialing portability, and cost reimbursement.

**Incident Coordination.** During a regional surge event, the coalition may activate a Healthcare Coalition Incident Management Team (IMT) to coordinate system-wide response, interface with ESF-8 and regional emergency management, manage resource requests to state and federal authorities, and support regional information dissemination.

**Planning, Training, and Exercise.** Coalitions coordinate regional hazard vulnerability analysis, joint planning for regional surge scenarios, tabletop and functional exercises involving multiple facilities, and after-action review processes that feed improvement planning.

### The Coalition-Hospital Relationship

The healthcare coalition operates as a coordinator, not a commander. Individual hospitals retain operational authority over their own facilities. The coalition cannot direct a hospital to accept patients, release staff, or change clinical protocols. Its power is the power of coordination: it can facilitate resource flow, share information, and convene decision-makers — but compliance with coalition recommendations depends on the relationships, trust, and shared protocols built during non-emergency periods.

This structural reality has an important implication for emergency managers: the strength of regional healthcare surge capacity is a direct function of the quality of the coalition's pre-event relationships and planning. A coalition that has conducted joint exercises, established tested communication protocols, and built mutual trust among member facility leadership will respond significantly more effectively than one that exists primarily as a directory and a conference call list.

## 12.5 Altered Standards of Care

When the healthcare system is overwhelmed — when demand for care genuinely exceeds the capacity to deliver it under normal standards — the concept of altered standards of care becomes operationally necessary.

### Defining the Concept

Altered standards of care (also called crisis standards of care, or CSC, in their most severe form) refers to a legally and ethically authorized shift in the standard of medical practice during a public health emergency or disaster. Under altered standards, care decisions are made with the goal of doing the greatest good for the greatest number rather than providing the maximum possible care to each individual patient.

The concept exists on a spectrum. The Institute of Medicine [CITATION] (now the National Academies of Medicine) described three levels:

**Conventional:** Normal standards apply. The healthcare system is stressed but functioning within licensed bed capacity and using available resources.

**Contingency:** Adaptive strategies — such as cohorting patients, canceling elective procedures, using hallways for overflow — allow care to remain functionally equivalent to conventional care, though delivery methods are modified.

**Crisis:** Substantial deviations from usual standards are unavoidable because demand exceeds all available capacity. Resource allocation decisions must be made at the population level. Individual patients may receive less than the usual standard of care.

Most surge events never reach the crisis level. Contingency-level adaptations, if implemented early and coordinated regionally, can usually prevent the system from crossing into crisis standards. The relevant planning failure is not the implementation of crisis standards per se but the failure to implement contingency strategies early enough to prevent unnecessary progression to crisis.

### Crisis Standards Activation

Crisis standards of care is not an individual clinician decision. It is a system-level determination that requires formal declaration — typically by the state health officer or equivalent authority — that the healthcare system is operating in conditions that legally justify deviation from normal standards [CITATION]. This declaration has legal significance: it provides liability protection for clinicians making resource allocation decisions in good faith under declared conditions, and it activates the modified regulatory environment (including potential scope of practice expansions and documentation requirement modifications) that supports surge operations.

Healthcare coalitions play a central role in the crisis standards activation pathway. They provide the systemic situational awareness data — bed availability, patient volume, supply levels, workforce status — that informs the state health officer's determination of whether crisis conditions exist.

### Resource Allocation Under Crisis Standards

The most ethically charged aspect of crisis standards is allocation — how to distribute genuinely insufficient resources (ventilators, ICU beds, blood products, dialysis capacity) when there are more patients who need them than the supply can accommodate.

Most resource allocation frameworks used in crisis standards are utilitarian in structure, prioritizing patients most likely to benefit from the scarce resource, as measured by short-term survival likelihood. Commonly used clinical scoring tools include the Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score for critical illness [CITATION] and modified triage protocols that incorporate expected survival probability.

Utilitarian allocation frameworks have well-documented equity concerns. Clinical scoring systems trained on historical patient populations may systematically underestimate survival likelihood for people with disabilities, older adults, or members of populations with higher baseline chronic disease burden related to structural inequities [CITATION]. Several state crisis standards frameworks have incorporated explicit equity protections, including prohibition of disability status as an exclusion criterion and requirements for independent review processes.

Emergency managers should be familiar with their state's crisis standards of care framework — not because they will make bedside allocation decisions, but because they will be involved in the activation process, the public communication strategy, and the oversight mechanisms that ensure equitable implementation.

## 12.6 Hospital Decompression Strategies

Decompression refers to strategies that reduce patient volume in a facility by moving patients out, moving patients around, or preventing new patients from entering, in order to restore functional capacity. Decompression is typically pursued as a contingency-level strategy to avoid reaching crisis conditions.

### Discharge and Disposition Acceleration

The fastest decompression strategy is accelerating the discharge of patients already in the facility who are clinically ready or near-ready for discharge. This includes activating reverse triage protocols (identifying which admitted patients can safely be discharged earlier than planned), expediting social work and case management to resolve discharge barriers, activating home health and community-based care to receive patients who need continued monitoring, and coordinating with skilled nursing facilities and rehabilitation centers to accelerate transfer acceptance.

Each of these requires pre-established relationships and protocols. A hospital that calls a skilled nursing facility for emergency transfer capacity during a surge event without a pre-existing transfer agreement will often be unsuccessful. A hospital with standing transfer agreements, practiced communication protocols, and established relationships with receiving facility administrators will decompress far more efficiently.

### Elective Procedure Cancellation

Canceling or deferring elective surgical and procedural admissions frees preoperative, operative, and postoperative capacity that can be repurposed for surge patients. This is a standard contingency-level decompression tool. The operational challenge is determining the threshold for cancellation — too aggressive leads to patient harm from delayed care; too conservative provides insufficient decompression benefit.

Most hospital emergency plans define specific criteria (typically ED volume thresholds or ICU occupancy levels) that trigger automatic consideration of elective procedure cancellation, with authority to implement resting with the Chief Medical Officer or designee.

### Alternate Care Sites

When the hospital physical plant is fully utilized, patient care can extend to alternate care sites (ACS): convention centers, school gymnasiums, community centers, armories, or other large-venue facilities that can be configured for medical care. ACS operations are a significant undertaking — they require medical equipment, supplies, power and water infrastructure, pharmaceutical support, staffing, and communication connectivity that must be pre-positioned or rapidly mobilized.

Hospitals and healthcare coalitions typically maintain ACS annexes in their emergency plans specifying pre-identified locations, memoranda of understanding with facility owners, equipment lists and storage locations, and activation protocols. The quality of these plans varies significantly; facilities that have exercised ACS activation are substantially better positioned than those that have written plans but never tested them [CITATION].

ACS operations raise specific equity considerations. Patients relocated to an ACS may have reduced access to the clinical resources of the main hospital facility. If ACS placement decisions are made without attention to patient communication needs, mobility requirements, or technology dependence, disproportionate burdens fall on people with disabilities and people with limited English proficiency. ACS plans should explicitly address accessibility, translation services, and medical equipment support.

### Diversion Coordination

Emergency department diversion — notifying EMS to transport ambulance patients to another facility — is a common short-term decompression tool for individual hospitals. When used in isolation, however, diversion creates a system-level problem: if multiple hospitals in a region simultaneously declare diversion, there may be no available diversion destination, and EMS is left with patients and no clear receiving facility.

Regional diversion coordination, managed through the healthcare coalition or regional emergency medical coordination center, provides a system view that prevents all-hospitals-on-diversion situations by managing diversion declarations as a system-level resource rather than allowing each hospital to declare independently. Some regions have implemented coordinated diversion protocols that require coalition consultation before individual hospital diversion declarations exceeding a defined duration [CITATION].

## 12.7 Long-Term Care Facilities and Specialty Hospitals in Regional Surge

Regional surge management is broader than the acute care hospital network. Long-term care (LTC) facilities — nursing homes, skilled nursing facilities, assisted living — are healthcare facilities that both absorb and generate surge demand. Specialty hospitals — psychiatric hospitals, rehabilitation hospitals, pediatric specialty centers, cancer centers — hold patients whose discharge pathways run through the acute care system.

### Long-Term Care Facilities

LTC facilities face distinctive surge challenges. They typically have limited nursing staff per patient, minimal on-site pharmacy and laboratory resources, no emergency department, and patient populations with complex chronic conditions, cognitive impairment, and high susceptibility to environmental disruption.

During disaster events, LTC facilities face two simultaneous pressures: their own residents may be at heightened risk (particularly in weather or infrastructure events affecting building safety or utility supply), and they may receive pressure to accept early discharge or transfer patients from overwhelmed acute care hospitals.

Emergency managers should understand that LTC capacity is not simply "available beds." Accepting a post-surgical patient or a patient with a new neurological injury into a nursing facility requires matching clinical capability to patient need. A facility with primarily dementia residents and limited skilled nursing staff may not be able to safely accept a patient requiring wound care and IV medication management, even if it has physical bed space.

Effective regional surge planning includes LTC facilities in coalition membership and exercises, establishes clear communication protocols between hospitals and LTC facilities during surge events, and develops realistic assessments of LTC acceptance capability rather than treating licensed bed count as equivalent to surge capacity [CITATION].

### Psychiatric Facilities

Psychiatric inpatient facilities are frequently overlooked in surge planning. They hold patients who may be awaiting discharge to community placement, and whose placement barriers include housing unavailability, insurance coverage limitations, and community service capacity — factors that are typically worsened during and after disaster events.

Simultaneously, disasters generate significant new behavioral health demand. Emergency department behavioral health visits increase during and after many disaster types [CITATION], adding to acute care burden. If regional psychiatric inpatient capacity is already constrained — as it is in many regions with long histories of psychiatric facility closures — the system has limited capacity to absorb this increased demand.

Surge planning that ignores psychiatric capacity is incomplete. Regional healthcare coalitions should include psychiatric hospital representatives and should exercise scenarios that include behavioral health surge as a component.

### Pediatric Specialty Resources

As discussed in Chapter 10, pediatric patients have distinct physiological and developmental characteristics that require specialized equipment and staff competency. Most acute care hospitals have some pediatric capability; most lack the capacity to manage a large surge of critically ill or injured children.

Regional pediatric surge planning requires clarity about which facilities can manage what level of pediatric acuity, pre-established transfer agreements and transport protocols, and cache supplies of pediatric-specific equipment at both pediatric specialty centers and community hospitals likely to receive pediatric patients in a mass casualty event.

## Applied Vignette: Greenfield Regional Medical Center — Industrial Ammonia Release

At 9:15 a.m. on a Tuesday, a refrigeration system failure at a cold storage facility in an industrial district releases approximately [PLACEHOLDER: estimated ammonia quantity] of anhydrous ammonia into a predominantly warehouse and light industrial area. The facility employs [PLACEHOLDER: number] workers on the day shift; the surrounding industrial district has an estimated daytime population of [PLACEHOLDER: number].

Greenfield Regional Medical Center is the Level II trauma center serving the region, located 4.2 miles from the release site. A community hospital, Valley View Medical Center, is located 2.1 miles from the facility in the opposite direction.

Within 30 minutes of the release, EMS reports multiple patients with respiratory distress, ocular burns, and skin irritation. Initial field triage estimates 15 red-tagged patients, 40 yellow, and an unknown number of green-tag walking wounded.

### Hospital Response

At Greenfield Regional, the Emergency Department charge nurse activates a Level 3 HICS response at 9:32 a.m. based on incoming EMS notification. The Hospital Incident Commander (duty administrator) arrives at the Hospital EOC at 9:45. By 9:55, a Level 2 activation is declared based on confirmed patient volume estimates.

The Operations Section Chief activates the mass decontamination protocol; Greenfield Regional has an outdoor decontamination corridor behind the emergency department that can process [PLACEHOLDER: number] patients per hour with trained staff. The corridor had been exercised twice in the preceding 18 months.

The Logistics Section begins contacting the hospital pharmacy to confirm antidote stockpiles. For severe ammonia inhalation, supportive care is the primary approach, but the hospital identifies that its bronchodilator and nebulized medication supply may be insufficient for the projected patient volume and contacts the healthcare coalition supply coordinator.

Valley View Medical Center, closer to the release site, receives a large number of walk-in patients who self-transported. Valley View activates a Level 3 response and contacts the healthcare coalition about potential overflow support.

### Regional Coordination

The healthcare coalition activates its Incident Management Team at 10:10 a.m. The coalition bed tracking system shows Greenfield Regional at 78% ED capacity and Valley View at 91%. The IMT contacts a third hospital, North County General, which has not yet received patients, and pre-notifies them for potential overflow.

At 10:45 a.m., Valley View declares ED diversion. The coalition IMT confirms North County General diversion availability and coordinates with EMS dispatch to route subsequent ambulance transports.

The coalition supply coordinator identifies that the region's combined bronchodilator medication supply, while adequate for the projected patient volume, is unevenly distributed. The IMT facilitates a supply transfer of [PLACEHOLDER: number] unit doses from North County General (which has excess) to Valley View (which is at high utilization).

### Outcomes and Lessons

The event is managed without activating crisis standards. Post-event analysis identifies several findings:

First, Valley View Medical Center's decontamination capability was undertrained. Staff were unfamiliar with the decontamination corridor layout, and early self-presenting patients were not efficiently routed to decontamination before entering the main ED, creating potential secondary contamination risk. The after-action recommendation was a joint decontamination exercise involving Valley View, EMS, and the local fire department hazmat team.

Second, the healthcare coalition supply redistribution was effective but slow. The process of identifying excess inventory, negotiating transfer, arranging transport, and completing chain-of-custody documentation took 2.5 hours. The coalition's after-action recommendation was to pre-establish supply sharing authorizations for defined chemical exposure antidotes and supportive care medications within the coalition mutual aid framework, reducing negotiation time during events.

Third, several patients with severe asthma in the green-tag category deteriorated over several hours after initial triage and required escalation to yellow or red. This reflects the well-documented phenomenon of delayed respiratory injury from ammonia exposure [CITATION] and reinforced the need for extended observation protocols for all patients with any respiratory symptom exposure, regardless of initial triage category.

## Key Terms

**Surge Capacity:** The ability of a healthcare facility or system to expand patient throughput volume beyond routine operational levels.

**Surge Capability:** The ability of a healthcare facility or system to manage patient clinical needs that differ from the routine patient mix, including specialized conditions or acuity levels.

**Four S's:** Framework for mapping hospital surge limitations: Staff, Stuff, Structure, and Systems.

**Hospital Incident Command System (HICS):** The adaptation of ICS principles to hospital emergency management; the standard organizational framework for hospital incident response.

**Hospital Incident Commander (HIC):** The senior authority responsible for all incident management functions at a hospital during an activated HICS response.

**Hospital EOC:** Hospital Emergency Operations Center; the physical location from which HICS command, planning, logistics, and finance functions coordinate during an incident.

**Healthcare Coalition:** A regional organization of hospitals, EMS, public health, emergency management, and other healthcare entities that plans, trains, and coordinates healthcare system surge response.

**Altered Standards of Care (ASC):** A legally and ethically authorized shift in the standard of medical practice during a declared public health emergency or disaster when demand exceeds supply.

**Crisis Standards of Care (CSC):** The most severe level of altered standards; requires formal declaration by state health authority; permits resource allocation decisions at the population level.

**Contingency Care:** Adaptive strategies that modify care delivery while maintaining functional equivalence to conventional standards; the target level of surge management.

**Reverse Triage:** Systematic reassessment of admitted inpatients to identify those who can be safely discharged earlier than planned, to free inpatient capacity for surge admissions.

**Alternate Care Site (ACS):** A facility outside the hospital building used to extend patient care capacity during surge; typically a large-venue facility converted for medical operations.

**Diversion Coordination:** Regional management of emergency department diversion declarations to prevent simultaneous all-hospitals-on-diversion situations.

**SOFA Score:** Sequential Organ Failure Assessment; a clinical scoring tool widely used in crisis standards resource allocation frameworks to assess short-term survival likelihood.

**Healthcare Coalition Incident Management Team (IMT):** A coordination function activated by the coalition during regional surge events to support system-wide response, resource sharing, and external coordination.

## Scenario Exercise: Cedar Falls University Hospital — Multi-Hospital System Activation

### Scenario Overview

Cedar Falls University Hospital (CFUH) is a 450-bed academic medical center and Level I trauma center in a mid-sized city of approximately 300,000. It is part of a three-hospital healthcare system that also includes Cedar Falls Community Hospital (200 beds, Level III trauma) and Riverside Rehabilitation Hospital (85 beds, rehabilitation only). The Cedar Falls Healthcare Coalition includes six hospital members and interfaces with the regional EMS system, county public health, and two-county emergency management.

At 6:15 a.m. on a weekday, a major gas main rupture causes a building collapse in a downtown commercial district. The collapse involves a six-story mixed-use building occupied by a hotel, restaurant, and office spaces. Early reports estimate 150-200 people may be trapped or injured.

All three facilities in the Cedar Falls system are already operating at elevated census levels due to an ongoing respiratory virus season. CFUH ED is at 92% capacity, Cedar Falls Community Hospital ED at 85%, and Riverside Rehabilitation is full.

### Phase 1 Discussion (0-60 Minutes)

Divide into small groups. Each group is assigned one facility or the coalition IMT role. Discuss: What is the appropriate HICS activation level for your assigned facility? What are the first five actions your Hospital Incident Commander or IMT coordinator would take? What information do you need most urgently?

### Phase 2 Discussion (60-180 Minutes)

The event has produced 22 confirmed red-tag patients, 45 yellow-tag, and an estimated 100+ green-tag. CFUH has received 18 red-tag patients and is approaching ICU capacity. Cedar Falls Community Hospital has received 7 red-tags, 25 yellow-tags, and 60 green-tags in the ED. Riverside Rehabilitation has received no acute patients but has been contacted about accepting 10 stable admitted patients from CFUH to free inpatient capacity.

For each facility: What decompression strategies are available? What resource needs require coalition support? What triggers would shift your HICS activation level?

For the coalition IMT: What does your regional picture look like? What actions can you take to support regional balance? What state-level resource requests, if any, are warranted at this stage?

### Phase 3 Discussion (Hours 6-24)

It is now 6 hours into the event. Rescue operations are ongoing; additional patients are expected. Several CFUH ICU nurses have reached the end of their normal 12-hour shifts. CFUH surgical suite has been in continuous operation. The elective surgical schedule for tomorrow has been partially canceled, but 8 organ transplant patients are in the hospital who cannot safely be deferred.

Discuss: How does the staffing dimension of the Four S's present itself in this scenario? What workforce sustainment decisions must be made? Are there triggers for contingency-level care adaptations? Does any element of this scenario approach crisis standards territory? What is the coalition's role in workforce support?

## Practitioner Takeaways

Healthcare administrators and emergency planners working with hospital systems can apply the following from this chapter:

**Activate HICS early and scale down rather than late and scramble up.** Early activation preserves the opportunity for organized response. It is far easier to demobilize a partially activated HICS structure than to stand one up in the middle of a chaotic surge event. Over-activation has modest costs; under-activation has potentially catastrophic ones.

**Test your Hospital EOC before you need it.** The physical space, the information displays, the communications systems, and the documentation processes should all be exercised routinely. The first time a duty administrator tries to navigate the Hospital EOC should not be during a real event.

**Map the Four S's as a planning exercise, not just a checklist.** Work through each category and identify your facility's specific limiting factors. Where is your supply inventory most vulnerable? Which staff categories are hardest to surge? Which systems have the weakest downtime procedures? Knowing your vulnerabilities before an event allows targeted preparedness investment.

**Invest in coalition relationships outside of events.** The effectiveness of healthcare coalition coordination during a surge event is almost entirely determined by the quality of the relationships and protocols built during non-emergency periods. Joint exercises, regular communication, and cross-facility familiarity with key personnel are infrastructure — they take time to build and cannot be improvised.

**Understand your state's crisis standards framework before you need it.** Know who has authority to declare crisis standards, what the activation criteria are, what legal protections attach to the declaration, and what your facility's role is in the activation and oversight process. This is not a conversation to have for the first time during a surge event.

**Include LTC and behavioral health in your coalition planning.** Surge plans that address only acute care hospitals are incomplete. Long-term care facilities, psychiatric hospitals, and community behavioral health capacity are integral components of regional healthcare surge management.

## Chapter Notes

**On the Four S's and Systems Thinking:** The Four S's framework is a practical categorization tool, not a theoretical model. Its value is that it surfaces limiting factors in a form that generates actionable planning questions. Emergency managers working with hospitals should approach it as a structured conversation starter: "Show me your weakest link in each category, and let's design a mitigation strategy." The goal is not to eliminate all surge vulnerability — that is not achievable — but to ensure that the hospital's limiting factors are known, documented, and addressed in the mutual aid structure.

**On Altered Standards of Care and Communicating with the Public:** The public communication dimension of altered standards activation is genuinely difficult. "We may not be able to give everyone the care they would normally receive" is a statement that, if communicated poorly, generates panic, undermines trust in the healthcare system, and can trigger preemptive self-discharge of patients who should remain in care. State crisis standards frameworks should include communication protocols that are honest about conditions without triggering disproportionate behavioral responses. Emergency managers should review their state's communications guidance on this topic as part of pre-event preparedness.

**On the Coalition-Command Boundary:** The tension between the coalition's coordination role and hospitals' operational autonomy is a recurring theme in coalition management literature. Some coalition leaders attempt to exercise directive authority during events, which can create conflict and undermine the relationships that make coordination effective. Other coalition leaders are so deferential to individual hospital authority that they provide insufficient coordination to actually reduce system-level burden. The optimal position — which must be built through exercise, trust, and pre-established protocols — is directive enough to produce coordinated action but respectful enough of facility autonomy to maintain long-term coalition cohesion.

**For Clinically Trained Readers:** This chapter addresses surge management from an organizational and systems perspective. For clinical protocols applicable to specific surge conditions — chemical exposure management, mass trauma care, infectious disease surge protocols — consult Part IV of this text and applicable clinical guidance documents from relevant professional societies (ACEP, ACS-COT, IDSA, and others).
