# Chapter 14: Environmental Health Emergencies and Disaster Sanitation

## Chapter Overview

When a major disaster strikes, the visible casualties are only part of the public health story. Behind the immediate trauma and injuries lies a slower, quieter threat: the collapse of the environmental infrastructure that keeps populations healthy between emergencies. Safe drinking water, functional sewage systems, managed solid waste, controlled vectors, and breathable air are not luxuries. They are the engineered foundation of modern public health, and disasters tear that foundation apart in hours while the consequences unfold over weeks and months.

Environmental health emergencies are among the oldest documented causes of disaster mortality. The great disease epidemics that followed nineteenth-century famines, floods, and wars killed more people than the precipitating events themselves, overwhelmingly through waterborne illness, waste-contaminated food supplies, and vector-borne infections that flourished in disrupted sanitary conditions. The engineering and public health advances of the twentieth century dramatically reduced this toll in high-income countries, but those advances depend entirely on infrastructure that remains vulnerable to disruption. When hurricanes, earthquakes, floods, and industrial accidents sever water mains, collapse sewage systems, disrupt solid waste collection, and generate toxic air conditions, the disease risks that modern sanitation suppressed come rushing back.

This chapter addresses environmental health emergencies as a core competency domain for emergency managers and public health professionals. It covers the health consequences of infrastructure failure across water, sanitation, solid waste, vector control, and air quality domains; the operational methods for managing each during disaster response; the specific challenges of environmental health management in mass care shelter settings; and the equity dimensions that make environmental health failures consistently worse for lower-income, minority, rural, and marginalized communities. Understanding this domain requires integrating engineering, epidemiology, behavioral health, and community dynamics. It is, in that sense, a microcosm of emergency management itself.

## 14.1 Environmental Infrastructure and Disaster Vulnerability

Public health gains over the past century are sometimes attributed primarily to medical advances: antibiotics, vaccines, surgical technique, and intensive care. That attribution is only partly correct. The single most powerful contributor to declining infectious disease mortality in industrialized nations was the construction of clean water and sewage infrastructure — a transformation that preceded the antibiotic era by decades and that public health historians credit with reducing infectious disease mortality by roughly [PLACEHOLDER: percentage] before the first sulfa drug entered clinical use [CITATION].

This history matters for emergency management because it defines what is at stake when disaster damages infrastructure. The health protection that infrastructure provides is not visible in ordinary times, but its absence becomes apparent very quickly in emergencies.

**Water Supply Infrastructure.** Municipal water systems in the United States serve roughly [PLACEHOLDER: percentage] of the population and typically include source water intake (surface water or groundwater wells), treatment (coagulation, sedimentation, filtration, and disinfection), pressurized distribution networks, and consumer connections. This system depends on continuous electrical power, functioning pumps, intact pipes, available chemical supplies, and operational treatment plants. Each of these dependencies creates a disaster vulnerability. Power outages disable pumps. Earthquakes rupture mains. Floods inundate treatment plants. Hurricanes damage intake structures. Chemical supply chains are disrupted by transportation network failures. Any one of these can compromise water safety for thousands to millions of people.

Groundwater-dependent communities (primarily smaller systems and rural users) face different vulnerabilities. Private wells lack disinfection treatment and are directly susceptible to contamination from surface water intrusion during floods, from septic system failures when the water table rises, and from agricultural or industrial runoff mobilized by storm events. Well contamination is often not immediately apparent to users, particularly when the contaminating agent is a pathogen rather than a visible substance.

**Wastewater and Sewage Infrastructure.** Sewage collection and treatment infrastructure is hydraulically complex. Gravity-flow sewer systems transport waste to treatment plants, which require electrical power and continuous chemical inputs for primary, secondary, and sometimes tertiary treatment processes. Treatment plant flooding is a recurring catastrophe in major hurricanes. When treatment plants fail, raw or partially treated sewage often discharges to surface water — the same surface water that downstream communities may use for drinking or recreation.

Combined sewer systems — found in older urban areas — carry both stormwater and sanitary sewage in the same pipes. During heavy rainfall, these systems frequently overflow, discharging untreated sewage directly to receiving waters. This combined sewer overflow (CSO) problem is endemic in older American cities during ordinary storms; in major disaster flooding, the volumes involved can overwhelm any capacity margin.

Septic systems serving roughly [PLACEHOLDER: percentage] of U.S. households depend on soil absorption for final treatment. Saturated soils during flooding prevent absorption, causing surface ponding of partially treated effluent. This is particularly significant in rural and exurban communities where septic system density is high.

**Solid Waste Infrastructure.** Routine waste collection prevents the accumulation of organic waste that creates odor, pathogen exposure, and vector habitat. Disasters disrupt collection in two directions: they generate dramatically increased waste volumes (debris, damaged property, refrigerator contents, flood-contaminated materials, human waste from improvised latrines) while simultaneously disabling the trucks, fuel supplies, road access, and staffing that collection requires. The result is waste accumulation that creates conditions favorable to vermin infestation, fly breeding, surface water contamination, and fire hazard.

**Vector Control Infrastructure.** Arthropod and rodent populations are normally held at manageable levels by habitat management, chemical control, and natural predation. Disasters disrupt all of these: flooding creates extensive standing water breeding habitat for mosquitoes, debris accumulation provides rodent harborage and food sources, and displaced populations living in shelters or temporary housing create concentrated human targets for vector populations that have expanded beyond normal control.

**Air Quality.** Wildfires generate particulate matter and toxic combustion products at continental scale. Industrial accidents release chemicals that travel downwind. Mold colonizes water-damaged structures within [PLACEHOLDER: timeframe], producing spores that cause respiratory illness in occupants. Damaged or inoperable natural gas infrastructure creates explosion and carbon monoxide hazard. Each of these air quality threats generates distinct public health consequences and requires distinct protective responses.

## 14.2 Waterborne Disease Risk and Emergency Water Supply Operations

The relationship between contaminated water and infectious disease is one of the most thoroughly documented in public health. After disasters, the relevant pathogens fall into three broad categories: enteric bacteria, enteric viruses, and parasitic protozoa — each with distinct survival characteristics, infectious doses, and treatment vulnerabilities.

**Waterborne Pathogens in Disaster Settings.** *Vibrio cholerae* produces the most dramatic waterborne disease, with cholera capable of killing an untreated patient through dehydration within hours. Cholera is endemic in parts of South Asia, sub-Saharan Africa, and the Caribbean; disasters in these regions consistently trigger outbreaks. The 2010 Haiti earthquake and the cholera outbreak that followed — introduced through a contaminated water source connected to a UN peacekeeping camp — killed an estimated [PLACEHOLDER: number] people over subsequent years, a figure that exceeded the direct earthquake toll [CITATION]. In the United States, cholera is not endemic but imported cases occur, and disaster conditions in areas with poor sanitation baselines could theoretically support transmission.

*Escherichia coli* O157:H7 and other Shiga toxin-producing strains cause severe diarrheal illness and can produce hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS), particularly dangerous in children. These organisms are commonly associated with agricultural runoff and animal fecal contamination of water supplies, which is exactly what occurs when floodwaters sweep through farmland.

Hepatitis A virus is highly stable in water, resistant to many disinfection approaches, and infectious at low doses. Outbreaks following disasters in communities with low vaccination coverage can be substantial; hepatitis A vaccination is frequently recommended during disaster response when water safety is uncertain and sanitation compromised.

*Cryptosporidium parvum* and *Giardia lamblia* are protozoan parasites that form environmentally resistant cysts. Their principal public health significance in disaster water supply is that both are resistant to chlorine disinfection at standard concentrations. Safe water production for these organisms requires filtration (which removes cysts mechanically) or ultraviolet treatment, not just chemical disinfection. Communities relying on post-disaster chemical disinfection without filtration remain vulnerable to protozoan illness even when bacterial and viral risks are controlled.

**Emergency Water Supply Operations.** Emergency water supply begins with needs assessment: how many people, what their current access status is, what alternative sources are available, and how long the disruption is expected to last. The operational options stack in order of sustainability and logistical complexity.

*Bottled water distribution* is the fastest initial response but is logistically intensive, expensive, and generates significant solid waste. It is appropriate for short-duration gaps of a few days but is not scalable for large populations over extended periods.

*Water hauling and tank distribution* uses tanker trucks or trailers to deliver bulk water to community distribution points. Residents carry water away in personal containers. This approach covers larger populations than bottled water but requires functioning road access, refill infrastructure for the tankers, and sufficient distribution points. The container-carry model disadvantages people who cannot physically carry water — elderly individuals, people with disabilities, those without transportation to distribution points.

*Field water treatment* using portable systems is appropriate for sustained operations where local water sources (surface water, wells) are available but not safe. The technology options are well established: portable filtration units that produce safe water from surface sources, reverse osmosis units for high-salinity or chemically contaminated sources, ultraviolet treatment systems for pathogen inactivation after filtration, and chemical disinfection (chlorine or iodine) for situations where filtration is not available. Military and international humanitarian organizations have extensive experience deploying these systems; domestic civilian agencies vary considerably in their pre-positioned capacity.

*Water system flushing and restoration* — getting the municipal system back online — is the goal of all emergency water supply operations. This involves repairing physical damage, restoring pressure, and verifying water quality before lifting any boil-water notice. Regulatory requirements for testing and notice procedures vary by state and system size.

**Boil-Water Notices and Public Communication.** Boil-water notices are formal public health advisories instructing consumers to boil tap water before drinking, cooking, or brushing teeth. They are issued when water system pressure drops (creating backflow risk), when treatment failures occur, or when contamination is detected or suspected. Notifying the public requires reaching all affected consumers through multiple channels — and in disaster conditions, this is harder than it sounds. Residents may lack power for radio or television, may be displaced from their homes, or may speak languages other than English. Reaching all affected residents with a boil-water notice requires coordination across emergency alert systems, direct community outreach, multilingual communication, and coordination with shelters and other mass care facilities.

Water quality monitoring during emergency operations requires field testing capacity. Basic field kits test for turbidity, chlorine residual, and common coliform indicators. More complex chemical or heavy metal contamination requires laboratory analysis, which may be available through state laboratories, EPA assets, or contracted mobile lab units deployed for disaster response.

## 14.3 Sanitation in Disaster Settings

The word "sanitation" covers the full range of waste management practices that separate human excreta from human contact — an ancient public health challenge that remains incompletely solved in much of the world and that disasters reliably worsen even in high-income countries. In emergency management, sanitation problems cluster in two distinct settings: field conditions during immediate response and mass care shelter environments during short to medium-term population displacement.

**The Sanitation-Disease Chain.** Human feces contain the pathogens responsible for the most common infectious diseases in disaster settings. The route from feces to human infection follows predictable pathways: contaminated water, contaminated hands that prepare or handle food, contaminated food directly, flies that move between feces and food, and direct fecal-oral contact in crowded conditions. Each pathway can be interrupted; effective sanitation addresses multiple pathways simultaneously rather than relying on any single intervention.

Handwashing with soap is one of the highest-impact single interventions in disaster sanitation — inexpensive, rapidly deployable, and effective against the full spectrum of enteric pathogens. Yet handwashing requires accessible water, soap, and a facility for people to actually use. In the chaos of disaster response, providing handwashing stations is frequently deprioritized relative to more visible needs, with predictable consequences for diarrheal illness rates.

**Field Sanitation Standards.** In immediate post-disaster field conditions, when permanent sanitation infrastructure is unavailable, internationally recognized minimum standards provide operational guidance. The Sphere Handbook — a collaborative standards document developed by humanitarian organizations and widely used in international disaster response — specifies minimum toilet-to-population ratios, latrine construction requirements, and maximum distances between toilet facilities and living areas [CITATION]. The key metrics include:

The ratio of toilet facilities to population: Sphere specifies a maximum of [PLACEHOLDER: number] people per toilet in the initial emergency phase, with the goal of improving to [PLACEHOLDER: number] people per toilet as response matures. Female toilet facilities should be at least equal in number to male facilities, accounting for the additional time women require. Dedicated facilities for persons with disabilities and for menstrual hygiene management are required, not optional add-ons.

Distance requirements reflect the tension between convenience (closer facilities are more likely to be used) and safety (facilities near water sources or living areas create contamination risk). Sphere guidance specifies that toilet facilities should be no more than [PLACEHOLDER: distance] from sleeping areas and that a minimum separation distance from water sources must be maintained.

Latrine design for field conditions ranges from simple pit latrines (adequate for short-duration use in stable soil conditions) to chemical portable toilets (commonly used in U.S. domestic disaster response) to more sophisticated designs used in international humanitarian settings. The practical consideration for domestic emergency managers is that portable toilet supply chains are limited and are stressed in major regional disasters.

**Shelter Sanitation.** Mass care shelters present a concentrated version of all sanitation challenges: high population density, mixed health status, vulnerable subpopulations, shared facilities, and the psychological stress of displacement that can compromise normal hygiene behaviors. Shelter sanitation planning must account for:

Toilet facility numbers relative to shelter population, with adjustments for populations using incontinence products, children in diapers, and persons with mobility limitations who take longer per facility use. Many shelter facilities — particularly schools and public buildings used as improvised shelters — were not designed for the population densities that disaster sheltering involves, and their toilet-to-population ratios may fall below basic standards when the building is fully occupied.

Waste removal from toilets requires functioning sewage connections (not always available when water pressure is compromised) or a service contract for portable toilet pump-out. In extended sheltering, the logistics of human waste removal become a daily operational challenge requiring dedicated staffing and a clear contract or agency responsible for service.

Handwashing stations require a continuous supply of soap, which is consumed faster than most shelter managers expect. A [PLACEHOLDER: number]-person shelter requires [PLACEHOLDER: quantity] of hand soap per day during active use — a figure that should drive supply ordering rather than being addressed reactively when dispensers run empty.

Diaper changing and infant hygiene require dedicated changing stations separate from food preparation areas and accessible to the caregivers who will use them. The absence of these facilities creates behavioral workarounds (changing diapers on food preparation surfaces, on sleeping areas) that create contamination pathways.

Menstrual hygiene management is frequently overlooked in shelter planning. Women and girls of menstruating age require a private changing facility and a disposal mechanism for used hygiene products. Without these, women may restrict fluid intake to reduce bathroom trips, choose not to use shelters, or manage hygiene in ways that create contamination risk and profound dignity violations.

**Managing Diarrheal Disease Outbreaks in Shelters.** Despite best sanitation efforts, diarrheal disease outbreaks occur in shelters. When outbreak conditions are identified — typically through case clustering in time and space within the shelter population — response includes enhanced hand hygiene enforcement (staff rounding on handwashing stations), exclusion of symptomatic food handlers, intensified cleaning and disinfection of shared surfaces, isolation of severely ill individuals, and collection of stool specimens for pathogen identification. Outbreak investigation in a shelter population follows the same principles as any foodborne or waterborne outbreak investigation: identify the case definition, map the cases in time and space, identify common exposures, and determine the most likely source.

## 14.4 Solid Waste and Medical Waste Management in Disaster Settings

Solid waste management is a logistical, environmental, and public health challenge that grows dramatically in magnitude during disasters while the systems designed to address it are simultaneously disrupted. The volume of debris generated by a major hurricane, earthquake, tornado, or flood can exceed the normal annual waste volume of the affected jurisdiction by orders of magnitude.

**Disaster Debris Categories.** Disaster debris is not a single homogeneous waste stream; it includes multiple categories with different management requirements:

*Vegetative debris* — downed trees, shrubs, branches — is typically the largest volume category following wind events and can be chipped, mulched, or burned (where regulations permit) without complex handling requirements. However, vegetative debris management is labor-intensive, requires significant equipment and fuel, and competes with other response priorities for road access.

*Construction and demolition debris* — collapsed structures, building materials, interior contents — is heterogeneous and may contain asbestos, lead paint, and other legacy hazardous materials that require testing and controlled handling before disposal. Post-earthquake and post-tornado debris in particular may contain asbestos from older construction that was in acceptable condition before the event but is now fragmented and airborne.

*White goods and appliances* — refrigerators, washing machines, air conditioners — require separate handling because of refrigerants, oil, and mercury-containing components. Post-disaster, refrigerators present an additional challenge: they contain spoiled food that creates significant odor, vermin attraction, and public health hazard if not managed promptly.

*Electronic waste* — computers, televisions, communication equipment — contains heavy metals and other materials requiring specific recycling or disposal pathways, not routine landfilling.

*Hazardous materials* — household chemicals, pesticides, paint, motor oil, solvents — are displaced and mixed with general debris during flooding and structural damage. Post-flood debris is commonly contaminated with a broad spectrum of household chemicals at concentrations that are individually low but create occupational exposure risk for debris removal workers and environmental contamination risk at collection sites.

*Human remains and animal carcasses* — while human remains management is addressed separately in emergency planning, animal carcasses in flood debris are a significant public health and vector management issue. Decomposing carcasses attract scavengers and flies, and in flood conditions, carcasses from agricultural facilities (livestock operations) may be present in large numbers.

**Debris Management Operations.** Effective debris management requires pre-event planning that identifies disposal sites, contracts with debris contractors, and establishes regulatory authority for expedited permitting. During response, debris clearance follows a priority sequence: first, arterial roads needed for emergency access; then routes to critical facilities (hospitals, water treatment plants, utility infrastructure); then residential roads; then remaining areas. Debris staging sites — temporary accumulation points where debris is sorted before final disposal — are typically needed to manage the volume and logistics of large-scale debris operations.

**Medical Waste in Disaster Settings.** Healthcare facilities that continue operating during disasters generate medical waste — sharps, contaminated materials, biological specimens, pharmaceutical waste — that requires regulated management regardless of the circumstances. In practice, disasters disrupt the commercial medical waste collection services that facilities depend on, and temporary storage capacity may be quickly exhausted. Emergency plans should address medical waste management specifically, including the regulatory authorities that govern medical waste handling (typically state environmental agencies) and the emergency authorities or waivers that may be available when normal collection is disrupted.

Field medical operations generate a smaller but still significant medical waste stream. Mass casualty triage sites, shelter medical clinics, and field treatment units produce sharps, biological waste, and pharmaceutical materials requiring secure containment and appropriate disposal. The human behavior challenge is that under disaster conditions, informal disposal shortcuts (burying sharps, burning waste, dumping in general debris) are tempting and create ongoing hazards. Training, pre-positioned supplies (sharps containers, biohazard bags), and clear accountability for field medical waste are necessary preventive measures.

## 14.5 Vector Control in Post-Disaster Environments

The term "vector" in public health refers to an organism — typically an arthropod or rodent — that transmits pathogens from one host to another. Disasters consistently worsen the conditions that allow vector populations to grow, concentrate human targets in ways that increase exposure, and disrupt the control measures that normally keep vector populations at manageable levels.

**Mosquito-Borne Risks Post-Disaster.** Flooding creates extensive standing water breeding habitat for mosquitoes, and post-flood mosquito population explosions are well documented following major events. The public health significance depends on which mosquito species are present and what pathogens they carry. In the United States, the relevant concerns include:

*West Nile Virus*, transmitted primarily by *Culex* species mosquitoes, is endemic across much of the continental United States. Post-flood mosquito surges amplify West Nile transmission risk during the warm months when *Culex* are active. West Nile causes a range of illness from mild febrile disease to severe neuroinvasive illness (encephalitis, meningitis); elderly individuals and immunocompromised persons are at highest risk for severe outcomes.

*Eastern equine encephalitis (EEE)*, transmitted by *Culiseta melanura* and bridge vectors, causes severe neurological disease with high case-fatality rates. EEE is less common than West Nile but significantly more lethal in cases that progress to encephalitis.

*Aedes aegypti* and *Aedes albopictus*, the primary vectors for dengue, Zika, and chikungunya, breed in small containers of standing water rather than large water bodies. Their breeding habitat is ubiquitous in flood debris — clogged gutters, discarded tires, bottle caps, tarps — and their container-breeding biology makes them particularly difficult to control through conventional broad-area larval source reduction.

Post-disaster mosquito control operations use surveillance data to guide response: larval surveys identify breeding sites for source reduction; adult trap data identifies population levels and species distribution; human case data identifies transmission hot spots. Control methods include larviciding (applying biological or chemical larvicides to standing water), adulticiding (aerial or ground-based adult mosquito spraying), and source reduction (draining, covering, or removing standing water). Each method has specific applications, limitations, and community communication requirements.

**Rodent Control Post-Disaster.** Flood displacement drives rodents — primarily rats and mice — into new areas in search of food and harborage. Debris accumulation provides both. Post-flood areas commonly experience significant rodent population increases, which create risks including:

Leptospirosis, transmitted through contact with water or soil contaminated with urine from infected rodents. Post-flood leptospirosis transmission is well documented in tropical settings and occurs in the United States, particularly in areas with high rat populations. Occupational exposure risk is significant for flood cleanup workers who have skin contact with contaminated water and debris.

Hantavirus pulmonary syndrome, caused by hantavirus species carried by deer mice and other wild rodents, transmitted through inhalation of dried rodent urine, droppings, or nesting material. Disturbing rodent-contaminated debris and materials in buildings — a common activity during disaster cleanup — is the classic exposure mechanism.

Rodent control post-disaster combines exclusion (sealing entry points to structures), food source elimination (securing garbage, removing debris that provides food), trapping, and baiting. Public education about safe cleanup practices — wearing N95 respirators when disturbing materials that may contain rodent droppings, cleaning contaminated surfaces with disinfectant before sweeping — is essential for reducing hantavirus and leptospirosis exposure during the recovery phase.

**Integrated Pest Management in Mass Care Settings.** Shelters, temporary housing sites, and disaster recovery centers that house displaced populations for extended periods require ongoing pest management. The principles of integrated pest management (IPM) — emphasizing prevention, monitoring, and targeted intervention over broad-spectrum pesticide application — are particularly appropriate in settings where vulnerable populations (including children, pregnant women, and individuals with chemical sensitivities) may have continuous exposure.

Pesticide application inside occupied shelters requires careful selection of products and application timing to minimize human exposure. All pesticide applications in or near shelters should be conducted by licensed applicators, with occupants informed in advance and excluded from treated areas during and immediately after application. Documentation of all pesticide applications, products used, and application locations is required both for safety monitoring and for regulatory compliance.

## 14.6 Air Quality Emergencies and Indoor Environmental Hazards

Air quality emergencies associated with disasters fall into three broad categories: outdoor air contamination from wildfire smoke and industrial releases; indoor air contamination from mold and combustion products; and occupational air quality hazards for disaster workers. Each requires distinct risk communication and protective action strategies.

**Wildfire Smoke.** Wildfire smoke is a complex mixture of gases and particulate matter generated by combustion of vegetation and — in the wildland-urban interface — structural materials including plastics, treated lumber, asbestos-containing materials, paints, and other building components. The public health impact of wildfire smoke has grown substantially with the expansion of wildfire size, frequency, and duration driven by drought conditions and land management changes in Western states.

Fine particulate matter (PM2.5) is the primary driver of wildfire smoke health effects. Particles in the PM2.5 size range penetrate deep into the respiratory tract, reach the alveoli, and enter systemic circulation. Short-term exposure exacerbates asthma and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), triggers cardiovascular events in susceptible individuals, and causes upper and lower respiratory symptoms in healthy people. Prolonged exposure across smoke seasons may produce long-term cardiovascular and respiratory effects.

Protective actions for wildfire smoke include staying indoors with windows and doors closed (effective when air infiltration is low), using portable air cleaners with HEPA filtration, wearing N95 or higher-rated respirators when outdoor exposure is unavoidable, and evacuating areas with sustained extreme smoke concentrations. Air Quality Index (AQI) values above [PLACEHOLDER: number] for PM2.5 indicate conditions at which sensitive groups (children, elderly, those with respiratory or cardiovascular disease) should avoid outdoor exposure entirely.

The wildfire smoke risk communication challenge is that unlike point-source air pollution events with clear boundaries, wildfire smoke can travel hundreds to thousands of miles from the fire perimeter, affecting populations who feel no sense of emergency and who may dismiss protective action recommendations as overcautious. Communication strategies that tie AQI values to specific behavioral recommendations — calibrated to the population's level of risk — are more effective than general advisories.

**Indoor Mold.** Water intrusion from flooding, roof damage, or plumbing failures creates conditions for mold colonization of building materials. Mold can begin growing on wet materials within [PLACEHOLDER: timeframe] under warm conditions, and once established, colonies generate spores continuously. The health effects of indoor mold exposure include allergic rhinitis, asthma exacerbation, hypersensitivity pneumonitis (in occupationally exposed individuals), and — in immunocompromised persons — invasive fungal infections from species including *Aspergillus* and *Mucor*.

The public health guidance on mold remediation is clear: effective remediation requires physical removal of mold-contaminated porous materials (drywall, insulation, carpet, ceiling tiles), not surface treatment with bleach or other disinfectants. Bleach can kill surface mold but does not penetrate into the material, and mold returns quickly if the material remains in place. Workers conducting mold remediation require appropriate respiratory protection, typically N95 respirators for small-scale work and supplied air or powered air-purifying respirators (PAPRs) for extensive remediation.

Re-occupancy of flood-damaged structures before adequate mold remediation is complete exposes returning residents to ongoing mold inhalation. The pressure on displaced families to return home — even to damaged structures — is substantial, creating a conflict between immediate housing needs and long-term respiratory health. Public health messaging that clearly explains the health risks of premature reoccupancy and connects displaced individuals to remediation resources, rental assistance, and alternative housing is essential.

**Carbon Monoxide.** Generator use during power outages is one of the most predictable causes of disaster-associated death in the United States, consistently killing [PLACEHOLDER: number] people after major hurricanes and ice storms. Gasoline-powered generators, grills, and camp stoves produce carbon monoxide (CO) in concentrations that are immediately lethal in enclosed or partially enclosed spaces. Despite decades of public education, CO poisoning from improper generator use recurs predictably after every major power outage event.

Effective prevention requires behavioral change communication that goes beyond general awareness: specific placement instructions (generators must be operated at least [PLACEHOLDER: distance] from any door, window, or vent), demonstration that CO has no smell and provides no warning before incapacitation, and explanation that outdoor placement is not always adequate if wind carries exhaust toward building openings. Carbon monoxide detector distribution and installation during the pre-event or early post-event period reduces fatalities in communities that actively pursue this intervention.

**Industrial and Hazardous Material Air Releases.** Disasters that affect industrial facilities — refineries, chemical plants, wastewater treatment plants, agricultural facilities — may trigger uncontrolled releases of hazardous materials to the atmosphere. The affected population may extend downwind of the facility for miles, and the released materials may include substances for which standard personal protective equipment provides inadequate protection.

Emergency response to industrial air releases follows the shelter-in-place or evacuation decision framework: shelter-in-place is protective for many airborne releases when structures provide meaningful infiltration reduction, but is inappropriate for releases that create explosion risk or for contaminants that penetrate building envelopes effectively. Evacuation removes people from the release area but creates its own risks (traffic accidents, medical emergencies during transport, exposure during evacuation route travel) and is only effective when the evacuation zone and routes are known in advance of the release. The actual protective action decision requires knowledge of the specific agent released, atmospheric conditions, and structure types in the affected area — information that is often incomplete in the early hours of an industrial emergency.

## 14.7 Equity in Environmental Health Emergencies

The distribution of environmental health burdens in disasters follows the same patterns as baseline environmental inequity: lower-income communities, communities of color, and rural communities experience worse outcomes for reasons that are structural rather than individual.

**Pre-Existing Environmental Burdens.** Environmental justice research documents that lower-income communities and communities of color are disproportionately located near industrial facilities, hazardous waste sites, and areas with poorer baseline air and water quality [CITATION]. These pre-existing burdens mean that when disaster adds additional environmental stressors, the cumulative exposure for these communities exceeds that of higher-income communities facing the same disaster event. A community already experiencing elevated PM2.5 from industrial sources that then absorbs wildfire smoke is at greater cumulative respiratory risk than a community starting from a clean air baseline.

Housing quality is a central determinant of environmental health vulnerability. Older housing stock — disproportionately found in lower-income and rural communities — is more likely to contain lead paint, asbestos, and inadequate weatherization. When this housing is damaged by flood or wind, hazardous materials are released, and inadequate structural integrity means more rapid and extensive water intrusion and mold colonization.

**Infrastructure Investment Disparities.** Water and sewer infrastructure serving lower-income communities, communities of color, and rural areas is on average older, more fragile, and less well-maintained than infrastructure serving affluent communities [CITATION]. This infrastructure gap means that disaster events that would create manageable disruptions in well-maintained systems may cause extended and severe failures in older, more fragile systems. The Flint, Michigan water crisis — while not a natural disaster in origin — illustrates how infrastructure failure in a lower-income community of color can produce severe and lasting public health consequences that would have triggered faster response in a higher-income community.

Rural communities face particular vulnerabilities: many rely on private wells that lack treatment and monitoring; septic systems are prevalent; solid waste collection may be infrequent or absent; and the distance from waste disposal facilities means debris management is logistically and economically difficult. Rural vector control programs may be minimal or nonexistent compared to urban counterparts.

**Shelter Environmental Conditions.** The quality of mass care shelter environments is not uniform. Improvised shelters in inadequate facilities — gymnasiums without functioning HVAC, buildings with mold histories, structures in flood-prone locations — may expose displaced persons to environmental health hazards within the shelter itself. Selection of shelter sites requires assessment of environmental conditions including building water damage history, proximity to hazardous facilities, HVAC functionality, and bathroom-to-population ratio adequacy.

Displaced individuals with the least resources — those who cannot afford hotels, those without vehicles to reach better-resourced shelters, those without social networks that enable alternative housing arrangements — are most likely to remain in lower-quality shelter environments for the longest periods. This means the population bearing the greatest environmental health burden from shelter conditions is the population already facing the greatest overall disaster vulnerability.

**Language, Literacy, and Risk Communication.** Environmental health risk communication — boil-water notices, air quality advisories, mold remediation guidance, vector control warnings, CO generator safety — depends on reaching all affected individuals with comprehensible, actionable information. Communities with high proportions of limited English proficiency (LEP) residents, communities with lower average literacy levels, and communities with limited access to mainstream media and alert systems face structural barriers to receiving and acting on this information.

The consequence is predictable: illness rates from environmentally preventable causes (contaminated water consumption, generator CO poisoning, premature re-occupancy of moldy structures) are higher in communities where risk communication has not effectively reached residents. This is not a failure of individual behavior but of systems-level communication planning that failed to account for the actual communication landscape of the affected community.

**Rebuilding and Long-Term Environmental Exposure.** The recovery period introduces additional environmental equity concerns. Lower-income homeowners may lack the financial resources for adequate mold remediation, instead returning to partially remediated homes and experiencing ongoing respiratory exposure. Temporary housing sites — FEMA trailer parks and similar arrangements from past disasters — have produced their own environmental health controversies, including documented elevated formaldehyde levels in temporary housing units following Hurricane Katrina [CITATION]. Environmental monitoring of temporary housing must be a standard component of recovery operations, with results transparently communicated and acted upon.

## Applied Vignette: The Aftermath of Hurricane Serena — Environmental Health Response in Coastal Magnolia County

Hurricane Serena made landfall as a Category 4 storm, driving a significant storm surge across Magnolia County's coastal communities and producing [PLACEHOLDER: inches] of rainfall over 36 hours. Three days after landfall, Magnolia County's emergency management team is coordinating what has become a complex, multi-front environmental health response.

**The Water Situation.** The county's main water treatment plant flooded and shut down automatically when surge inundated its electrical controls. Two of five water storage tanks serving the county lost pressure integrity during the storm. Three communities on the eastern end of the county are on private wells. The county health department has issued a county-wide boil-water notice, but three days in, compliance monitoring at water distribution points shows that significant numbers of residents are unclear on the notice's scope or requirements. A survey of [PLACEHOLDER: number] households in the Rosario Crossing neighborhood — predominantly Vietnamese and Spanish-speaking — found that [PLACEHOLDER: percentage] had not received the boil-water notice in a language they could read.

Emergency water distribution is operating from four sites — two community centers, a fire station, and a church that offered its parking lot. Bottled water is adequate for today but the supply forecast for day 5 shows a gap. A National Guard water tanker unit is being requested but availability is uncertain. The county's emergency manager is wrestling with an allocation decision: if supply falls short, which distribution sites get priority?

**The Shelter Sanitation Crisis.** Lakeside High School, serving as the largest shelter with [PLACEHOLDER: number] occupants, is running into sanitation problems. The school's sewage connection is intact, but the girls' restrooms on the east wing are not functioning due to a pressure issue, reducing available toilet capacity by roughly a third. Portable toilets have been requested but delivery is delayed — every vendor in the region is backlogged. The shelter's stock of hand soap ran out on day 2; volunteers have been using personal supplies. A cluster of six diarrheal illness cases in the past 24 hours has been reported to the shelter medical station.

The shelter manager, a Red Cross volunteer named Marcus, has identified the handwashing gap and is requesting emergency supply delivery, but does not know who in the county system is responsible for that procurement. The county health department and emergency management office have a handwashing supply line in the emergency purchase authority, but the connection between Marcus's request and that authority is not established.

**The Debris and Vector Situation.** Aerial survey of the barrier island communities shows extensive debris accumulation including livestock carcasses from two small farms that were inundated. Standing water from surge and rainfall remains extensive across low-lying areas. The county's mosquito control district is activated and conducting larval surveys but has limited adulticiding capacity — one truck, and fuel supply is uncertain. A request to the state for aerial mosquito control support is in the queue but the state has received [PLACEHOLDER: number] similar requests from counties across the region.

The debris contractor pre-established under the county's emergency debris management plan has mobilized but is working on arterial clearance first. Residential debris, including the animal carcasses, will not be reached for several more days. Meanwhile, residents returning to check on their homes are moving carcass debris informally, creating both pathogen exposure and vector attraction problems.

**After-Action Findings.** When the Magnolia County after-action review examined the environmental health response three months after Serena, three findings drove major plan revisions:

*Finding 1: Language access for environmental health notices was an afterthought.* The boil-water notice communication plan relied on English-language media and the county's standard alert system, which was not enrolled by many LEP households. The plan was revised to establish pre-event agreements with Vietnamese and Spanish-language community organizations, bilingual volunteers, and multilingual text alert capability as standard components of boil-water notice activation.

*Finding 2: Shelter supply accountability had a gap.* Basic consumable supplies — soap, toilet paper, diapers — had no designated ownership in the shelter operations structure. The revised shelter operations annex assigns a specific supply officer position with procurement authority and a restocking checklist triggered by daily population counts.

*Finding 3: The debris contractor scope did not include animal carcasses.* The pre-established debris contract covered construction debris and vegetative material but excluded animal carcasses, which fell into a regulatory and contractual gap. The revised plan establishes a separate animal carcass disposal protocol coordinated with the state department of agriculture and the county's solid waste management authority.

## Key Terms

**Boil-Water Notice:** A public health advisory instructing consumers to boil water before consumption when safety of the water supply cannot be confirmed.

**Combined Sewer Overflow (CSO):** Discharge of untreated or partially treated sewage and stormwater from combined sewer systems during periods of high precipitation.

**Cryptosporidium:** A chlorine-resistant protozoan parasite transmitted through contaminated water; a significant concern in disaster water supply situations where filtration may not be available.

**Debris Management Plan:** A pre-disaster planning document establishing the legal authority, site locations, contractor relationships, and operational procedures for managing disaster-generated debris.

**Environmental Justice:** The principle that all communities, regardless of race, income, or national origin, should be protected equally from environmental hazards and have equal access to environmental benefits.

**Integrated Pest Management (IPM):** An approach to pest control that emphasizes prevention, monitoring, and targeted intervention over routine broad-spectrum pesticide application.

**Leptospirosis:** A bacterial infection transmitted through contact with water or soil contaminated with urine from infected rodents or other animals; associated with post-flood cleanup exposure.

**Medical Waste:** Waste generated by healthcare activities that may contain infectious agents, sharps, or pharmaceutical materials requiring specific containment and disposal.

**PM2.5:** Fine particulate matter with an aerodynamic diameter of 2.5 micrometers or less; the primary health-relevant component of wildfire smoke and many industrial air pollutants.

**Sanitation:** The range of practices and infrastructure that separate human excreta from human contact, including latrines, sewage systems, and waste disposal.

**Source Reduction:** In vector control, the elimination of standing water or other breeding habitats to reduce mosquito populations before adulticide application is needed.

**Sphere Handbook:** A standards document developed by the humanitarian community specifying minimum standards for disaster response across multiple sectors including water, sanitation, food, and shelter.

**Vector:** An organism, typically an arthropod or rodent, that transmits pathogens from one host to another.

**West Nile Virus:** A mosquito-borne flavivirus that is the most common cause of arboviral disease in the continental United States, with potential for severe neuroinvasive illness in elderly and immunocompromised individuals.

## Discussion Questions

1. The Sphere Handbook provides minimum standards for toilet-to-population ratios in disaster settings, but these standards were developed primarily in the context of international humanitarian response. What challenges arise in applying these standards to domestic U.S. emergency shelter operations, and how should emergency managers address them?

2. A county health officer is weighing the decision to issue a countywide boil-water notice after a major hurricane damages portions of the water system. Some areas may have unaffected service; a county-wide notice may cause residents to unnecessarily seek bottled water and may create public alarm. What factors should guide this decision, and how should the communication strategy account for the likelihood that the notice will not reach all residents?

3. Post-disaster mold remediation guidance consistently recommends physical removal of contaminated materials rather than surface disinfection, but many homeowners returning to flood-damaged structures apply bleach and repaint damaged walls rather than conducting full remediation. What are the public health and behavioral factors that drive this pattern, and what interventions could shift behavior in a more protective direction?

4. Environmental justice research demonstrates that lower-income communities and communities of color bear disproportionate environmental health burdens both in normal times and in disasters. As an emergency manager, what concrete steps can you take — before, during, and after a disaster — to reduce rather than replicate these inequities through your environmental health response?

5. Generator-associated carbon monoxide poisoning kills a predictable number of people after every major U.S. hurricane and ice storm, despite decades of public education. What does this persistent mortality pattern suggest about the limits of information-based behavior change communication, and what structural or regulatory interventions might be more effective?

## Practitioner Takeaways

Environmental health failures in disaster settings are almost always predictable, and predictable failures can be planned for. The practitioner who understands the basic disease ecology of waterborne illness, the infrastructure dependencies of water and sewer systems, and the behavioral determinants of hygiene compliance is equipped to design response operations that actually reduce illness — not just document the response effort.

The equity dimension of environmental health emergency response requires moving beyond awareness to pre-event structural change: pre-enrolled bilingual communication pathways, shelter sites assessed for environmental quality before they are needed, and debris contracts that explicitly address the full range of waste streams that disasters produce. These preparations are not complex, but they require an organizational culture that takes equity planning as seriously as logistics planning.

The air quality domain is growing in importance as wildfire smoke events expand geographically and seasonally. Emergency managers in regions not previously accustomed to wildfire smoke as a hazard should now be developing AQI-triggered public protective action protocols, pre-positioned air cleaner distribution systems for high-vulnerability households, and coordination pathways with state and EPA air quality monitoring networks.

Finally, carbon monoxide from generator misuse is an almost entirely preventable cause of disaster death. The recurrence of CO fatalities after every major power outage event reflects inadequate attention to behavior change at the community level, not insufficient general awareness. Pre-staging CO detectors for distribution, direct demonstration of proper generator placement, and targeted outreach to communities where generator ownership is common but CO awareness is low would reduce this preventable toll. This is a solvable problem, and the fact that it remains unsolved is itself an equity issue — CO deaths are more common in communities with less economic resilience, older housing stock with less ventilation, and less effective connection to protective information systems.

## Chapter Notes

14.1 The quantitative attribution of mortality decline to water and sanitation infrastructure is contested in some historical epidemiology literature, but the broad finding — that sanitation investment preceded and exceeded the mortality impact of early medical interventions — is consistent across major analyses including the Cutler-Miller work cited here. The emergency management practitioner's takeaway is the same regardless of the precise attribution: infrastructure is the foundation of health protection, and its disruption is a genuine public health emergency requiring urgent response.

14.2 The Haiti cholera epidemic is one of the most thoroughly studied post-disaster disease events in recent history and illustrates both the catastrophic potential of waterborne disease transmission in vulnerable populations and the complexity of outbreak attribution when multiple institutional actors are involved. The full epidemiological and policy history is relevant reading for practitioners working in international disaster response contexts.

14.3 The Sphere Handbook minimum standards are worth reading in full by anyone responsible for mass care shelter operations. The standards are periodically revised and the current edition should always be consulted. Domestic application of Sphere standards is not mandated by U.S. regulatory authorities but represents a defensible, internationally recognized baseline that exceeds many improvised shelter operations.

14.4 FEMA's Debris Management Guide provides the primary federal framework for domestic debris operations, including cost-sharing eligibility requirements under the Stafford Act that significantly influence how jurisdictions structure their debris management programs. Understanding the eligibility rules is important for budget planning and for ensuring that debris management activities are documented in ways that support reimbursement claims.

14.5 Integrated pest management guidance for disaster settings has been developed by CDC and by state vector control programs. The key tension in post-disaster mosquito control is between the urgency of population protection and the legitimate community concerns about aerial adulticiding, particularly in communities with prior experience of health impacts from chemical exposures. Pre-event community engagement and transparent communication about control methods, timing, and monitoring are investments that reduce resistance to necessary control operations when disasters occur.

14.6 The CDC Air Quality Index is publicly available at airnow.gov and provides real-time AQI values and health guidance calibrated to PM2.5 and ozone concentrations. Emergency managers should be familiar with the AQI system and should incorporate AQI monitoring into their wildfire smoke response protocols.

14.7 The environmental justice literature is extensive. Robert Bullard's work on environmental racism in the American South provides foundational historical context. More recent research by Dorceta Taylor, Manuel Pastor, and others documents the persistence of environmental health disparities and the policy mechanisms that produce them. Emergency managers are not typically trained in environmental justice as an academic discipline, but a working familiarity with its core findings is necessary for designing response operations that do not simply replicate and amplify pre-existing inequities.
