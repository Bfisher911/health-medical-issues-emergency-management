# Chapter 16: Shelter Medicine and Congregate Care Settings

---

## Chapter Overview

When a disaster displaces people from their homes, the emergency shelter becomes a temporary community. It feeds, houses, and shelters people who have nowhere else to go — and it does so at precisely the moment those people are most physically and psychologically vulnerable. A shelter that opens without a functioning health operation is not a safe refuge. It is a vector for disease, a stressor for chronic illness, and a crisis in slow motion for people whose medications, medical equipment, or behavioral health supports did not survive the disaster.

Shelter medicine is a distinct clinical and operational discipline. Its practitioners must deliver primary care in improvised settings, manage infection control in buildings not designed for it, support people with complex chronic conditions who may have lost all of their medications and supplies, address acute mental health crises in crowded and chaotic environments, and do all of this while navigating the legal and ethical complexity of providing care to populations who may not have consented to be there. It requires clinical skill, public health instinct, operational thinking, and an understanding of equity dynamics that shape who seeks shelter, who avoids it, and who is most harmed by gaps in shelter health operations.

This chapter examines the full scope of shelter health operations: shelter typing and the spectrum from general population to medical needs shelters; health screening and the entry process; infection control in congregate settings; chronic disease management and medication continuity; mental health in shelter environments; care for populations with access and functional needs; documentation and confidentiality; and the transition process as shelters demobilize. Throughout, the chapter emphasizes that shelter health equity is not an add-on. The populations most likely to be displaced — and least likely to return home quickly — are the same populations whose pre-existing conditions, medication complexity, documentation gaps, and institutional distrust create the most demanding clinical challenges.

## 16.1 The Shelter Spectrum: Typing, Triggering, and the Health Function

Not all emergency shelters are the same, and the distinctions between shelter types have direct implications for health operations staffing, supply, and scope.

**General population shelters** are the baseline. They are activated to house individuals and families displaced by disaster who are ambulatory, do not require continuous medical monitoring, and can generally manage their own activities of daily living. The American Red Cross is the primary nongovernmental organization operating general population shelters under agreements with state and local emergency management agencies, and the organization maintains shelter standards covering space per occupant, cot and supply ratios, hygiene facilities, and food service. Health operations in a general population shelter typically consist of a health suite staffed by nurses or emergency medical technicians capable of first aid, medication management support, basic triage, and referral. Physicians and advanced practice clinicians are not always present and may rotate through or be available on call.

**Access and functional needs (AFN) shelters**, sometimes called special needs shelters or functional needs support services shelters, are designed for individuals who require more support than a general population shelter can provide but do not require inpatient-level medical care. This population includes people who use wheelchairs or other mobility aids, people who use home medical equipment such as nebulizers, CPAP machines, or home oxygen concentrators, people with cognitive or developmental disabilities who require behavioral support or familiar routines, people with serious mental illness who require medication monitoring, people who are deaf or hard of hearing, people who are blind or have low vision, and elderly individuals with significant functional limitations. AFN shelters require accessible physical facilities, higher staff-to-resident ratios, and clinical staff capable of managing a broader range of conditions. Registration systems for AFN shelters ideally capture functional needs during pre-disaster planning so that individuals can be matched to appropriate shelters before or immediately after displacement.

**Medical needs shelters**, sometimes called shelter medical units or disaster medical stations, serve individuals who require a higher level of clinical monitoring than an AFN shelter can provide but who do not require hospitalization. This includes post-surgical patients who have been discharged early, dialysis patients who are not currently in acute failure, ventilator-dependent individuals with stable settings, and individuals with wound care needs. Medical needs shelters require licensed clinical staff present around the clock, medical equipment supplies, access to specialty consultation, and relationships with nearby hospital facilities for rapid transfer when residents deteriorate.

**The trigger question** — meaning which shelter type a person needs — must be answered at the point of entry and re-evaluated continuously. A person who arrives at a general population shelter appearing ambulatory and stable may disclose at intake that they use a home oxygen concentrator. That disclosure triggers reassessment and potentially transfer to an AFN or medical needs shelter. A person who arrives in mental health crisis may initially be appropriate for emergency behavioral health services, then stabilize into the general shelter population, and then decompensate again three days later when the noise and disorientation of shelter life overwhelm their coping capacity. Shelter health operations must build for dynamic re-triage, not one-time classification at the door.

The **pet question** deserves explicit mention here because it has demonstrated shelter occupancy consequences. Research following Hurricane Katrina documented that significant numbers of people refused to evacuate or refused to enter shelters because they would not leave their pets [CITATION]. This created a public health problem: people who stayed in place with their animals were exposed to flood hazards that the shelter would have prevented. Many jurisdictions now operate pet-friendly shelters or co-located animal sheltering facilities. These settings introduce zoonotic disease considerations, requiring hand hygiene protocols, separation of animal care areas from food preparation and sleeping areas, and policies about what documentation of animal vaccination is required.

## 16.2 Shelter Health Screening and the Entry Process

The health screening process at shelter entry serves four functions that are worth naming separately because they pull in different directions operationally.

The first function is **placement triage** — identifying which shelter tier is appropriate for the arriving individual. This requires a brief structured intake that asks about functional limitations, home medical equipment, regular medications, and current medical conditions. The goal is not a comprehensive medical history. It is a rapid sort.

The second function is **communicable disease identification** — identifying individuals who may have active communicable diseases that create transmission risk in a congregate setting. A person presenting with fever and cough requires assessment before being placed in a general population sleeping area. A person with visible skin lesions or rash requires evaluation before entering the common areas. This function creates tension with the first because it can feel to arriving shelter residents like a barrier rather than a service, and managing that tension requires training and communication.

The third function is **medication reconciliation** — identifying people who take regular medications and assessing whether they have adequate supplies on hand. Disasters routinely separate people from their medications. A person with insulin-dependent diabetes who evacuated without their insulin is a time-sensitive clinical problem. A person with hypertension who is three days from their last dose of antihypertensive medication is a less immediate but still important problem. Identifying medication needs at entry allows the health team to begin securing replacement supplies early, before the clinical consequences arrive.

The fourth function is **establishing a health record** — creating a shelter health record that can follow the individual through the shelter stay and, ideally, be shared with follow-up care providers upon shelter closure. Shelters have historically been poor at generating health records, which means that clinically significant findings and interventions during shelter stays often disappear from the continuity of care. This is a solvable problem with modest investment in documentation systems.

The entry process must be designed to not deter access. Arriving at a shelter already represents a failure of other protective systems — the home is uninhabitable, the social network has been overwhelmed, the normal routine is destroyed. Adding a burdensome intake experience to that entry moment pushes people away from a resource they need. Intake should be conducted by trained personnel with a trauma-informed approach, in a private or semi-private space away from the general arrival flow, in the individual's primary language, and without requiring documentation that many displaced persons will not have. Requiring identification documents as a condition of shelter entry is a policy that has been adopted in some jurisdictions and should be examined critically: it excludes undocumented immigrants, people experiencing homelessness who have no fixed address, and anyone whose documents were lost or destroyed in the disaster itself.

## 16.3 Infection Control in Congregate Settings

The congregate shelter environment creates transmission conditions that are essentially ideal for several classes of pathogens. Understanding why helps clarify which control measures work and which do not.

**Respiratory pathogens** spread when people breathe shared air in enclosed spaces. A shelter sleeping area with [QUANTITY] people per [QUANTITY] square feet, operating 24 hours a day with limited ventilation, is a transmission amplifier. Influenza, rhinovirus, respiratory syncytial virus (RSV), COVID-19, and in longer shelter stays, tuberculosis, are all concerns. Control measures include maximizing ventilation and outdoor air exchange, cohorting individuals with respiratory symptoms away from the general sleeping area, providing surgical masks to individuals with cough, and ensuring hand hygiene supplies (soap, water, alcohol-based hand rub) are positioned throughout the shelter. Routine respiratory illness surveillance — tracking the daily count of individuals with fever and respiratory symptoms — provides early warning of a developing outbreak.

**Enteric pathogens** — primarily norovirus, salmonella, and campylobacter — spread via fecal-oral routes. In shelters, the primary transmission mechanisms are inadequate hand hygiene after using toilets, contaminated food handling, and shared surfaces in food service areas. Norovirus is a particular challenge because it is highly contagious at very low inocula, it survives on surfaces for extended periods, and it spreads rapidly through shared toilet facilities. A single case of norovirus in a shelter can seed an outbreak within [TIMEFRAME] if hand hygiene and surface disinfection are not in place. The ratio of toilet facilities to shelter population is a fundamental infection control metric. The commonly referenced standard is [QUANTITY] toilets per [QUANTITY] persons for general populations; more conservative ratios are recommended for populations with higher rates of diarrheal illness, elderly individuals, or children under five.

**Skin and soft tissue infections** — primarily impetigo, tinea (ringworm), and scabies — spread through direct contact and shared surfaces including cots, blankets, and clothing. Scabies deserves particular attention because the incubation period can be [TIMEFRAME] for a primary infestation, meaning a shelter can operate for weeks before an outbreak becomes apparent. Once scabies is identified in a shelter population, treatment requires treating all close contacts, laundering all bedding and clothing at high temperatures, and environmental decontamination of the sleeping area. Shelter health teams should include skin assessment in ongoing health rounds and should maintain a low threshold for reporting clusters of pruritic rash.

**Wound infections** are common in the immediate post-disaster period. People who evacuated through flood water, debris fields, or fire damage frequently present with lacerations, puncture wounds, and abrasions that have been inadequately cleaned or inadequately dressed. In the shelter setting, wound infections can progress rapidly in immunocompromised individuals, diabetics, and elderly persons. The shelter health team should maintain wound care supplies and the capacity for wound assessment and basic wound care as a standard service.

**Respiratory illness surveillance and outbreak response** should be standing protocols, not ad hoc responses. Surveillance requires daily counts of symptomatic individuals and clear thresholds for escalating to public health authorities. Outbreak response for most respiratory pathogens involves cohorting symptomatic cases, intensifying hand hygiene and surface cleaning, improving ventilation, and notifying the local or state health department. For pathogens of higher consequence — such as a novel respiratory pathogen or a confirmed influenza outbreak in a shelter with elderly residents — response may involve shelter-wide antiviral prophylaxis, accelerated transfer to higher-level care for vulnerable individuals, and coordination with public health regarding potential shelter closure.

## 16.4 Chronic Disease Management and Medication Continuity

Chronic disease is not suspended by disaster. A person with heart failure continues to require diuretics. A person with epilepsy continues to require anticonvulsants. A person with schizophrenia continues to require antipsychotic medications. A person with Type 1 diabetes continues to require insulin. These needs do not pause, and interruptions carry consequences that range from uncomfortable to fatal depending on the medication and the individual.

The gap between chronic disease need and chronic disease supply in emergency shelters has been documented repeatedly [CITATION]. Common barriers include: medication supplies lost or left behind during evacuation; medications requiring refrigeration that were not maintained in cold chain during displacement; pharmacies closed or inaccessible after the disaster; no documentation of medication names or dosages; and insurance systems that will not authorize early refills. Shelter health teams must be designed to address all of these barriers, not to document them and apologize.

**Medication replacement** in shelters relies on several mechanisms. Emergency prescription programs — in which state pharmacist boards issue emergency orders allowing pharmacists to dispense limited supplies of certain controlled and non-controlled medications without a current prescription — are among the most effective. Most states have established emergency prescription authorization frameworks, but activation varies. Shelter health teams should know the status of emergency prescription authority in their operating jurisdiction before an event. Emergency pharmaceutical caches, maintained by some local health departments and CHEMPACK program sites, provide supplies of certain critical medications (including nerve agent antidotes, oral rehydration salts, and basic primary care medications). Mobile pharmacy units operated by state health departments or national pharmacy chains have been deployed to shelter environments in major disasters. The shelter health operation should have contacts and protocols for each of these mechanisms.

**Cold-chain medications** require particular planning. Insulin is the paradigm case. Refrigerated insulin can be stored at room temperature for [TIMEFRAME] after initial opening under most product guidelines; unopened refrigerated insulin has a shorter room-temperature window. Persons with insulin-dependent diabetes who have been displaced for more than [TIMEFRAME] without refrigeration face a real question about insulin efficacy, not just supply. Shelter health operations should maintain a refrigerator or access to a refrigerated storage area specifically for medications, and should document which residents require cold-chain medications at intake. Other medications requiring refrigeration include certain biologics and liquid antibiotics compounded for pediatric use.

**Home medical equipment** presents logistical rather than pharmacological challenges, but they are equally consequential. Continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) machines require electricity, a CPAP mask with the correct sizing, and ideally the individual's own machine (because CPAP settings are individualized). Individuals who use CPAP for severe obstructive sleep apnea and go without it for multiple nights face risks including hypoxemia during sleep, cardiac arrhythmias in susceptible individuals, and worsening daytime function that compounds the psychological stress of displacement. Oxygen concentrators require electricity and deliver a specific flow rate matched to the individual's prescription. Nebulizers for asthma management require electricity and medication supplies. The AFN and medical needs shelter environments must include reliable electrical access, power backup, and an inventory of loaner equipment for individuals who arrived without their own devices.

**Dialysis** is among the most complex medical needs in the shelter environment. Individuals on hemodialysis require treatment three times per week under normal circumstances; missing sessions creates rapidly accumulating fluid and electrolyte burdens with cardiac and neurological consequences. Shelter health teams cannot provide hemodialysis. They must have a map of operating dialysis facilities within accessible range and a transportation mechanism to get dialysis-dependent residents to those facilities on schedule. In major disasters, dialysis facilities themselves may be damaged or without power, and the mismatch between dialysis need and dialysis supply can become a regional crisis. Disaster dialysis coordination, involving renal specialty networks, portable dialysis equipment staging, and mutual aid between dialysis facilities, is an area of disaster medicine that remains underdeveloped in many regions.

## 16.5 Mental and Behavioral Health in Shelter Settings

Shelters concentrate psychological stressors in a single physical space. The people in a shelter have just experienced a traumatic event, lost or temporarily lost their homes, may not know the status of family members or pets, face uncertainty about their financial recovery, and are now living in a crowded, noisy, unfamiliar environment with strangers, with no privacy, on cots, for an indefinite period. Against that background, mental health presentations in shelters are not anomalies. They are expected.

The **spectrum of behavioral responses** in shelter settings ranges from normal acute stress reactions — tearfulness, heightened startle, irritability, sleep disruption, difficulty concentrating — to acute stress disorder and post-traumatic stress presentations, to exacerbations of pre-existing serious mental illness, to substance withdrawal, to domestic violence and interpersonal conflict, to rare but high-risk presentations of suicidality and acute psychosis.

**Psychological first aid (PFA)** is the recommended framework for addressing the normal acute stress reactions of shelter populations. PFA is not therapy. It is a structured supportive response designed to provide safety, calm, connection to information and resources, connection to social supports, and access to hope. PFA can be delivered by trained paraprofessionals, disaster behavioral health volunteers, shelter staff, and community health workers. Its core principles — meeting basic needs first, listening without pressure to disclose, normalizing distress responses, and connecting people to practical help — are achievable at scale in shelter environments and have a meaningful effect on the trajectory of stress responses.

**Pre-existing serious mental illness** in the shelter population requires clinical response, not PFA alone. Individuals with schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, major depressive disorder, and severe anxiety disorders who are displaced from their care systems — their psychiatrist, their case manager, their community mental health center, their group home — face decompensation risk that increases with each day of disruption. Medication continuity (addressed above) is part of the answer. Continuity of care relationships is the other part: shelter behavioral health teams should prioritize establishing contact with the community mental health system to transfer care rather than attempting to provide ongoing psychiatric care from the shelter.

**Substance withdrawal** is a life-threatening emergency that is not always recognized in shelter settings. Alcohol withdrawal, in particular, can produce seizures within [TIMEFRAME] of last drink in individuals with alcohol use disorder and high baseline consumption. Benzodiazepine withdrawal has a similar and in some cases longer risk window. Opioid withdrawal, while agonizing and disabling, is rarely directly fatal but creates significant suffering and functional impairment. Shelter health teams should include the capacity to recognize and stabilize substance withdrawal presentations, and should have protocols for transfer to emergency departments when withdrawal severity exceeds shelter management capacity. Shelter intake should include a sensitive and non-punitive inquiry about substance use history and current use, because the information is clinically necessary.

**Interpersonal violence and safety** in shelter environments deserve mention. The shelter setting — crowded, stressed, with compromised privacy — creates conditions that can escalate domestic violence and sexual assault. Shelter design should include sleeping area layout that minimizes the ability of abusers to isolate victims, clear policies and reporting mechanisms for violence within the shelter, on-site or on-call victim services, and protocols for separating abusive individuals from their victims. Children in shelters face particular vulnerability and shelter operations should include child-safe play spaces, supervision, and the capacity to respond to concerns about child welfare.

## 16.6 Populations with Access and Functional Needs

The access and functional needs (AFN) framework is the current operational standard for thinking about shelter populations who require modified or enhanced support. It replaces older frameworks that used the term "special needs" and focused on medical diagnoses, because the relevant factor in a shelter context is not a diagnosis but a functional requirement: what does this person need to participate safely and meaningfully in the shelter environment?

**Mobility limitations** affect shelter design at the most basic level. Cots on the floor are not accessible to individuals who cannot get up from a floor-level sleeping surface. Shower facilities with curbs, step-over thresholds, or no grab bars are not accessible to individuals with balance impairments. Toilet stalls that are not wheelchair-accessible exclude wheelchair users from accessible hygiene. Shelters should be assessed against the Americans with Disabilities Act accessibility standards before they are designated, not after they are opened, and shelter managers should have protocols for addressing accessibility deficits discovered on site.

**Cognitive and developmental disabilities** create communication and behavioral challenges that shelter staff are frequently not prepared for. An individual with severe autism spectrum disorder who is removed from their home environment, their routine, and their support structure may exhibit behavioral responses — self-stimulatory behaviors, meltdowns, difficulty following instructions — that are misinterpreted by untrained shelter staff as aggression or non-compliance. Shelter staff training should include basic information about the range of presentations associated with cognitive and developmental disabilities and strategies for de-escalation and support. Caregiver continuity is also important: individuals with cognitive or developmental disabilities who arrive with a family member or professional caregiver should be sheltered in proximity to that caregiver.

**Deaf and hard of hearing individuals** require communication accommodations that shelters routinely fail to provide. Emergency announcements over a public address system are inaccessible. Written information in a language that is not American Sign Language (ASL) may not be fully accessible to deaf individuals whose primary language is ASL. Shelter health teams should include or have rapid access to ASL interpreters, and all critical communications should be provided in visual formats in addition to audio formats.

**Children** as a population have needs that differ from those of adults in several dimensions. Pediatric medication dosing is weight-based, which means the shelter formulary must include weight-calibrated dosing information and scales. Children dehydrate faster than adults during diarrheal illness. Children are more susceptible to heat illness than adults. Children respond to disaster stress through behavioral regression, clinginess, sleep disturbance, and somatic complaints that parents may interpret as physical illness. Shelter health teams should include pediatric clinical competency, and shelters serving families with children should include child-appropriate food, play spaces, and activity programming that helps maintain routine and normalcy.

**Pregnant individuals** require specific health monitoring in shelter settings. Pre-term labor risk is increased by dehydration, physical and psychological stress, and disruption of prenatal care. Individuals in the third trimester require ready access to obstetric emergency services, which means that shelter placement of individuals at or near term should take into account distance to labor and delivery facilities. Shelter health teams should include the capacity to assess obstetric risk and to facilitate urgent transfer when labor onset or obstetric complications are identified.

**Elderly individuals** require heightened attention to hydration, thermal regulation, fall risk, and medication management. Heat illness and hypothermia risk are both elevated in elderly individuals who have impaired thermoregulatory capacity. Fall risk in unfamiliar environments with inadequate lighting and crowded pathways is a genuine injury risk. The intersection of aging and chronic disease management creates complex polypharmacy management challenges in the shelter setting.

## 16.7 Documentation and Confidentiality in Shelter Health Operations

Shelter health records are health records. This sounds self-evident, but in practice shelters have often operated as if health information shared in a shelter context exists outside the normal legal and ethical framework that governs medical records. It does not.

**HIPAA applicability** in the shelter context is a source of persistent confusion. The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) applies to covered entities — health care providers, health plans, and health care clearinghouses. A nurse providing care in a shelter who is operating as a licensed health care provider is likely a covered entity. A shelter staffed by trained volunteers who are not health care providers operates in a more ambiguous space. The practical guidance from the HHS Office for Civil Rights in the disaster context is that HIPAA-covered entities retain their obligations in disaster settings, and that the disaster context does not create a general permission to share health information without authorization [CITATION]. At the same time, HIPAA includes specific exceptions for public health activities and care coordination that are frequently relevant in shelter settings.

**Practical documentation standards** for shelter health operations should include: a shelter health log that records encounters by date and shelter-assigned ID number (not necessarily name) to protect privacy; individual health records maintained in a secured location with access limited to health team members; a medication administration log for any medications dispensed or administered by shelter health staff; and a transfer log documenting any shelter residents transported to emergency departments or other health facilities.

**Confidentiality and law enforcement** is an area of particular sensitivity. In some shelter activations, local law enforcement has a presence within or adjacent to the shelter. For shelter residents who are undocumented immigrants, individuals with outstanding warrants, or individuals with domestic violence history, the presence of law enforcement may deter health care seeking and may create genuine safety risks. Shelter health operations should have a clearly articulated and communicated policy about the relationship between health information and law enforcement access, including who can request health records, under what circumstances, and what process governs those requests. The policy should be communicated to shelter residents in accessible language and in multiple languages.

## 16.8 Equity in Shelter Health Operations

The populations most likely to be displaced by disasters and most likely to remain displaced for extended periods are not a random cross-section of the general population. They are disproportionately low-income, elderly, chronically ill, uninsured, without personal vehicles, living in older and more vulnerable housing stock, and in many communities, disproportionately persons of color [CITATION]. These are not coincidences. They are the products of housing policy, urban planning history, and economic stratification that have placed certain populations in higher-exposure locations with lower adaptive capacity.

These same populations arrive in shelters with greater health needs and fewer resources to address them. They are more likely to have complex chronic disease, more medications to replace, more functional limitations, less documentation of their medical history, and more reasons to distrust institutions including the shelter itself.

**Institutional distrust** as a barrier to shelter health utilization deserves particular examination. Surveys following major disasters have documented that undocumented immigrants, formerly incarcerated individuals, and some communities of color underutilize shelter services even when those services are available and needed [CITATION]. The reasons are not irrational. Presenting oneself at an official shelter — which may be staffed by government employees, located in a public building, and co-located with law enforcement — is a risk calculus, not a simple decision to accept or decline a service. Health teams working in shelter settings must understand this dynamic and must actively work to communicate the shelter's policies regarding immigration enforcement, law enforcement referral, and confidentiality in ways that are credible, not merely stated.

**Language access** in shelter health operations is not a courtesy. It is a clinical necessity. A person who cannot communicate their symptoms, their medication history, their allergies, or their understanding of their diagnosis and treatment plan to the health provider treating them is receiving a materially diminished standard of care. Shelters should be staffed for the linguistic demographics of the communities they serve, not for the linguistic demographics of the emergency responder workforce. In practice this requires pre-contracted telephonic and video medical interpretation services, bilingual community health workers, and advance arrangements with community organizations serving non-English-speaking populations.

**Housing recovery inequity** shapes shelter duration. Shelter stays that were designed to be short — days to weeks — become extended for populations who have no insurance to fund hotel accommodations, no credit cards to secure a rental deposit, no relatives with space to offer. Long-term shelter residents develop compounding health problems: respiratory illness from sustained congregate exposure, psychological deterioration from prolonged displacement, worsening chronic disease from sustained care disruption, and physical deterioration from sustained inadequate sleep and nutrition. The health system in the shelter must be designed for this realistic population, not for a theoretical population that will return home in a week.

## Applied Vignette: The Marshfield Elementary School Shelter

A late-summer derecho storm tracked across a densely populated metropolitan area, producing [QUANTITY] miles of sustained [QUANTITY] mph wind gusts, widespread utility failure, and structural damage to approximately [QUANTITY] homes in a lower-income residential corridor. The storm struck in the evening; temperatures forecast to remain in the mid-90s Fahrenheit for the following [TIMEFRAME] created immediate heat illness risk for displaced residents without electrical power for air conditioning.

The county opened three shelters within [TIMEFRAME] of the storm. Two were general population shelters, one housed in an elementary school gymnasium. The third was an AFN shelter at a community center adjacent to a dialysis clinic. A local health system deployed a shelter health team consisting of two registered nurses, one emergency medical technician, and one community health worker fluent in Spanish to the elementary school shelter.

By the end of the first night, [QUANTITY] individuals had registered. Health intake revealed several problems that had not been anticipated. A disproportionate number of arriving residents — approximately [QUANTITY] percent of the initial intake — reported taking three or more daily medications, consistent with the high rate of chronic disease in the surrounding neighborhood. Approximately [QUANTITY] individuals required insulin, all of whom had evacuated without cold-chain preservation. Four individuals identified as dialysis-dependent; three were referred to the AFN shelter adjacent to the dialysis clinic, but one declined transfer, citing prior negative experience with the AFN shelter staffing. Two individuals arrived in apparent alcohol withdrawal with tremor and diaphoresis, requiring stabilization and transfer to the emergency department.

On day two, the community health worker identified a cluster of approximately [QUANTITY] Spanish-speaking households who had registered but had not presented to the health intake station. When she approached them directly in Spanish, she learned that several adults had not sought medical care because they feared that engaging with shelter health staff — who they understood to include a person in a county government vest — would lead to contact with immigration authorities. The community health worker spent [TIMEFRAME] on informal education about the shelter's non-enforcement policy and facilitated medical intakes for the entire group, including two children with asthma who had left their nebulizers at home.

On day three, the shelter identified its first case of norovirus. The individual had been symptomatic since arrival but had not disclosed symptoms during intake. The health team implemented immediate cohorting, intensified toilet cleaning schedules, activated the telephonic interpretation line to communicate hygiene instructions to non-English-speaking residents, and notified the county health department. A total of [QUANTITY] secondary cases were identified over the following [TIMEFRAME] before the cluster resolved.

The after-action review identified three findings. First, the insulin cold-chain gap: the shelter formulary and supply cache did not include a refrigerator dedicated to medication storage, and the kitchen refrigerator available to the health team was inaccessible to them during meal preparation hours. Recommendation: all shelter health suites should have a designated, locked medication refrigerator with a temperature log as part of the standard shelter setup kit. Second, the immigration-related underutilization pattern: the shelter's non-enforcement policy was stated on a posted sign at the registration desk but was not communicated verbally or in Spanish, and the visual cues of the shelter environment (government-logoed vests, uniformed law enforcement intermittently present) undermined the posted policy. Recommendation: the shelter operations plan should include a community trust communication component delivered by community-embedded personnel at shelter activation, not at registration. Third, the dialysis transfer refusal: the individual who declined transfer to the AFN shelter near the dialysis clinic had a history with that specific facility that was not known to the shelter health team. Recommendation: dialysis-dependent individuals should be engaged in a preference conversation that acknowledges prior experience and explores alternatives, not simply directed to the designated site.

## Key Terms

**Access and Functional Needs (AFN):** A framework for identifying and supporting shelter residents who require modified or enhanced services based on their functional requirements, not their diagnosis.

**Cold-Chain Medication:** A medication that requires refrigerated storage to maintain efficacy and safety, including insulin and certain biologics.

**Congregate Setting:** Any setting in which a group of people share living space, facilities, and potentially airborne exposure, creating conditions favorable for infectious disease transmission.

**General Population Shelter:** A shelter designed for individuals who are ambulatory, do not require continuous medical monitoring, and can manage their own activities of daily living.

**Health Intake:** The structured assessment process at shelter entry that screens for communicable disease risk, identifies placement tier, reconciles medications, and establishes a health record.

**Medical Needs Shelter:** A shelter providing a level of clinical monitoring above AFN capacity but below inpatient hospitalization, typically staffed by licensed clinical personnel around the clock.

**Medication Reconciliation:** The process of identifying, documenting, and securing replacement supplies for a shelter resident's regular medication regimen.

**Norovirus:** A highly contagious enteric pathogen responsible for epidemic vomiting and diarrhea illness; a frequent cause of outbreaks in congregate settings including emergency shelters.

**Psychological First Aid (PFA):** A structured, evidence-informed framework for supporting disaster-affected individuals, focused on safety, calm, connection, and access to practical resources; distinct from clinical therapy.

**Scabies:** A skin infestation caused by *Sarcoptes scabiei* mite; spreads through direct contact and shared surfaces; requires treatment of all close contacts when identified in a shelter population.

**Shelter Medicine:** The clinical and operational discipline of providing health services in emergency shelter settings, integrating primary care, public health, infection control, and behavioral health.

1. A shelter intake policy requires that all individuals present a photo ID to register. What populations does this policy disproportionately exclude, and what are the health consequences of that exclusion? How should the policy be modified?

2. A shelter health team is caring for a resident whose psychiatric decompensation is worsening and who requires a medication adjustment, but the resident's psychiatrist is in a different city and the local community mental health center is closed due to storm damage. What resources does the team have, and what are the boundaries of shelter-based psychiatric management?

3. A shelter is on day four of operations and has identified three norovirus cases. The shelter director wants to continue operating rather than close, arguing that displaced residents have nowhere else to go. What infection control measures are appropriate? What is the threshold at which closure and transfer should be recommended?

4. An elderly resident refuses transfer to a medical needs shelter despite clinical indicators suggesting she would benefit from higher-level monitoring. She says she is afraid that if she goes to the medical shelter, she will be placed in a nursing home and will not be allowed to return to her home. How does the team address this situation?

5. A shelter is serving a community with a significant population of undocumented immigrants. Three days into the shelter operation, local law enforcement officers begin conducting patrols inside the shelter, citing security concerns. What is the health significance of this development, and what actions should the shelter health team and shelter management take?

## Scenario Exercise: The Riverside Gardens Shelter — Days 1 Through 7

**Background:** A wildfire displaced approximately [QUANTITY] people from a mobile home community, a section 8 apartment complex, and a neighborhood of older single-family homes in a rural county. The county opened a single general population shelter in the county fairgrounds exhibition hall, with the county health department providing health operations support. ## Practitioner Takeaways

Shelter health is a distinct operational discipline that requires advance planning, not improvisation after activation. The clinical knowledge required overlaps with emergency medicine, primary care, public health, and behavioral health — but the operational context is unique.

The health intake process at shelter entry is the single most important clinical function because it determines where each resident is placed, what medication needs are identified early, and what communicable disease risks are flagged before they seed the shelter population. Intake that is rushed, inaccessible, or experienced as threatening will drive the highest-need residents away from the health function.

Medication continuity planning must be in place before the shelter opens. This means knowing which emergency prescription authorities are activated in the operating jurisdiction, having a medication refrigerator in the health suite, and having a working contact list for mobile pharmacy resources and dialysis facilities before the event.

Mental health is a core shelter health function, not a supplement to it. Psychological first aid can be delivered by non-clinicians but must be trained and coordinated. Behavioral health crisis presentations require clinical response pathways, including transfer protocols.

Equity gaps in shelter health will not close themselves. Identifying which populations are underutilizing health services and designing active outreach strategies to reach them is as much a clinical responsibility as treating the patients who do present.

Infection control in shelters requires the same systematic approach as infection control in any congregate facility. This means surveillance, threshold-triggered response protocols, and the willingness to escalate to public health authorities and recommend closure when transmission is not controlled.

Every shelter has a last day. The transition out of shelter — the moment when health records, active care relationships, medication supplies, and referrals must be transferred to the post-shelter care system — is where the continuity of care built during the shelter stay is either preserved or lost. Plan for demobilization from day one.

## Chapter Notes

The access and functional needs (AFN) framework formalized in federal guidance represents a significant improvement over the earlier "special needs" framing, which tended to create binary eligible/ineligible categories based on diagnosis rather than function. The AFN approach is more operationally accurate: the relevant question for shelter placement is not whether a person has a diagnosis but whether their needs exceed what the shelter tier can support. This same logic should extend to health operations design more broadly — the question is what services the population needs, not what services fit the template.

The shelter medicine literature is less developed than the pre-hospital and hospital disaster medicine literature, in part because shelter health operations have historically been conducted by nongovernmental organizations with limited academic affiliation. This gap is closing, and the scholarly work of researchers like [NAME] on shelter disease dynamics and [NAME] on health equity in disaster shelter settings is beginning to provide the evidence base that shelter health protocols have historically lacked.

The equity dimensions of shelter health are not peripheral to the discipline. They are the discipline. The populations most likely to shelter and most likely to shelter for extended periods are the populations with the most complex health needs and the most barriers to accessing health services within the shelter. A shelter health operation designed for a median-income, English-speaking, fully documented, medically uncomplicated adult will fail the people who most need it to succeed.

The relationship between shelter health teams and public health authorities deserves explicit attention during planning. Surveillance data from shelters is public health data. Outbreak identification in a shelter is a reportable event. The relationship between the shelter health team and the local or state health department should be formalized in the shelter operations plan before activation, with clear reporting pathways, data-sharing protocols, and a decision tree for escalation and closure recommendations.
