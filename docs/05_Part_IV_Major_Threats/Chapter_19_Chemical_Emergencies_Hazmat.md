# Chapter 19: Chemical Emergencies and Hazardous Materials

---

## Chapter Overview

Chemical emergencies encompass a wide range of events, from industrial accidents at manufacturing facilities to deliberate chemical weapons attacks, from highway tanker spills to terrorist use of toxic industrial chemicals. What unites these events is the potential for rapid, large-scale harm driven by the physical and toxicological properties of released substances, and the requirement that responders understand those properties well enough to protect themselves and act effectively without becoming casualties.

This chapter provides emergency managers and public health professionals with a working framework for understanding chemical emergencies. It covers the primary classes of hazardous materials and chemical threat agents, the tiered response system built around hazmat operational levels, the health consequences and clinical management approaches for major chemical exposures, the environmental decontamination challenge, and the regulatory and legal framework governing hazardous materials in transportation and fixed facilities. The chapter is not a chemistry or toxicology course; it is a management and public health course that treats chemical properties as operational variables rather than academic content.

---

## Learning Objectives

Upon completing this chapter, students will be able to:

1. Describe the primary classes of hazardous materials and chemical threat agents relevant to emergency management and public health.
2. Explain the hazmat response tiered system (Awareness, Operations, Technician, Specialist/Incident Commander levels) and the operational implications of each.
3. Identify the major toxic mechanisms of nerve agents, blister agents, choking agents, and blood agents, and describe the associated clinical presentations.
4. Apply the concepts of the Emergency Response Guidebook (ERG) to initial isolation and protective action decision-making.
5. Describe the operational requirements of mass decontamination for chemical casualties.
6. Explain the regulatory framework governing fixed facility and transportation chemical hazards under HAZWOPER, SARA Title III, and related statutes.
7. Discuss the public health role in long-term consequence management following chemical incidents.

## 19.1 The Landscape of Chemical Emergencies

Chemical emergencies occur on a spectrum from routine to catastrophic, and from unintentional to deliberate. Understanding this spectrum is the starting point for planning.

**Toxic Industrial Chemicals (TICs) and Hazardous Materials (HazMat)**

The vast majority of chemical emergencies in the United States involve toxic industrial chemicals, substances manufactured or used in legitimate industrial, agricultural, or commercial processes. The most commonly involved materials in hazmat incidents include chlorine (used in water treatment and chemical manufacturing), ammonia (refrigeration and fertilizer production), hydrogen sulfide (petroleum refining and wastewater treatment), sulfuric acid (industrial manufacturing), and flammable gases including propane and natural gas.

The U.S. Department of Transportation (DOT) regulates the transportation of hazardous materials and maintains the Emergency Response Guidebook (ERG), updated every four years, which provides initial action guides for the most commonly transported hazardous materials. The ERG is a primary resource for first responders making initial isolation and protective action decisions before a hazmat team arrives.

Fixed-facility chemical releases, including pipeline ruptures, tank farm fires, and process vessel failures, are governed by different regulatory frameworks but produce similar operational challenges: defining the hazard zone, protecting shelter-in-place versus evacuation decisions, and managing public health consequences in the surrounding community.

**Chemical Weapons and Terrorism**

Chemical weapons are a specific subset of chemical threats involving substances designed specifically to harm people. The Chemical Weapons Convention (CWC), to which the United States is a signatory, prohibits the development, production, stockpiling, and use of chemical weapons. Historical state chemical weapons programs produced agents including nerve agents (sarin, VX, novichoks), blister agents (sulfur mustard, lewisite), choking agents (chlorine, phosgene), and blood agents (hydrogen cyanide).

Several factors make toxic industrial chemicals potentially more accessible for terrorist use than military chemical weapons: they are manufactured and transported in large quantities, they are available in concentrated forms that can cause mass casualties, and their intentional release as weapons is a lower technical barrier than synthesis of military agents. The [YEAR] sarin attack in the Tokyo subway and the [YEAR] chlorine attacks in [LOCATION] demonstrated both the operational feasibility and the public health consequences of chemical terrorism.

**The Dual-Use Problem**

Many toxic industrial chemicals are chemically similar or functionally identical to recognized chemical weapons. Chlorine was used as a chemical weapon in World War I and is also a widely used water treatment chemical. Phosgene was among the deadliest agents of the First World War and is also an important industrial precursor. This dual-use reality means that response protocols for chemical emergencies must account for both intentional and unintentional release scenarios, and that the initial response posture cannot always distinguish between them.

## 19.2 Hazmat Response: Operational Levels and Zones

The hazmat response system is organized around three operational levels defined by the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) under 29 CFR 1910.120, the HAZWOPER standard.

**Awareness Level**

First responders at the Awareness level are trained to recognize a hazardous materials incident, initiate protective actions (isolation and evacuation), and call for appropriate resources. They do not enter the hazard zone and do not attempt to control the release. Most fire service, law enforcement, and emergency medical personnel are trained to the Awareness level.

**Operations Level**

Operations-level responders are trained to respond defensively to hazardous materials incidents, protecting people and the environment without attempting source control. Operations-level personnel can conduct gross decontamination of casualties, establish decontamination corridors, and implement protective actions for the surrounding community. They operate with appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE) and from positions that minimize exposure.

**Technician and Specialist Levels**

Hazmat Technicians are trained to intervene offensively in hazardous materials releases, including entering the hazard zone with highest-level PPE (typically Level A, the encapsulated suit with self-contained breathing apparatus), patching containers, stopping releases at the source, and conducting confined-space rescue in chemical environments. Hazmat Specialists have additional expertise in specific chemical families or operational contexts.

**Hot Zone, Warm Zone, Cold Zone**

Chemical emergency response is organized around three concentric geographic zones:

- **Hot Zone (Exclusion Zone):** The area of immediately dangerous to life or health (IDLH) concentration. Entry requires the highest level of PPE appropriate to the agent. Only trained hazmat personnel with specific mission requirements enter the hot zone.
- **Warm Zone (Contamination Reduction Zone):** The area between the hot zone and the clean perimeter. Decontamination operations occur in the warm zone. PPE requirements are high but may be lower than the hot zone.
- **Cold Zone (Support Zone):** The clean area where command is established, medical treatment of decontaminated casualties occurs, and support functions operate. Personnel in the cold zone wear no special PPE unless drift or unanticipated release changes the hazard boundary.

Zone boundaries are dynamic and must be continuously reassessed based on atmospheric conditions, wind direction, and updated plume modeling.

**Personal Protective Equipment Levels**

OSHA defines four levels of chemical protective equipment:

- **Level A:** Maximum skin, eye, and respiratory protection. Vapor-proof suit with self-contained breathing apparatus (SCBA). Required when agent identity is unknown, when skin-toxic vapor agents (mustard, VX) are suspected, or when highest-risk operations are conducted.
- **Level B:** Highest respiratory protection with less skin protection. SCBA with splash-resistant suit. Used when an inhalation hazard exists but vapor skin penetration is not a primary concern.
- **Level C:** Air-purifying respirator (APR) rather than SCBA. Used when the specific hazardous substance is known and at concentrations that a filtered respirator can manage. Not appropriate for immediately dangerous conditions.
- **Level D:** Standard work uniform with minimal protection. Used in clean zones with no contamination potential.

PPE selection requires knowledge of the agent involved. Incorrect PPE selection is a documented cause of responder casualties in chemical incidents.

## 19.3 Chemical Threat Agents: Toxicology and Clinical Response

Emergency managers need not master organic chemistry to work effectively in chemical incidents, but they do need to understand the major classes of chemical threat agents well enough to understand the clinical consequences they produce and the treatment implications those consequences create.

**Nerve Agents**

Nerve agents, including sarin (GB), tabun (GA), soman (GD), VX, and novichok agents, are organophosphate compounds that inhibit acetylcholinesterase, the enzyme that breaks down the neurotransmitter acetylcholine. The result is overstimulation of cholinergic receptors throughout the body, producing the characteristic SLUDGE/DUMBELS syndrome: Salivation, Lacrimation, Urination, Defecation, Gastrointestinal distress, Emesis; or Diarrhea, Urination, Miosis (pinpoint pupils), Bradycardia/Bronchospasm/Bronchorrhea, Emesis, Lacrimation, Salivation.

Nerve agent exposure is a medical emergency requiring immediate antidote administration. Atropine blocks the muscarinic effects of acetylcholine accumulation. Pralidoxime (2-PAM) reactivates inhibited acetylcholinesterase if administered before the inhibitor-enzyme bond becomes permanent (a process called "aging"). Benzodiazepines control seizure activity. CHEMPACK, a federal program, pre-positions nerve agent antidote kits in [NUMBER] locations nationally to enable local treatment before the SNS can be deployed.

Sarin is a volatile liquid that vaporizes at room temperature, making inhalation the primary exposure route in open environments. VX is a persistent oily liquid with lower volatility, making skin contact the primary exposure route. This difference in physical properties drives both the protective equipment requirements and the decontamination priority.

**Blister Agents (Vesicants)**

Blister agents, including sulfur mustard (HD), nitrogen mustard, and lewisite, cause cellular damage to any tissue they contact: eyes, skin, airways, and gastrointestinal mucosa. Sulfur mustard exposure is insidious because there is a delay of [TIMEFRAME] hours between exposure and symptom onset, during which no immediate discomfort signals the exposure. By the time patients present with eye pain and skin blistering, the cellular damage has already occurred. There is no specific antidote for sulfur mustard; treatment is supportive, focused on wound care and infection prevention.

Lewisite causes immediate pain on contact, distinguishing it from mustard. British Anti-Lewisite (BAL, dimercaprol) is a specific antidote for systemic lewisite toxicity.

Mass casualty events from blister agents produce enormous demands on burn care and ophthalmology resources.

**Choking Agents (Pulmonary Agents)**

Choking agents, primarily chlorine and phosgene, cause pulmonary edema: fluid accumulation in the alveolar spaces that impairs gas exchange. Death results from asphyxiation hours to days after exposure. Both agents are industrial chemicals available in large quantities. Chlorine has a characteristic yellow-green color and bleach odor; phosgene has a freshly mowed hay odor and was historically far more lethal than chlorine in military use.

Treatment for pulmonary agent exposure is supportive: supplemental oxygen, positive pressure ventilation for respiratory failure, and observation for delayed pulmonary edema. Patients who appear well immediately after chlorine exposure may still develop significant pulmonary edema over the following 24 hours. This observation requirement creates healthcare system surge demands.

**Blood Agents**

Hydrogen cyanide (AC) and cyanogen chloride (CK) are classified as blood agents because they interfere with cellular oxygen utilization (cytochrome c oxidase inhibition) rather than oxygen delivery. Victims die from cellular hypoxia despite adequate tissue oxygen levels. Cyanide is used in metal processing industries and may be present in smoke from structural fires that involve synthetic materials.

Hydroxocobalamin (Cyanokit) and the Lilly Cyanide Antidote Kit (sodium nitrite and sodium thiosulfate) are the primary antidotes. Hydroxocobalamin is preferred because it does not cause methemoglobin formation, which is contraindicated in smoke inhalation victims who may also have carbon monoxide poisoning.

**Riot Control Agents (RCAs)**

Riot control agents, including CS gas (orthochlorobenzalmalononitrile), CN gas (chloroacetophenone), and OC (oleoresin capsicum, pepper spray), are not scheduled under the Chemical Weapons Convention but can cause significant injury, particularly in enclosed spaces, in individuals with underlying respiratory conditions, and when used in high concentrations. Their medical management is primarily decontamination and supportive care; the public health implications of large-scale RCA use in crowd control contexts include both acute injuries and behavioral health consequences.

## 19.4 Decontamination Operations

Decontamination is the process of removing or neutralizing hazardous materials from people, equipment, and the environment. In a chemical mass casualty event, patient decontamination is a life safety operation: continued dermal or inhalation exposure kills or injures patients who might otherwise survive.

**Mass Casualty Decontamination**

Mass casualty decontamination is operationally distinct from hazmat team decontamination in important ways. Hazmat technicians entering hot zones are decontaminated systematically by trained team members after mission completion. Chemical mass casualty victims arrive in variable numbers, from unexpected directions, often before a formal decontamination corridor is established, frequently self-transporting to the nearest hospital.

Hospitals in communities that host hazmat risks must have a decontamination capability at their perimeter, because contaminated self-presenting casualties will otherwise contaminate the hospital interior, disabling the facility for other patients and injuring healthcare workers. Hospital decontamination systems typically involve outdoor shower corridors, with separate lines for ambulatory and non-ambulatory patients. Even simple water irrigation (remove-and-shower decontamination: removing clothing reduces approximately [PERCENT]% of surface contamination) is highly effective for most chemical exposures.

The primary technical challenge in mass decontamination is maintaining patient dignity and privacy while operating at scale outdoors in any weather conditions. Plans that require patients to disrobe publicly without privacy measures will experience patient resistance and incomplete decontamination. Plans that account for privacy screening, gendered decontamination lines, and the practical reality of a population including children, elderly individuals, and people with disabilities perform better in exercises and actual events.

**Environmental Decontamination**

Persistent chemical agents, including VX nerve agent and some industrial chemicals, can remain in the environment in hazardous concentrations for extended periods. Environmental decontamination at fixed facilities or transportation accident sites involves specialized contractors, regulatory oversight from the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) under CERCLA and other statutes, and long-term monitoring requirements. The emergency management role is coordination and community communication; the technical work belongs to hazmat specialists and environmental contractors.

**Waste Management**

Decontamination generates contaminated water, clothing, and materials that require management as hazardous waste. Improper decontamination waste disposal is a secondary contamination hazard and a legal liability. Pre-event plans must address collection, containment, and disposal.

## 19.5 Protective Actions: Shelter-in-Place and Evacuation

The core protective action decision for chemical emergencies is whether to tell people to shelter in place or to evacuate. This decision has major consequences for public safety and is complicated by time pressure, incomplete information, and the properties of specific chemicals.

**Shelter-in-Place**

Shelter-in-place directs people to remain indoors, close windows and doors, turn off HVAC systems, and seal gaps with tape or towels. It is most effective for short-duration plume events with rapidly dissipating agents, for agents that have greater outdoor than indoor concentration, and for situations where evacuation routes are in or near the plume path. For agents that penetrate typical building construction (nerve agent vapors, chlorine), shelter-in-place provides limited but real reduction in exposure.

The National Weather Service's ALOHA (Areal Locations of Hazardous Atmospheres) and CAMEO (Computer-Aided Management of Emergency Operations) plume modeling tools, along with similar commercial platforms, allow incident commanders to model anticipated plume behavior based on atmospheric conditions and chemical properties. Plume models are estimates rather than precise predictions; real-world plume behavior is affected by terrain, urban canyon effects, and atmospheric stability classes that models approximate.

**Evacuation**

Evacuation removes people from the hazard zone but creates its own risks: traffic accidents, medical emergencies during evacuation, and the possibility that evacuees drive through the hazard plume. For fast-moving events like a sudden gas cloud, evacuation may expose evacuees to higher concentrations than shelter-in-place.

In practice, both protective actions are often employed simultaneously: populations immediately adjacent to the source may be evacuated while those farther away are directed to shelter in place. The transition between these zones, and the communication required to ensure that people understand which action applies to them, is a substantial public communication challenge.

**Public Communication in Chemical Emergencies**

Chemical emergency protective action messages require a difficult balance: they must create enough urgency to produce compliance, without triggering panic that results in spontaneous mass evacuation. Post-event analyses consistently identify protective action communication as one of the most challenging dimensions of chemical emergencies, both because of the technical complexity of explaining an invisible hazard and because of the multi-agency coordination required to produce a single, consistent public message [CITATION].

## 19.6 Regulatory Framework for Chemical Hazards

The regulatory landscape governing chemical hazards is complex and multi-layered. Emergency managers need a working knowledge of the key statutes and the planning obligations they create.

**HAZWOPER (29 CFR 1910.120)**

The Hazardous Waste Operations and Emergency Response standard establishes training and operational requirements for workers engaged in hazardous materials cleanup and emergency response. It defines the operational levels discussed in Section 19.2 and establishes training hour requirements for each level. HAZWOPER compliance is both a worker protection requirement and an operational competency standard.

**SARA Title III (Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act)**

The Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act (EPCRA), enacted as Title III of the Superfund Amendments and Reauthorization Act (SARA) of 1986, created the national framework for local emergency planning for chemical hazards. Key provisions include:

- **Section 302:** Facilities that store Extremely Hazardous Substances (EHS) above threshold planning quantities must notify their State Emergency Response Commission (SERC) and Local Emergency Planning Committee (LEPC). This notification requirement creates the LEPC's inventory of hazardous materials in the community.
- **Section 311-312:** Facilities covered under OSHA's Hazard Communication Standard must submit Material Safety Data Sheets (now Safety Data Sheets under GHS) to their LEPC, SERC, and local fire department, and must file annual chemical inventory reports (Tier II reports).
- **Section 313:** Facilities must report annual releases of listed toxic chemicals to the EPA's Toxics Release Inventory (TRI), creating a publicly accessible database of community chemical exposure information.

**LEPC Role in Chemical Emergency Planning**

Local Emergency Planning Committees, required under SARA Title III in every jurisdiction, are the primary local planning body for chemical emergencies. The LEPC brings together local officials, first responders, industry representatives, media, community organizations, and healthcare providers to develop and exercise a local hazardous materials emergency response plan. In practice, LEPC capacity varies enormously across jurisdictions. Well-functioning LEPCs are a genuine preparedness asset; underfunded or inactive LEPCs represent a planning gap.

**DOT Hazardous Materials Regulations**

The Department of Transportation regulates the transportation of hazardous materials under 49 CFR Parts 100-185, establishing requirements for packaging, labeling, marking, placarding, documentation, and training for hazmat shippers and carriers. The placard system visible on trucks and rail cars is the primary field identification tool for first responders approaching a transportation incident before other information is available.

## Key Terms

**ALOHA (Areal Locations of Hazardous Atmospheres):** Atmospheric dispersion modeling software used to predict the spread of chemical plumes in emergency scenarios.

**CHEMPACK:** A federal program pre-positioning nerve agent antidote kits in communities to enable rapid treatment before the Strategic National Stockpile can be deployed.

**Choking agents (pulmonary agents):** Chemical agents, including chlorine and phosgene, that cause pulmonary edema and respiratory failure.

**Decontamination:** The process of removing or neutralizing hazardous materials from people, equipment, or the environment.

**Emergency Response Guidebook (ERG):** A DOT-published reference guide providing initial isolation and protective action recommendations for hazardous materials commonly transported by road, rail, or water.

**HAZWOPER:** OSHA standard 29 CFR 1910.120, establishing training and operational requirements for hazardous waste operations and emergency response workers.

**Hot Zone:** The area immediately surrounding a hazardous materials release where contamination is present at dangerous levels and specialized PPE is required.

**Level A protection:** The highest level of personal protective equipment, involving a vapor-proof encapsulated suit and self-contained breathing apparatus.

**Local Emergency Planning Committee (LEPC):** A locally based planning body required under SARA Title III to develop and exercise hazardous materials emergency response plans.

**Nerve agents:** Organophosphate chemical weapons that inhibit acetylcholinesterase, causing cholinergic crisis.

**SARA Title III (EPCRA):** Federal legislation requiring facilities to notify communities about hazardous chemicals stored on-site and to report releases to federal and local agencies.

**Shelter-in-place:** A protective action directing people to remain indoors and take steps to reduce infiltration of outdoor contaminants.

**Toxic industrial chemicals (TICs):** Chemicals manufactured or used in legitimate industrial, agricultural, or commercial processes that can cause mass casualties if released in sufficient quantities.

**Vesicants (blister agents):** Chemical agents, including sulfur mustard and lewisite, that cause cellular damage to skin, eyes, and mucous membranes.

## Practitioner Takeaways

Chemical emergencies present a constant tension between the speed required for effective protective action and the uncertainty inherent in characterizing an evolving hazard. The Emergency Response Guidebook and ALOHA plume modeling provide structured tools for initial decision-making under uncertainty; they are not precise instruments but they are the best available tools in the first minutes and hours of an incident.

The training investment required to maintain a functional hazmat capability is continuous and significant. HAZWOPER requires documented annual refresher training; Level A suit donning and doffing, which is a life-safety skill, requires regular practice under conditions that replicate operational stress. Communities that maintain this investment have meaningfully different outcomes than those that treat hazmat training as a box-checking exercise.

Hospital chemical casualty preparedness is frequently the weakest link in the community chemical emergency response chain. The scenario in which contaminated self-presenting patients arrive at an emergency department before the hospital has been notified is not a rare edge case; it is the most common entry point for chemical casualties into the healthcare system. Emergency managers should include hospital decontamination capability assessment in community hazard analyses and should exercise the hospital-prehospital coordination interface specifically.

The regulatory framework under SARA Title III and HAZWOPER creates substantial planning obligations that many small and rural jurisdictions struggle to fulfill. Emergency managers in these jurisdictions should use the LEPC structure as an honest assessment of community chemical risk, even if the planning documents that result are modest. Knowing where the chlorine is stored and who would respond if the tank ruptured at 2 a.m. on New Year's Day is more valuable than a beautifully formatted plan that no one has read.

## Chapter Notes

This chapter addresses chemical weapons and toxic industrial chemicals in an emergency management and public health context. The toxicological content is oriented toward recognition, clinical management, and protective action decision-making rather than toward synthesis or weaponization. All chemical agent information presented is drawn from published federal preparedness documents, peer-reviewed literature, and openly available emergency response references.

The environmental justice dimensions of chemical emergency planning deserve emphasis. Proximity to industrial facilities associated with chemical hazards correlates with community demographics in documented patterns across the United States; communities in the fence-line zone of chemical facilities have historically been disproportionately low-income and communities of color. Chemical emergency planners who do not explicitly account for this reality in vulnerability assessments, protective action communication, and long-term health monitoring will systematically underprotect the most exposed communities.

Instructors should note that the Tokyo subway sarin attack of [YEAR] and related chemical terrorism events may have personal resonance for students with connections to affected communities. The chapter treats these events as documented operational cases rather than political statements; instructors should establish appropriate context.
