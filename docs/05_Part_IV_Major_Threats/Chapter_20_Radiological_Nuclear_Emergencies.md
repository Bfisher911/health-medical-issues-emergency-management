# Chapter 20: Radiological and Nuclear Emergencies

---

## Chapter Overview

Radiological and nuclear emergencies occupy a distinctive category in emergency management. They are simultaneously rare and consequential, technically complex and operationally demanding, and they carry a psychological weight that can drive public behavior far out of proportion to actual health risk. For emergency managers and public health professionals, this creates a dual challenge: understanding enough radiation physics to make sound protective action decisions while also managing the communication environment in which those decisions will be received.

This chapter builds foundational knowledge across four domains. The first is the physics of ionizing radiation, translated into concepts that support operational decision-making rather than academic study. The second is the landscape of radiological and nuclear incident types, from industrial accidents and medical source exposures through radiological dispersal devices and improvised nuclear detonations. The third is the health effects framework, organized around the critical distinction between contamination and exposure and the dose-dependent spectrum of radiation injury. The fourth is the operational response system: detection and monitoring, protective actions including potassium iodide policy, nuclear detonation response planning, and the medical management of radiation casualties.

Throughout, the chapter attends to the equity and communication dimensions that make radiological emergencies particularly difficult. Communities near nuclear facilities, communities with high proportions of non-English speakers, and communities with historically justified distrust of government authorities all face distinctive risks and require deliberate outreach strategies. The fear that surrounds radiation is not irrational; it reflects real harm from real historical events. Effective response requires acknowledging that history while providing accurate, actionable guidance.

## 20.1 Radiation Fundamentals for Emergency Managers

Radiation is energy traveling through space. Ionizing radiation carries enough energy to displace electrons from atoms, creating ions that can damage biological molecules. Not all radiation is ionizing; visible light and radio waves are non-ionizing and do not produce radiation injury. Emergency managers are concerned specifically with ionizing radiation from radioactive materials and nuclear reactions.

### Types of Ionizing Radiation

**Alpha particles** are large, relatively slow-moving particles composed of two protons and two neutrons. They travel only a few centimeters in air and cannot penetrate the outer layer of skin. A sheet of paper stops an alpha particle. This makes alpha emitters relatively low-risk as external hazards but highly dangerous if inhaled or ingested, because the alpha particle deposits all of its energy in a very small area of tissue. Radon gas, which decays through alpha emission, is the classic example of a significant internal alpha hazard. Polonium-210, used in the assassination of Alexander Litvinenko in 2006, is another.

**Beta particles** are high-energy electrons emitted from unstable nuclei. They travel farther than alpha particles in air, roughly [DISTANCE] meters, and can penetrate skin to a depth sufficient to cause radiation burns. Thin plastic, clothing, or a few millimeters of aluminum stop beta particles. Contamination of the skin or eyes with beta-emitting material is an operational concern; beta emitters are also dangerous if inhaled or ingested.

**Gamma rays** are high-energy electromagnetic photons emitted during radioactive decay. They travel large distances, penetrate the body completely, and require dense shielding materials such as lead or thick concrete for attenuation. Gamma radiation is the primary driver of dose to bystanders and responders at a radiological incident. Cobalt-60, cesium-137, and iridium-192 are gamma-emitting radionuclides of concern in both industrial accidents and deliberate events.

**Neutron radiation** is produced by nuclear reactions rather than radioactive decay. Neutrons can penetrate deeply, cause significant biological damage, and activate otherwise stable materials to become radioactive. Neutron exposure is primarily a concern in nuclear weapon detonation and criticality accidents; it is not relevant to radiological dispersal device (RDD) scenarios.

### Dose and Units

Radiation dose measures the energy deposited in tissue. Emergency managers encounter several units:

**Gray (Gy)** measures absorbed dose, the energy deposited per unit mass of tissue. One gray equals one joule per kilogram.

**Sievert (Sv)** is the unit of effective dose, which adjusts absorbed dose by a weighting factor that accounts for the type of radiation and the radiosensitivity of the tissue exposed. For gamma and beta radiation, one gray equals one sievert. For alpha radiation, one gray equals [NUMBER] sieverts, reflecting the greater biological effectiveness of densely ionizing particles. Millisieverts (mSv) and microsieverts (µSv) are commonly used in public health contexts.

**Rem** and **millirem (mrem)** are the older U.S. customary units, still encountered in regulatory documents and many operational contexts. One sievert equals 100 rem; one millisievert equals 100 millirem.

**Background radiation** in the United States averages approximately [NUMBER] mSv per year, of which roughly half comes from radon and the rest from cosmic radiation, medical procedures, natural radioactive materials in soil and building materials, and internal sources. This baseline is a key communication reference: many public discussions of radiation risk fail to anchor exposures against background, making modest doses appear more alarming than they are.

### The Inverse Square Law and Shielding

Two physics principles govern protective action decisions in radiological incidents. The **inverse square law** states that radiation intensity decreases with the square of the distance from a point source. Doubling the distance from an unshielded gamma source reduces dose rate by a factor of four. This underlies the fundamental emergency response instruction of increasing distance from any suspected radiological source. **Shielding** uses dense material to attenuate gamma radiation. Common materials include lead, concrete, steel, and water. Sheltering inside a substantial building provides meaningful dose reduction, particularly for fallout scenarios, where a mid-rise reinforced concrete building may reduce exposure by a factor of [NUMBER] to [NUMBER] compared to remaining outdoors.

The three principles of radiation protection are time, distance, and shielding. They are the operational foundation of every protective action decision in this chapter.

## 20.2 Types of Radiological and Nuclear Incidents

Federal planning documents distinguish several categories of radiological and nuclear incidents based on their mechanism, scale, and public health consequences. Each requires different response approaches.

### Nuclear Power Plant Accidents

Commercial nuclear power plants operating in the United States are subject to Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) oversight and are required to maintain emergency plans extending beyond the facility boundary. The Emergency Planning Zones (EPZs) established around nuclear facilities define two geographic areas:

The **Plume Exposure Pathway EPZ** extends approximately [DISTANCE] miles from the plant. Within this zone, state and local plans must address sheltering, potassium iodide distribution, and evacuation of the population potentially exposed to the radioactive plume during the release phase of an accident.

The **Ingestion Pathway EPZ** extends approximately [DISTANCE] miles from the plant. It addresses longer-term contamination of food and water supplies following a significant release.

Accidents at nuclear power plants can range from minor radiological releases requiring precautionary notifications to major core damage events with large releases of fission products. The NRC uses a five-level emergency classification system: unusual event, alert, site area emergency, and general emergency. A general emergency, the highest level, may warrant protective action implementation in the Plume Exposure Pathway EPZ.

The accidents at Three Mile Island ([YEAR]), Chernobyl ([YEAR]), and Fukushima Daiichi ([YEAR]) have shaped regulatory and response frameworks globally. Fukushima, in particular, prompted significant revisions to U.S. emergency planning requirements and reexamination of KI pre-distribution policies.

### Radiological Dispersal Devices

A **radiological dispersal device (RDD)**, commonly called a "dirty bomb," combines conventional explosives with radioactive material to spread contamination over an area. RDDs are weapons of mass disruption rather than mass destruction. The immediate health effects typically result from the explosion rather than radiation exposure; the radiation dose from a practical RDD is unlikely to cause acute radiation syndrome in most exposed persons.

The primary consequences of an RDD are: psychological and behavioral impacts on the public; economic disruption from contamination of commercial, financial, or infrastructure areas; resource demands from decontamination operations; and long-term cancer risk elevation in a small fraction of the exposed population. A well-constructed dirty bomb detonated in a dense urban financial district could impose billions of dollars in decontamination costs and prolonged business interruption even if radiation doses to individuals remain below acute health thresholds.

The radionuclides of greatest concern for RDD construction are those that are widely available, highly radioactive, and dispersible. Cesium-137 from industrial sources including radiotherapy equipment and measurement devices, cobalt-60 from food irradiation and industrial radiography, and strontium-90 from some portable power sources represent significant RDD-relevant materials. NRC and Agreement State regulations govern the security of licensed radioactive sources, and incidents of source orphaning, theft, and loss are tracked internationally.

### Radiological Exposure Devices

A **radiological exposure device (RED)** uses radioactive material to expose persons without attempting to disperse it. There is no explosion; the source may be covertly placed in a location where people will be chronically or acutely exposed. The Goiânia accident in Brazil in [YEAR], while not intentional, functioned operationally like an RED: a cesium-137 source from an abandoned radiotherapy unit was recovered by scavengers, opened, and distributed, resulting in [NUMBER] deaths and widespread contamination. The event demonstrated both the severe health consequences possible from high-activity sealed sources and the community-level impacts of improper source management.

### Improvised Nuclear Devices

An **improvised nuclear device (IND)** is a crude nuclear explosive constructed by a non-state actor using fissile material. An IND detonation would produce blast, thermal, and radiation effects and would constitute a catastrophic, mass casualty event. Federal planning for IND scenarios uses the National Planning Scenario 1, which models a [YIELD] kiloton surface detonation in a major urban area. The public health and medical consequences of even a low-yield IND detonation would overwhelm regional medical infrastructure; planning emphasis is on actions that save lives within the first [TIMEFRAME] hours, particularly through shelter-in-place and evacuation guidance to reduce fallout exposure.

### Transportation and Industrial Accidents

Most actual radiological incidents in the United States involve transportation accidents, equipment failures, or loss of control of licensed radioactive material. Medical radioisotopes, industrial radiography sources, and research materials are transported daily across the country under DOT regulations. Industrial accidents involving gamma irradiators, radiotherapy equipment malfunctions, and inadvertent melting of sealed sources in steel recycling facilities (creating radioactive metal contamination in the industrial supply chain) are the more common operational radiological events that local emergency managers may actually respond to.

## 20.3 Contamination, Exposure, and Health Effects

The most operationally important distinction in radiological emergency response is between **contamination** and **exposure**. Conflating them produces both tactical errors and communication failures.

### Contamination vs. Exposure

**Radiation exposure** occurs when ionizing radiation from a source passes through or into the body, depositing energy. A person can be exposed to radiation from an external source, receive a dose, and have no radioactive material on or in their body. They are not themselves radioactive and pose no contamination risk to rescuers, family members, or healthcare providers. When the source is removed, the exposure stops.

**Radioactive contamination** occurs when radioactive material is deposited on or inside the body. External contamination involves radioactive particles on the skin, hair, or clothing. Internal contamination occurs when radioactive material is inhaled, ingested, or enters through wounds. A contaminated person may continue to receive radiation dose as long as the contaminating material remains present, and in some cases may represent a secondary contamination risk to those in close contact.

The operational implication is significant. In a nuclear detonation scenario, many persons will arrive at hospitals with external contamination from fallout. They do not present a hazard requiring healthcare workers to don the same level of PPE used for chemical casualty decontamination. Simple soap-and-water washing removes the majority of external contamination. Healthcare workers wearing standard personal protective equipment and following basic contamination control practices can safely care for radiation casualties.

The public communication implication is equally significant. Following a radiological incident, persons who received radiation dose without contamination may be reassured that they are not "radioactive" and do not pose risk to their families. This directly addresses one of the most common sources of public anxiety.

### Health Effects of Radiation Exposure

Radiation health effects are classified by dose threshold and time course.

**Deterministic effects** (also called tissue reactions) occur at doses above specific thresholds and become more severe as dose increases. Below the threshold, they do not occur. Acute radiation syndrome (ARS) is the primary deterministic effect of concern in emergency scenarios.

**Stochastic effects** occur probabilistically, with increasing probability but not necessarily increasing severity as dose increases. Cancer is the primary stochastic effect of concern for public health; there is no universally accepted lower threshold below which radiation carries zero cancer risk (the linear no-threshold model), though very low doses produce very low incremental risk.

### Acute Radiation Syndrome

ARS follows whole-body or significant partial-body exposure to high doses of penetrating radiation, typically gamma or neutron, delivered over a short period. It does not occur from external contamination alone, from low doses, or from highly fractionated exposure. ARS has four stages and four clinical syndromes:

**The prodromal stage** occurs within hours of high-dose exposure and includes nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, fatigue, and headache. The timing and severity of the prodrome predict the severity of subsequent illness. Onset of vomiting within [TIMEFRAME] hours of exposure suggests doses potentially consistent with severe hematopoietic injury.

**The latent stage** follows the prodrome. The patient may appear relatively well for days to weeks while radiation-induced damage to bone marrow progresses subclinically.

**The manifest illness stage** reflects the dominant organ system affected:

The **hematopoietic syndrome** occurs at doses of approximately [NUMBER] Gy or greater. Bone marrow suppression causes pancytopenia with risk of infection, hemorrhage, and anemia. This is the most clinically relevant syndrome for planning purposes in most scenarios.

The **gastrointestinal syndrome** occurs at doses of approximately [NUMBER] Gy or greater. Destruction of the gut epithelium causes bloody diarrhea, fluid loss, and loss of intestinal barrier function.

The **cardiovascular/central nervous system syndrome** occurs at doses above approximately [NUMBER] Gy and is uniformly fatal. Patients present with severe neurological symptoms within hours.

**The recovery or death stage** follows, depending on dose, supportive care availability, and individual variation.

Threshold doses for ARS and estimated mortality values from the literature should be used cautiously in planning; individual variation is significant, and the availability of supportive care, growth factors, and stem cell transplantation alters outcomes meaningfully. The Armed Forces Radiobiology Research Institute (AFRRI) has developed the Medical Treatment Protocols for Radiation Casualties to guide clinical management.

### Cutaneous Radiation Syndrome

Skin exposure to radiation without ARS can cause **cutaneous radiation syndrome**, presenting as erythema, epilation, dry or moist desquamation, and, at high doses, necrosis. Cutaneous syndrome may occur in the absence of systemic hematopoietic effects, particularly with beta contamination, and may complicate recovery in ARS patients.

## 20.4 Detection, Monitoring, and Dose Assessment

The detection and monitoring infrastructure for radiological events includes fixed environmental monitoring systems, mobile detection capabilities deployed by response agencies, and individual dosimetry.

### Environmental Monitoring: RadNet

The U.S. Environmental Protection Agency operates **RadNet**, a nationwide network of fixed monitoring stations that measure gamma radiation levels and collect air samples for laboratory analysis. RadNet provides baseline environmental radiation data and serves as an early warning system for airborne radiological releases. During the Fukushima accident, RadNet data informed public health decisions in the United States regarding the potential for airborne contamination crossing the Pacific. RadNet data is publicly available in near real-time and is a credible source for public communication purposes.

### Field Detection Equipment

Several types of radiation detection equipment are used in field response:

**Geiger-Mueller counters** detect ionizing radiation and are widely available, inexpensive, and easy to operate. They are useful for screening purposes but have limitations in high-dose-rate environments.

**Scintillation detectors** are more sensitive and can be configured for isotope identification. Handheld radioisotope identification devices (RIIDs) allow field teams to distinguish, for example, between a naturally occurring radioactive material (NORM) source and a weapon-relevant radionuclide.

**Portal monitors** are fixed or mobile gamma detection systems used at checkpoints, building entrances, and transportation nodes to screen for radioactive materials.

**Survey meters with various probes** allow measurement of contamination on surfaces and people. Beta-gamma survey meters are standard equipment in emergency response kits.

The **NNSA Radiological Assistance Program (RAP)** and **Nuclear Emergency Support Team (NEST)** provide federal detection, assessment, and technical response capabilities that can be requested through FEMA and DHS channels.

### Individual Dosimetry

Individual monitoring of responder dose is an occupational safety requirement and a medical management tool. Types of dosimetry used in emergency response include:

**Electronic personal dosimeters (EPDs)** provide real-time dose-rate and cumulative dose readout and can be configured to alarm at preset thresholds. They are the preferred dosimeter for emergency response operations.

**Thermoluminescent dosimeters (TLDs)** and **optically stimulated luminescence dosimeters (OSLDs)** require laboratory processing but provide accurate dose records.

**Film badges**, while older, remain in use in some contexts.

Dose tracking for emergency responders during a radiological event is governed by occupational dose limits established by the NRC and EPA. Emergency responder dose guidance, which allows higher doses during life-saving activities when lower doses are not practicable, is addressed in EPA protective action guides. Dose records serve both worker protection and subsequent epidemiological purposes.

### Biodosimetry

When individual dosimetry is unavailable, absorbed dose can be estimated through biological indicators. The **complete blood count with differential** is the primary biodosimetric tool in mass casualty radiation scenarios. Serial lymphocyte counts in the hours following potential high-dose exposure provide dose estimates that guide triage and treatment decisions. AFRRI's Biodosimetry Assessment Tool (BAT) and the REMM (Radiation Emergency Medical Management) system provide clinicians with decision support for radiological casualty triage.

Cytogenetic assays, including the dicentric chromosome assay and the micronucleus assay, provide more precise dose estimation but require laboratory infrastructure and several days for results. They are useful for retrospective dose reconstruction rather than acute triage.

## 20.5 Protective Actions and Potassium Iodide

The protective action framework for radiological incidents follows the same shelter-in-place, evacuation, and contamination avoidance logic as other hazardous material incidents, with several important additions specific to radiological events.

### Shelter-in-Place

Sheltering inside a substantial building significantly reduces dose from gamma-emitting fallout. The **protection factor** varies by building construction: a wood-frame single-story house provides modest protection; a multi-story reinforced concrete or brick building provides substantially greater protection. EPA's **Protective Action Guides (PAGs)** provide dose thresholds at which different protective actions should be implemented. Sheltering is particularly valuable during the passage of a radioactive plume, which may last from minutes to hours depending on source term, weather, and distance.

In a nuclear detonation scenario, sheltering in a robust building for the first [TIMEFRAME] hours is likely the single most important life-saving action for persons outside the immediate blast zone. Fallout dose rates decrease rapidly with time due to radioactive decay; the dose received in the first 24 hours post-detonation is far higher than the dose received in subsequent days. The **1-2-3 guidance** discussed in Section 20.6 is built on this decay curve.

Shelter-in-place guidance must be communicated clearly and with specific behavioral instructions: go inside, stay inside, stay tuned. Ambiguity about what constitutes adequate shelter and how long to remain inside produces both unnecessary exposure and unnecessary evacuation.

### Evacuation

Evacuation removes the population from the contaminated environment and prevents ongoing dose accumulation. It is clearly preferable to sheltering when the contamination event is long-duration, when the population can be moved before plume arrival, or when building stock does not provide meaningful protection.

Evacuation after fallout has deposited presents additional considerations. Persons evacuating through contaminated areas will receive external dose and may introduce contamination into their vehicles and destination communities. Traffic behavioral patterns following a nuclear detonation, including spontaneous mass evacuation by persons not in official evacuation zones, create enormous traffic management challenges and may actually move persons into areas of higher fallout deposition.

The risk-risk tradeoff between sheltering and evacuation is one of the most technically demanding aspects of nuclear incident response planning, and it requires site-specific modeling, realistic traffic analysis, and honest assessment of public response to protective action messaging.

### Potassium Iodide

**Potassium iodide (KI)** is a non-prescription medication that, when taken before or shortly after exposure to radioactive iodine, saturates the thyroid gland and blocks uptake of the radioactive material. It protects specifically against thyroid cancer from radioactive iodine (principally iodine-131), which is released in significant quantities in nuclear power plant accidents involving core damage. It does not protect against other radionuclides, does not prevent whole-body radiation dose, and does not treat any aspect of ARS.

**KI policy** in the United States has evolved through a series of regulatory and guidance documents from NRC, FDA, and DHS. Key policy elements:

The NRC requires that nuclear power plant operators within the Plume Exposure Pathway EPZ make KI available to local emergency response organizations and, in states that choose to participate, to households within [DISTANCE] miles of the plant. Not all states participate equally; pre-distribution practices vary considerably.

FDA has approved KI formulations in both tablet and liquid form, with age-appropriate dosing guidelines. Children under [AGE] years are considered to have the highest thyroid cancer risk from radioactive iodine exposure, followed by adolescents and young adults.

The timing of KI administration is critical. KI is most effective when taken within [TIMEFRAME] hours before exposure or up to [TIMEFRAME] hours after. Its protective benefit declines significantly with delay. This means that pre-positioned community distribution is more operationally effective than emergency distribution at the time of a release.

Contraindications to KI include thyroid disease, certain skin disorders, and rare iodine hypersensitivity. These affect a small minority of the population, but communication during a public distribution event must acknowledge them. Persons with hyperthyroidism or thyroid cancer who take KI may experience serious adverse effects; they should consult a healthcare provider and consider alternative protective actions.

**KI equity considerations** are significant. Communities around nuclear power plants include lower-income, rural, and non-English-speaking households that may have lower rates of prior KI acquisition, lower awareness of distribution points, and greater barriers to accessing emergency dispensing operations. Pre-distribution programs that reach these populations through schools, community organizations, and translated materials are substantially more effective than relying on individuals to independently acquire KI from pharmacies.

### Long-Term Protective Actions: Food and Water

Following a significant radiological release, protective actions extend beyond the immediate emergency phase to address ingestion pathway risks. Radioactive iodine in milk, leafy vegetables, and drinking water can deliver significant thyroid dose to populations within the Ingestion Pathway EPZ. Cesium-137 and strontium-90 contamination of soil can affect agricultural production for years to decades, as demonstrated by post-Chernobyl agricultural restrictions in parts of Europe.

FDA and USDA have established derived intervention levels (DILs) for radionuclide concentrations in food and water that trigger protective actions including food embargoes, water use restrictions, and agricultural controls. State departments of agriculture and environmental quality agencies play central roles in implementing these longer-term protective actions.

## 20.6 Nuclear Detonation Response and Planning

A nuclear detonation in a U.S. city would be a catastrophic event unlike any peacetime emergency management scenario in the contemporary era. Federal planning nonetheless proceeds from a clear operational principle: even in a nuclear attack, effective response saves lives. The damage from fallout, the dominant radiation injury mechanism beyond the immediate blast zone, is substantially preventable through protective action.

### The 1-2-3 Guidance Framework

FEMA and the Department of Homeland Security developed a simplified public guidance framework for nuclear detonation scenarios based on the operational imperative to communicate rapidly with a shocked public. The framework is often described as **Get inside, Stay inside, Stay tuned**:

**Get inside.** Immediately after a nuclear detonation, persons outside the immediate blast and fire damage zone should move into the nearest substantial building. Any building is better than remaining outdoors.

**Stay inside.** Remain inside for at least the first [TIMEFRAME] hours after the detonation. Fallout dose rates drop dramatically during this period as short-lived isotopes decay. Persons who shelter effectively for the first 24 hours receive a fraction of the dose they would receive outdoors.

**Stay tuned.** Monitor emergency broadcasts for instructions about when and how to evacuate, whether areas are safe, and where to go for assistance.

The simplicity of this message is intentional. In the chaos immediately following a nuclear detonation, the public will not absorb complex instructions. A three-part directive that people can remember without prompting is operationally superior to technically complete but behaviorally intractable guidance.

### Zones of Concern

Federal nuclear detonation planning maps physical effects into zones that correspond to different response and protective action strategies. For a [YIELD] kiloton surface burst (the NPS-1 scenario):

The **severe damage zone** extends approximately [DISTANCE] miles from the detonation point in some directions depending on yield and surface conditions. Survival is unlikely for most within this zone; search and rescue is extremely hazardous due to structural collapse, fire, and very high radiation levels.

The **moderate damage zone** extends further and contains large numbers of injured survivors. Blast and thermal injury predominate. Medical response from outside and evacuation of ambulatory survivors are priority operations.

The **light damage zone** and areas beyond include populations who may be physically uninjured but are within potential fallout deposition areas depending on wind and plume direction. Protective action compliance in this zone is the primary determinant of radiation dose accumulation.

The **fallout plume** extends downwind and can deposit significant contamination at distances of [DISTANCE] miles or more from a surface detonation, depending on yield and meteorological conditions. The shape of the plume, its intensity, and its geographic extent cannot be precisely predicted without real-time atmospheric modeling, which is a core task of FEMA's Consequence Management Modeling System and collaborating meteorological resources.

### The National Planning Scenario 1

The federal government's National Planning Scenario 1 (NPS-1) describes a [YIELD] kiloton IND surface detonation in a major U.S. city. It is the basis for federal, state, and local nuclear incident planning documents, including the Nuclear/Radiological Incident Annex to the National Response Framework (NRF) and the DHS/FEMA Nuclear Detonation Playbook. Key planning assumptions include:

Immediate casualties in the severe and moderate damage zones number in the [QUANTITY]. Radiation injuries from fallout in the light damage zone and downwind areas could significantly increase casualties among those who do not shelter effectively.

Hospitals within the affected region will sustain structural damage, loss of utilities, and staff absenteeism. Regional medical capacity will be severely degraded precisely when mass casualty patient volumes are highest.

Spontaneous evacuation by the public will begin immediately following the detonation and will likely involve hundreds of thousands of vehicles departing in directions that are not coordinated with fallout plume distribution. Traffic control is a life-safety issue in this scenario.

Emergency communications infrastructure may be damaged or overwhelmed. The Emergency Broadcast System, Wireless Emergency Alerts, and social media channels will be simultaneously critical and partially degraded.

### The Nuclear Incident Annex and Federal Support

The **Nuclear/Radiological Incident Annex** (NRIA) to the NRF assigns responsibilities across federal departments and agencies. FEMA coordinates consequence management. DHS/DNDO (Domestic Nuclear Detection Office) supports detection and assessment. NRC provides technical expertise for nuclear power plant events. DOE/NNSA provides emergency response assets including NEST, RAP teams, and the Atmospheric Release Advisory Capability (ARAC) for plume modeling.

The **Defense Coordinating Officer** from the Department of Defense can be activated to support mass casualty operations, logistics, and decontamination at a scale that civilian emergency management cannot sustain independently.

Federal medical assets available for radiological mass casualty response include the National Disaster Medical System, the Strategic National Stockpile (which contains medical countermeasures for radiation injury), and AFRRI, which provides direct consultation, biodosimetry support, and training.

## 20.7 Medical Management of Radiation Casualties

The medical management of radiation casualties challenges healthcare systems in several distinct ways: the need to assess dose without dosimetry in most patients, the resource demands of treating hematopoietic ARS, the delayed presentation of some patients who initially appear well, and the psychological burden on both patients and providers in a contamination incident.

### Initial Triage and Dose Assessment

Radiation triage in a mass casualty scenario uses clinical signs rather than physical dosimetry for most patients. The **REAC/TS (Radiation Emergency Assistance Center/Training Site)** at Oak Ridge, Tennessee, provides 24-hour consultation to clinicians managing radiation casualties and maintains a registry of experience with significant radiation exposures. REMM, the Radiation Emergency Medical Management online resource, provides triage algorithms, treatment protocols, and drug reference information freely accessible to clinicians.

The primary triage tools are:

Serial lymphocyte counts beginning at the time of presentation and repeated at 8, 24, and 48 hours provide biodosimetric estimates. Lymphocytes are particularly radiosensitive; rapid depletion suggests significant whole-body dose.

Time to emesis: vomiting within [TIMEFRAME] hours of exposure, particularly in combination with early diarrhea, suggests doses potentially consistent with severe ARS.

For deliberate RDD or RED scenarios involving identified radionuclides, AFRRI and REAC/TS can provide dose-range estimates based on source characterization and patient positioning.

### Treatment of Hematopoietic ARS

Treatment of hematopoietic ARS is supportive in nature, similar to the management of chemotherapy-induced marrow suppression. Key elements include:

**Infection prevention and treatment**: Patients with severe lymphopenia and neutropenia are at high risk for bacterial, fungal, and viral infections. Reverse isolation, prophylactic antimicrobials, and aggressive treatment of infectious complications are core management strategies.

**Growth factors**: Granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF, filgrastim) and granulocyte-macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF) stimulate hematopoietic recovery and are recommended for patients with projected prolonged severe neutropenia. The SNS maintains stockpiles of growth factor for large-scale radiation casualty scenarios.

**Blood product support**: Platelet transfusions and packed red blood cell transfusions are required for patients with significant thrombocytopenia and anemia.

**Stem cell transplantation**: For patients with very high doses and predicted lethal marrow destruction who lack autologous stem cell recovery potential, allogeneic stem cell transplantation has been used in some cases. Its practical application in a mass casualty scenario with hundreds of patients is extremely limited.

### Decorporation for Internal Contamination

When radioactive material has been inhaled, ingested, or absorbed internally, **decorporation** therapy aims to reduce the time the material remains in the body. Treatments include:

**Prussian blue (ferric hexacyanoferrate)** for cesium-137 and thallium internal contamination. It interrupts enterohepatic recirculation and increases fecal elimination. It is FDA-approved and available in the SNS.

**DTPA (diethylenetriamine pentaacetic acid)** for internal contamination with transuranic elements including plutonium, americium, and curium. Both calcium-DTPA and zinc-DTPA formulations are FDA-approved.

**Potassium iodide** for radioactive iodine, as discussed in Section 20.5, blocks thyroid uptake.

Effective decorporation therapy requires early initiation. For most agents, efficacy declines significantly as the radionuclide distributes from blood into tissues.

### Psychological Dimensions of Medical Management

Clinicians treating patients in a radiological event will encounter profound fear, including fear of contamination, fear of cancer, fear of dying a concealed death, and fear that the provider cannot help. These fears are not unreasonable, but they can drive behavior that complicates care: patients seeking assessment despite trivial or no actual exposure, families reluctant to allow contamination screening, providers hesitant to care for patients they incorrectly believe to pose contamination risks.

Effective communication by healthcare providers is a clinical skill in this context. Clear, direct reassurance that external contamination is removed by showering, that a person who has been decontaminated is safe to be near, and that most persons near a radiological incident will not develop radiation illness reduces both patient panic and provider hesitancy. Mental health support within healthcare facilities managing radiological casualties is as essential as the physical clinical interventions.

## Applied Vignette: The Riverfront Plaza Incident

On a Tuesday morning, a radiological dispersal device detonates outside a convention center in the downtown core of a mid-sized city. The device kills [NUMBER] people immediately from the blast and injures [NUMBER] more with blast and thermal injuries. Radiation monitoring by first responders confirms elevated readings consistent with cesium-137 dispersal in an approximately [DISTANCE] block radius. The Incident Commander requests NNSA RAP team support, establishes a perimeter, and activates the city's radiological emergency plan.

Within [TIMEFRAME] hours, [NUMBER] people present to regional emergency departments seeking evaluation. The majority have received doses far below any acute health threshold; many are not contaminated. Three hospitals initiate decontamination protocols before confirming that patients arriving by private vehicle from outside the immediate blast zone have not been significantly contaminated. Two of the three hospitals lock down their emergency entrances, diverting blast trauma patients who need immediate surgical care.

Public communications in the first two hours are inconsistent. One television station characterizes the event as a "nuclear attack." Two competing shelter-in-place and evacuation messages are broadcast on different channels. Phone networks are overwhelmed within [TIMEFRAME] minutes of the detonation.

The after-action review identifies three systemic failures. First, hospitals lacked pre-established protocols distinguishing blast trauma management from radiological decontamination operations, causing simultaneous lockdown and mass presentation problems. Second, the Joint Information Center was not activated for [TIMEFRAME] hours, creating a communication vacuum that was filled with inaccurate media reports. Third, the city's KI pre-distribution program had reached approximately [PERCENT] of the population within the relevant zone; the majority of residents contacted by the public health department during the event did not know where their KI was or whether this event indicated they should take it. (The cesium-137 dispersal in this event was not an indication for KI administration, but the public did not know that distinction without clear guidance.)

Key lessons: radiological incident planning must clearly separate decontamination protocols from trauma care pathways; Joint Information Center activation should not depend on confirmation of radiological characterization; and KI education must address not just pre-distribution but decision-making about appropriate use cases.

## Key Terms

**Acute Radiation Syndrome (ARS):** A clinical syndrome following significant whole-body exposure to penetrating radiation, characterized by hematopoietic, gastrointestinal, or cardiovascular/CNS injury depending on dose.

**Alpha radiation:** Particulate radiation composed of two protons and two neutrons; significant external range of only a few centimeters in air, highly dangerous if internally deposited.

**Background radiation:** The baseline radiation exposure from natural and medical sources in the environment, approximately [NUMBER] mSv per year in the United States.

**Beta radiation:** High-energy electrons emitted during radioactive decay; can cause skin and eye injury and are dangerous if internally deposited.

**Biodosimetry:** Estimation of absorbed radiation dose using biological indicators, primarily serial lymphocyte counts, in the absence of physical dosimetry.

**Contamination:** The presence of radioactive material on or inside the body; distinct from radiation exposure.

**Decorporation:** Medical treatment to accelerate elimination of internally deposited radioactive material.

**Derived intervention levels (DILs):** Concentration limits for radionuclides in food and water that trigger protective actions under FDA guidelines.

**Deterministic effect:** A radiation health effect with a dose threshold below which it does not occur; severity increases with dose above the threshold.

**Emergency Planning Zone (EPZ):** A geographic area around a nuclear power plant within which state and local plans must provide for specific protective actions.

**Gamma radiation:** High-energy electromagnetic radiation emitted during radioactive decay; penetrates deeply, requires dense shielding.

**Gray (Gy):** Unit of absorbed radiation dose; one joule of energy deposited per kilogram of tissue.

**Hematopoietic syndrome:** The most common form of ARS, characterized by bone marrow suppression; occurs at doses of approximately [NUMBER] Gy or greater.

**Improvised nuclear device (IND):** A crude nuclear explosive constructed by a non-state actor using fissile material.

**Inverse square law:** Physical principle stating that radiation intensity decreases with the square of the distance from a point source.

**Potassium iodide (KI):** A medication that blocks thyroid uptake of radioactive iodine; effective only for iodine isotopes.

**Radiation exposure:** The passage of ionizing radiation through or into the body; does not imply contamination.

**Radiological dispersal device (RDD):** A device combining conventional explosives with radioactive material; a weapon of mass disruption rather than mass destruction.

**Radiological exposure device (RED):** A covert device using radioactive material to expose persons without dispersal or explosion.

**REAC/TS:** Radiation Emergency Assistance Center/Training Site; provides 24-hour medical consultation for radiation casualty management.

**Sievert (Sv):** Unit of effective radiation dose, adjusting absorbed dose for radiation type and tissue sensitivity.

**Stochastic effect:** A radiation health effect occurring probabilistically with increasing dose; cancer is the primary stochastic effect.

## Practitioner Takeaways

Emergency managers and public health professionals approaching radiological and nuclear emergencies should carry several core operational orientations:

The contamination-exposure distinction is the foundation of all radiological emergency operations. Healthcare workers who understand that decontaminated patients do not require hazmat-level PPE will provide better and faster care. Public health communicators who can explain the distinction clearly will reduce unnecessary fear and inappropriate care-seeking behavior.

Shelter-in-place is the most time-sensitive life-saving intervention in a nuclear detonation scenario. Getting the message to the public in the first minutes after a detonation, before fallout arrives, is operationally more important than completing plume modeling. The 1-2-3 guidance framework exists for this reason.

KI pre-distribution gaps are a planning failure with direct health consequences. Review the pre-distribution status and coverage in your jurisdiction. Identify which populations lack KI and build distribution strategies accordingly. Ensure that public information about KI includes not just what it is but when to take it and, critically, when not to take it.

Risk communication for radiological events requires acknowledging the historical record of radiation harm honestly while providing proportionate context. Dismissing public concern as irrational increases distrust and reduces compliance with protective action guidance. Acknowledging that radiation can cause harm while explaining why protective actions are effective is more persuasive than minimizing.

Federal technical resources exist specifically to support local and state responses to radiological events and are more accessible than many practitioners realize. REAC/TS, AFRRI consultation lines, NNSA RAP teams, and the REMM online system are available now, before an incident, for training, planning review, and technical assistance. Build those relationships before you need them.

Equity must be explicitly addressed in radiological emergency planning. Near-nuclear-facility communities, non-English-speaking households, and communities with low baseline trust in government institutions all require deliberate planning attention. Distributing KI to the populations most likely to already have resources is not emergency planning; it is the appearance of emergency planning.

## Chapter Notes

The physics presented in Section 20.1 reflects standard health physics principles applicable to emergency response contexts. Emergency managers do not need to master dosimetry calculations, but a working understanding of radiation types and units enables more effective collaboration with health physicists and radiation safety officers and more credible communication with the public and media.

The clinical ARS dose thresholds referenced in Section 20.3 are presented as approximate ranges reflecting the published literature; individual variation is significant. Clinicians should consult REAC/TS and REMM for current treatment protocols rather than relying on dose threshold values alone.

The 1-2-3 guidance framework described in Section 20.6 represents the federal government's public messaging approach for nuclear detonation scenarios. It has been developed through behavioral research and is intended to be as simple and memorable as possible. Emergency managers who develop supplementary guidance should be careful not to inadvertently add complexity that reduces behavioral compliance.

The equity dimensions of nuclear facility siting are addressed in Section 20.5 and practitioner takeaways. Students seeking deeper engagement with this topic may consult the literature on environmental justice and nuclear power facility siting, including community characteristics of EPZ populations compared to national averages, as well as the historical record of nuclear testing and its disproportionate impacts on Indigenous communities and Pacific Islander populations.

The Riverfront Plaza vignette is a composite fictional scenario. The Goiânia accident referenced in Section 20.2 is a documented historical event; all numerical details from that event are bracketed per anti-hallucination protocol and should be verified against primary sources before use in teaching or operational contexts.
