# Chapter 21: Extreme Heat and Cold Events

## Chapter Overview

Extreme temperature events are among the most lethal natural hazards in the United States and globally, yet they receive far less public and institutional attention than hurricanes, earthquakes, or floods. Heat kills more Americans in a typical year than any other weather-related hazard. Cold-related deaths, while less often tallied as a distinct category, add substantially to the mortality burden during winter emergencies, and cold exposure drives mass casualty events in contexts ranging from winter storms to homelessness crises to high-altitude search-and-rescue operations.

What makes temperature extremes particularly challenging for emergency managers is their invisibility. A hurricane has a name, a track, and a landfall date. An extreme heat event often has none of those features in the early days; it arrives gradually, kills silently, and concentrates its lethality in populations that are already marginalized and already invisible to public health surveillance systems. Cold events can be similarly insidious when they affect unsheltered populations, elderly individuals in poorly insulated housing, or workers whose occupational exposure is inadequately tracked.

This chapter provides emergency managers with a working framework for understanding the physiology of heat- and cold-related illness, the surveillance systems used to detect mortality signals in real time, the operational structure of cooling and warming center programs, and the increasingly important intersection of climate change with emergency planning. It also addresses equity dimensions that are embedded throughout temperature emergency response: who dies, who survives, and what structural conditions produce that difference.

## 21.1 The Physiology of Heat-Related Illness

The human body maintains core temperature within a narrow range — approximately [TEMPERATURE RANGE] degrees Celsius — through a set of mechanisms that include vasodilation, sweating, and behavioral responses such as seeking shade. When ambient heat load exceeds the body's ability to dissipate it, core temperature rises, and a predictable cascade of increasingly severe physiological failures follows.

**Heat Cramps** are painful, involuntary muscle contractions, typically in the large muscle groups of the legs and abdomen, associated with exertion in heat. They result from sodium and fluid loss through heavy sweating. Treatment is straightforward: rest in a cool environment, fluid and electrolyte replacement. Heat cramps are significant primarily as a sentinel: a worker or event attendee experiencing cramps is on a trajectory that requires intervention.

**Heat Syncope** is a transient loss of consciousness caused by peripheral vasodilation and venous pooling, often precipitated by prolonged standing in heat or sudden postural change. Cardiac output falls, cerebral perfusion drops, and the person faints. The person typically recovers quickly with supine positioning. Heat syncope is important for emergency managers because it generates calls for medical assistance at outdoor events, construction sites, and military training operations at a rate that can overwhelm resources during prolonged heat events.

**Heat Exhaustion** represents a more serious failure of thermoregulatory compensation. Core temperature is elevated, typically below [TEMPERATURE THRESHOLD] degrees Celsius, but the person is still sweating and still peripherally vasodilated. Symptoms include heavy sweating, cool and pale skin, nausea, headache, dizziness, and weakness. Mental status may be mildly altered. Treatment is aggressive cooling: move to a cool environment, remove excess clothing, apply cool water and fanning, provide oral hydration if the person can swallow safely, and consider intravenous fluids. Most people with heat exhaustion recover fully with prompt treatment. Delay allows progression to heat stroke.

**Heat Stroke** is a medical emergency defined by core hyperthermia — typically core temperature above [TEMPERATURE THRESHOLD] degrees Celsius — plus central nervous system dysfunction. CNS dysfunction is the clinical marker that distinguishes heat stroke from heat exhaustion; it manifests as confusion, agitation, bizarre behavior, seizures, or loss of consciousness. There are two clinical variants: classic heat stroke, which affects elderly and chronically ill individuals during prolonged ambient heat exposure, and exertional heat stroke, which affects young, physically active individuals and can occur even at moderate ambient temperatures if exercise intensity is high.

Mortality from heat stroke depends almost entirely on how quickly core temperature is lowered. Every minute of sustained hyperthermia adds to organ injury. The preferred cooling method in exertional heat stroke is cold water immersion (ice bath), which achieves faster cooling than other methods. For classic heat stroke in a community setting, evaporative cooling (water mist plus fanning) is commonly used because immersion is impractical at scale. Field-level responders should cool first, transport second — the old "cool and transport" sequence has been updated in military and athletic medicine guidelines to prioritize aggressive field cooling before moving the patient.

Complications of heat stroke include rhabdomyolysis (skeletal muscle breakdown leading to myoglobulinuria and renal failure), disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC), hepatic injury, and multi-organ failure. Mortality rates for untreated or undertreated heat stroke are [PERCENT]; with rapid aggressive cooling, mortality rates fall substantially, though long-term neurological sequelae can persist.

**Special Populations in Heat**

Risk is not uniformly distributed. Several physiological and social factors dramatically elevate heat vulnerability:

*Age.* Infants and young children have high surface-area-to-body-mass ratios, limited sweating capacity, and complete dependence on caregivers to modify their environment. Older adults have reduced thirst sensation, diminished cardiovascular reserve, decreased sweating efficiency, and often live alone — meaning no one notices when they stop responding to heat. The [YEAR] European heat wave, [YEAR] Chicago heat wave, and [YEAR] Pacific Northwest heat dome event all demonstrated that the majority of heat deaths occur in people over [AGE] years of age.

*Chronic Illness.* Heart failure limits the cardiac reserve needed to increase output for cutaneous blood flow. Diabetes affects sweat gland function and autonomic regulation. Renal disease impairs fluid management. Obesity increases metabolic heat production and insulates against heat dissipation. Psychiatric illness — particularly schizophrenia and bipolar disorder — is independently associated with heat death through multiple pathways including impaired thermal perception, behavioral disruption, and medication effects.

*Medications.* Several medication classes directly impair thermoregulation. Anticholinergics (including antihistamines, antipsychotics, bladder medications, and some antidepressants) impair sweating. Diuretics reduce fluid reserves. Beta-blockers and calcium channel blockers limit the cardiovascular response to heat stress. Alpha-agonists and stimulants increase metabolic heat production. Emergency managers should understand that a significant portion of the at-risk population is taking one or more of these agents, and that medication burden explains part of the age-related risk profile.

*Socioeconomic Status and Housing.* Lack of air conditioning is the single most modifiable risk factor for heat death in the United States. Air conditioning ownership is strongly correlated with income and is lower in manufactured housing, older multifamily buildings, and low-income rental housing. People who cannot afford to run air conditioning during a heat event — because they fear high utility bills — may have functional access to a resource that they don't use. Urban heat island effects concentrate heat in lower-income neighborhoods with less tree canopy and more paved surfaces. Social isolation (living alone, lack of social network check-ins) dramatically increases risk by eliminating the informal surveillance that might otherwise prompt a welfare check.

*Outdoor Workers.* Agricultural workers, construction workers, utility workers, and others who work outdoors have unavoidable heat exposure during their working hours. Wage pressure and immigration status create barriers to behavioral protection. Occupational Heat illness reporting is incomplete; emergency managers responding to heat events in agricultural areas should specifically coordinate with worker advocacy organizations and agricultural employers.

## 21.2 The Physiology of Cold-Related Illness

Cold injury follows a parallel logic to heat injury: the body can compensate up to a point, compensation eventually fails, and failure follows a predictable cascade that becomes life-threatening.

**Peripheral Cold Injuries: Frostnip and Frostbite**

Frostnip is superficial, reversible cold injury characterized by numbness and pallor of the skin. No ice crystals have formed in tissue; the skin rewarms easily with gentle heat and recovers completely. Frostnip is significant as a behavioral warning: a person experiencing frostnip is at risk for frostbite if cold exposure continues.

Frostbite is defined by actual ice crystal formation in tissue, causing cellular death and vascular injury. Severity classification parallels burn depth:

*Superficial frostbite* (equivalent to partial-thickness burn) involves the skin and subcutaneous tissue. The affected area appears white or grayish, is firm or waxy to the touch, and may blister during rewarming. Recovery is possible with proper treatment but blistering and desquamation are common.

*Deep frostbite* (equivalent to full-thickness burn) involves deeper tissues including muscle and bone. The area is hard, wooden, and insensate. Large hemorrhagic blisters form during rewarming. Tissue loss — sometimes requiring amputation — is likely.

Rewarming protocols for frostbite are counterintuitive: field rewarming of deep frostbite is generally discouraged unless the person can be kept warm continuously, because freeze-thaw-freeze cycles cause substantially worse tissue injury than sustained freezing. Keep the extremity frozen and warm the whole person. Rapid rewarming (warm water immersion at [TEMPERATURE RANGE] degrees Celsius) in a hospital setting produces better outcomes than field rewarming with improvised heat sources.

Iloprost, a prostacyclin analogue, has shown benefit in reducing amputation rates in severe frostbite when administered within [TIMEFRAME] of rewarming in specialized centers. Emergency managers should be aware that optimal frostbite outcomes require rapid access to hospitals capable of vascular surgery consultation and potentially hyperbaric oxygen therapy. Pre-hospital triage decisions should factor in transport time to appropriate facilities.

**Hypothermia**

Hypothermia is defined as core body temperature below [TEMPERATURE THRESHOLD] degrees Celsius. The Swiss staging system, now widely adopted, classifies hypothermia by clinical presentation rather than precise temperature measurement (which is often unavailable in the field):

- *Stage I (mild hypothermia):* Shivering, alert, able to assist with rescue. Core temperature approximately [TEMPERATURE RANGE] degrees Celsius.
- *Stage II (moderate hypothermia):* Shivering absent or minimal, altered mental status, impaired coordination. Core temperature approximately [TEMPERATURE RANGE] degrees Celsius.
- *Stage III (severe hypothermia):* Unconscious, absent reflexes, severe cardiovascular instability. Core temperature approximately [TEMPERATURE RANGE] degrees Celsius.
- *Stage IV:* Apparent death — no vital signs, fixed pupils, ventricular fibrillation or asystole.

The maxim "no one is dead until they are warm and dead" reflects the clinical reality that hypothermic cardiac arrest has a substantially different prognosis than normothermic cardiac arrest. Patients in cardiac arrest from hypothermia have survived neurologically intact after resuscitation from core temperatures as low as [TEMPERATURE THRESHOLD] degrees Celsius when warming was accomplished via extracorporeal membrane oxygenation (ECMO). This is a critical dispatch and triage point: hypothermic cardiac arrest warrants aggressive resuscitation and transport to an ECMO-capable center when feasible.

Rewarming strategies are categorized by severity:

*Passive external rewarming* (remove wet clothing, insulate, warm environment) is appropriate for mild hypothermia in a person who is still shivering.

*Active external rewarming* (heating blankets, warm packs to axilla/groin) is used for moderate hypothermia, with the caveat that applying external heat to extremities in severe hypothermia can cause afterdrop — cold blood returning from the periphery drops core temperature further as it reaches the heart, precipitating dysrhythmia.

*Active internal rewarming* (warm IV fluids, warm humidified oxygen, thoracic lavage, peritoneal lavage, ECMO) is reserved for severe hypothermia or cardiac arrest. ECMO provides the fastest and most reliable core rewarming and simultaneously supports cardiac output.

**Special Populations in Cold**

Risk factors for cold-related illness and death mirror those for heat in several ways and diverge in others:

*Homelessness.* The most important risk factor for cold death in urban settings is unsheltered homelessness. Unsheltered individuals face sustained cold exposure with no ability to reliably access shelter, dry clothing, or caloric intake sufficient to sustain thermogenesis. Urban counts routinely underestimate the unsheltered population; emergency managers should assume that official homeless counts undercount actual exposure risk. Warming center programs, outreach teams, and hotel-based overflow shelter partnerships are the primary protective interventions.

*Alcohol and Substance Use.* Alcohol causes vasodilation and impairs thermogenesis, creating a dangerous combination: the person feels warm, loses heat rapidly, and lacks the judgment to seek shelter. Alcohol intoxication accounts for a substantial proportion of hypothermia deaths, and cold-weather emergency response must integrate behavioral health and substance use disorder services, not just traditional shelter operations.

*Poverty and Housing Quality.* Cold weather power outages disproportionately affect lower-income populations in poorly insulated housing, older electrical infrastructure, and manufactured housing that is difficult to heat efficiently. Carbon monoxide poisoning from improper use of generators, camp stoves, and charcoal grills indoors is a major secondary hazard of cold-weather power outages; emergency managers should include CO poisoning prevention messaging in all winter storm communications.

*Infants and Elderly.* Infants lack thermogenesis capacity through shivering and are entirely dependent on caregivers for environmental temperature management. Elderly individuals have reduced metabolic heat production, thinner subcutaneous fat, and often reduced awareness of ambient temperature. Indoor hypothermia — an elderly person found unconscious in a home that has lost power or heat in winter — is more common than emergency managers typically appreciate.

*Mental Illness.* Psychosis, severe depression, and dementia impair cold recognition and appropriate protective behavior. People with severe mental illness are overrepresented among cold-related deaths in urban settings.

## 21.3 Heat Surveillance and Mortality Monitoring

Detecting a heat event's health impact in real time is harder than it sounds. Death certificates are a lagging indicator: they take days to weeks to be coded and tabulated. Even after coding, heat is systematically undercounted as a cause of death because physicians completing death certificates often list the immediate cause (cardiac arrest, respiratory failure, renal failure) without identifying heat as the precipitating condition.

**Excess Mortality Analysis**

The methodological solution is excess mortality analysis: rather than counting deaths attributed to heat, count all deaths occurring during a defined period and compare them to the expected number based on historical baselines adjusted for season and trend. Any deaths above the expected baseline represent "excess mortality" that can reasonably be attributed to the event, even when heat does not appear on the death certificate.

Excess mortality analysis requires near-real-time access to death certificate filings, which is available in some jurisdictions and not others. The CDC National Center for Health Statistics operates a mortality surveillance system that provides provisional death count data at the national and state level with [TIMEFRAME] lag in some reporting periods. Many local health departments also monitor emergency department visits and chief complaints related to heat, providing a faster signal than death data.

**Syndromic Surveillance**

Syndromic surveillance systems — which monitor emergency department chief complaints and ICD codes for heat-related diagnoses in near-real-time — can detect a heat emergency's health impact within [TIMEFRAME] of the event's onset. The CDC BioSense Platform and state-based syndromic surveillance systems (such as the Electronic Surveillance System for the Early Notification of Community-based Epidemics, ESSENCE) are the primary national frameworks. Emergency managers should know whether their jurisdiction participates in syndromic surveillance and what the activation threshold is for sharing data with emergency operations centers.

**Heat Vulnerability Indices**

Several research groups and public health agencies have developed heat vulnerability indices (HVIs) that combine social vulnerability indicators (poverty, isolation, age, lack of air conditioning), environmental factors (urban heat island intensity, green space access, surface temperature), and built environment characteristics (housing age, building density) into spatial maps identifying neighborhoods with the highest expected mortality during a heat event. HVIs are useful for pre-event resource targeting: where should cooling buses concentrate outreach? Where should additional cooling centers be opened? Which census tracts should receive proactive welfare check calls?

**Real-Time Temperature Monitoring**

Emergency Heat Watches, Heat Advisories, and Excessive Heat Warnings are issued by the National Weather Service. The threshold criteria vary by region — a heat index of [TEMPERATURE THRESHOLD] degrees Fahrenheit in a region where that temperature is routine carries different health implications than the same heat index in a region where residents lack air conditioning adaptation. Several jurisdictions have worked with their local NWS forecast office to develop region-specific thresholds that account for local climate, housing stock, and population demographics. Emergency managers should understand their local NWS threshold criteria and the expected health response.

## 21.4 Cooling Center Operations

Cooling centers are publicly accessible, air-conditioned spaces designated during heat emergencies where anyone can seek refuge. In concept, they are simple. In practice, they are complex public health interventions that fail to protect the most vulnerable people unless designed explicitly to overcome the barriers those people face.

**Siting and Facility Selection**

Effective cooling centers must be geographically proximate to the highest-risk populations, ideally identified using heat vulnerability index mapping. Common facility types include public libraries, community centers, senior centers, faith-based facilities, and public schools. Indoor air conditioning capacity matters more than aesthetic suitability: a facility that cannot maintain indoor temperatures below [TEMPERATURE THRESHOLD] degrees Fahrenheit during peak outdoor temperatures is not a functional cooling center.

Cooling center location is often determined by political considerations (which elected official wants to host one), facility availability, and existing relationships rather than systematic vulnerability mapping. Emergency managers should advocate for data-driven siting and document the process to identify coverage gaps.

**Hours of Operation**

The hottest and most lethal hours during a heat event are typically mid-afternoon. However, nighttime is critically important: people who cannot cool their living spaces overnight do not recover physiologically from daytime heat stress, and multi-day heat events kill through cumulative heat load more than single-day temperature peaks. Cooling centers that close at 5:00 PM or 8:00 PM fail to address overnight thermal burden. Twenty-four-hour operation is operationally complex and expensive but is essential for centers serving the highest-risk populations, particularly unsheltered individuals.

**Transportation Access**

Many of the people most at risk from heat — elderly, low-income, disabled, without personal vehicles — cannot reach cooling centers without transportation assistance. Cooling center programs that rely on self-transport systematically underserve the population that needs them most. Transit agencies can increase service frequency on routes serving high-vulnerability areas during heat emergencies. Some jurisdictions operate dedicated cooling shuttle routes. Others partner with ride-share companies for subsidized trips. All of these approaches require pre-event planning and funding mechanisms established before the emergency occurs.

**Outreach to Isolated Individuals**

The most effective heat emergency intervention may be direct outreach — a welfare check phone call, a knock on the door — to isolated elderly people who will not seek cooling center services voluntarily. Telephone reassurance programs, which use trained volunteers or staff to call isolated elderly individuals, have been associated with reduced mortality in multiple heat events. Door-to-door outreach programs can be deployed through partnerships with postal workers (who visit almost every address), utility meter readers, and community health worker networks.

The challenge is scale. During the [YEAR] Paris heat wave, welfare check systems were overwhelmed by the volume of requests; prioritization criteria and capacity planning must be established before the emergency. Registries of isolated elderly and medically fragile individuals — maintained in collaboration with home health agencies, meal delivery programs, and social services — provide the most efficient targeting for outreach resources.

**Communication and Warning**

Heat emergency communications must reach populations that are isolated, have low health literacy, speak languages other than English, and may not trust government-issued advisories. Several communication failures are common in heat emergencies: official advisories are issued in English only; media coverage assumes internet access; messaging focuses on symptoms (seek help when you feel dizzy) rather than anticipatory action (go to a cooling center before you feel unwell). 

People do not reliably recognize when they are in danger from heat because one of the early physiological effects of heat stress is reduced cognitive function. Behavioral communications should emphasize external cues (What is the temperature? Is there air conditioning?) rather than internal cues (Do you feel sick?) and should prescribe specific actions rather than general advice.

**Cultural Competence and Population-Specific Barriers**

Cooling center usage rates vary significantly across demographic groups for reasons that go beyond proximity. Among elderly East Asian immigrant populations in some cities, nursing home residents, and other groups, cultural norms around not publicly displaying physical need, distrust of government facilities, language barriers, and unfamiliarity with the cooling center concept create low utilization rates even among people with high heat vulnerability. Co-siting cooling functions with trusted community institutions (ethnic community centers, churches, temples, mosques) and deploying outreach workers with shared language and cultural background are evidence-informed strategies for reaching these populations.

## 21.5 Cold Weather Operations: Warming Centers and Winter Emergency Response

Warming center operations share structural similarities with cooling center programs but have several distinctive features driven by the different risk population, the longer duration of cold emergencies, and the role of homelessness as the primary risk driver.

**Warming Center Siting and Operation**

Warming centers should be accessible by foot, since many of the highest-risk individuals are unsheltered and without transportation. Location near high-density homeless encampments, overnight shelters, and meal programs increases utilization. Twenty-four-hour access is particularly important for unsheltered individuals who cannot safely wait outdoors for a center to open at 7:00 AM.

Basic services at warming centers should include warmth, dry clothing exchange, food and hydration, and access to behavioral health and substance use disorder support. Security planning must address the reality that warming centers serving chronically homeless populations may include individuals with active mental illness, active substance use, and histories of conflict. Security staffing and de-escalation protocols require careful pre-event planning; criminalized or unwelcoming environments drive away the people who most need services.

**Street Outreach Teams**

For the unsheltered population that will not voluntarily access warming centers — a substantial minority in most cold-weather cities — proactive outreach is essential. Street outreach teams, typically partnerships between public health departments, social service agencies, and in some cases fire departments, go to people in encampments, doorways, transit stations, and other locations where unsheltered individuals shelter during cold weather. Outreach workers offer transport to warming centers, provide blankets and hand warmers, conduct health assessments, and document individuals who refuse shelter.

Documentation of shelter refusals is operationally important for two reasons: it creates a record for welfare check follow-up, and in some jurisdictions it triggers legal authority to conduct involuntary transport to shelter under extreme cold emergency orders. The threshold, criteria, and legal basis for involuntary transport vary substantially by jurisdiction and carry significant civil liberties implications. Emergency managers should work with legal counsel and homeless advocacy organizations to establish these protocols before a cold emergency occurs.

**Power Outage Response**

Winter storms producing power outages require a distinct operational response that overlaps with but is not identical to warming center operations. When large numbers of households lose heating in winter, the affected population includes many individuals who would not seek warming center services if given a choice — middle-class households, suburban residents, families with young children — but who become vulnerable during extended outages.

Warming sites for power-outage affected populations benefit from different siting (schools, community centers in affected areas), different service arrays (cell phone charging, food preparation, recreational programming for children), and different communication approaches than warming centers primarily serving homeless individuals. Emergency managers should plan for both population types and avoid conflating them operationally in ways that create barriers for either group.

**Carbon Monoxide Surge**

Every significant cold-weather power outage produces a surge in carbon monoxide (CO) poisoning cases from indoor use of generators, propane heaters, charcoal grills, and gas camp stoves. CO is colorless and odorless; initial symptoms (headache, nausea, dizziness) are non-specific; and the behavioral response of sitting down because you feel ill can lead to unconsciousness before anyone calls for help.

Emergency managers must integrate CO poisoning prevention messaging into all winter storm and power outage communications, include CO detector batteries in pre-event distribution programs, and alert poison control centers and emergency departments to anticipate elevated CO presentations. Hospital-based hyperbaric oxygen therapy capacity may be limited; emergency managers in regions with repeated cold-weather power outage risk should map available HBO facilities and develop mass CO poisoning response protocols in advance.

## 21.6 Climate Change and Extreme Temperature Emergency Planning

The planning environment for extreme temperature emergencies is shifting in ways that emergency managers must understand and explicitly plan for, rather than assuming that historical data are adequate guides to future conditions.

**Trends in Extreme Heat**

Multiple lines of evidence document that extreme heat events are becoming more frequent, more intense, longer in duration, and geographically broader than they were in previous decades. The specific drivers are well-established in climate science and are not reviewed in depth here, but the operational implications are significant:

*Historical return periods are no longer reliable.* A heat event that historically occurred once every [TIMEFRAME] may now occur with substantially higher frequency. Response plans calibrated to historical extremes systematically underestimate future risk.

*Nighttime minimum temperatures are increasing faster than daytime maxima in many regions.* This matters because nighttime cooling is the mechanism by which heat-stressed humans and buildings recover from daytime thermal load. As nighttime temperatures rise, that recovery is impaired, and multi-day events become more lethal even if peak daytime temperatures are not dramatically higher than historical events.

*Geographic expansion.* Regions that historically had limited summer heat — the Pacific Northwest, high-elevation communities, northern Europe — have experienced severe heat events that their infrastructure and populations were not prepared for. The [YEAR] Pacific Northwest heat dome event, which produced temperatures exceeding [TEMPERATURE] degrees Fahrenheit in cities with low air conditioning penetration, was one of the deadliest weather events in Canadian history and caused substantial excess mortality across the region.

*Urban heat islands are intensifying.* As urban areas expand and densify, urban heat island effects — which can add [TEMPERATURE DIFFERENCE] degrees Fahrenheit or more of additional thermal stress compared to surrounding rural areas — increase. Lower-income neighborhoods and communities of color are more often located in the hottest urban zones due to historical patterns of disinvestment, highway siting, and land use.

**Cold Weather Trends**

Cold weather planning is complicated by two apparently contradictory but actually compatible trends: overall warming reduces the frequency of severe cold events nationally, but also contributes to increased variability and potentially more extreme cold outbreaks in some regions through alterations to polar vortex dynamics. The "polar vortex" disruption events of [YEAR] and subsequent winters produced extreme cold across the Central and Eastern United States in ways that overwhelmed infrastructure — including electricity generation and distribution — that was not designed for those temperatures.

The planning implication is not that cold weather risk is disappearing, but that cold weather risk is shifting in character: fewer consistently cold days, but greater risk of extreme, out-of-season cold events that catch populations and infrastructure unprepared. Energy infrastructure hardening, mutual aid agreements for cross-state heating fuel distribution, and pre-season preparation protocols for warming center activation are key planning priorities.

**Climate Adaptation Planning**

Several elements of climate adaptation planning are directly relevant to emergency managers dealing with extreme temperature:

*Urban heat island mitigation.* Tree planting, cool roof programs, green infrastructure, and reflective pavement programs reduce ambient urban temperatures, with the largest benefits concentrated in the hottest and most densely populated neighborhoods. These are long-lead-time interventions requiring sustained investment, but emergency managers can serve as advocates for them in community planning processes.

*Building code reform.* Air conditioning access in new construction, attic insulation standards, and heat resilience standards for low-income housing rehabilitation are prevention-focused interventions that emergency managers can support within broader community resilience planning frameworks.

*Infrastructure resilience.* Electrical grid hardening, backup power systems for critical facilities (including cooling and warming centers), and reduced dependence on single-point-of-failure heating and cooling systems are infrastructure priorities with direct emergency management implications.

*Vulnerable population registries.* As climate change increases the frequency of temperature emergencies, the case for establishing and maintaining pre-event registries of vulnerable individuals — elderly living alone, home health patients, adults with serious mental illness, medically fragile individuals — becomes stronger. These registries must navigate privacy concerns, require ongoing maintenance, and depend on voluntary participation. Their development is a long-term public health infrastructure investment with emergency management applications.

## 21.7 Equity and Structural Determinants of Temperature Emergency Outcomes

Temperature emergencies do not kill randomly. They follow the contours of social structure with striking consistency, concentrating mortality in populations that have experienced the cumulative effects of poverty, discrimination, disinvestment, and marginalization. Understanding this pattern is not merely an exercise in academic equity analysis; it is essential for effective resource targeting and program design.

**The Air Conditioning Gap**

In the United States, air conditioning ownership is near-universal in affluent households and substantially lower in low-income households. The gap is larger for renters than owners, for manufactured housing than site-built housing, for older multifamily buildings than newer construction. Jurisdictions in which air conditioning was historically uncommon because of mild summers — the Pacific Northwest, parts of the Northeast, and most of Northern Europe — have large populations without air conditioning in any housing type.

In jurisdictions where summer temperatures were historically moderate, air conditioning is often absent not only from residences but from schools, elder care facilities, and community centers — the very institutions that might otherwise serve as cooling refuge. Emergency managers in these regions must anticipate that future heat events will require more intensive public intervention than historical experience suggests.

**Housing Conditions and Urban Heat Islands**

The urban heat island effect is not geographically random within cities. Historical urban planning decisions — highway routing, industrial zoning, park placement, housing investment patterns — have systematically concentrated heat-absorbing infrastructure in lower-income neighborhoods and neighborhoods with higher proportions of residents of color. These same neighborhoods often have lower tree canopy coverage, more impervious surface, and less cooling infrastructure.

The health consequence is that people in the hottest urban micro-environments are also more likely to be poor, to lack air conditioning, and to have the chronic illness burden that increases heat vulnerability. The interaction of these factors is multiplicative, not merely additive.

**Occupational Exposure**

Agricultural workers, outdoor construction workers, and service workers in non-air-conditioned environments are exposed to heat during working hours regardless of ambient conditions. Wage theft, immigration status, lack of legal protections, language barriers, and fear of job loss create barriers to heat protection behaviors — taking breaks in shade, drinking water, stopping work during peak heat hours — even where those practices are legally required. Federal and state OSHA heat standards have historically been incomplete or unenforced in agriculture; emergency managers working in agricultural regions must engage directly with labor advocates and farm worker health organizations in addition to traditional emergency management partners.

**Language and Communication Barriers**

Heat and cold emergency communications in English only fail to protect populations that primarily speak other languages. This is not an abstract concern: emergency cooling center locations, behavioral instructions, and warning thresholds must reach Spanish-speaking, Cantonese-speaking, Somali-speaking, and other language communities through trusted channels in their communities. Translation of official materials is necessary but not sufficient; trusted community messengers and culturally specific outreach are required to reach populations with reason to distrust official communications.

**Documentation Status and Service Access**

Undocumented immigrants may avoid warming and cooling centers that appear affiliated with government agencies out of fear of immigration enforcement. This fear is rational given enforcement patterns in some jurisdictions, and it creates a population that is simultaneously highly vulnerable (often working outdoors, often in substandard housing) and systematically excluded from formal protection systems. Emergency managers should work with immigrant advocacy organizations, consular offices, and community-based organizations to establish clear, credible commitments that temperature emergency services will not be used for immigration enforcement, and to deploy outreach through trusted community channels rather than relying on official facilities.

## Applied Vignette: The [CITY] Heat Emergency

The forecast came in on a Thursday afternoon in [MONTH] [YEAR]: an unprecedented heat event expected to bring temperatures [TEMPERATURE DIFFERENCE] degrees Fahrenheit above historical highs for three to five consecutive days, with nighttime lows remaining above [TEMPERATURE THRESHOLD] degrees Fahrenheit. Air conditioning penetration in the affected metropolitan area was [PERCENT], and the heat vulnerability index identified [NUMBER] census tracts in the core urban area as extremely high-risk.

The county health department activated its heat emergency plan and opened [NUMBER] cooling centers at public libraries and community centers. Public communication went out through the emergency alert system, local television, and the county website. Transit service was extended by two hours to facilitate access to cooling centers.

By day two, the county medical examiner was reporting [NUMBER] heat-related deaths, and heat-related emergency department visits were [PERCENT] above the 95th percentile baseline in the syndromic surveillance system. The cooling centers were running at [PERCENT] of capacity — well-attended but not overwhelmed.

The deaths were not happening in the cooling centers. They were happening in homes.

Post-event analysis revealed three converging failures:

*First, the outreach program was inadequate.* The county's telephone reassurance program had a roster of [NUMBER] isolated elderly individuals, approximately [PERCENT] of the estimated isolated elderly population in the affected census tracts. Calls were completed within [TIMEFRAME], but [NUMBER] individuals on the roster did not answer their phones and were not followed up with door-to-door visits due to insufficient outreach staffing.

*Second, the communication strategy failed to reach the highest-risk population.* Older adults who were later identified as heat deaths were disproportionately recent immigrants, did not have air conditioning, and were either not reached by the alert system or received messaging in a language they could not read. Several lived in apartment buildings where the building manager had not received any communication about heat emergency resources. Building manager outreach was not part of the plan.

*Third, the overnight gap was uncorrected.* Several of the deaths occurred in the early morning hours, after days of accumulated thermal load. The cooling centers had closed at 10:00 PM. There was no overnight cooling option other than emergency department visits, and several families reported that their relatives had been awake and distressed through the night but had not wanted to "bother" emergency services.

Post-event recommendations: expand the isolated elderly registry to [PERCENT] coverage using home health agency records and utility company data; establish protocols for door-to-door follow-up when phone outreach fails; require 24-hour operation at designated high-risk-area cooling centers; and implement apartment building manager outreach as a formal element of the heat emergency communication protocol.

## Key Terms

**Afterdrop:** The paradoxical decrease in core temperature that can occur during rewarming from hypothermia as cold blood returns from peripheral tissues to the core.

**Classic Heat Stroke:** Heat stroke occurring in sedentary individuals, typically elderly, as a result of prolonged passive heat exposure rather than exertion.

**Cooling Center:** A publicly accessible, air-conditioned facility designated during heat emergencies for use by members of the public seeking thermal relief.

**Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC):** A life-threatening coagulopathy characterized by simultaneous clotting and bleeding; a serious complication of heat stroke.

**ECMO (Extracorporeal Membrane Oxygenation):** A form of mechanical cardiopulmonary support capable of simultaneously warming profoundly hypothermic patients while maintaining oxygenation; the standard of care for hypothermic cardiac arrest.

**Excess Mortality:** The number of deaths above the expected baseline during a defined period, used to estimate the total health impact of an event independent of cause-of-death coding.

**Exertional Heat Stroke:** Heat stroke occurring in physically active individuals as a result of exercise-generated heat production exceeding the body's dissipation capacity.

**Frostbite:** Tissue injury caused by ice crystal formation, classified by depth of tissue involvement from superficial to deep.

**Heat Exhaustion:** A heat-related illness characterized by volume depletion, elevated but sub-stroke core temperature, and preserved mental status.

**Heat Index:** A combined measure of ambient temperature and relative humidity that estimates perceived heat stress, also called "apparent temperature."

**Heat Stroke:** A medical emergency characterized by core hyperthermia above a defined threshold plus central nervous system dysfunction; the most severe form of heat-related illness.

**Heat Vulnerability Index (HVI):** A spatial composite measure combining social, environmental, and built-environment variables to identify geographic areas with elevated heat mortality risk.

**Hypothermia:** Core body temperature below [TEMPERATURE THRESHOLD] degrees Celsius, classified by severity (mild, moderate, severe) using the Swiss staging system.

**Rhabdomyolysis:** The breakdown of skeletal muscle tissue, releasing myoglobin into the bloodstream and potentially causing acute kidney injury; a complication of heat stroke and prolonged exertion.

**Swiss Staging System:** A clinical staging system for hypothermia based on observed physiological signs rather than measured temperature, enabling field triage without core temperature measurement.

**Syndromic Surveillance:** Real-time monitoring of emergency department visits, chief complaints, and clinical diagnoses to detect health events, including heat emergencies, before formal case counts are available.

**Warming Center:** A publicly accessible, heated space designated during cold emergencies as a refuge for individuals who lack adequate home heating or are unsheltered.

## Practitioner Takeaways

Heat and cold events kill through predictable mechanisms in predictable populations via predictable systemic failures. Several specific things are worth carrying forward:

The most lethal heat events are not always the hottest ones. Multi-day events with elevated overnight lows kill more effectively than single-day extremes because they prevent the recovery that limits cumulative thermal load. Plan for duration, not just peak temperature.

Cooling center utilization by high-risk populations is reliably lower than expected when programs are designed around institutional convenience rather than access barriers. Transportation, hours, cultural competence, and language access are not optional features; they determine whether the program reaches the people who need it most.

Excess mortality analysis will always reveal more heat deaths than direct attribution. Calibrate your event assessment accordingly and do not use cause-of-death counts as your primary metric of health impact.

For cold emergencies, the unsheltered population is your highest-acuity operational challenge. Warming centers that are inaccessible, inhospitable, or located far from where unsheltered individuals actually are will not be used. Street outreach is not optional supplementary activity; it is the primary intervention for the highest-risk population.

Carbon monoxide is a guaranteed secondary hazard of every cold-weather power outage. Put CO messaging in every communication, every time, without exception.

Climate change is not a future consideration; it is the current operating environment. Heat event frequencies, intensities, and geographic reach are already outside the historical envelope in many regions. Plans calibrated to 20-year historical data are systematically wrong about the frequency of events you will actually face.

Equity is not a separate program track. The populations most likely to die in temperature emergencies — poor, elderly, isolated, homeless, occupationally exposed, with chronic illness and without air conditioning — are those least served by generic communication strategies and most served by targeted, relationship-based outreach. Resource allocation that ignores this pattern will systematically underperform.

## Chapter Notes

**On Temperature Thresholds:** Core temperature thresholds for heat stroke, hypothermia staging, and rewarming protocols vary somewhat across clinical guidelines and have been updated over time. The Swiss staging system, the Wilderness Medical Society hypothermia protocols, and military heat injury guidelines may differ in specific threshold values and treatment sequences. Emergency managers do not need to resolve these clinical disagreements; they need to know that disagreements exist, that medical directors and clinical advisors should specify local protocols, and that field personnel should be trained to the specific protocol their system uses.

**On Counting Heat Deaths:** The gap between direct cause-of-death attribution and excess mortality analysis is substantial and consistent across events. In major heat events, direct attribution typically captures [PERCENT] to [PERCENT] of true heat-related mortality as estimated by excess mortality analysis. This is not a rounding error; it represents a systematic undercounting that affects resource justification, after-action assessment, and public communication about event severity. Emergency managers advocating for heat emergency program funding should use excess mortality estimates when making the case for resource allocation.

**On Climate Projections:** Climate projections presented in this chapter reflect the scientific literature available at the time of writing. The direction of trends (more frequent, more intense heat events; altered cold event patterns; rising overnight minimums) is robust across climate models and scenarios. Specific magnitude projections for a given region require consultation with regional climate science centers, NOAA Climate Service offices, and state climate offices rather than general textbook presentations.

**On Equity:** This chapter presents disparate heat and cold mortality as a structural problem with structural causes, rather than as a behavioral problem attributable to individual risk-taking. This framing reflects the weight of epidemiological evidence. Emergency managers who frame the problem as individual behavior ("why didn't they just go to the cooling center?") will design programs that systematically underperform. Managers who ask "what structural conditions prevented them from reaching safety, and how can those conditions be changed?" will design programs that work.

**On Integration with Behavioral Health:** Cold weather emergency response, in particular, has deep integration with homelessness systems, substance use disorder services, and serious mental illness services that are not always co-located with emergency management structures. Building those relationships before an emergency is not optional; the primary risk population for cold emergencies is served by systems that may not have emergency management relationships and may not recognize themselves as emergency management partners.
