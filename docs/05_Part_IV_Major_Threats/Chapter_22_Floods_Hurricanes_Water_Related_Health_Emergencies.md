# Chapter 22: Floods, Hurricanes, and Water-Related Health Emergencies

---

## Chapter Overview

Water is simultaneously one of humanity's most essential resources and one of its most destructive forces. Floods and hurricanes account for some of the highest disaster-related mortality totals on record, yet the health consequences of these events extend far beyond the immediate drowning and trauma deaths that dominate early news coverage. The weeks and months following a major flooding event bring a secondary wave of health consequences — waterborne disease outbreaks, toxic exposures, mold-related respiratory illness, vector-borne disease surges, mental health crises, and the disruption of chronic disease management — that can ultimately exceed the direct toll of the event itself.

This chapter addresses the full spectrum of health and medical consequences associated with floods, hurricanes, and other major water-related emergencies. It begins with flood epidemiology and the mechanisms of direct mortality, then addresses the distinctive health profile of hurricanes, which combine flood impacts with wind injury, infrastructure destruction, and prolonged displacement. The chapter devotes significant attention to waterborne disease surveillance and outbreak response, emergency water safety operations, and the environmental health consequences of flood-affected built environments. It closes with the mental health and chronic disease dimensions of flooding and an equity analysis of why flood impacts consistently concentrate in specific communities.

Emergency managers working with water-related disasters must understand that the medical surge threat is not only acute. Planning must account for the slow-building disease burden that emerges as contaminated water infiltrates supply systems, as displaced populations lose medication access, and as chronically underfunded communities confront damaged infrastructure they lacked the resources to maintain before the event.

Upon completing this chapter, students will be able to:

7. Discuss the mental health and chronic disease management challenges that arise in flood-affected populations.
8. Analyze the structural and social determinants that drive differential flood vulnerability and post-disaster recovery capacity.

## 22.1 Flood Epidemiology and Direct Mortality Mechanisms

Floods are the most common and most lethal natural disaster type globally, responsible for an estimated [NUMBER] deaths annually [CITATION]. In the United States, flooding causes more weather-related deaths each year than any other hazard category, including tornadoes and hurricanes [CITATION]. This counterintuitive mortality profile reflects a core epidemiological reality: floods are ubiquitous, frequently sudden, and consistently underestimated by the public. The hazard that kills the most Americans is not the dramatic, photographed tornado — it is the flowing water across a roadway that traps a driver who made a judgment error about depth and current.

### 22.1.1 Drowning as the Dominant Direct Cause

Drowning accounts for approximately [PERCENT] of direct flood fatalities in the United States [CITATION]. A consistent pattern emerges across disaster mortality studies: a disproportionate share of drowning deaths occur in vehicles. The vehicle drowning problem reflects several converging factors. Drivers approach flooded roadways and misjudge the depth and velocity of water — water as shallow as [DEPTH] inches flowing at sufficient velocity can move a standard passenger vehicle. The vehicle provides a false sense of security. Doors become impossible to open against water pressure once the vehicle begins to fill. By the time occupants recognize the danger and attempt escape, egress is already compromised.

Emergency management messaging around the "Turn Around, Don't Drown" campaign addresses this problem directly, and [CITATION] studies have evaluated its effectiveness with mixed findings. The problem is not primarily informational — most drivers know abstractly that flooded roads are dangerous. The problem is perceptual and decisional: people systematically underestimate the danger of specific flooded road crossings they are evaluating in real time, particularly in familiar locations and during time pressure. Effective prevention requires not just messaging but physical barriers, enforcement, and road design features that prevent vehicle entry onto flooded roadways.

Beyond vehicle drowning, direct flood mortality includes pedestrian drowning (individuals swept away while walking, wading, or evacuating on foot), structural collapse (floodwaters undermining foundations, walls, or roofs), and electrocution (downed power lines in flooded areas). The electrocution hazard is particularly underappreciated; standing water in flooded areas can carry lethal current from downed or submerged lines at distances significant enough to create a perimeter exclusion challenge for rescue operations.

### 22.1.2 Indirect and Delayed Mortality

Excess mortality analyses conducted after major flood events consistently find that direct drowning and trauma deaths undercount total flood-related mortality. The mechanisms of indirect and delayed death include:

**Medical care disruption.** Hospital evacuations, road access failures, and loss of dialysis centers, cancer treatment facilities, and surgical capacity create gaps in life-sustaining care. Patients who miss dialysis sessions, chemotherapy cycles, or cardiac monitoring face heightened mortality risk that does not appear in flood death counts.

**Medication loss.** Displacement disrupts medication access for patients with diabetes, heart failure, seizure disorders, psychiatric illness, and other conditions requiring continuous pharmacotherapy. Insulin requiring refrigeration is particularly vulnerable. The downstream mortality from medication disruption is real but distributed across weeks and months and rarely attributed to the flood in surveillance systems.

**Carbon monoxide poisoning.** Generator use during power outages produces CO as a combustion byproduct. After major flood events, emergency departments routinely see CO poisoning surges from generators operated indoors or in garages. This is a consistently predictable and preventable flood health consequence that kills a significant number of people in the recovery phase of major events.

**Hypothermia and hyperthermia.** Loss of heating and cooling systems in flood-affected housing creates temperature vulnerability, particularly for elderly residents who remain in damaged homes rather than evacuating to shelters.

**Cleanup injuries.** The weeks following a flood event produce a predictable surge in injuries: lacerations from debris, falls from ladders during roof or ceiling repair, chainsaw injuries, back injuries from lifting water-saturated materials. These injuries are not captured in flood mortality statistics but represent a significant medical burden that emergency planning often underestimates.

**Mental health-associated mortality.** Suicide rates have been documented to increase following major flood events, with the increase extending well beyond the acute phase [CITATION]. The stress of displacement, property loss, insurance disputes, and recovery delays accumulates over months and contributes to mental health crises discussed further in Section 22.6.

### 22.1.3 Flash Flood Versus Riverine Flood Dynamics

Flood epidemiology differs by flood type. Flash floods — defined by a rapid onset typically within six hours of a causative event such as intense precipitation, dam failure, or ice jam release — produce higher acute mortality per flood area. Warning time is compressed, evacuation opportunity is limited, and water velocity is high. Riverine floods that develop over days to weeks allow more time for warning, evacuation, and preparation, but they tend to affect larger areas, produce longer inundation periods, and generate greater total displacement and infrastructure damage. The health consequences differ accordingly: flash floods produce more drowning and trauma; riverine floods produce more waterborne disease, prolonged shelter need, and slow-building health burden.

Coastal floods associated with storm surge have a distinctive profile: the inundation is saltwater rather than freshwater, which has implications for vegetation mortality, soil contamination, and well water intrusion. Storm surge events associated with hurricanes are addressed in Section 22.2.

## 22.2 Hurricane Health Impacts Across the Storm Cycle

Hurricanes are multi-hazard events. The health consequences of a major hurricane are not reducible to wind damage alone; they include storm surge flooding, inland freshwater flooding from rainfall, tornado spawning, prolonged power outages, infrastructure destruction, and displacement that can last for months to years. A comprehensive hurricane health impact analysis requires examining each phase of the storm cycle.

### 22.2.1 Pre-Landfall Health Threats

The days before hurricane landfall are not medically neutral. Evacuation-related health events include cardiac and pulmonary emergencies triggered by physical exertion, heat exposure, and stress in individuals who should not have been undertaking strenuous activity. Motor vehicle crashes increase with evacuation traffic volume. Patients in dependent care situations — nursing homes, hospitals, dialysis centers — face the difficult calculus of evacuation risk versus shelter-in-place risk. Several of the most catastrophic mortality events in recent United States hurricane history have involved deaths during nursing home evacuations or failures to evacuate [CITATION].

Pre-landfall, emergency managers must coordinate medical needs evacuation for non-ambulatory residents who cannot use general evacuation transportation. Special needs shelters require pre-identified locations, staffing, and supply caches. The operational complexity of medical needs evacuation is addressed further in Chapter 9 (Mass Evacuation and Shelter Operations).

### 22.2.2 Acute Phase: Wind, Surge, and Trauma

During the storm itself, traumatic injury from wind-borne debris is the primary direct injury mechanism. Flying debris at hurricane wind speeds produces penetrating trauma, blunt trauma, and crush injuries. Structural collapse adds entrapment injuries. Storm surge, which accounts for a disproportionate share of hurricane mortality in major events, produces drowning under conditions even more dangerous than riverine flooding — storm surge can rise dramatically fast, may arrive at night, and overtops protective barriers with little warning in some forecast scenarios.

Wound management in a post-storm environment is complicated by the contamination characteristics of floodwater. Lacerations and puncture wounds sustained in floodwater must be treated as contaminated from the outset. Standard wound care principles — thorough irrigation, debridement, appropriate antibiotic coverage — apply, but the specific organisms of concern in flood-water-contaminated wounds include gram-negative organisms, Vibrio species in coastal and estuarine floodwater, and Aeromonas hydrophila. The Vibrio wound infection risk is particularly elevated in Gulf Coast flooding events involving estuarine water exposure; Vibrio vulnificus can produce rapidly progressive soft tissue infection in immunocompromised hosts with a high case fatality rate [CITATION].

### 22.2.3 Infrastructure Destruction and Medical System Failure

Major hurricanes frequently produce simultaneous failure of multiple healthcare infrastructure components: hospital flooding and evacuation, water system damage, pharmacy closure, pharmaceutical cold chain disruption, dialysis center closure, and ambulance service degradation from road damage and fuel scarcity. The [HURRICANE] [YEAR] response illustrated the cumulative consequence of these failures when [CITY] health system capacity was reduced to [PERCENT] of pre-storm levels for [TIMEFRAME] following landfall [CITATION].

The medical needs that do not pause during this infrastructure failure include: dialysis (typically three sessions per week for ESRD patients, with acute consequences for missed sessions), insulin and other diabetes medications, psychiatric medications, chemotherapy and other cancer treatments, and anticoagulation management. Emergency managers must integrate healthcare surge capacity planning with hospital preparedness coordinators, dialysis network emergency coordinators (ESRD Networks), and state health departments to identify and protect continuity mechanisms for these high-dependency populations.

### 22.2.4 Extended Displacement and Recovery Phase Health

Major hurricanes produce displacement measured in months to years rather than days to weeks. The extended displacement experience is associated with a distinctive health burden. Children in displacement shelters and temporary housing show elevated rates of respiratory illness, gastrointestinal illness, and behavioral health symptoms. Adults show elevated rates of cardiovascular events, mental health crises, and exacerbation of chronic disease [CITATION]. The health consequences of inadequate temporary housing — mold exposure, overcrowding, poor sanitation, lack of privacy — are addressed in Section 22.5.

The recovery phase also produces insurance claim disputes, housing contractor fraud, and prolonged administrative uncertainty that compound stress. Individuals whose pre-hurricane housing was already economically marginal face the hardest recovery trajectories; they lack insurance, savings, and social capital to navigate the reconstruction process and may cycle through shelter, temporary housing, and inadequate permanent housing for years.

## 22.3 Waterborne Disease Surveillance and Outbreak Response

Flooding disrupts the physical barriers that normally separate human water supplies from fecal contamination. This fundamental hydraulic fact makes waterborne disease outbreaks a predictable consequence of major flooding events. The detection, characterization, and control of post-flood waterborne disease outbreaks requires a coordinated surveillance system that functions under the degraded conditions a disaster produces.

### 22.3.1 Mechanisms of Post-Flood Water Contamination

Flooding contaminates drinking water supplies through multiple pathways:

**Wellhead submersion.** Private wells whose wellheads are submerged in floodwater are directly contaminated by surface water carrying fecal bacteria, chemical runoff, agricultural waste, and industrial effluents. Well decontamination after submersion is not optional — it requires complete pump removal, disinfection of the casing, testing, and confirmation before the well is returned to service. Emergency managers must anticipate that rural and suburban communities with high private well prevalence will have a significant fraction of their drinking water supply compromised after major flooding events.

**Treatment plant flooding and power failure.** Municipal water treatment plants in flood plains — a siting pattern that reflects the historical use of surface water sources — are vulnerable to direct inundation. Even when the plant itself is not flooded, power failure can interrupt the disinfection step. Loss of chlorine residual in distribution systems allows bacterial growth within the pipe network, converting a nominally treated water supply into a contamination risk.

**Distribution system damage.** Broken water mains create negative pressure zones where contaminants can be pulled into the pipe network from surrounding soil. Cross-connections between distribution systems and sewage infrastructure become relevant when infrastructure is damaged.

**Sewage system overflow.** Combined sewer overflows (CSOs) discharge untreated sewage directly into surface water during heavy rainfall events that exceed system capacity. In major flooding events, the volume of sewage overflow can be staggering, contaminating surface water bodies, coastal areas, and flooded areas with high concentrations of enteric pathogens.

### 22.3.2 Major Waterborne Pathogens of Concern

The pathogen spectrum in post-flood waterborne disease outbreaks differs from routine waterborne disease surveillance. Key pathogens include:

**Enteric bacteria.** Escherichia coli O157:H7 and other Shiga toxin-producing E. coli (STEC), Salmonella, Campylobacter, and Shigella are all associated with flood-related outbreaks. STEC is of particular concern because of the risk of hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS) in children and vulnerable adults.

**Vibrio species.** As noted in Section 22.2, Vibrio vulnificus and Vibrio cholerae are concerns in specific geographic and hydrological contexts. V. cholerae O1 and O139 produce epidemic cholera and must be considered in international disaster response contexts and in events affecting communities with inadequate sanitation infrastructure. In domestic contexts, non-O1/non-O139 Vibrio species can still cause gastroenteritis and wound infections.

**Cryptosporidium and Giardia.** These protozoal parasites are chlorine-resistant and can contaminate treated water supplies following filtration failures. Cryptosporidiosis in immunocompromised hosts can be severe and protracted.

**Hepatitis A virus.** Fecal-oral transmission via contaminated water and food is the primary route. Hepatitis A outbreaks following flooding are well documented in resource-limited settings and can occur domestically in populations with low vaccination coverage.

**Leptospira.** Leptospirosis, caused by spirochetes shed in the urine of infected rodents, is transmitted by contact with contaminated water through skin abrasions or mucous membranes. It is a significant concern in tropical flood events and has caused outbreaks among flood rescue workers and residents doing wading work in contaminated floodwater. Clinical presentation ranges from mild flu-like illness to severe disease with hepatorenal syndrome (Weil's disease).

**Norovirus and rotavirus.** Displacement shelters with congregate living conditions and sanitation challenges create favorable conditions for enteric viral transmission. Shelter outbreaks can develop rapidly and overwhelm facility sanitation resources.

### 22.3.3 Surveillance and Outbreak Detection

Post-flood waterborne disease surveillance operates under two simultaneous pressures: the need for rapid detection of emerging outbreaks and the operational degradation of surveillance systems by the disaster itself. The health departments responsible for waterborne disease surveillance may be operating from damaged facilities, with displaced staff, with disrupted data systems, and while managing multiple competing response functions.

Core surveillance mechanisms in the post-flood period include:

**Syndromic surveillance for acute gastrointestinal illness (AGI).** Emergency department syndromic surveillance systems tracking diarrheal illness, vomiting, and fever can provide early signal for waterborne disease clusters. Baseline establishment is critical: syndromic surveillance signal requires comparison to expected rates, and those baselines may be disrupted in a post-disaster period by population displacement and altered healthcare-seeking behavior.

**Laboratory-based confirmation.** Clinical specimens from symptomatic patients must be collected and tested to identify specific pathogens, characterize outbreak strains, and inform treatment recommendations. Laboratory capacity may be degraded after a major disaster; mutual aid arrangements with state and federal laboratory partners (including the CDC's Laboratory Response Network) are essential pre-event planning components.

**Boil water advisory compliance monitoring.** Issuing a boil water advisory is a public health action; ensuring the advisory reaches the affected population and that they understand and comply with it is the harder operational challenge. Monitoring compliance requires community health worker outreach, surveys, and feedback mechanisms — particularly for populations who lack English language access, have limited media exposure, or lack the fuel, appliances, or physical capacity to boil water.

**Environmental water quality testing.** Water quality testing of distribution systems, private wells, and surface water bodies provides environmental data to complement case surveillance. Testing priorities, methods, and communication of results must be planned and resourced pre-event.

## 22.4 Emergency Water Safety Operations

When the public water supply is compromised or interrupted following a flooding event, emergency managers must activate emergency water safety operations. This encompasses three related functions: safe water distribution, point-of-use treatment, and water quality testing and advisory management.

### 22.4.1 Safe Water Distribution

Emergency water distribution addresses the immediate need for safe drinking and sanitation water when the normal supply is unavailable. Planning parameters include:

**Volume requirements.** The minimum water quantity for survival is approximately [QUANTITY] liter per person per day; for basic hygiene, cooking, and sanitation, a commonly cited planning figure is [QUANTITY] liters per person per day. These figures reflect acute emergency minimums. Return to near-normal water use requires restoration of the distribution system, which may take days to weeks depending on the scope of infrastructure damage.

**Distribution site planning.** Distribution sites must be sited for access, staffed sufficiently to manage anticipated volume, supplied with appropriately packaged water (bulk tanker for filling personal containers, bottled water for areas where fill stations cannot be operated), and communicated to the affected population with sufficient specificity (location, hours, acceptable container types, quantity limits per household) to enable equitable access.

**Tanker and bottled water sourcing.** Pre-event water supply agreements with commercial vendors and mutual aid agreements for potable water tankers from neighboring jurisdictions address the sourcing challenge. National Guard and federal assets (including FEMA water tanker assets) are available through emergency support function coordination but require lead time to mobilize.

**Container hygiene.** Distribution of safe water into contaminated containers creates a pathway for re-contamination. Distribution site operations should include container inspection and, where feasible, disinfection resources for personal containers.

### 22.4.2 Point-of-Use Water Treatment

For populations who have access to water of uncertain safety — such as those with private wells of unconfirmed status, or those using surface water during distribution gaps — point-of-use treatment guidance is essential. The primary methods are:

**Boiling.** Bringing water to a rolling boil for [TIMEFRAME] (or longer at high altitudes) kills virtually all biological pathogens. Boiling is effective but requires fuel, heat source, appropriate containers, and cooling time before use — barriers that are not trivial for all populations. The instruction to boil water assumes these resources exist, an assumption that deserves explicit attention in planning for low-resource communities.

**Chemical disinfection.** Unscented household bleach (sodium hypochlorite at approximately [PERCENT]% concentration) can be used for water disinfection using a standard formula based on bleach concentration and turbidity [CITATION]. Chemical disinfection does not address heavy metal contamination, chemical pollutants, or chlorine-resistant parasites (Cryptosporidium).

**Filtration.** Portable filtration devices appropriate for household use vary widely in their pathogen removal capabilities. Emergency managers issuing point-of-use guidance should specify which device types are appropriate for the specific contamination concerns of the event. Filters effective against bacteria and protozoa may not remove viruses; in settings with significant viral contamination, combined filtration and disinfection may be necessary.

**Distribution of treatment supplies.** Point-of-use treatment guidance is only effective if the affected population has access to the recommended supplies. Emergency distribution of bleach, appropriate containers, and clear instructions in accessible language should be integrated into the emergency water response.

### 22.4.3 Boil Water Advisories: Issuance and Lifting

Boil water advisories (BWAs) are formal public health orders directing affected populations to boil water before consumption. They are issued when water quality testing, treatment monitoring data, or infrastructure event assessment indicates that water may not be safe to drink without treatment.

**Issuance.** BWA issuance authority typically rests with the state or local public health department in coordination with the water utility and regulatory agency. The authority and process for issuing BWAs varies by state. Emergency managers must know the BWA issuance authority in their jurisdiction and the communication channels for rapid public notification.

**Communication challenges.** The same communication infrastructure disruptions that characterize major disasters — power failures, cell system congestion, road damage limiting distribution of physical advisories — complicate BWA communication. Redundant communication channels (emergency alert systems, social media, community radio, outreach workers, faith community networks) must be activated together. Communication must be provided in all languages used by the affected population.

**Lifting.** BWAs are lifted only after confirmation that the conditions that prompted the advisory have been corrected and that water quality testing meets regulatory standards. The testing and confirmation process takes time; premature advisory lifting creates a false safety signal. The process for BWA lifting should be pre-specified in water system emergency response plans and communicated transparently to the public during the advisory period.

## 22.5 Post-Flood Environmental Health

The post-flood environment presents a cluster of health threats that emerge after the acute phase and persist through recovery. These threats are distinct from the acute drowning and trauma hazards of the event itself and from the infectious disease hazards of the immediate post-event period. They reflect the condition of the built environment after sustained water damage.

### 22.5.1 Mold and Indoor Air Quality

Mold growth in water-damaged structures is one of the most consistent and medically significant post-flood environmental health threats in domestic disaster contexts. Mold colonizes porous building materials — drywall, insulation, wood framing, ceiling tiles, carpet — within [TIMEFRAME] of water intrusion in warm conditions. Once established, mold can persist and expand long after visible water has receded.

Health consequences of mold exposure include upper and lower respiratory symptoms, asthma exacerbations, allergic rhinitis, and, in some species and concentrations, more serious pulmonary disease. The clinical evidence base for specific mold species health effects at typical indoor exposure concentrations is complex and contested in some dimensions [CITATION], but the consistent finding across post-flood health surveillance studies is that populations living in or working in water-damaged buildings report elevated respiratory symptom rates.

Remediation guidance emphasizes that mold removal requires addressing the moisture source first; remediation without moisture control will result in recurrence. Personal protective equipment — N95 respirators, gloves, eye protection — is required for cleanup workers. Porous materials that cannot be dried and cleaned should be removed. The scale of mold remediation required after major flooding events frequently exceeds both household capacity and the workforce available through licensed contractors; this creates a gap that emergency planning must address through contractor credentialing systems, public information campaigns on safe DIY remediation, and occupational health surveillance for cleanup workers.

### 22.5.2 Chemical and Hazardous Waste Contamination

Floodwater in populated areas is not simply displaced water; it is a chemical mixture reflecting the land uses and infrastructure it has inundated. Floodwater contaminants include petroleum products from underground storage tanks and damaged pipelines, pesticides and herbicides from agricultural and residential sources, industrial chemicals from flooded facilities, heavy metals from industrial sites and urban stormwater, and sewage from overwhelmed collection systems.

Sediment that settles after floodwaters recede concentrates these contaminants. Children who play in post-flood environments are exposed through dermal contact, ingestion of contaminated soil, and inhalation of resuspended particles during cleanup. The public communication challenge is significant: visual normalization of the drying environment does not correspond to chemical safety.

In areas with documented contaminated sites (Superfund sites, underground storage tank sites, industrial facilities), hazardous waste dispersal is a specific environmental assessment priority. The EPA and state environmental agencies coordinate environmental assessment functions under Emergency Support Function 10 (Hazardous Materials), but the integration of these assessments with public health communication requires active coordination between emergency management, environmental agencies, and health departments.

### 22.5.3 Vector-Borne Disease

Standing water created by flooding produces breeding habitat for mosquitoes. The relationship between flooding and mosquito-borne disease is not simple — rapidly moving water and turbid, contaminated water are poor breeding habitats — but the slow-draining pools that accumulate in yards, containers, and low-lying areas after floodwaters recede do support Aedes and Culex mosquito breeding. Post-flood mosquito population surges have been documented following major events [CITATION].

The specific disease risk depends on the geographic context and which vector-borne pathogens are endemic. In areas where West Nile virus is established, post-flood mosquito surges increase transmission risk. In areas endemic for La Crosse encephalitis, Eastern equine encephalitis, or other arboviruses, the risk profile differs. In tropical and subtropical contexts, dengue, chikungunya, Zika, and malaria are relevant.

Rodent displacement following flooding produces secondary vector concerns: increased rodent contact with human habitats, potential for leptospiral contamination of water and soil from rodent urine, and increased rodent-human contact that elevates risk for rodent-borne illnesses including hantavirus in endemic areas.

Vector control response in the post-flood period involves coordination between local mosquito control programs, state public health entomologists, and CDC/EPA for vector control guidance and, if indicated, aerial insecticide application. Emergency managers should pre-identify the vector control authority and resources in their jurisdiction as part of flood response pre-planning.

## 22.6 Mental Health and Chronic Disease Management in Flood Events

The mental health and chronic disease consequences of major flooding events frequently exceed the immediate physical injury burden in population-level impact. Emergency managers must integrate these health dimensions into response and recovery planning, not treat them as secondary concerns to be addressed after the acute phase resolves.

### 22.6.1 Mental Health Consequences

Post-flood mental health burden follows patterns established across disaster mental health literature. The most consistently documented consequences include:

**Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).** PTSD prevalence increases substantially in flood-affected populations, with higher rates among those who experienced direct life threat, those who lost family members, and those who experienced prolonged displacement [CITATION]. The elevated PTSD rates persist for years after major events.

**Major depression and generalized anxiety.** Beyond PTSD, elevated rates of major depressive disorder and generalized anxiety disorder are documented in flood-affected populations. The recovery phase stressors — property loss, insurance disputes, financial instability, displacement — create chronic stress conditions that maintain elevated mental health burden long after the immediate event.

**Grief.** Flood events that involve deaths of family members or neighbors produce grief responses that interact with other mental health consequences. Anticipatory grief in communities awaiting official death counts, and the complicated grief associated with deaths by drowning (where remains may not be immediately recovered), have distinctive clinical and community dimensions.

**Substance use.** Alcohol and substance use increases have been documented following major flood events, particularly in communities with pre-existing substance use issues and limited mental health treatment access [CITATION].

**Children and adolescents.** Flood-affected children show elevated rates of behavioral symptoms, sleep disturbance, school performance declines, and anxiety. School disruption compounds these effects by removing a key stabilizing structure. School-based mental health programs are an important re-integration component of flood recovery.

The mental health response to flooding is addressed further in Chapter 24 (Behavioral Health in Disasters and Public Health Emergencies). Key emergency management implications include: integrating behavioral health staff into emergency shelters from the initial response phase; activating crisis counseling programs through FEMA's Crisis Counseling Program (CCP) in Presidentially declared disasters; ensuring that mental health services are co-located with other recovery assistance services to reduce access barriers; and addressing stigma that inhibits help-seeking, particularly in rural and culturally conservative communities.

### 22.6.2 Chronic Disease Management Disruption

Flood events disrupt chronic disease management across multiple mechanisms. The clinical consequences of these disruptions are serious and, in some patients, life-threatening.

**Diabetes management.** Insulin requiring refrigeration is particularly vulnerable to power outages and displacement. Patients on insulin may face the question of whether to use insulin of uncertain integrity or to go without. Non-insulin diabetes medications may be lost with flooded property or inaccessible due to pharmacy closure. Blood glucose monitoring equipment and test strips are often damaged or left behind in rapid evacuations. Hyperglycemic crises in displaced diabetic patients are a predictable post-flood medical consequence.

**Dialysis.** End-stage renal disease patients on hemodialysis require treatment three times per week. Missing dialysis sessions produces fluid overload, hyperkalemia, and uremia. Even a one-session gap can produce a life-threatening electrolyte emergency in a patient with no residual renal function. Post-flood healthcare provision must specifically identify and address dialysis access continuity. ESRD Network emergency contacts and dialysis facility relocation databases should be pre-established and accessible to emergency health operations centers.

**Cardiovascular disease.** Exertion, heat, stress, and medication interruption all elevate cardiovascular event risk in flood-affected populations. The well-documented post-disaster pattern of cardiac event surges has been attributed to this convergent risk factor increase [CITATION]. Emergency shelters and recovery operation centers should have AED availability and staff with Basic Life Support training.

**Psychiatric medications.** Medication interruption for patients on antipsychotics, mood stabilizers, or antidepressants can produce rapid psychiatric decompensation. Patients displaced from pharmacies, who lost medications in flooding, or who lack records to obtain refills from unfamiliar pharmacies represent a predictable demand on behavioral health services and emergency medication dispensing operations. Emergency pharmacies and pharmacy mutual aid should be components of the health and medical response plan.

## 22.7 Equity, Vulnerability, and Structural Factors in Flood Risk

Flood impacts are not randomly distributed across populations. The consistent geographic concentration of flood mortality and morbidity in lower-income communities and communities of color reflects structural factors that predate the flood event itself — factors that shape where people live, the quality of the housing they can afford, the protective infrastructure their communities receive, and their capacity to survive and recover when water arrives.

### 22.7.1 Floodplain Residence and Housing Quality

Lower-income households are disproportionately concentrated in floodplain areas, reflecting the historical relationship between land value, elevation, and financial access to housing. Floodplain land has historically been cheaper — in part because of flood risk — and has been the location where lower-income families could access homeownership or affordable rental housing. Mobile homes and manufactured housing, disproportionately occupied by lower-income households, are particularly vulnerable to flood and wind damage and are often sited in lower-elevation areas.

Housing quality amplifies flood vulnerability through drainage, foundation, and construction characteristics. Homes with inadequate drainage systems, crawl space construction, lower floor elevations, and aging building materials sustain more damage and are more difficult to remediate after flooding. The cost to remediate flood damage to a lower-quality home may approach or exceed the home's market value, creating a recovery calculus that effectively forces abandonment and permanent displacement.

### 22.7.2 Protective Infrastructure Disparities

Levee systems, storm drainage infrastructure, and flood control projects are distributed unevenly across communities. The historical patterns of infrastructure investment have systematically directed flood protection resources toward higher-value property and higher-income communities. Lower-income and minority communities have received less comprehensive flood protection and have sometimes been directly harmed by infrastructure decisions: the siting of drainage canals, pumping infrastructure, and levee routes has in some cases served to direct floodwaters toward lower-income neighborhoods to protect higher-value areas [CITATION].

FEMA National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) participation patterns reflect similar disparities. Homeowners in lower-income communities are less likely to hold flood insurance, either because they cannot afford the premiums, because their mortgages are not federally backed (eliminating the insurance purchase requirement), or because they are renters whose building insurance does not cover their personal property. The absence of flood insurance at the household level translates directly into inadequate recovery resources, particularly for owner-occupied lower-income properties.

### 22.7.3 Evacuation Capacity and Barriers

Evacuation requires resources: a vehicle, fuel money, somewhere to go, the financial capacity to stay away from home for an uncertain period, and the ability to leave employment without catastrophic economic consequence. These resources are not equally distributed. Lower-income households are more likely to lack personal vehicles, less likely to have savings to fund extended displacement, and more likely to be employed in jobs without paid leave or job protection.

Language barriers complicate evacuation decision-making when evacuation orders are issued in English only or through media channels not regularly accessed by non-English-speaking communities. Fear of immigration enforcement has been documented to deter evacuation in mixed-status families even when the evacuation order is clear and the danger is understood [CITATION]. The effective evacuation of all community members requires multilingual communication, guaranteed non-enforcement of immigration law at evacuation points, and transportation assistance programs — pre-planned, pre-resourced, and pre-communicated before the event.

### 22.7.4 Recovery Capacity and Structural Barriers

The recovery phase is where pre-existing disparities are most durably inscribed. The administrative complexity of FEMA Individual Assistance programs, insurance claims, SBA loans, and community development programs creates barriers for households with limited education, limited English proficiency, no internet access, and no prior experience navigating government benefit programs. Community organizations with deep roots in affected communities — trusted, culturally competent, and already present — are essential intermediaries in recovery access.

Communities whose economies depended on flood-damaged commercial and industrial activity face compounded recovery challenges when the economic engine that sustained jobs and tax revenue is disrupted. Recovery in these communities is not merely a matter of rebuilding physical structures; it requires deliberate economic recovery interventions that rebuild the economic base alongside the built environment.

## Applied Vignette: The [REGION] Riverine Flood Event

## Key Terms

**Boil water advisory (BWA):** A formal public health directive instructing the affected population to bring water to a rolling boil before drinking, preparing food, or brushing teeth, issued when water safety cannot be confirmed.

**Combined sewer overflow (CSO):** A discharge of untreated sewage and stormwater from a combined sewer system that occurs when rainfall exceeds the system's hydraulic capacity.

**Excess mortality analysis:** An epidemiological method that estimates disaster-related deaths by comparing observed mortality during and after a disaster to the mortality that would have been expected in the absence of the disaster, capturing deaths not directly attributed to the event in official death counts.

**Flash flood:** A flood characterized by a rapid rise in water level, typically within six hours of a causative event, driven by intense precipitation, dam failure, ice jam release, or similar sudden event.

**Floodplain:** The area adjacent to a river, stream, or coastal area that is subject to periodic inundation; typically defined by regulatory frameworks using the 100-year (1% annual chance) or 500-year (0.2% annual chance) flood level as the reference standard.

**Leptospirosis:** A bacterial zoonotic disease caused by Leptospira spirochetes, transmitted by contact with water contaminated by infected animal (particularly rodent) urine; relevant in post-flood contexts where wading in contaminated water is common.

**Point-of-use treatment:** Water treatment performed at the household or individual level (boiling, chemical disinfection, filtration) as an alternative or supplement to centralized treatment when normal supply safety is uncertain.

**Riverine flood:** A flood produced by a river or stream exceeding its banks, typically developing over hours to days as watershed runoff accumulates; distinguished from flash floods by longer warning time and longer inundation duration.

**Storm surge:** An abnormal rise in sea level produced by the low pressure and strong winds of a hurricane or tropical storm; the leading cause of hurricane mortality in the United States.

**Vibrio vulnificus:** A gram-negative bacterium naturally present in warm estuarine waters that causes rapidly progressive wound infections and septicemia, particularly in immunocompromised hosts; associated with coastal flooding events.

**Waterborne disease outbreak:** Two or more cases of a similar illness resulting from consumption of the same contaminated water supply, or one case of a disease with a known waterborne etiology directly attributable to a specific water source.

## Practitioner Takeaways

1. **Vehicle drowning is the leading cause of direct flood fatality in the United States and is predictable and preventable.** Physical barriers to flooded roadway access and active law enforcement of roadway closures are more effective than messaging alone. Pre-event coordination of these actions with transportation and law enforcement partners is essential.

2. **Post-flood waterborne disease risk begins before floodwaters recede.** Surveillance activation, laboratory coordination, and boil water advisory decision-making should begin with the flood event itself, not wait for confirmed cases.

3. **Boil water advisories are only as effective as their communication.** Issuance through English-language emergency alert systems fails to reach significant proportions of many affected populations. Multilingual, multichannel communication using trusted community intermediaries is required to achieve protective compliance.

4. **Private well owners in rural and suburban areas represent a systematically under-served population in flood water safety response.** Active outreach, testing assistance, and clear guidance — not public advisories alone — are required to protect this population.

5. **Dialysis access failure is a foreseeable, time-sensitive medical emergency following flood events.** ESRD patient tracking, dialysis facility emergency contact networks, and medical transport protocols for dialysis patients should be pre-established components of the health and medical response plan.

6. **Mold remediation guidance should reach returning residents before re-entry, not after.** Co-locating environmental health information with re-entry permitting and re-entry checkpoint operations improves the reach of this guidance at a moment of high public receptivity.

7. **Flood recovery timelines are long; mental health and chronic disease consequences are cumulative.** Mental health services, medication continuity programs, and chronic disease case management must be resourced for months to years, not just the acute response phase.

8. **Structural equity factors are legible and addressable.** The disproportionate flood mortality and damage burden in lower-income communities is not random misfortune — it reflects floodplain residence patterns, infrastructure investment disparities, housing quality differences, and evacuation resource gaps that are identifiable and amenable to targeted mitigation, planning, and response equity interventions.

9. **Environmental contamination in post-flood areas is complex and multi-sourced.** Communication about environmental hazards must address the gap between visual normalization of the recovering environment and the continued presence of biological, chemical, and physical hazards in sediments, buildings, and water systems.

10. **The excess mortality of flood events substantially exceeds direct death counts.** Planning for flood health response must account for the full burden of indirect mortality — medication disruption, medical care access failure, generator CO poisoning, cleanup injuries, and mental health sequelae — not only the drowning and trauma deaths that dominate early reporting.

## Chapter Notes

**On the "Turn Around, Don't Drown" campaign and behavioral economics of flood risk:** The intuitive expectation that public awareness campaigns about flood hazards will reduce vehicle drowning deaths has not been robustly supported by outcome data. The behavioral literature suggests that the problem is not knowledge deficit but perceptual bias: individuals evaluate specific flooded crossings with optimism bias, social proof (observing other vehicles crossing), time pressure, and familiarity effects that override abstract risk knowledge. This does not make public education useless — it correctly characterizes the problem as requiring a different intervention tier. Physical access prevention (gates, barriers, rapid deployment tools) and law enforcement that removes individual decision-making from the equation address the actual mechanism of failure more directly than messaging alone.

**On excess mortality methodology and flood death undercounting:** Just as with heat mortality, direct flood death counts captured in death certificate data substantially undercount the total mortality burden. Excess mortality analyses comparing observed all-cause mortality to expected mortality based on historical baselines provide more complete estimates. The gap between direct count and excess mortality estimates varies by event and by the analytical approach applied. Emergency managers should be cautious about citing direct death counts as definitive statements of event impact; the full burden, particularly of indirect and delayed deaths, typically takes months of data maturation to characterize.

**On Vibrio wound infections and climate change:** The geographic range of Vibrio species in U.S. coastal and estuarine waters is expanding northward as sea surface temperatures increase [CITATION]. Flood events involving estuarine water exposure in geographic areas where Vibrio was not historically endemic present a novel wound care challenge for providers who may not consider these organisms. Regional emergency preparedness communications should include awareness of this shifting pathogen geography for clinical providers in coastal flood response roles.

**On NFIP reform and equity:** The National Flood Insurance Program has been the subject of significant policy debate regarding premium structures, mapping methodology, and the cross-subsidy dynamics that have historically charged lower-risk properties higher rates while providing relatively subsidized rates to high-risk properties in desirable coastal areas. The Risk Rating 2.0 methodology introduced in [YEAR] attempted to address some of these distortions but has raised affordability concerns for lower-income households in high-risk areas. Emergency managers should be aware that NFIP participation rates and the adequacy of coverage vary significantly across community income levels and that this variation directly affects recovery capacity.

**On the integration of behavioral health into emergency shelters from Day 1:** A consistent finding across post-disaster mental health research and operational review is that mental health services integrated into emergency shelters from the initial response phase produce better outcomes than separate, delayed mental health service delivery. The practical barrier is staffing: behavioral health professionals available in the immediate post-impact phase are scarce. Pre-event agreements with community mental health centers, hospital behavioral health departments, and licensed clinical social work organizations to provide shelter-based services beginning on Day 1 of shelter activation — rather than after an assessment of need days later — are a high-leverage pre-event planning investment.
