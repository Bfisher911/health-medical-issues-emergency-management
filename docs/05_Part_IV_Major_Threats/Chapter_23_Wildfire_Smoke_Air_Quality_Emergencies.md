# Chapter 23: Wildfire Smoke and Air Quality Emergencies

---

## Chapter Overview

Wildfire has always been a feature of North American landscapes, but the scale, duration, and geographic reach of wildfire smoke events have expanded substantially in recent decades. Fires that once burned for days now burn for weeks. Smoke plumes that once affected nearby communities now travel thousands of miles, exposing populations who have never seen a wildfire to hazardous air quality conditions. Emergency managers in every region of the country — including those whose jurisdictions have no history of local fire — must now understand wildfire smoke as a recurring, cross-jurisdictional public health threat.

This chapter builds professional competency in wildfire smoke and air quality emergency management from the ground up. It begins with the physical and chemical composition of wildfire smoke, because understanding what is in smoke explains why standard interventions (stay inside, avoid exercise) are necessary and what their limits are. It proceeds through the health effects of smoke exposure, organized by population vulnerability, and then addresses the Air Quality Index as a communication and decision support tool. The chapter then turns to operational response: respiratory surge planning, shelter-in-place guidance, evacuation decision-making, and the coordination challenges that arise when smoke events span multiple jurisdictions and days. The chapter closes with long-term air quality recovery, wildfire-related mental health burden, and the structural inequities that shape who bears the greatest exposure burden.

Wildfire smoke emergencies require public health professionals to work at the intersection of meteorology, toxicology, clinical medicine, behavioral communication, and environmental justice. This chapter provides the conceptual foundation for each of those domains and the operational skills to apply them in practice.

## 23.1 Wildfire Smoke Composition and Exposure Dynamics

Wildfire smoke is not a single substance. It is a complex, dynamic mixture of gases, particles, and aerosols whose composition changes as fire conditions, fuel types, atmospheric temperature, and distance from the source change. Emergency managers who treat wildfire smoke as simply "dirty air" will design inadequate protective strategies. Understanding what is in smoke, and how its composition shifts across space and time, is the foundation for competent operational response.

### Particulate Matter: The Primary Health Driver

Fine particulate matter — particles with an aerodynamic diameter of 2.5 micrometers or less, designated PM2.5 — is the component of wildfire smoke most consistently linked to adverse health outcomes. PM2.5 particles are small enough to penetrate beyond the upper respiratory tract, depositing in the small airways and alveolar sacs where gas exchange occurs. Unlike larger particles that are intercepted by nasal hairs and mucociliary clearance mechanisms, PM2.5 reaches the deep lung, triggers local inflammatory responses, and in sufficient concentrations can enter the systemic circulation.

Wildfire smoke PM2.5 differs from the PM2.5 generated by vehicles or industrial sources in several ways. Wildfire smoke particles are younger, less oxidized, and more chemically reactive at the time of initial community exposure. They carry a higher organic carbon fraction. They are often in size ranges that exhibit greater alveolar deposition efficiency. These characteristics have led some researchers to propose that wildfire PM2.5 is more toxic per unit mass than PM2.5 from other combustion sources, though this remains an area of active investigation [CITATION: Morgan, [YEAR]]. What is clear from epidemiological studies is that wildfire smoke events produce measurable increases in respiratory emergency department visits, hospitalizations, and mortality above what PM2.5 alone from other sources would predict at comparable concentrations [CITATION: Reid, [YEAR]].

Coarse particulate matter — PM10, particles between 2.5 and 10 micrometers — is also present in wildfire smoke and contributes to upper airway irritation, conjunctival irritation, and rhinitis. PM10 is intercepted before reaching the alveoli in most cases, but irritation of the upper respiratory tract is uncomfortable, can trigger asthma exacerbations in sensitive individuals, and reduces the protective function of the nasal passage as a filtration barrier.

### Gaseous Pollutants in Wildfire Smoke

Carbon monoxide (CO) is generated by incomplete combustion and is present in wildfire smoke at concentrations that are hazardous close to the fire perimeter. For most community-level smoke events, CO concentrations diminish with distance and dilution to levels below immediately dangerous thresholds, though persons working in enclosed smoky environments or using generators during power outages associated with wildfire events remain at risk. The interaction between elevated ambient CO from smoke and CO generated by indoor combustion appliances and generators warrants specific attention in public messaging.

Volatile organic compounds (VOCs) comprise a chemically diverse category that includes benzene, formaldehyde, acrolein, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), and many other compounds. Benzene is a known human carcinogen; acute high-concentration exposure is associated with acute myeloid leukemia, and community-level benzene monitoring during major wildfire events has documented concentrations of concern. Formaldehyde and acrolein are respiratory irritants that produce epithelial injury at high concentrations. PAHs include compounds with well-established carcinogenic and mutagenic properties. The VOC fraction of wildfire smoke is increasingly recognized as a toxicological concern beyond PM2.5, particularly during the earliest hours of fire encroachment on a community when fresh smoke concentrations are highest.

Nitrogen oxides (NOx) and ozone (O3) are present in wildfire smoke at varying concentrations depending on fire temperature and atmospheric chemistry. Ozone, which is a secondary pollutant formed by photochemical reactions involving VOCs and NOx, can be produced or consumed in the wildfire smoke plume depending on conditions. Areas downwind of fires have experienced both elevated and depressed ozone concentrations, with local atmospheric chemistry determining the outcome. O3 is a potent respiratory irritant with well-documented associations with respiratory exacerbations.

### Structural Fires and Toxic Co-Exposures

When wildfire burns through communities — what the fire science community terms the wildland-urban interface (WUI) — the composition of the smoke changes substantially. Burning structures release heavy metals (lead, cadmium, arsenic, chromium), asbestos, flame retardants, pesticides, synthetic polymers, and household chemicals. Several studies of WUI fire events have documented soil and ash contamination with heavy metals and semi-volatile organic compounds at concentrations that warrant remediation [CITATION: Massey, [YEAR]]. Residents returning to burned areas face both inhalation and dermal exposure risks from ash contact. The presence of lead-containing paint in older housing stock in burn areas is a particular concern for children during cleanup and re-entry phases.

### Smoke Plume Behavior and Exposure Variability

Wildfire smoke plumes are not static. They move with prevailing winds, shift with diurnal atmospheric mixing patterns, and vary in concentration based on fire behavior, topography, and the distance between the emission source and the community. In mountainous terrain, smoke may pool in valleys overnight as the atmosphere becomes stable, creating very high local concentrations in communities far from the actual fire perimeter. Coastal communities may experience onshore flow patterns that pull smoke into populated coastal valleys. Communities hundreds or thousands of miles from a fire may receive smoke that has traveled long distances, has aged and undergone chemical transformation, but still retains health-relevant PM2.5 concentrations.

Monitoring networks, including the EPA AirNow network of ground-based monitoring stations and satellite-derived products, provide real-time and forecasted information on smoke distribution. However, monitoring station density is uneven. Rural communities, which bear substantial wildfire smoke exposure burden, often have few or no monitoring stations within practical response distance. This creates a data gap that affects both situational awareness and the quality of public protective action guidance.

## 23.2 Health Effects of Wildfire Smoke by Population

The health consequences of wildfire smoke exposure span from nuisance-level upper airway irritation in healthy individuals to life-threatening exacerbations of underlying cardiopulmonary disease. Understanding which populations face which risks, and why, enables emergency managers to prioritize resources and target protective action guidance effectively.

### Respiratory Effects

Respiratory effects are the most consistently documented health consequence of wildfire smoke exposure across the epidemiological literature. Acute effects include cough, throat irritation, burning or stinging sensations in the nose and throat, shortness of breath, and wheezing. These effects occur even in healthy individuals at high PM2.5 concentrations and typically resolve within hours to days of smoke clearance.

In persons with pre-existing respiratory conditions — asthma, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), bronchiectasis, cystic fibrosis, interstitial lung disease — wildfire smoke exposure produces exacerbations that can be severe and life-threatening. Asthma exacerbations triggered by wildfire smoke events have been documented in epidemiological studies as increased emergency department visits and hospitalizations occurring concurrently with smoke events [CITATION: Delfino, [YEAR]]. COPD exacerbations in smoke events can require hospitalization, mechanical ventilation, and ICU-level care. The surge these populations create in the healthcare system during extended wildfire smoke events is a critical operational planning consideration.

Healthy adults who perform vigorous outdoor exercise during high-PM2.5 conditions experience a substantially increased dose-response relationship relative to sedentary individuals, because exercise increases minute ventilation and shifts breathing from the nose to the mouth, bypassing the upper airway's particle filtration capacity. Public messaging that specifically discourages outdoor exercise is therefore both evidence-based and actionable.

### Cardiovascular Effects

Wildfire smoke's cardiovascular effects are mediated by several mechanisms. PM2.5 that reaches the systemic circulation triggers endothelial inflammation, promotes coagulation, and may cause direct myocardial effects. Hypoxemia induced by respiratory compromise increases cardiac workload. Acute stress responses contribute to blood pressure elevation and heart rate variability changes. The cumulative result is a documented association between wildfire smoke exposure and acute myocardial infarction, cardiac arrhythmia, and cardiovascular mortality [CITATION: Wettstein, [YEAR]].

Persons with pre-existing cardiovascular disease — including coronary artery disease, heart failure, and arrhythmias — have heightened vulnerability. Older adults carry a disproportionate share of this burden both because of the higher prevalence of cardiovascular disease in older age groups and because of reduced physiological reserve. The combination of heat stress (which often accompanies fire weather) and smoke inhalation creates a synergistic cardiovascular stress.

### Children and Developmental Considerations

Children are not simply small adults with respect to wildfire smoke exposure. Their respiratory tracts are anatomically and physiologically different — narrower airways amplify the functional consequence of inflammation and mucus production; faster respiratory rates mean higher mass doses per unit body weight; and their developing respiratory and neurological systems may be more susceptible to the toxic effects of specific pollutants such as benzene and acrolein. Epidemiological studies have documented increases in pediatric asthma exacerbations, lower respiratory infections, and emergency department visits for respiratory complaints during wildfire smoke events [CITATION: Ghazipura, [YEAR]].

Infants and toddlers face particular challenge because they cannot communicate symptoms, are more likely to be in enclosed spaces with adults who are themselves trying to manage smoke exposure, and may have limited access to appropriate respiratory protection (standard N95 respirators do not fit small children and are not approved for children under 2 years).

Children's behavioral patterns also create exposure risk: outdoor play during smoke events, school outdoor recess during periods when air quality guidance would indicate indoor restriction, and sports activities. School and childcare facility air quality protocols are a key implementation target for public health guidance during smoke events.

### Older Adults

Older adults face compounded vulnerability: higher prevalence of pre-existing cardiopulmonary disease, reduced physiological resilience, greater likelihood of polypharmacy with medications that affect cardiovascular and respiratory responses, and — in lower-income and socially isolated older adults — reduced capacity to implement protective actions such as indoor filtration, finding clean air spaces, or relocating temporarily to avoid smoke. Older adults in residential care settings present a distinct management challenge; facilities that lack adequate HVAC filtration capacity may need to shelter populations that cannot be easily moved, while simultaneously managing clinical needs of residents with baseline respiratory and cardiovascular conditions.

### Pregnant Individuals

Pregnancy is associated with physiological changes that increase susceptibility to respiratory compromise from smoke exposure, including upward diaphragm displacement that reduces functional residual lung capacity, elevated baseline respiratory rate, and increased minute ventilation. Epidemiological evidence links maternal wildfire smoke exposure to adverse birth outcomes including preterm birth, low birth weight, and stillbirth, particularly with exposure during sensitive windows of fetal development [CITATION: Abdo, [YEAR]]. The placenta does not provide a barrier to fine particles or many of the gaseous compounds in wildfire smoke, and some toxic components may reach the developing fetus. Pregnancy-specific guidance and targeted outreach to prenatal care providers are warranted public health actions during extended smoke events.

### Individuals with Mental Health Conditions

Beyond the mental health consequences of wildfire events discussed in Section 23.6, persons with existing mental health conditions face specific challenges during smoke events. Some psychiatric medications — including certain antipsychotics and mood stabilizers — have anticholinergic properties that impair thermoregulatory capacity, which is physiologically linked to respiratory heat load management. Persons with serious mental illness may have impaired ability to perceive or respond to air quality guidance, may be more likely to engage in outdoor activity or smoking during smoke events, and are more likely to live in housing with inadequate filtration. Community health workers and case managers working with this population warrant specific guidance and resources during smoke emergencies.

### Outdoor Workers

Agricultural workers, landscapers, construction workers, utility workers, and others who work outdoors face occupational exposures during wildfire smoke events that may exceed what voluntary protective actions can mitigate. In many jurisdictions, Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) or state-equivalent regulations govern employer obligations during smoke events, including air quality monitoring, provision of respirators, and work modification options. The intersection of occupational protection law, worker power disparities (particularly for agricultural workers and day laborers), and language access creates an equity dimension that emergency managers should specifically address in planning and response. [CITATION: Balmes, [YEAR]]

## 23.3 Air Quality Index Interpretation and Emergency Communication

The Air Quality Index (AQI) is the primary tool used in the United States to communicate ambient air quality status to the public. Emergency managers, public health officers, and media communicators use AQI values and categories to trigger protective action guidance and to document the severity of smoke events for operational and public health purposes. Effective use of the AQI requires understanding what it measures, what it does not measure, how to translate it into actionable guidance for diverse audiences, and where it has operational limitations.

### AQI Structure and PM2.5 Calculation

The AQI is an index that ranges from 0 to 500. It is calculated for several pollutants — PM2.5, PM10, ozone, carbon monoxide, sulfur dioxide, and nitrogen dioxide — and the reported AQI value for a given location at a given time reflects the highest value among all calculated pollutant sub-indices. During wildfire smoke events, PM2.5 typically drives the AQI value.

AQI values are divided into six color-coded categories:

- **0-50 (Green: Good).** Air quality is satisfactory; no health concerns for the general public.
- **51-100 (Yellow: Moderate).** Acceptable air quality; unusually sensitive individuals (children with asthma, older adults with heart disease) may notice effects from prolonged outdoor exertion.
- **101-150 (Orange: Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups).** Sensitive groups — those with cardiopulmonary disease, older adults, children — should limit prolonged or heavy outdoor exertion.
- **151-200 (Red: Unhealthy).** Everyone may begin to experience health effects; sensitive groups may experience more serious effects. Avoid prolonged or heavy outdoor exertion.
- **201-300 (Purple: Very Unhealthy).** Health warnings of emergency conditions for the entire population. Avoid all outdoor exertion; sensitive groups should remain indoors.
- **301-500 (Maroon: Hazardous).** Health emergency. Everyone should avoid all outdoor activity; sensitive groups should remain indoors and take additional protective measures.

During major wildfire smoke events, AQI values in the Very Unhealthy and Hazardous ranges — values that most communities had little experience communicating before the recent era of prolonged large-fire events — have become increasingly common in Western states and have occurred in Midwestern and Eastern states from transported smoke.

### Interpreting AQI for Public Communication

AQI communication requires translating numerical values and color categories into specific protective behaviors. Several principles guide effective AQI public messaging:

Sensitive group identification must be explicit. The general public often underestimates their own membership in a sensitive group. Persons with mild or well-controlled asthma, persons over 65, persons who are pregnant, and persons with cardiovascular disease may not self-identify as "sensitive" without explicit messaging. Public guidance that specifies the conditions that define sensitivity produces better behavioral response than generic guidance.

Action guidance should be specific and achievable. "Stay indoors" is a more actionable message than "limit outdoor activity." Specifying that windows and doors should be closed, that air filtration options exist (recirculation settings on central HVAC, portable HEPA air purifiers, or DIY filter-and-fan units), and that outdoor exercise is the highest-risk outdoor activity provides the recipient with a clear decision framework.

Duration matters as much as peak concentration. An AQI event lasting four days at Unhealthy levels represents a cumulative exposure burden substantially greater than a 24-hour spike to the same AQI. Public messaging that communicates duration — and explains that protective actions should be maintained across the full event rather than only during peak readings — improves protective behavior compliance over extended events.

Conflicting guidance creates confusion. When school districts, local public health agencies, recreational venues, and employers issue different guidance in response to the same AQI conditions, the public receives mixed signals. Pre-event coordination on tiered action thresholds — agreed-upon AQI levels that trigger school recess restrictions, outdoor sports cancellations, outdoor worker protection obligations, and clean air refuge center activation — reduces conflicting public guidance during events.

### AQI Limitations in Wildfire Contexts

Several AQI limitations become operationally significant during wildfire smoke events.

Monitoring station density is uneven. Jurisdictions with sparse monitoring networks may not have a valid AQI reading for a community that is actually experiencing substantial smoke exposure. Satellite-derived PM2.5 products (PurpleAir, AirNow Fire and Smoke Map, EPA's AirFire) provide supplementary spatial coverage, but ground-truth validation of these products varies. Public health guidance based on interpolated values from distant monitoring stations may understate or overstate actual community exposure.

PurpleAir and low-cost sensors have expanded coverage but require correction. Low-cost optical particle counters such as PurpleAir sensors report PM2.5 values that are systematically biased during wildfire smoke events. The EPA and others have developed correction equations specifically for wildfire conditions; applying uncorrected PurpleAir values to AQI communication will overestimate PM2.5 and AQI. Practitioners using low-cost sensor networks should apply validated correction factors. [CITATION: Holder, [YEAR]]

The AQI does not capture the full chemical toxicity of wildfire smoke. Because the AQI for PM2.5 is based entirely on PM2.5 mass concentration, it does not reflect the variable chemical composition of wildfire smoke across events or locations. A given AQI value from wildfire smoke may represent a different health risk than the same AQI value from vehicle exhaust, depending on the fire's fuel composition and atmospheric processing. This is an area where the public health protective value of the AQI as currently structured may underestimate actual risk during certain wildfire events.

## 23.4 Respiratory Surge Planning and Healthcare System Response

Wildfire smoke events create predictable surges in demand for emergency and outpatient healthcare services that can overwhelm systems not prepared for them. Respiratory surge planning translates smoke event forecasting into healthcare capacity management before the surge arrives rather than during it.

### Anticipating Demand: The Respiratory Surge Curve

Emergency department chief complaints driven by wildfire smoke include shortness of breath, chest pain, cough, eye irritation, and exacerbation of asthma and COPD. These complaint categories rise within [TIMEFRAME] of smoke onset, peak during the highest AQI days of an event, and often remain elevated for days after smoke clearance as inflammatory processes triggered during peak exposure continue to produce symptoms [CITATION: Haikerwal, [YEAR]]. Healthcare systems in high-fire-risk jurisdictions should develop historical relationship estimates between AQI values and emergency department visit increases for smoke-sensitive complaint categories, using their own utilization data, to inform demand projections for future events.

Outpatient demand also rises during smoke events. Primary care clinics and urgent care facilities see increased presentations for respiratory complaints; pharmacy dispensing of albuterol inhalers, antihistamines, and corticosteroids increases. Ambulatory surge capacity planning — ensuring that primary care and urgent care can absorb increased respiratory volume that does not require emergency-level care — reduces unnecessary ED utilization and preserves emergency capacity for high-acuity cases.

### Prescription Access and Medication Continuity

Persons with asthma and COPD who use daily controller medications may exhaust their supply during extended smoke events, particularly if they were already approaching refill time. Proactive communication with pharmacies to maximize refill flexibility during smoke emergencies, and coordination with insurers or state Medicaid programs to temporarily waive early refill restrictions, is a tangible pre-event action that reduces medication-associated morbidity. Emergency dispensing authority for pharmacists — available in many states under public health emergency declarations — enables refills without a new prescription for chronic medications.

Persons in underserved communities with lower rates of primary care linkage and higher rates of under-controlled asthma or COPD face a compounded risk during smoke events: more severe underlying disease, less medication on hand, less capacity to access additional medical care, and more exposure due to the housing and work factors discussed in Section 23.7. Community health center outreach and mobile health unit deployment to underserved neighborhoods during high-smoke days are evidence-informed interventions.

### Hospital Capacity Management During Prolonged Events

When smoke events extend across multiple days or weeks, hospital capacity management considerations arise that go beyond normal surge protocols. Elective procedures scheduled during the smoke event window may need to be postponed to preserve operating room and recovery capacity for respiratory emergencies. Transfer agreements with facilities in unaffected areas allow regional redistribution of high-acuity patients when local capacity is constrained. Burn center capacity — relevant when WUI fires produce thermal injury patients simultaneously with smoke-exposed populations — requires specific coordination.

Respiratory therapist, pulmonologist, and critical care provider staffing requires anticipatory management. These specialists experience simultaneous elevated demand from across the healthcare network during smoke events. Staffing augmentation agreements with regional partners, locum arrangements, and telehealth consultation capacity for facilities without on-site pulmonologists all warrant pre-event planning.

### Dialysis Continuity Considerations

Smoke events that coincide with air quality deterioration inside dialysis facilities — which are not always equipped with high-efficiency filtration — create a clinical environment of concern for the already-vulnerable ESRD population, who have cardiovascular comorbidities, diminished physiological reserve, and cannot miss dialysis sessions. Dialysis chains and networks should have air quality monitoring protocols and air filtration upgrading plans for facilities in high-wildfire-risk regions. During extreme events, coordination with ESRD Networks (the federal contractor organizations that provide oversight for dialysis facilities under Medicare) enables regional situational awareness of facility operational status.

### Clean Air Refuge Centers

Clean air refuge centers — typically community centers, libraries, shopping malls, or other large public spaces with effective air filtration — provide populations who cannot adequately filter indoor air at home a place to reduce cumulative smoke exposure during high-AQI periods. Planning for refuge center siting requires assessment of HVAC filtration capacity of prospective facilities (MERV-13 or higher filtration is generally recommended for wildfire smoke), accessibility by public transit and for persons with mobility limitations, availability of power for individuals dependent on electricity-dependent medical equipment, and capacity to address the needs of persons bringing pets.

Refuge center activation thresholds, staffing, communication, and supply logistics should be incorporated into smoke event annexes to emergency operations plans in jurisdictions with wildfire smoke risk.

## 23.5 Shelter-in-Place Guidance and Indoor Air Quality Protection

Shelter-in-place is the primary protective action during wildfire smoke events for most community members who are not in the fire's direct path. Its effectiveness depends on building characteristics, occupant behavior, available filtration, and the duration and intensity of the smoke event. Emergency managers who communicate shelter-in-place recommendations without acknowledging these dependencies provide incomplete guidance.

### How Sheltering Reduces Exposure

The infiltration of outdoor air into a building interior is driven by pressure differentials (air leakage through cracks, gaps, and penetrations) and deliberate ventilation (windows, doors, HVAC systems drawing in outdoor air). The effectiveness of sheltering depends on reducing infiltration and ventilation pathways. In a sealed, well-constructed building with mechanical recirculation of air through effective filtration, indoor PM2.5 concentrations during a smoke event can be substantially lower — by [PERCENT] or more — than outdoor concentrations [CITATION: Barn, [YEAR]]. In a leaky older building without filtration, indoor PM2.5 may equilibrate rapidly with outdoor concentrations, providing little or no protective benefit from sheltering.

The practical guidance for occupants includes closing all windows and doors, setting HVAC systems to recirculate indoor air rather than drawing in outdoor air, using portable HEPA air purifiers in the room(s) where occupants spend the most time, sealing window gaps and HVAC dampers with tape during extreme events, and using N95 respirators if indoor air quality is inadequate despite these measures.

### Portable Air Purifiers: Evidence and Limitations

Portable HEPA (High-Efficiency Particulate Air) purifiers are effective at reducing PM2.5 concentrations in the room in which they operate when sized appropriately for the room volume. A general sizing rule is to select a unit with a Clean Air Delivery Rate (CADR) for smoke at least two-thirds of the room area in square feet. The evidence base for their effectiveness in reducing acute respiratory outcomes during smoke events includes randomized and quasi-experimental studies [CITATION: Allen, [YEAR]]. However, effectiveness declines in rooms with high air exchange rates, very leaky construction, or high-smoke-loading from very high outdoor concentrations.

Do-it-yourself (DIY) box fan air purifiers — typically a box fan with a MERV-13 or higher residential furnace filter attached to the intake side — have been validated as providing meaningful PM2.5 reduction at substantially lower cost than commercial HEPA units. The design developed and studied by researchers (sometimes called the Corsi-Rosenthal Box, after the engineers who popularized it) provides several-fold PM2.5 reduction in a normal-sized room. This low-cost option has significant equity relevance and warrants inclusion in public messaging alongside commercial options.

Equity in air purifier access is a genuine operational concern. Commercial HEPA units at adequate capacity cost [AMOUNT] to [AMOUNT] or more; many lower-income households cannot purchase them on short notice. Emergency management pre-positioning of air purifiers for distribution through community organizations, public health departments, or community health centers in advance of fire season provides a tangible preparedness intervention.

### Limits of Shelter-in-Place as a Protective Strategy

Shelter-in-place is not effective indefinitely during extreme, prolonged smoke events. As event duration extends and indoor PM2.5 accumulates — particularly in leaky buildings without filtration — the cumulative indoor dose may approach the outdoor dose over days to weeks. Very high outdoor concentrations (AQI Hazardous, AQI > 300) create indoor infiltration rates that overwhelm most household filtration capacity within hours.

Shelter-in-place guidance also breaks down for populations who cannot meaningfully implement it: outdoor workers with no indoor workplace option, unhoused individuals without indoor shelter, residents of mobile homes and other low-quality housing with high infiltration rates, and households that cannot seal their homes against smoke because of extreme heat that makes closing windows a thermal health risk. Public messaging that presents shelter-in-place as universally available and universally protective misrepresents the situation for a substantial fraction of the most-exposed population.

### Mask Use in Smoke Environments

N95 and KN95 respirators, when properly fitted and worn correctly, filter at least [PERCENT] of airborne particles at the size range relevant to PM2.5 and are effective in reducing inhalation of wildfire smoke particles during short-duration outdoor exposures or in situations where adequate indoor sheltering is not available. They do not filter gases (CO, VOCs, ozone) and they do not substitute for avoiding outdoor exposure when sheltering is available.

Surgical masks and cloth face coverings are not effective at filtering PM2.5-range particles; their filtration efficiency for this particle size range is well below that of N95 respirators. Public guidance that recommends any mask as protective against smoke PM2.5 without specifying N95 or better creates a false sense of protection.

Children's fit and sizing limitations for N95 respirators create a gap in guidance. Standard adult N95s do not fit young children. Guidance for parents of young children during extreme smoke events should focus on creating a clean indoor environment rather than relying on respiratory protection for children who cannot be adequately fitted.

## 23.6 Evacuation Decision-Making and Protective Action in WUI Communities

When wildfire threatens rather than just smokes — when communities in the wildland-urban interface face potential direct fire exposure — evacuation becomes the primary life-safety action. Evacuation decision-making in WUI fire contexts is one of the most behaviorally complex challenges in emergency management, and the consequences of failures at any point in the decision-evacuation sequence can be fatal.

### The Evacuation Decision Cascade

Effective protective evacuation requires a cascade of sequential events: authorities must detect the threat in time to issue orders, issue orders with sufficient lead time for residents to evacuate before fire cuts off routes, communicate orders effectively to all residents, residents must receive and correctly interpret the order, residents must decide to comply, and residents must be capable of complying (transportation, mobility, medical needs). Failure at any point breaks the cascade. Major WUI fire fatalities, including those in the 2018 Camp Fire (which destroyed the town of Paradise, California, killing [NUMBER] people) and earlier events, have involved failures at multiple cascade points [CITATION: California Department of Forestry and Fire Protection, [YEAR]].

### Evacuation Zones and Warning Systems

Tiered evacuation zone systems — typically three levels (Evacuation Order, Evacuation Warning, and Evacuation Watch, though naming varies by jurisdiction) — are intended to give residents differentiated protective action guidance based on proximity and immediacy of threat. Evacuation Orders mandate immediate departure; Evacuation Warnings indicate the area should be prepared and ready to leave; Evacuation Watches indicate conditions are developing. Research on behavioral compliance shows that even Evacuation Orders are not universally followed, and that early voluntary evacuation during Warning phases substantially reduces road congestion and injury risk compared to last-minute order compliance [CITATION: Cohn, [YEAR]].

Wireless Emergency Alerts (WEAs), the system used to push evacuation alerts to mobile phones in a geographic area, have improved notification reach but have known limitations: geographic boundaries may be imprecise, alerts are received only by individuals with compatible phones and adequate signal, and non-English speakers may not understand alert content if translation is not included. Local alert and warning systems (LawS) — including CodeRed, Everbridge, and similar platforms — require prior registration and have well-documented enrollment disparities that correlate with age, English proficiency, and technology access.

### Barriers to Evacuation Compliance

Multiple structural and situational factors reduce evacuation compliance beyond simple refusal to follow guidance.

Transportation access is a primary barrier. Households without a vehicle face an evacuation barrier that cannot be overcome by clearer messaging. Pre-planned evacuation transportation assistance — identifying vehicle-dependent residents through preparedness surveys, pre-positioning transportation contracts, and coordinating with public transit and school bus fleets — is the operational response. In many communities, this planning exists on paper but has not been adequately exercised.

Medical and functional needs create evacuation complexity. Individuals on home oxygen therapy, home dialysis, or other medical life support, and individuals with mobility limitations or cognitive impairment, require assisted evacuation with specialized resources. Special needs registries — voluntary programs that allow residents to pre-identify as needing evacuation assistance — have enrollment disparities and operational gaps. Medical needs shelter planning, including coordination with home health agencies and durable medical equipment providers, is essential for WUI-risk jurisdictions.

Re-entry pressure creates premature return behavior. Residents, business owners, farmers and ranchers, and individuals with pets or livestock who evacuated face strong economic and social pressure to return as quickly as possible after fire has passed. Premature re-entry into areas with ongoing fire activity, compromised structural integrity from burned structures, downed power lines, contaminated water, and ash-laden air creates secondary injury. Re-entry credentialing systems, staged zone-by-zone re-entry protocols, and clear communication of re-entry criteria (including air quality and water safety standards) reduce premature return injuries.

### Pets and Livestock as Evacuation Obstacles

Studies of evacuation compliance consistently find that a substantial proportion of evacuation refusals and delays are linked to unwillingness to leave without pets or livestock [CITATION: Zottarelli, [YEAR]]. Residents who cannot find pet-friendly evacuation accommodations, who have livestock they cannot transport, or who fear for animals left behind will delay or refuse evacuation. Pet-friendly emergency shelter policy, large animal evacuation coordination (involving county agricultural agencies and fairgrounds as staging areas), and explicit communication that pets can be brought to designated shelters reduces this barrier. These accommodations are not solely an animal welfare matter; they are a human life-safety strategy.

## 23.7 Long-Term Air Quality Recovery, Mental Health, and Equity

Wildfire events do not end when flames are extinguished. The recovery phase encompasses weeks to months of continued air quality concern, contaminated water and soil, ash management, structural rebuilding, and health system demand, layered over a mental health burden that can persist for years.

### Post-Fire Air Quality Challenges

After active burning ends, air quality concerns persist from multiple sources. Smoldering combustion in large fires — particularly in organic-rich soils, peat, or debris piles — can continue for weeks or months, emitting PM2.5 and CO at rates that maintain community-level air quality problems. Ash and fine particulate material deposited across burned areas is mobilized by wind and vehicle traffic during re-entry and cleanup operations, creating secondary exposure risk. Demolition activities on burned structures release settled ash, heavy metals, and asbestos from older construction.

Workers engaged in debris removal and structure demolition face concentrated exposure to the full range of combustion products that settled on burned areas. Proper personal protective equipment — including N95 respirators at minimum, P100 half-mask respirators for heavy ash exposure, disposable coveralls, and gloves — is necessary, and occupational health guidance specific to WUI fire debris environments is warranted. This guidance is especially important for unregulated or informal labor markets where worker protection oversight is limited.

Air quality monitoring should continue through the debris clearance phase, not only during active fire. Post-fire PM2.5 monitoring in burn areas and downwind communities provides the data foundation for decisions about re-entry safety, school reopening, and outdoor worker protection.

### Contamination of Water and Soil

WUI fire events can compromise water systems through physical destruction of infrastructure and through a less-visible pathway: smoke and combustion chemistry that alters the organic chemistry of water distribution systems. High-temperature combustion products deposited on or dissolved into water distribution pipes can leach benzene, styrene, naphthalene, and other compounds into tap water through processes that are not prevented by boiling [CITATION: Proctor, [YEAR]]. The 2018 Camp Fire in California produced well-documented widespread benzene contamination of the Paradise, Magalia, and Butte County water supply serving the burn area; remediation required flushing, pipe replacement, and months of intensive testing before the system was declared safe. This mechanism — thermal desorption of contaminants from plastic pipe components in the distribution system — was not anticipated by most water utilities prior to that event and has since become a focus of utility preparedness planning in fire-risk regions.

Soil and ash contamination with heavy metals, semi-volatile organic compounds, and asbestos in burn areas represents both a direct exposure risk for residents returning to clean properties and a long-term environmental remediation concern. State environmental agencies, EPA Region offices, and local public health departments coordinate post-fire environmental assessment and cleanup oversight.

### Mental Health Consequences of Wildfire Events

The mental health burden of wildfire events is substantial, multifaceted, and often underestimated in the emergency response phase when physical health needs are more visible. Mental health consequences extend across a spectrum of populations, from direct fire survivors who lost homes and community to smoke-exposed populations hundreds of miles from the fire who experienced sustained anxiety, disruption, and air quality-driven physical symptoms.

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) has been documented at elevated rates in communities affected by WUI fires, with some studies finding PTSD symptom prevalence rates of [PERCENT] or higher in directly affected populations one year post-fire [CITATION: Leppold, [YEAR]]. Major depressive disorder and generalized anxiety disorder are also elevated. Grief from loss — of homes, of community, of landscape, of sense of security, and of neighbors who died — is a normal but often clinically significant response that requires access to grief support beyond standard crisis counseling frameworks.

Children and adolescents exposed to wildfire events show increased rates of anxiety, depression, sleep disturbance, and behavioral regression. Youth who experienced home loss or school disruption are at particular risk. Schools reopening after fire events carry both educational recovery and mental health support functions simultaneously, and school staff need training in trauma-informed approaches and capacity to identify students who need referral.

Firefighters and other emergency responders who spent weeks on fire lines or in smoke-exposure conditions face both acute respiratory consequences and significant occupational mental health burden. Cumulative smoke exposure studies among wildland firefighters have documented airway inflammation, lung function changes, and elevated PTSD and depression prevalence relative to the general population [CITATION: Jahnke, [YEAR]]. Responder mental health support programs, peer support systems, and occupational health monitoring are essential components of wildfire workforce health protection.

Community identity and collective grief are dimensions of wildfire mental health burden that clinical frameworks sometimes fail to capture. When an entire town burns — Paradise, California; Greenville, California; Lahaina, Hawaii — the community as a social entity is destroyed alongside individual properties. Displaced survivors lose proximity to neighbors, to familiar landscape, to community institutions, and to the embedded social support networks that constitute functioning community life. Mental health recovery that addresses only individual PTSD and depression, without addressing social reconnection and community rebuilding, misses a core dimension of the problem.

The Crisis Counseling Program (CCP), administered by FEMA and SAMHSA following Presidentially declared disasters, provides outreach-based behavioral health services designed specifically for disaster-affected communities. Unlike clinical mental health treatment, CCP is an outreach and psychoeducational model that meets people where they are, normalizes stress reactions, and links individuals to clinical care when needed. CCP activation should be planned as a standard component of wildfire disaster response.

### Equity in Wildfire Smoke Exposure and Recovery

Wildfire smoke exposure is not equally distributed across social groups, and neither are the resources available for recovery. Several equity dimensions require explicit attention in emergency management planning and response.

Housing quality and neighborhood location shape baseline smoke vulnerability. Older housing stock — more common in lower-income neighborhoods — has higher infiltration rates and less effective filtration. Mobile and manufactured housing has very high infiltration. Residents of these housing types have fundamentally less capacity to shelter in place effectively, regardless of how clearly and repeatedly shelter-in-place guidance is communicated.

Access to air filtration and clean air spaces requires financial and transportation resources that lower-income individuals may lack. The ability to buy a HEPA purifier, move to a hotel with effective air conditioning, visit a clean air refuge center with reliable transportation, or take days off work to remain sheltered requires resources that are unequally distributed. Emergency management programs that treat air quality protective action as a matter of individual choice without addressing these structural access barriers will have predictably worse outcomes in lower-income communities.

Agricultural and outdoor workers — disproportionately composed of low-income workers of color, including Indigenous and Latino workers in Western states — face the highest occupational exposure burden. Regulatory protections for outdoor workers during smoke events vary by state; in states with strong outdoor worker smoke regulations, enforcement capacity and language access for workers in non-English-speaking communities remain challenges.

Unhoused individuals are among the most exposed during smoke events, with no shelter from smoke, no filtration capacity, and often no pathway to clean air refuge. Emergency management smoke response planning that does not specifically address unhoused populations — through targeted outreach, expanded shelter capacity during smoke events, or mobile clean air resource deployment — effectively excludes the most exposed members of the community from protective action.

Immigration status creates barriers to service access during smoke events. In communities with substantial undocumented immigrant populations, fear of government contact may deter individuals from accessing public clean air refuge centers, free air purifier distribution programs, or public health services. Community-based organizations with established trust in these communities are essential partners for protective action outreach and service delivery.

Recovery disparities replicate and compound the pre-event equity pattern. Access to insurance, the legal capacity to navigate federal disaster assistance, credit access for rebuilding, and social capital for navigating recovery bureaucracy all correlate with pre-disaster socioeconomic status. Communities of color and lower-income communities consistently show slower and less complete recovery in post-disaster studies [CITATION: Fothergill, [YEAR]].

## Applied Vignette: Multi-Day Smoke Event in a [REGION] Mountain Valley Community

A regional wildfire ignites in [MONTH] during a period of extended drought, dry conditions, and offshore wind flow in [REGION]. The fire, burning in mixed conifer and chaparral at [DISTANCE] miles from [CITY], generates a dense smoke plume driven toward the city by overnight drainage winds. By Day 2, the NWS Air Quality Alert indicates AQI values at the Purple (Very Unhealthy) level, with forecasts suggesting the event will persist for [TIMEFRAME] days pending wind shifts.

The county's single EPA-certified monitoring station, located in an urban park, reads AQI 185 (Very Unhealthy). PurpleAir sensors in the eastern side of the county — a lower-income agricultural area where farmworkers live in older housing — read uncorrected values equivalent to [AQI VALUE] but have not had the EPA correction factor applied by local officials communicating to the public. A community health promoter who knows the correction formula contacts the local health department; after a [TIMEFRAME]-hour delay, corrected values are communicated, showing conditions substantially worse than the central monitor indicated.

Three operational gaps are identified during the event and documented in the After Action Review.

**Gap 1: Spanish-language outreach lagged English-language guidance by [TIMEFRAME] hours.** The largest agricultural employer received English-language outdoor worker guidance from OSHA; Spanish-language guidance did not reach supervisors until the following morning, one day after the guidance was issued. Workers received no instruction on N95 use or the right to reduce outdoor exposure during this window.

**Gap 2: Clean air refuge centers were inaccessible to the most-exposed residents.** The three designated clean air refuge centers were located in the central city, accessible by private vehicle or one bus line with limited evening service. The eastern county agricultural communities — where housing infiltration was highest and outdoor worker exposure was greatest — had no refuge center and no transit route to existing centers. Several residents with asthma who called 211 seeking shelter were told no center was available in their area.

**Gap 3: Air purifier distribution program ran out within three hours.** The county distributed [NUMBER] HEPA air purifiers at a central public health facility during business hours on Day 2. Units were exhausted by mid-morning. No additional procurement had been pre-authorized. Families who arrived after the supply was gone — including many working families who could not leave employment — received no units.

The After Action Review generates the following planning commitments for the next fiscal year cycle: pre-authorization of emergency HEPA unit purchasing, identification and AQI-equipped siting of eastern county refuge center in partnership with agricultural employer and local school district, and development of bilingual smoke event alerting protocol within the regional alert and warning system.

## Key Terms

- **PM2.5**: Fine particulate matter with an aerodynamic diameter of 2.5 micrometers or less; the primary driver of adverse health outcomes from wildfire smoke.
- **Air Quality Index (AQI)**: A standardized 0-500 scale used to communicate ambient air quality to the public, with six color-coded categories from Good to Hazardous.
- **Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI)**: The zone where developed land and wildland vegetation intermix, creating fire risk to structures and communities.
- **Shelter-in-Place**: The protective action of remaining indoors and taking steps to reduce infiltration of outdoor air, used during smoke events and other hazardous air quality conditions.
- **HEPA**: High-Efficiency Particulate Air; a filtration standard that removes at least [PERCENT] of particles at the most penetrating particle size; used in portable air purifiers.
- **CADR (Clean Air Delivery Rate)**: A measure of an air purifier's effectiveness at reducing specific pollutants in a given room volume; used for sizing portable air purifiers.
- **Smoke Inhalation Injury**: Clinical syndrome from direct inhalation of combustion products, including thermal injury, CO poisoning, and chemical airway injury; distinct from smoke-related ambient air quality health effects.
- **Crisis Counseling Program (CCP)**: A FEMA/SAMHSA-administered outreach-based behavioral health service program deployed following Presidentially declared disasters.
- **VOC (Volatile Organic Compound)**: A broad class of carbon-containing compounds that volatilize at room temperature; present in wildfire smoke and associated with both acute irritation and carcinogenic risk.
- **Evacuation Zone Tiering**: A system of graduated protective action zones (Order, Warning, Watch) used in WUI fire evacuations to provide differentiated guidance based on proximity and imminence of threat.
- **Wireless Emergency Alert (WEA)**: A geotargeted alert system that pushes emergency notifications to compatible mobile phones within a defined geographic area.
- **Excess Mortality**: Deaths attributable to an event or exposure beyond what would be expected without it; a standard epidemiological measure for quantifying total wildfire health burden.
- **Corsi-Rosenthal Box**: A DIY air purifier design using a box fan and high-MERV residential furnace filters, validated as effective for PM2.5 reduction at substantially lower cost than commercial HEPA units.
- **ESRD Network**: A regional contractor organization operating under Medicare to provide oversight and support for dialysis facilities and ESRD patients, including coordination of continuity during emergencies.

## Practitioner Takeaways

1. Wildfire smoke is a chemically complex mixture that goes beyond PM2.5 mass concentration; VOCs, CO, and heavy metals from burning structures add toxicological dimensions not captured in AQI values alone.

2. PM2.5 from wildfire smoke may carry greater health impact per unit mass than PM2.5 from other sources; the AQI provides a useful communication tool but may underestimate actual risk during certain fire events.

3. Sensitive populations for smoke health effects include persons with respiratory disease, cardiovascular disease, older adults, children, pregnant individuals, and outdoor workers. Each group warrants targeted protective action guidance rather than generic "stay indoors" messaging.

4. AQI interpretation requires attention to monitoring station density and the limitations of low-cost sensor networks; PurpleAir values should have validated correction factors applied before use in public communication.

5. Shelter-in-place effectiveness is a function of housing quality and filtration capacity, not just behavioral compliance. Residents of lower-quality housing may receive near-zero protective benefit from standard shelter guidance without access to air purification.

6. Portable HEPA air purifiers, including DIY Corsi-Rosenthal Box designs, are evidence-supported and can be pre-positioned by emergency management for distribution to lower-income households before fire season.

7. Respiratory surge planning should begin before smoke arrives. Hospital demand planning, pharmacy coordination, outpatient capacity augmentation, and clean air refuge center logistics cannot be effectively initiated after the AQI crosses into unhealthy ranges.

8. WUI evacuation is a complex behavioral system with failure points at every cascade step; equity in evacuation requires addressing transportation access, medical needs, language access, and pet/livestock barriers as operational planning requirements.

9. Post-fire water contamination through benzene leaching into distribution systems is a documented, serious public health consequence of WUI fires that requires long-term monitoring well after firefighting operations end.

10. Mental health burden from wildfire events extends from direct fire survivors to smoke-exposed populations, with PTSD, depression, and community grief requiring sustained outreach-based support through mechanisms such as the Crisis Counseling Program.

11. Equity analysis of wildfire smoke health impact reveals that exposure burden, protective capacity, and recovery resources all correlate with socioeconomic position and race — meaning disparate health outcomes in smoke events are structurally produced, not randomly distributed.

## Chapter Notes

**Note 23-A: The Evolving Geography of Wildfire Smoke Risk.**
The historical framing of wildfire smoke as primarily a Western United States concern is no longer accurate. Major fires in Canada — including the record-setting 2023 Canadian wildfire season, which produced PM2.5 concentrations in the Hazardous AQI range in New York City, Washington, D.C., and Chicago — demonstrated that wildfire smoke is now a national public health threat. Emergency managers in every region must have smoke event annexes in their emergency operations plans. The Western experience in respiratory surge planning, clean air refuge center operations, and outdoor worker protection programs represents a body of operational knowledge that should be systematically transferred to non-Western jurisdictions in advance of events rather than improvised during them.

**Note 23-B: Occupational Health Regulations and Outdoor Worker Protection.**
California's Outdoor Worker Protection from Wildfire Smoke Regulation (CCR Title 8, Section 5141.1) established a model for occupational smoke protection that includes mandatory AQI monitoring, provision of N95 respirators at AQI > 150 for sensitive employees, and employee training requirements. As of this writing, few other states have implemented comparable occupational protection frameworks. The absence of regulatory requirements in many states means that outdoor worker protection during smoke events is largely dependent on voluntary employer action — an inadequate foundation for protecting the most-exposed workforce. Emergency managers should understand the regulatory landscape in their state and identify the gaps between existing protections and what would be needed to adequately protect outdoor workers.

**Note 23-C: The Corsi-Rosenthal Box and Democratizing Air Quality Protection.**
The DIY box fan air purifier design pioneered by Drs. Richard Corsi and Jim Rosenthal during the COVID-19 pandemic was adapted by public health practitioners for wildfire smoke applications and validated in several studies for PM2.5 reduction effectiveness. At a materials cost well under commercial HEPA units, the Corsi-Rosenthal Box enables bulk procurement by public health agencies for community distribution. It also enables self-manufacture from commercially available materials. The equity value of this technology for smoke response is substantial: it makes effective air filtration accessible to households that cannot afford commercial units and allows emergency management to pre-position air quality protection for distribution through community organizations at a cost that is scalable to budget realities. Any smoke event response plan should include Corsi-Rosenthal Box construction and distribution as a standard preparedness element.

**Note 23-D: Post-Fire Water Safety — A Newly Recognized Hazard.**
The Camp Fire (Paradise, California, 2018) produced evidence that combustion of plastic pipe materials and other hydrocarbon-rich materials in distribution system pipes can result in benzene and related compound contamination of treated drinking water that persists for months and is not eliminated by boiling. This mechanism — previously not on the radar of most water utilities or public health agencies — has now been documented in multiple post-WUI-fire water system investigations. As WUI fire events increase in frequency and severity, post-fire water system benzene screening should become a standard protocol, initiated immediately after fire threat has passed through a service area. Emergency managers should ensure that their water utility contacts are aware of this protocol requirement, and that long-term water system remediation planning is part of recovery operations for affected systems.

**Note 23-E: Long-Haul Mental Health and the Limits of Crisis Counseling.**
The Crisis Counseling Program is an outreach-based model designed for the acute to sub-acute disaster mental health response period. It is not a clinical treatment program and is not designed to provide sustained individual therapy for PTSD or major depression. The gap between what CCP provides and what fire survivors with clinically significant mental health conditions need — ongoing individual therapy, psychiatric medication management, trauma-focused evidence-based treatments — is real and results in persons falling out of care as CCP winds down, typically 18 months post-disaster. Emergency managers and public health officers should understand this gap and advocate for transition planning between CCP and community mental health systems, including targeted expansion of community mental health capacity in disaster-affected areas as part of the long-term recovery framework.
