# Chapter 24: Risk Communication and Public Information

---

## Chapter Overview

When a disaster threatens public health, the quality of information reaching the public determines whether people take protective action, comply with official guidance, or dismiss warnings entirely. Risk communication is not a support function — it is a core operational capability. A technically sound response can fail if the public does not understand what is happening, does not trust the source delivering the message, or receives conflicting guidance from multiple agencies.

This chapter presents the theory and practice of health risk communication in emergency settings. It begins with the psychological and social foundations that determine how people receive and act on risk information. It then examines the organizational structures — the Joint Information System and Joint Information Center — that coordinate public messaging across agencies. Subsequent sections cover message development, social media management, communicating with linguistically and culturally diverse populations, the special challenge of communicating under scientific uncertainty, and evaluating whether communication efforts achieved their goals.

Throughout, the chapter treats communication not as packaging for decisions made elsewhere, but as an inseparable part of emergency management itself. The decision to issue a shelter-in-place order, the choice to announce that resources are limited, the timing of a "all-clear" message — each of these is simultaneously an operational decision and a communication act with consequences for trust, compliance, and community resilience.

## 24.1 Foundations of Health Risk Communication

Risk communication draws from communication theory, social psychology, cognitive science, and public health practice. Understanding why people respond the way they do to risk information — rather than simply how to push messages out — is the foundation of effective practice.

### 24.1.1 Risk Perception Is Not Risk Calculation

People do not process risk information the way actuaries do. A hazard that is involuntary, unfamiliar, catastrophic in potential, and not personally controllable generates far more concern than a hazard that is statistically larger but voluntary, familiar, and under personal control. This observation, grounded in decades of psychometric research by Paul Slovic and colleagues [CITATION], explains why communities react with alarm to a chemical plant accident producing [NUMBER] parts per billion of a contaminant — while accepting tobacco use that causes far more measurable harm.

For emergency managers and public health communicators, this means that technical risk estimates alone rarely drive protective behavior. The emotional and social dimensions of a threat shape perception more reliably than probability numbers. Communicators who respond to high public concern by producing more detailed technical briefings are often solving the wrong problem.

### 24.1.2 The Sandman Model: Hazard and Outrage

Peter Sandman's framework [CITATION] offers a practical framework for diagnosing communication problems. In Sandman's formulation, public risk response is determined by two components: hazard (the technical magnitude of harm) and outrage (the emotional and social factors that amplify or suppress concern). The full formulation — Risk = Hazard + Outrage — implies that high-outrage, low-hazard situations (industrial accidents near residential neighborhoods) generate disproportionate public alarm, while low-outrage, high-hazard situations (sedentary lifestyles, medication nonadherence) generate insufficient concern.

Emergency managers encounter both failure modes. Communicating about a genuine but low-visibility slow-burn threat — carbon monoxide from portable generators after a power outage, for example — requires increasing appropriate concern. Communicating about a high-visibility threat with manageable risk — visible smoke plumes from a controlled industrial burn — requires managing alarm without dismissing legitimate concern. The appropriate communication strategy differs substantially between these two situations, and applying a generic "reassurance" template to the high-outrage scenario typically backfires.

### 24.1.3 Trust as the Prerequisite

Extensive research, including foundational work by Vincent Covello [CITATION], confirms that trust is the non-negotiable precondition for effective risk communication. Once trust is lost, technically accurate messages are perceived as self-serving, incomplete, or deceptive. Trust is built from perceived competence, perceived honesty, perceived concern for the affected community, and consistency between words and actions.

Trust takes years to build and hours to lose. Agencies that issue an overly reassuring message early in an event and then revise guidance upward — as occurred in the first weeks of multiple pandemic responses — face a trust deficit that persists through the remainder of the event. The operational implication: err toward honesty about uncertainty early, rather than projecting false confidence that later requires correction.

### 24.1.4 The Action Compact

Every emergency communication should be evaluated against a single standard: does it give the audience a specific, achievable action they can take now? The "action compact" principle [CITATION] holds that risk communication without a clear behavioral call creates anxiety without direction — which can increase harm rather than reduce it.

"Stay informed" is not an action compact. "Go inside now, close your windows, and turn your HVAC to recirculate" is an action compact. The difference matters. In the immediate acute phase of an emergency, messages without specific action instructions fail the populations most in need of guidance.

## 24.2 The Joint Information System and Joint Information Center

When multiple agencies respond to the same event, uncoordinated public communication creates confusion, contradicts protective actions, and undermines trust. The National Incident Management System addresses this through the Joint Information System (JIS) and, in major activations, the Joint Information Center (JIC).

### 24.2.1 The Joint Information System

The JIS is a coordination concept, not a physical facility. It establishes that all responding agencies — federal, state, local, and voluntary — share information, coordinate messages, and approve communications through a unified process. The JIS connects Public Information Officers (PIOs) across all participating agencies, establishes a single review and clearance process for public releases, and provides the mechanism through which a single authoritative voice emerges from a multi-agency response.

The JIS functions whether or not a formal JIC is activated. For smaller events, the JIS may operate virtually, with PIOs from the county public health department, the hospital coalition, and local emergency management reviewing and approving messages by phone and email. For large activations, the JIS is physically instantiated in a JIC.

### 24.2.2 Joint Information Center Structure and Operations

The JIC is a facility — physical or virtual — where PIOs from multiple agencies co-locate to produce, review, and release unified public information. A functional JIC includes the following roles: JIC Director (overall coordination and final release authority), Deputy Director, Writing Unit (message drafters), Media Relations Unit (press briefing coordination, press release distribution, media inquiry intake), Monitoring Unit (media monitoring, social media monitoring, rumor tracking), Stakeholder and Community Relations Unit (liaisons to community organizations, ethnic media, access-and-functional-needs networks), and Web and Digital Unit (website management, social media management).

JIC activation requires pre-established agreements among participating agencies covering who provides staff, where the JIC is located (including backup locations), how decisions are made, who holds final release authority, and how disagreements among agency PIOs are resolved. These agreements cannot be negotiated during an event — they must exist in advance.

Credentialing for media access, establishing a public inquiry hotline, and coordinating translation and interpretation services are operational functions that become logistically significant within hours of JIC activation. Agencies that pre-position these capabilities — including contract agreements with translation vendors, hotline staffing plans, and digital media protocols — activate far more effectively than those building these capabilities under operational pressure.

### 24.2.3 The Public Information Officer in Health Emergencies

The PIO in a health or medical emergency occupies a position that requires both communication skill and substantive health literacy. A PIO who cannot accurately explain the difference between isolation and quarantine, or who cannot answer basic questions about vaccine mechanism from a spokesperson position, creates a credibility liability for the response.

In large activations, health agencies often have a dedicated health spokesperson — a physician, epidemiologist, or public health official — who provides the substantive content of press briefings, with the PIO managing logistics, media relations, and the approval process. This division of labor works well when the health spokesperson has received at least basic media training and understands the communication principles that govern framing, message discipline, and handling hostile questions.

The spokesperson selection decision is consequential. Research consistently finds that trusted messengers — figures perceived as sharing the community's interests and values, not just institutional authority figures — achieve higher message uptake [CITATION]. In some communities, the health department director is that trusted figure. In others, a community health worker, a faith leader, or a recognized ethnic media personality carries more trust. Identifying and preparing these trusted messengers before an event is a preparedness investment with significant returns.

## 24.3 Message Development and Testing

Effective risk communication messages do not emerge spontaneously. They are developed through a deliberate process that begins with audience analysis, moves through structured message construction, and ideally incorporates pre-deployment testing.

### 24.3.1 Risk Communication Message Mapping

Message mapping, developed by Vincent Covello and colleagues [CITATION], provides a structured approach to key message construction. The standard architecture: three key messages per communication objective, with three supporting facts per key message, for a total of nine discrete factual statements organized under a coherent frame. The structure disciplines message development and produces a hierarchy that allows spokespeople to stay on-message under questioning.

For a boil-water advisory, a mapped message set might look like this. Key Message 1: The water supply has been affected and you must boil water before drinking it. Supporting facts: (1) [PATHOGEN/HAZARD] was detected at [LOCATION] in [TIMEFRAME]; (2) boiling water for at least one minute at a rolling boil kills disease-causing organisms; (3) affected areas include [GEOGRAPHIC AREA] — the map is at [URL]. Key Message 2: Bottled water is available. Key Message 3: We will tell you when it is safe to drink tap water without boiling. Each key message then has its own three supporting facts.

This structure is particularly valuable when multiple spokespeople are delivering messages across multiple channels. Message discipline — staying on the three key messages — prevents the scenario where a county health official, a city emergency manager, and a hospital spokesperson each emphasize different aspects of an event, leaving the public uncertain about what to do.

### 24.3.2 Plain Language and Health Literacy

Federal plain language requirements [CITATION] establish that government communications should be written in language the intended audience can understand on first reading. This standard is rarely met in health emergency communications, which routinely use clinical terminology, probabilistic language, and passive voice constructions that significantly reduce comprehension.

Health literacy — the capacity to understand and act on health information — varies widely across populations. Approximately [PERCENT] of U.S. adults read at or below a [GRADE LEVEL] reading level [CITATION]. Emergency communications written at a college reading level fail a substantial portion of the population during a period when clear action guidance is most critical.

Practical plain language principles for emergency health communications include: use active voice; write for a sixth to eighth grade reading level (as measured by validated readability tools); avoid medical jargon or define technical terms when unavoidable; use short sentences and simple paragraph structure; place the most important information first; provide the action instruction before the explanation; and use numbers only when essential, replacing probabilistic language with concrete descriptions of what that probability means for a person's decision.

### 24.3.3 Numeracy and Risk Numbers

Risk communication frequently involves numbers — case counts, mortality rates, vaccine efficacy percentages, parts per billion, distance thresholds. These numbers are processed very differently by different audiences. Research on numeracy [CITATION] demonstrates that a substantial portion of adults cannot correctly interpret percentage-based risk information, and that framing effects (presenting the same risk as "1 in 100" versus "1% chance" versus "100 per 10,000") substantially alter risk perception.

For operational emergency communications, the implication is to translate statistical risk statements into concrete, action-relevant terms wherever possible. "You have a 1% chance of serious illness" is less useful than "Most people who follow this guidance will recover at home; if you experience [SPECIFIC SYMPTOMS], call [NUMBER] for guidance on whether to go to a hospital." The latter tells the audience what to watch for and what to do, rather than asking them to interpret a probability.

### 24.3.4 Pre-Deployment Message Testing

Message testing before deployment — even rapid, informal testing under time pressure — consistently identifies failures that would have reduced communication effectiveness. Cognitive interviews with a small sample of community members from the target audience can reveal whether key terms are understood, whether the action instruction is clear, and whether unintended connotations are activated by specific word choices.

Few public health or emergency management agencies have built rapid message testing capacity into their preparedness infrastructure. Those that have — with pre-established community partner relationships, translator networks, and protocols for rapid [NUMBER]-hour cognitive interview processes — can test messages even in acute activations. The alternative is deploying untested messages and discovering failures after the fact through media monitoring and incoming inquiries.

## 24.4 Social Media and Digital Communication in Emergencies

Social media has fundamentally changed the risk communication environment. Official agencies no longer control the information space. They operate in an environment where false information propagates faster than corrective information, where platform algorithms reward engagement over accuracy, and where community trust networks — not agency authority — determine whose messages are believed.

### 24.4.1 Speed-Accuracy Tension

The canonical problem in social media emergency communication is the speed-accuracy tension. Official agencies, appropriately, apply verification, legal review, and clearance processes before releasing information. These processes take time. Informal social media networks propagate early, unverified, often inaccurate information into wide circulation before official agencies release verified messages.

The operational response to this tension is not to abandon verification, but to reduce clearance time through pre-established approval processes, to acknowledge events publicly and quickly even before the full picture is clear ("We are aware of reports of [INCIDENT]. We are investigating and will provide an update within [TIMEFRAME]."), and to establish the official channels as the fastest reliable source rather than the most accurate but slowest source.

Agencies that maintain active social media presence before emergencies — regularly posting accurate, useful public health information — have established credibility and audience when emergencies occur. Agencies that activate dormant channels during an emergency are disadvantaged from the start.

### 24.4.2 Rumor Monitoring and Rapid Response

JIC monitoring units and agency social media staff should be tracking specific rumor types throughout an activation: false symptom lists attributed to official sources, incorrect treatment claims, fabricated quotes from officials, manipulated images, and location-specific rumors that spread through neighborhood-level networks (Nextdoor, community Facebook groups).

The "acknowledge-explain-correct" protocol provides a structured approach to rumor response. Rather than simply stating "that is false," effective rumor correction acknowledges why the false belief is understandable (acknowledging the underlying concern), explains the accurate information, and provides a specific correct alternative. Direct contradiction ("That is false. The truth is X.") without acknowledgment tends to backfire with audiences already inclined toward distrust [CITATION].

Pre-bunking — informing audiences in advance about the types of misinformation likely to circulate, and providing them a frame for evaluating new information — is more effective than post-hoc correction when time allows. Inoculation theory [CITATION] provides the research basis: explaining the rhetorical techniques used to spread misinformation (false authority attribution, emotional manipulation, cherry-picked evidence) increases resistance to that misinformation even before specific false claims are encountered.

### 24.4.3 Platform-Specific Considerations

Different platforms carry different population segments and require different communication approaches. Twitter/X skews toward media professionals, policy analysts, and politically engaged audiences — it shapes what journalists report, but reaches a narrower slice of the general public than often assumed. Facebook remains the platform with highest reach across older adult and rural populations in the United States [CITATION]. TikTok carries substantial reach with younger adults and is increasingly a primary information source for the 18-34 demographic. Nextdoor and community-specific Facebook groups are the primary informal information channels at the neighborhood level and are critical for localized emergency guidance.

Ethnic media — Spanish-language television and radio, Vietnamese community newspapers, Somali diaspora news networks — reach populations that mainstream English-language media does not. Many ethnic media outlets maintain strong community trust precisely because they are seen as advocates for their communities rather than government mouthpieces. Establishing relationships with ethnic media before emergencies, including agreements for rapid translation and priority information access, is a preparedness investment that pays off substantially when community-specific guidance must be delivered quickly.

### 24.4.4 Managing Official Digital Infrastructure

During major activations, official websites, social media accounts, and public inquiry hotlines become critical public utilities. Many agencies discover during activations that their websites cannot handle the traffic surge, that their social media accounts lack the staffing to respond to incoming queries, and that their hotlines are overwhelmed within hours.

Pre-event infrastructure planning should include: website stress testing and content delivery network arrangements for high-traffic periods; staffing plans for extended social media monitoring and response operations; integration of chatbot tools for high-volume inquiry handling (with clear routing to human staff for complex inquiries); and emergency activation protocols for digital communications staff, including provisions for 24/7 coverage during sustained events.

## 24.5 Communicating with Diverse and Vulnerable Populations

A risk communication strategy that reaches only English-speaking, digitally connected, health-literate adults with reliable media access has failed before it began. Equitable emergency communication requires active effort to reach populations who are systematically underserved by default communication channels.

### 24.5.1 Legal Framework for Language Access

Title VI of the Civil Rights Act prohibits federal funding recipients — which includes virtually every public health department and emergency management agency in the United States — from discriminating on the basis of national origin [CITATION]. Executive Order 13166 [YEAR] requires federal agencies and funding recipients to take reasonable steps to provide meaningful access to programs and activities for persons with limited English proficiency (LEP). The Department of Justice and the Department of Health and Human Services have issued guidance establishing that language access to emergency services is covered by these requirements.

The "four-factor analysis" established in federal guidance provides a framework for determining the extent of language access obligations: (1) the number or proportion of LEP persons in the service area; (2) the frequency with which LEP persons encounter the program; (3) the nature and importance of the program; (4) available resources. For life-safety emergency communications, the four-factor analysis is heavily weighted toward extensive language access obligations — the consequences of inadequate communication are severe, and courts have found that emergencies do not suspend legal obligations.

### 24.5.2 Translation, Interpretation, and Cultural Competence

Translation (written) and interpretation (spoken/signed) are distinct functions requiring distinct resources. Emergency health communications require both. Translation of written materials — including press releases, fact sheets, social media posts, and shelter instructions — requires not just linguistic translation but cultural adaptation: health concepts do not translate directly across cultures, and direct translation of English-language content often produces technically accurate but culturally inappropriate or practically confusing materials.

Interpretation for public briefings, phone hotlines, and shelter operations requires access to qualified interpreters — not bilingual staff who are asked to perform interpretation as a secondary duty. The distinction matters: untrained bilingual interpreters consistently make substantive errors in medical and emergency contexts that can produce harmful guidance [CITATION].

Pre-established contracts with language service providers for translation and over-the-phone interpretation are a preparedness standard. Language Line and similar services provide immediate telephonic interpretation in [NUMBER] languages and can be activated within minutes. For planned press conferences or community briefings, in-person American Sign Language interpretation and provision of materials in accessible formats (large print, Braille on request, audio format) are required under the Americans with Disabilities Act.

### 24.5.3 Trusted Messenger Networks

The most effective channel for reaching communities with low institutional trust — which often correlates with communities that have historically experienced discrimination or harm from government institutions — is peer-to-peer communication through trusted community figures.

Community health workers (CHWs), promotoras, village health advocates, faith leaders, and ethnic media personalities often carry more credibility within their communities than any government official. They are embedded in the social networks through which health information actually flows in underserved communities. Emergency communication plans that identify and pre-establish relationships with these figures — that include them in planning, not just outreach — consistently outperform plans that treat community organizations as translation distribution channels.

The distinction between using community organizations as passive distributors versus active co-developers of communication is significant. Co-development involves community partners in message review, message testing, and adaptation before messages are deployed. It requires lead time and genuine relationship investment. The reward is messages that arrive in communities through trusted channels, framed in terms that are culturally meaningful, at a time when institutional channels may be distrusted.

### 24.5.4 Communication Access for People with Disabilities

The Americans with Disabilities Act and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act require emergency communications to be accessible to people with disabilities [CITATION]. In practice, this means: ASL interpretation at press briefings and community meetings; captioning of live video broadcasts; captioned versions of recorded video on all platforms; accessible website and PDF formatting (meeting WCAG 2.1 AA standards at minimum); easy-read format materials for people with cognitive disabilities; and accommodations for people who are blind or have low vision.

Emergency alert systems have specific accessibility gaps. The Wireless Emergency Alert (WEA) system delivers text-based alerts to mobile devices — a format that is inaccessible to many people who are blind without screen reader technology, and unavailable to people without mobile devices. Emergency notifications via television use Emergency Alert System (EAS) tones that are startling and disorienting without context. Planning for accessible alert delivery across multiple channels, including TTY/TDD notification, Notification Access System registration, and partnerships with disability service organizations, is a legal and ethical imperative, not an optional enhancement.

### 24.5.5 Rural Communication Infrastructure Gaps

Rural populations face structural communication barriers that urban planning assumptions do not address. Broadband internet access is unavailable or unreliable across large portions of rural America [CITATION]. Cell phone coverage gaps are common. Alert systems designed around mobile delivery fail in areas with limited wireless coverage. Commercial AM radio — often dismissed as obsolete — remains the most reliable broadcast medium in many rural areas during power outages and communication infrastructure disruptions.

Rural emergency communication plans must account for these realities. Pre-positioned partnerships with rural radio stations, reverse-notification systems using landline telephony, outdoor warning sirens with accompanying community education, and door-to-door notification capacity for areas without reliable electronic communication are all elements of equitable rural emergency communication planning.

## 24.6 Communicating Under Uncertainty

Many of the most consequential risk communication challenges in health emergencies involve communicating about what is not yet known. A novel pathogen with an uncertain case fatality rate; a chemical release with an incompletely characterized exposure; a food contamination event with an as-yet unidentified source — each requires communicating before the science is settled.

### 24.6.1 The Case for Honesty About Uncertainty

Research consistently shows that honest, explicit acknowledgment of uncertainty is better received — and produces more appropriate protective behavior — than false confidence that is later revised [CITATION]. Audiences are more capable of living with uncertainty than emergency managers typically assume. What they are not capable of tolerating is learning that they were given false confidence.

The operative formula for uncertainty communication: acknowledge what you know, acknowledge what you do not know, explain what you are doing to close the gap, and give the action implication of the current state of knowledge. "We know X. We do not yet know Y. We are doing [SPECIFIC ACTIONS] to find out. Based on what we know now, we recommend [ACTION]." This formula is repeatable, honest, and actionable.

### 24.6.2 Staged Decision Announcements

In rapidly evolving events, communicating about decisions as they are made — rather than waiting for the complete picture — reduces the information vacuum that misinformation fills. Staged announcements acknowledge that a situation is evolving, that additional information is being gathered, and that guidance may be updated. They commit to a specific timeline for updates and specify what information is being sought.

This approach requires coordination across agency PIOs and leadership to ensure that each staged announcement is accurate, that conflicting statements are not made across agencies, and that the commitment to update timelines is kept. Missing a committed update time — even if nothing new has been learned — damages credibility substantially.

### 24.6.3 Scientific Consensus vs. Evolving Evidence

Many health emergencies involve rapid evolution of scientific understanding. The challenge of communicating this to a public accustomed to receiving science as a fixed set of facts — rather than a process of evolving probabilistic knowledge — became acutely visible during the COVID-19 pandemic, when guidance on masking, surface contamination, and vaccine-acquired immunity was updated multiple times as evidence accumulated.

Communication strategies for evolving science include explaining the nature of scientific consensus and its basis; distinguishing between established consensus (broad scientific agreement based on accumulated evidence) and preliminary findings (early studies requiring replication and peer review); explaining why guidance changes as evidence improves (framing updates as the system working correctly, not as prior failure); and naming in advance the types of evidence that would trigger guidance changes, so that when changes occur, the audience has a framework for understanding why.

These strategies do not eliminate public confusion or distrust, but they reduce it. Communicators who treat the public as capable of understanding uncertainty — rather than protecting them from it — build more durable credibility.

### 24.6.4 Addressing Specific False Claims

False health claims spread fastest when they address a genuine anxiety that official communications have not yet answered, or when they originate from a source with apparent authority (a physician's social media post, a manipulated screenshot of an official document, a claim attributed to a named official who did not make the statement).

Effective response to specific false claims requires: rapid internal verification of what is accurate; a direct, factual correction that does not repeat the false claim prominently (repeating the false claim in the act of correcting it amplifies the falsehood via the "continued influence effect" [CITATION]); routing corrective information through the same channels where the misinformation spread; and if the false claim came from a specific named source, deciding case-by-case whether direct attribution of the error increases or decreases credibility — direct engagement with high-visibility figures amplifies them; engagement with institutional misinformation often requires direct attribution.

## 24.7 Evaluating Risk Communication Effectiveness

Communication without evaluation is activity, not strategy. Emergency communication programs should measure both process and outcome, building learning into the communication function itself.

### 24.7.1 Process Metrics

Process metrics measure what was done: number of press releases issued; number of briefings held; number of social media posts published; number of languages in which materials were produced; coverage in media monitoring (earned media); social media reach and engagement metrics; hotline call volume; website unique visitors and page views. These metrics are important for accountability and resource justification, but they measure output, not impact.

### 24.7.2 Outcome Metrics

Outcome metrics measure whether communication achieved its goals. The primary outcome metric is behavior change: did people who received the message take the protective action it recommended? Secondary outcome metrics include awareness (did people receive and recall the message?), knowledge (do people accurately understand the health threat and the recommended action?), and intention (do people intend to take the recommended action?).

Rapid knowledge, awareness, and intention surveys — deployed via random telephone sampling, online panels, or community health worker networks — can provide near-real-time feedback on message effectiveness during an event. Most public health agencies do not have this capacity built in advance. Those that have established survey instruments, sampling frames, and analysis capacity as a preparedness function have a significant advantage in adaptive communication management.

### 24.7.3 After-Action Communication Review

Post-event review of communication effectiveness should be a standard element of every After-Action Report. Communication-specific elements should include: timeline reconstruction of when key messages were developed, cleared, and released; comparison of official message timelines to misinformation spread timelines; analysis of which channels reached which population segments; documentation of language access failures and successes; and assessment of whether communication outcomes (awareness, behavior change) were achieved across demographic groups.

Communication after-action review is particularly important for assessing equity gaps. If protective action compliance was significantly lower among non-English-speaking populations, among populations with disabilities, or among rural communities, the communication system failed those populations regardless of how many press releases were issued. Naming those failures explicitly and building corrective actions into preparedness plans is the operational purpose of after-action review.

## Applied Vignette

**Setting:** [CITY], a [POPULATION] mid-sized city with a [PERCENT] Hispanic/Latino population, [PERCENT] of whom speak primarily Spanish, and a growing Somali community of approximately [NUMBER] residents. The [CITY] Public Health Department (CPHD) has one full-time PIO and two staff with social media responsibilities.

**Event:** Over a [TIMEFRAME] period in [MONTH], [YEAR], [CITY] experiences a cluster of severe respiratory illness cases primarily among residents of the [NEIGHBORHOOD] neighborhood, located near [INDUSTRIAL FACILITY TYPE]. Cases involve [NUMBER] confirmed hospitalizations, [NUMBER] deaths, and an unknown number of people with moderate illness who have not sought care. The cause is initially unclear — possibilities include a novel respiratory pathogen and an industrial emission event.

**Communication Challenges Encountered:**

Within the first [NUMBER] hours, a social media post from a [NEIGHBORHOOD] resident — claiming that the industrial facility had released [CHEMICAL] and that [NUMBER] people had died — spread to [NUMBER] shares across community Facebook groups and the [CITY] Nextdoor network. The post attributed a fabricated quote to the CPHD Director. The CPHD was not yet aware of the cluster; the social media monitoring function had not been activated.

When CPHD did issue a first public statement at [TIMEFRAME], the English-language press release went to [NUMBER] media outlets. No Spanish-language translation was produced for [TIMEFRAME] after the English release. The Somali community received no information in any form for [TIMEFRAME] — the department's pre-established Somali community liaison was not contacted until day [NUMBER] of the event. The Somali community's primary information source during this gap was a misinformation chain on [PLATFORM] attributing the cluster to a deliberate act.

CPHD's JIC activation was delayed by [TIMEFRAME] because the emergency director and health director disagreed about whether the event met the activation threshold. By the time the JIC was operational, [NUMBER] media inquiries had accumulated with no response, and community organizations were publicly criticizing the health department's silence.

**Three Identified Gaps:**

Gap 1: No social media monitoring protocol was in place. The rumor spread for [TIMEFRAME] before CPHD was aware of it, and the fabricated official quote remained uncorrected in wide circulation for [TIMEFRAME] after CPHD became aware.

Gap 2: Language access planning had a documented Spanish-language protocol but no Somali-language protocol despite the community's growth over the preceding [TIMEFRAME]. Correcting this gap during the event required [TIMEFRAME] of reactive improvisation that involved recruiting an unvetted community volunteer as an interpreter — a practice that poses both accuracy and confidentiality risks.

Gap 3: JIC activation criteria were ambiguous. The disagreement between directors reflected the absence of pre-negotiated threshold criteria — the protocol stated that the JIC would activate for "significant public health events" without defining significance. Clarifying thresholds in advance would have eliminated the delay.

**Planning Commitments:** Following the event, CPHD developed and signed a language access plan covering [NUMBER] languages based on census LEP population thresholds; established pre-event contracts with two language service vendors covering [NUMBER] languages; built a social media monitoring protocol into standard incident response procedures with a dedicated monitoring function activating within [NUMBER] hours of any public health incident above a defined threshold; and negotiated JIC activation criteria specifying automatic activation for events meeting any of [NUMBER] defined thresholds.

## Key Terms

**Action Compact** — The principle that every risk communication message should include a specific, achievable action the audience can take immediately.

**Crisis and Emergency Risk Communication (CERC)** — A framework developed by the CDC for applying risk communication principles throughout the emergency management cycle.

**Health Literacy** — The capacity to obtain, process, and understand basic health information and services needed to make appropriate health decisions.

**Inoculation Theory** — A communication strategy that pre-exposes audiences to weakened forms of misinformation to increase resistance to later exposure to full misinformation.

**Joint Information Center (JIC)** — A physical or virtual facility where Public Information Officers from multiple agencies co-locate to coordinate public communication during an emergency response.

**Joint Information System (JIS)** — The overarching coordination concept establishing unified public information processes across all participating agencies in an emergency response.

**Limited English Proficiency (LEP)** — A designation applying to individuals who do not speak English as their primary language and who have a limited ability to read, write, speak, or understand English.

**Message Mapping** — A structured approach to key message development using three main messages, each supported by three factual statements.

**Plain Language** — Writing that is clear, concise, and organized in a way the intended audience can understand and use on the first reading.

**Pre-Bunking** — A communication strategy that explains the techniques used to spread misinformation before audiences encounter specific false claims, increasing resistance.

**Public Information Officer (PIO)** — An individual designated to serve as the spokesperson and communication coordinator for an agency or jurisdiction in an emergency.

**Risk Perception** — The subjective assessment of the magnitude and nature of a risk, shaped by psychological, social, and cultural factors beyond statistical probability.

**Trusted Messenger** — A community figure whose credibility within a specific population is based on perceived shared interests and values, enabling higher message uptake than institutional authority alone.

**Trusted Messenger Network** — A pre-established system of community figures — including community health workers, faith leaders, and ethnic media personalities — integrated into emergency communication planning.

## Discussion Questions

1. A public health official is asked at a press briefing whether a newly identified cluster of respiratory illness cases is caused by a novel pathogen. The investigation is ongoing and no pathogen has been confirmed. The official is uncertain whether to say "we don't know yet" (accurate but potentially alarming) or to offer a more reassuring framing. What communication principles should guide this response, and what are the risks of each approach?

2. Your jurisdiction's limited-English-proficiency population includes [NUMBER] primary languages per census data, but your agency's current language access plan covers only [NUMBER]. Budget constraints make comprehensive multilingual capability difficult. How should you prioritize language access investment, and what legal and ethical frameworks govern that decision?

3. ## Practitioner Takeaways

Understanding risk perception theory — not just communication mechanics — is what separates effective emergency communicators from effective press release writers. The emotional and social dimensions of public response to risk require communicators who can diagnose why a community is responding the way it is, not just who know how to produce technically accurate messages.

Trust is built before emergencies. Agencies that engage communities through routine health communication, that show up in communities rather than issuing press releases about them, and that include community members in preparedness planning — those agencies have a trust account to draw on when a crisis depletes it. Agencies beginning from a trust deficit during an emergency face a substantially harder communication task.

Language access is a legal obligation and a moral one, but it is also a practical operational requirement. A community that cannot understand protective action guidance is not protected. Undocumented immigrants who fear contacting government agencies during a hazardous materials release are exposed to preventable harm. Communication equity is public health.

Uncertainty is not the enemy of credibility. Honest, specific acknowledgment of what is not yet known — accompanied by a clear description of what is being done to close the gap — consistently outperforms false confidence. The post-event credibility of emergency managers who communicate honestly about uncertainty is higher, not lower, than those who project certainty they do not have.

Social media monitoring is an operational function, not an optional enhancement. The information environment that exists before official messages arrive shapes how those messages are received. Agencies that monitor, assess, and respond to the social media information environment are operating in the real world; agencies that issue press releases into a vacuum are not.

## Chapter Notes

**Note on CERC and the CDC Framework:** The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention's Crisis and Emergency Risk Communication (CERC) framework provides the most widely used practitioner guidance for applying risk communication principles in public health emergencies. CERC organizes communication guidance around the phases of an emergency, provides specific direction for common communication challenges (including uncertainty communication and rumor response), and offers training materials that translate theory into practice. Emergency managers and public health communicators should be familiar with the CERC manual and the CERC Academy training platform. The framework has been updated through multiple editions and reflects lessons from major activations, including H1N1, Zika, and COVID-19. Students and practitioners should seek the current edition, as specific guidance evolves.

**Note on Title VI, EO 13166, and Enforcement Reality:** While Title VI and EO 13166 establish clear language access obligations for federally funded programs, enforcement has been uneven. The Office of Civil Rights at HHS has issued guidance affirming that language access obligations apply to emergency operations, and civil rights complaints have been filed following disasters in which LEP populations received inadequate communication. Practitioners should be aware that the compliance standard is "meaningful access" — not merely translation of some materials — and that a formal Language Access Plan meeting the four-factor analysis standard is considered a best practice that also serves as documentation of good-faith compliance effort. Jurisdictions without a current Language Access Plan are operating with both an equity gap and a legal exposure.

**Note on Trusted Messengers and the Undocumented Immigrant Population:** The hazardous materials communication challenge for undocumented immigrant populations — who may avoid calling 911 or attending public briefings due to immigration enforcement fears — illustrates the limits of standard emergency communication channels. Community health workers, community-based organizations, and faith leaders who serve immigrant communities can bridge this gap, but only if they have been included in pre-event planning and trust that the health department will keep its commitments (including not sharing personal information with enforcement agencies). Some jurisdictions have formalized this commitment through explicit policies or ordinances limiting information sharing. Emergency managers should understand their jurisdiction's policy environment and the community's perception of it, rather than assuming that formally neutral policies are experienced as protective by communities with historically grounded reasons for distrust.

**Note on the "Continued Influence Effect" and Correction Strategy:** Decades of psychological research have documented that corrections of misinformation are less effective than typically assumed, and that repeating the false claim in the process of correcting it can actually strengthen its retention. The "continued influence effect" describes the persistence of misinformation even after explicit correction — people's mental models of an event, once formed around a false claim, are resistant to revision. This finding has significant implications for how emergency communicators write corrections: corrections should lead with the correct information, not with a repetition of the false claim being corrected; they should provide a causal explanation (not just "that is wrong, this is right") that gives the audience a coherent narrative to replace the false one; and they should acknowledge the valid underlying concern that the misinformation addressed. This last element is the one most frequently omitted by institutional communicators who find acknowledging any validity in a false claim uncomfortable.

**Note on Digital Equity and Emergency Alerting:** The assumption that effective emergency alerting can be achieved through digital channels alone fails approximately [PERCENT] of the U.S. population that lacks reliable broadband access, and additional segments of the population that have access but lack the digital literacy to navigate emergency information online. WEA reaches cell phone users within designated alert zones but does not reach people without mobile devices, people with devices in airplane mode, and people in areas of inadequate cellular coverage. EAS reaches cable and broadcast television viewers but not cord-cutters accessing streaming-only. No single alerting channel achieves universal reach. The FCC's research on alert and warning effectiveness [CITATION] consistently identifies multi-channel redundancy as the only reliable approach, including outdoor siren systems, door-to-door notification for targeted high-risk areas, and partnerships with community organizations and ethnic media that operate independent distribution channels.
